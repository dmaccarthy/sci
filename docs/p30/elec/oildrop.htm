Millikan’s Experiment

Robert Millikan devised an experiment to measure the charge on a tiny droplet of oil.

  • A fine mist of tiny droplets is produced using an “atomizer” (spray bottle).
  • Oil is used rather than water or alcohol because the oil will not evaporate as easily.
  • The oil droplets may be charged by friction as they leave the atomizer.
  • Alternatively, an x-ray machine can be used to ionize air molecules; electrons lost from the air will attach themselves to an oil droplet.
  • Charged oil droplets enter the electric field between two parallel plates.
  • Observing through a microscope, Millikan adjusted the potential difference between the plates so that the upward electric force exactly balanced the downward gravity:

    F_e = F_g

    q|\va{E}| = mg

    q = \frac{mg}{|\va{E}|}

  • The electric field is easily calculated from the potential difference and the separation between the plates.
  • Measuring the mass is more difficult because the droplets are too small for an electronic balance...

Mass & Terminal Speed

  • Because they are so small, the oil droplets experience a significant air resistance as they move.
  • Without the electric field, droplets tend to fall at a steady rate called the terminal speed.
  • The terminal speed is faster for larger droplets; a droplet’s mass can be calculated from its terminal speed.
  • After recording the voltage necessary to suspend an oil droplet, Millikan turned off the electric field and measured how much time it took for the droplet to fall a known distance.
  • This measurement allows calculation of terminal speed, mass, and charge.
  • After calculating the charge of hundereds of different oil drops...
  • Millikan observed that the oil droplets always had a charge that was a multiple of \rm 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C.

  • Millikan concluded that this must be the elementary charge (the charge of a single electron or proton).

Practice

  1. In a Millikan-type experiment, a 3.21 × 10–14 kg, negatively-charged oil drop is suspended between charged plates that are 1.00 cm apart and are maintained at a potential difference of 3.94 × 103 V.
    1. Is the top plate positive or negative?

      2. The electric force must be upward to balance gravity. Since the oil drop is negative, the top plate must be positive.

    2. How many excess electrons does the oil drop have?

      3. The electric field is...

      |\va{E}| = \left|\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va{d}}\right| = \rm \left|\frac{3.93\times 10^3\ V}{0.0100\ m}\right| = 3.94\times 10^5\ N/C

      Since the forces balance...

      F_e = F_g

      |q\va{E}| = mg

      |q| = \frac{mg}{|\va{E|}} = \rm \frac{3.21\times 10^{-14}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{3.94\times 10^5\ N/C}

      |q| = \frac{mg}{|\va{E|}} = {\rm 7.99\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 5e

    3. If the oil droplet were exposed to x-rays, causing it to lose two of those excess electrons, to what potential difference would the plates need to be adjusted to keep the droplet suspended?

      4. Since the forces balance...

      F_e = F_g

      |q\va{E}| = mg

      |\va{E}| = \frac{mg}{|q|}

      \left|\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va{d}}\right| = \frac{mg}{|q|}

      \Delta V = \frac{mg|\Delta\va{d}|}{|q|}

      \Delta V = \rm \frac{3.21\times 10^{-14}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.0100\ m}{3\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 6.56\times 10^3\ V

    1. If an oil drop has a mass of 8.75 × 10–15 kg and has an excess of 7 protons, what electric field strength is required to suspend the drop between charged plates? What voltage would be needed if the plates were 2.54 cm apart?

      2. As in #1c...

      |\va{E}| = \frac{mg}{|q|} = \rm \frac{8.75\times 10^{-15}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{7\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 7.66\times 10^4\ N/C

      \Delta V = |\va{E}\cdot\Delta\va{d}| = \rm 7.66\times 10^4\ N/C \cdot 0.0254\ m = 1.94\times 10^3\ V

    2. Calculate the initial acceleration of the oil drop if the electric field strength is reduced to 5.00 × 104 N/C. Would the acceleration remain constant? Explain.

      3. \va{F}_{net} = \va{F}_g + \va{F}_e = m\va{g} + q\va{E}

      \va{F}_{net} = \rm 8.75\times 10^{-15}\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2) + 1.12\times 10^{-18}\ C\cdot (5.00\times 10^4\ N/C)

      \va{F}_{net} = \rm -2.98\times 10^{-14}\ N

      \va{a} = \frac{\va{F}_{net}}{m} = \rm \frac{-2.98\times 10^{-14}\ N}{8.75\times 10^{-15}\ kg} = -3.41\ m/s^2

      The acceleration would quickly fall to zero due to air resistance as the oil drop reaches its terminal speed.
