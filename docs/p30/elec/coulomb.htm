Coulomb’s Law

Coulomb’s Hypothesis

The force of gravity between two masses, according to Newton, is:

F_g = {Gm_1 m_2\over r^2}

Coulomb proposed that a similar relationship might describe the electric force between two charges:

F_e = \left|{kq_1 q_2\over r^2}\right|

  • The electric force should depend on the charges (q_1 and q_2) instead of the masses.
  • The constant (k) for electric force must be different than for gravity (G); charge and mass have different units.

Coulomb’s Experiment

To test whether his hypothesis was correct, Coulomb measured the electric force using a torsion balance.

  • A torsion balance was later used by Cavendish to measure gravitational force.
  • When a torsion (twisting force) is applied, the apparatus will twist until the wire’s tendency to untwist balances the torsion.
  • The angle at which the torsions balance tells us how strong the force is.
  • Coulomb manipulated the separation between the charges to see how the electric force would change.
  • If the hypothesis is correct a graph of the raw data (F_e vs. r) will be non-linear.
  • A graph of F_e vs. r^{-2} should show a direct relationship with a slope kq_1 q_2.
  • If we can measure the two charges, we can calculate k from the slope of the graph.

Coulomb’s Law

F_e = \left|{kq_1 q_2\over r^2}\right|

The accepted value of Coulomb’s constant is:

k = 8.99\times 10^9\ \rm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}

  • This means that two charges of 1 coulomb each that are 1 metre apart will repel (or attract if they have opposite signs) with a force of 8.99 billion newtons!
  • The electric force is typically much stronger than gravity.
  • Technically, Coulomb’s Law only applies to point charges.
  • Coulomb’s Law can be used as an approximation when the separation between the objects is much larger than the size of the objects.
  • The separation r is measured from the centres of the charges.
  • The equation tells us only the magnitude of the electric force.
  • The direction of the force depends on whether the charges attract (opposite sign) or repel (same sign).

Example 1

Calculate the electric force between a +5.00 μC charge and a −2.00 μC charge that are 20.0 cm apart.

F_e = \rm \frac{(8.99\times 10^9\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{C^2}) (5.00\times 10^{-6}\ C) (2.00\times 10^{-6}\ C)}{(0.200\ m)^2} = 2.25\ N

This force is attractive.

Example 2

Calculate the separation between two 75.0 nC charges that repel each other with a force of 1.50 newtons.

F_e = \left|{kq_1 q_2\over r^2}\right|

F_e r^2 = |kq_1q_2|

r^2 = {|kq_1q_2|\over F_e}

r = \sqrt{|kq_1q_2|\over F_e} = \rm{{8.99\times 10^9 (7.50\times 10^{-8})^2\over 1.50}\ m = 5.81\ mm}

Inverse Square Law

Coulomb’s Law is an inverse square law.

  • This means that when the separation is doubled, the force will be four times weaker!

Example 3

Two charges, q_1 and q_2, exert an electric force F_1 on each other when separated by a distance r. If one of the charges is doubled and the separation is tripled, the electric force becomes F_2. Calculate the ratio F_2:F_1.

F_1 = \left|{kq_1 q_2\over r^2}\right|

F_2 = \left|{k(2q_1)q_2\over(3r)^2}\right| = {2\over 9}\left|{kq_1 q_2\over r^2}\right|

{F_2 \over F_1} = {2\over 9} = 0.\bar{2}

{F_1 \over F_2} = {9\over 2} = 4.5

Practice

  1. Determine the electric force between charges of +6.00 microcoulombs and –6.00 μC that are separated by 32.0 cm. Is the force attractive or repulsive?

    2. F_e = \frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}

    F_e = \rm \frac{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\ (6.00\times 10^{-6}\ C)^2}{(0.320\ m)^2}

    F_e = \rm 3.16\ N

    The force is attractive because the charges have opposite signs.

  2. Two charges of +2.50 μC exert forces of 65.0 N on each other. What is the separation between the two charges? Is the force attractive or repulsive?

    3. F_e = \frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}

    F_e r^2 = k|q_1 q_2|

    r^2 = \frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{F_e}

    r = \sqrt{\frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{F_e}}

    r = \rm \sqrt{\frac{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\cdot (2.50\times 10^{-6}\ C)^2}{65.0\ N}}

    r = \rm 0.0294\ m

    The force is repulsive since the charges are both positive.

  3. When charges q_1 and q_2 are placed a distance r apart, the magnitude of the electrical force they experience is F_1. When charges 2.00 q_1 and 3.00 q_2 are placed a distance 2.00 r apart, the magnitude of the force is F_2. Determine the ratio F_2 : F_1.

    4. F_1 = \frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}

    F_2 = \frac{k|(2.00q_1) (3.00q_2)|}{(2.00r)^2}

    F_2 = \frac{6.00 k|q_1 q_2|}{4.00r^2} = 1.50 \frac{k|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}

    When we divide the forces, all of the variables cancel!

    \frac{F_2}{F_1} = 1.50

  4. An electron has a charge of –1.60 × 10–19 C and a proton has a charge of +1.60 × 10–19 C. In a hydrogen atom, the electron orbits the proton at a radius of about 5.29 × 10–11 m.

    1. Determine the electric force experienced by the electron.

      2. F_e = \frac{k|q_1q_2|}{r^2}

      F_e = \rm \frac{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\ (1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C)^2}{(5.29\times 10^{-11}\ m)^2}

      F_e = \rm 8.22\times 10^{-8}\ N

      The force is attractive because the charges have opposite signs.

    2. The mass of an electron is 9.11 × 10–31 kg. Determine the electron’s acceleration.

      3. Use Newton’s 2nd Law...

      F = ma

      a = \frac{F}{m} = \rm \frac{8.22\times 10^{-8}\ N}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg} = 9.03\times 10^{22}\ m/s^2

    3. Calculate the electron’s speed. Assume uniform circular motion.

      4. For uniform circular motion...

      a = \frac{v^2}{r}

      ar = v^2

      v = \sqrt{ar} = \rm \sqrt{9.03\times 10^{22}\ m/s^2\cdot 5.29\times 10^{-11}\ m} = 2.19\times 10^6\ m/s

    4. How many times larger is the electric force between the proton and electron compared to the gravitational attraction? The proton’s mass is 1.67 × 10–27 kg.

      5. F_g = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}

      F_g = \rm \frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ N\cdot m^2/kg^2\cdot 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg \cdot 9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}{(5.29\times 10^{-11}\ m)^2}

      F_g = 3.63\times 10^{-47}\ N

      Calculate the ratio of the electric to the gravitational force...

      \frac{F_e}{F_g} = \rm \frac{8.22\times 10^{-8}\ N}{3.63\times 10^{-47}\ N} = 2.27\times 10^{39}

  5. Two charges, +8.00 μC and –2.00 μC are placed 20.0 cm apart as illustrated.

    1. A third charge is positioned so that it experiences no net force. Should the third charge be positioned between the charges, to the left of the + charge, or to the right of the – charge? Explain.

      2. The third charge must be closer to the smaller of the charges (i.e. the negative one) if the forces are to balance. The charge can not be located between the + and – charge since the two forces would then be in the same direction and could not balance. Thus the third charge must be to the right of the negative charge.

    2. Determine the exact location (i.e. how many centimetres from the + or – charge) of the third charge.

      3. Let’s call the third charge q, the two charges illustrated Q_1 and Q_2, the separation between these r = 20.0 cm, and the unknown position of the third charge from the negative source x. Equate the two forces...

      F_1 = F_2

      \frac{k|Q_1q|}{(x+r)^2} = \frac{k|Q_2q|}{x^2}

      We can cancel the kq which appear on both sides...

      \frac{|Q_1|}{(x+r)^2} = \frac{|Q_2|}{x^2}

      \left|\frac{Q_1}{Q_2}\right| x^2 = (x+r)^2

      We know that Q_1 is 4 times larger than Q_2...

      4x^2 = (x+r)^2

      Square root both sides...

      2x = x+r

      Then subtract x...

      x = r = \rm 20.0\ cm

      This means that the test charge is exactly twice as far from Q_1 (40.0 cm) compared to Q_2 (20.0 cm) to compensate for the fact that the magnitude of Q_1 is four times as large.

  6. A charge, q_A = +6.25 × 10–7 C, experiences electrostatic forces due to two nearby charges. Charge q_B = –2.50 × 10–6 C is located 12.0 cm to the left of charge “A” and charge q_C = +1.85 × 10–6 C is located 7.50 cm below “A”.

    1. Use Coulomb’s Law to calculate the magnitude of the electrostatic force that “B” exerts on “A”. Is the force attractive or repulsive? Write the force vector as an ordered pair (2D Cartesian vector).

      2. F_{AB} = \frac{k|q_Aq_B|}{r_{AB}^2}

      F_{AB} = \rm \frac{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\cdot 6.25\times 10^{-7}\ C \cdot 2.50\times 10^{-6}\ C}{(0.120\ m)^2}

      F_{AB} = \rm 0.975\ N

      The force is attractive (charges are opposite sign) so “A” is pulled toward “B”; i.e. to the left...

      \va{F}_{AB} = \rm (-0.975, 0)\ N

    2. Use Coulomb’s Law to calculate the magnitude of the electrostatic force that “C” exerts on “A”. Is the force attractive or repulsive? Write the force vector in Cartesian form.

      3. F_{AC} = \frac{k|q_Aq_C|}{r_{AC}^2}

      F_{AC} = \rm \frac{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\cdot 6.25\times 10^{-7}\ C \cdot 1.85\times 10^{-6}\ C}{(0.0750\ m)^2}

      F_{AC} = \rm 1.85\ N

      The force is repulsive (charges are both positive) so “A” is pushed away from “C” ; i.e. up...

      \va{F}_{AC} = \rm (0, +1.85)\ N

    3. Calculate the net force that “A” experiences due to the combined effects of “B” and “C”. Express the answer in polar form.

      4. \va{F}_A = \va{F}_{AB} + \va{F}_{AC}

      \va{F}_A = \rm (-0.975, 0)\ N + (0, +1.85)\ N = (-0.975, +1.85)\ N

      We can convert this to polar form using the Pythagoras and \tan^{-1} formulas:

      \va{F}_A = \rm 2.09\ N\ [118^\circ]

    4. Charge “A” has a mass of 75.0 g. Neglecting gravity, what will its acceleration be? Is it reasonable to neglect gravity? Explain.

      5. Use Newton’s 2nd Law...

      F = ma

      a = \frac{F}{m} = \rm \frac{2.09\ N\ [118^\circ]}{0.0750\ kg} = 27.9\ m/s^2\ [118^\circ]

      It is not reasonable to ignore gravity since the calculated acceleration is only about 3g. Including gravity would change the answer by about one-third. It is only reasonable to ignore gravity when the acceleration would be many times larger than gravity. For example, an acceleration of 50g would be incorrect by no more than 2% if we neglected gravity.

  7. A van de Graff generator is used to give two metal spheres identical charges of –5.00 μC. The metal spheres are then placed far away from the van de Graff, 18.0 cm apart as illustrated below. A charged, cork ball of mass 2.25 g, placed 6.00 cm directly above one of the spheres as illustrated, is observed to accelerate at 690 m/s2 [95.25°]. (The force of gravity can be neglected.)

    1. Is the cork ball positive or negative? Explain.

      2. Negative. The direction of the force indicates that the sources are repelling the cork.

    2. Using the cork ball’s mass and acceleration, determine the net force it experiences.

      3. F = ma = \rm 2.25\times 10^{-3}\ kg\cdot 690\ m/s^2\ [95.25^\circ] = 1.55\ N\ [95.25^\circ]

    3. Use trigonometry to find the distance and angle between the cork ball and the farther sphere.

      4. By Pythagoras...

      r = \rm\sqrt{0.180^2 + 0.0600^2}\ m = 0.190\ m

      Using the tangent ratio...

      \theta = \rm \tan^{-1}\frac{0.0600\ m}{0.180\ m} = 18.4^\circ

    4. Draw a vector diagram showing the addition of the two electrostatic forces and the net force.

      5. Vector Diagram

    5. Determine the net charge of the cork ball. [Hint: Use the sine law to solve for either one of the two electrostatic forces. Then use Coulomb’s Law to find the charge.]

      6. The angles in the triangle are 5.25º (bottom, 95.25º – 90º), 108.4º (top right, 90º + 18.4º), and 66.35º (top left, 180º – (108.4º + 5.25º)). The force between the cork and Q_1 (at the origin) is...

      F_1 = F_{net}\rm \frac{\sin 66.35^\circ}{\sin 108.4^\circ} = 1.50\ N

      Now solve for the charge of the cork...

      F_1 = \frac{k|Q_1q|}{r_1^2}

      F_1r_1^2 = k|Q_1q|

      |q| = \frac{F_1r_1^2}{k|Q_1|} = \rm \frac{1.50\ N\ (0.0600\ m)^2}{8.99\times 10^9\ N\cdot m^2/C^2\cdot 5.00\times 10^{-6}\ C} = 1.20\times 10^{-7}\ C

      We have already determined that the charge is negative...

      q = \rm -1.20\times 10^{-7}\ C
