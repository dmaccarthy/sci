Lesson Notes

Motion in a Uniform Electric Field

Projecile Motion

Projectile motion is a type of 2D motion where the force (and therefore acceleration) remains constant.

  • We studied projectile motion in Physics 20, where the constant acceleration is caused by gravity.
  • The motion in the y-direction ({\vec{\bf a}}_y = \vec{\bf g}) is uniform accelerated motion.
  • The motion in the x-direction ({\vec{\bf a}}_x = 0) is uniform motion.

The equations for uniform accelerated motion / projectile motion are...

{\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_i + {\vec{\bf v}}_f}\over 2}\Delta t

{v_f}^2 = {v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = {\vec{\bf v}}_i\Delta t + {1\over 2}{\vec{\bf a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2

  • The shape of the trajectory is a parabola because when the acceleration is constant, the relationship between position and time is quadratic, as shown by the last equation above.

{\vec{\bf v}}_i {\rm{m/s}} 36.0\ \cos(52.0°)\\=22.236.0\ \sin(52.0°)\\=28.4

  • Time is a scalar; it does not have separate x- and y-components.

    {v_f}^2 = {v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

    {\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

    \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf v}_{avg}\Delta t

Motion in a Uniform Electric Field

If the electric field is uniform, a charge movingthrough the field will experience a constant acceleration, so its motion will be parabolic just like for projectile motion.

  • The constant acceleration is calculated using Newton’s 2nd Law:

    \vec{\bf a} = {{\vec{\bf F}}_e + {\vec{\bf F}}_g \over m} = {q{\vec{\bf E}} + m{\vec{\bf g}}\over m}

  • Typically, we would set up an experiment so that the electric and gravitational fields are both in the y-direction, so that {\vec{\bf a}}_x = 0.
  • Often (but not always) the electric force is so strong that the gravity term has no effect on the answer to the precision of our calculations.

Example

An electron moves through a uniform electric field. The initial velocity of the electron is 3.00 × 106 m/s perpendicular to the field and the electron is initially 5.00 cm above the positive plate. When the electron strikes the positive plate, it has move 12.0 cm parallel to the plate (perpendicular to the field). Calcuate the strength of the electric field.

xy
\vec{\bf a} {\rm{m/s^2}}0.00–6.25 × 1013
{\vec{\bf v}}_i {\rm{m/s}} 3.00 × 1060.00
{\vec{\bf v}}_f {\rm{m/s}} –2.50 × 106
\Delta\vec{\bf d} {\rm{m}} 0.120 –0.0500
\Delta t {\rm{s}} 4.00 × 10–8

\vec{\bf E} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_e}{q} = \frac{m\vec{\bf a} - m\vec{\bf g}}{q} = \frac{m(\vec{\bf a} - \vec{\bf g})}{q}

\vec{\bf E} = \rm {\frac{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot (-6.25\times 10^{13}\ m/s^2)}{-1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C}}

\vec{\bf E} = \rm {+356\ N/C}

Practice

  1. A small cork sphere has a mass of 1.50 grams and a net charge of –5.25 μC. It is placed in the vertical, uniform electric field between two parallel plates. Physics students observe that it takes 0.400 seconds for the cork to move 12.0 cm toward the top (positive) plate when released from rest.
    1. Calculate the cork’s acceleration and the net force.

      2. For uniform accelerated motion...

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf v}_i\Delta t + \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf a} (\Delta t)^2

      Since the sphere is initially at rest, we can ignore the first term on the right and the solve for acceleration...

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf a} (\Delta t)^2

      \frac{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{(\Delta t)^2} = \vec{\bf a}

      \vec{\bf a} = \rm \frac{2\ (+0.120\ m)}{(0.400\ s)^2} = +1.50\ m/s^2

      Now use Newton’s 2nd Law to find the net force...

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm 1.50\times 10^{-3}\ kg\cdot (+1.50\ m/s^2)

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \rm +2.25\times 10^{-3}\ N

    2. Calculate the weight (force of gravity) of the cork and then determine the electric force that the parallel plates’ field exerts on the cork.

      3. First calculate the gravity force...

      \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm 1.50\times 10^{-3}\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2)

      \vec{\bf F}_g = \rm -1.47\times 10^{-2}\ N

      Then use the net force to determine the electric force...

      \vec{\bf F}_e = \vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_g

      \vec{\bf F}_e = \rm (+2.25\times 10^{-3} - (-1.47\times 10^{-2}))\ N

      \vec{\bf F}_e = \rm +1.70\times 10^{-2}\ N

    3. Calculate the electric field strength.

      4. \vec{\bf E} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_e}{q} = \rm \frac{+1.70\times 10^ {-2}\ N}{-5.25\times 10^{-6}\ C} = -3.23\times 10^3\ N/C

  2. Show that for tiny, charged particles such as protons and electrons moving in an electric field, the gravitational force can usually be ignored. [Hint: Compare the electric and gravitational forces in a weak electric field such as 1.00N/C.]

    3. For a proton, the gravitational force on Earth is...

    F_g = mg = \rm 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 1.64\times 10^{-26}\ N

    The electric force is...

    F_e = q|\vec{\bf E}| = \rm 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 1.00\ N/C = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ N

    The ratio of the forces is...

    \frac{F_e}{F_g} = \rm \frac{1.60\times 10^{-19}\ N}{1.64\times 10^{-26}\ N} = 9.77\times 10^6

    This means that a field of 1.00 N/C exerts nearly 10 million times as much force on the proton as Earth’s surface gravity.

    For an electron the ratio is even bigger; the electric force is the same as for the proton (i.e. same magnitude charge) but the gravitational force is much weaker (smaller mass).

  3. Two charged parallel plates produce a (nearly) uniform electric field of 4000 N/C. The plates are 10.0 cm apart. An electron is emitted from the negative plate, initially at rest.
    1. Calculate the electron’s acceleration.

    2. Calculate the velocity with which the electron hits the positive plate, and the time it takes to move from the negative to the positive plate.

    The point of injection is 6.00 cm above the negative plate and the object is seen to hit the negative plate at a distance of 9.25 cm from the left side of the plate. The initial velocity of the object is 5.31 m/s [70.2º].

    1. Use trigonometry (“SOH CAH TOA”) to calculate the x- and y-components of the initial velocity. A vector diagram of \vec{\bf v}_i will be useful.
    2. Calculate the time it took for the projectile to strike the negative plate by solving the x-motion.

    2. If the object’s initial velocity was 8.50 m/s directed horizontally, what will its final velocity (magnitude and direction) be after it has traveled through a horizontal displacement of 12.0 cm?

    1. Calculate the particle’s charge-to-mass ratio, q/m.
    2. Assuming that the charge of the particle is exactly one elementary charge unit, what is the particle’s mass?
