Motion in a Uniform Electric Field

Projecile Motion

Projectile motion is a type of 2D motion where the force (and therefore acceleration) remains constant.

  • We studied projectile motion in Physics 20, where the constant acceleration is caused by gravity.
  • The motion in the y-direction ({\vec{\bf a}}_y = \vec{\bf g}) is uniform accelerated motion.
  • The motion in the x-direction ({\vec{\bf a}}_x = 0) is uniform motion.

The equations for uniform accelerated motion / projectile motion are...

{\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_i + {\vec{\bf v}}_f}\over 2}\Delta t

{v_f}^2 = {v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = {\vec{\bf v}}_i\Delta t + {1\over 2}{\vec{\bf a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2

  • The shape of the trajectory is a parabola because when the acceleration is constant, the relationship between position and time is quadratic, as shown by the last equation above.

Motion in a Uniform Electric Field

If the electric field is uniform, a charge movingthrough the field will experience a constant acceleration, so its motion will be parabolic just like for projectile motion.

  • The constant acceleration is calculated using Newton’s 2nd Law:

    \vec{\bf a} = {{\vec{\bf F}}_e + {\vec{\bf F}}_g \over m} = {q{\vec{\bf E}} + m{\vec{\bf g}}\over m}

  • Typically, we would set up an experiment so that the electric and gravitational fields are both in the y-direction, so that {\vec{\bf a}}_x = 0.
  • Often (but not always) the electric force is so strong that the gravity term has no effect on the answer to the precision of our calculations.

Example

An electron moves through a uniform electric field. The initial velocity of the electron is 3.00 × 106 m/s perpendicular to the field and the electron is initially 5.00 cm above the positive plate. When the electron strikes the positive plate, it has move 12.0 cm parallel to the plate (perpendicular to the field). Calcuate the strength of the electric field.

\begin{align}\vec{\bf E} &= \frac{\vec{\bf F}_e}{q} = \frac{m\vec{\bf a} - \vec{\bf F}_g}{q} \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot (-6.25\times 10^{13}\ m/s^2)}{-1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C}} \\[10pt] &= \rm {+356\ N/C} \end{align}
