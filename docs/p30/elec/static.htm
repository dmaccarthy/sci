Pre-Lab Review

  1. Describe the three particles that make up all matter (according to the Rutherford-Bohr Model) and compare the relative masses and electric charges of these particles.

    2. According to Rutherford’s “Planetary Model” of the atom (aka “Solar System” or “Nuclear” model ), matter is made up of protons and neutrons (which form the nucleus) and electrons (which orbit around the nucleus). Protons and neutrons have masses of 1.67 × 10–27 kg. Electrons are much smaller (about 1800×) with masses of 9.11 × 10–31 kg. Protons are positively charged while electrons have an equal amount of negative charge; neutrons have no electric charge. The volume of the atom is almost entirely empty space as the size of the individual particles is about 10000 times that of the atom.

  2. Explain what we mean when we say an object is “positively charged” or “negatively charged” as opposed to “neutral”.

    3. Positively charged objects have more protons than electrons. This usually occurs due to a loss of electrons. Negatively charged objects have more electrons than protons, usually due to gaining electrons.

  3. Describe the interaction between two objects with opposite sign charges. Describe the interaction between two objects with the same sign charge.

    4. Opposite sign charges attract. Like charges repel.

  4. Define an electrical conductor and an electrical insulator. Explain how conduction occurs.

    5. Conductors permit the movement of electric charges (current). Insulators do not allow charges to move. In solids, most electrons and all positive charges (nuclei) have limited mobility. In conductors, a fraction of electrons are free to move within the solid. These electrons are responsible for conduction.

Lesson Notes

Electrostatics

Electric Charge

Matter is made up of protons, neutrons, and electrons.

  • Protons have a positive charge.
  • Electrons have a negative charge.
  • Neutrons have no charge.
  • Objects that contain equal numbers of protons and electrons are neutral.
  • Objects that have an excess of electrons are negatively charged.
  • Objects that have a defecit of electrons (i.e. more protons) are positively charged.

For everyday charged objects, the difference between the protons and electrons is relatively small.

  • An excess of a single electron for every billion protons can produce a very large net charge!

The SI unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C).

  • A coulomb is defined as the amount of charge carried by a 1-ampere current in 1 second:

    \rm 1\ C = 1\ A\cdot s

  • The charge of a single proton or electron is called the elementary charge unit (e).
  • The conversion factor is:

    1\ e = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ {\mathrm C}

  • A coulomb is a very large amount of charge; we often use microcoulombs (μC) or even nanocoulombs (nC).

Objects with opposite sign charges attract; objects with the same sign charge repel.

Like energy and momentum, charge is a conserved quantity.

Charging Methods

There are three common methods for creating charged objects:

  • Friction: Two different materials are rubbed against each other. The material with the greater affinity for electrons becomes negative and the other becomes positive.
  • Conduction: A neutral object is placed in contact with a charged object so that the net charge is shared. Both objects end up with the same sign charge.
  • Induction: A charged object is used to attract the opposite sign charge onto a neutral object from a third object (which is often the Earth.)

Conduction

Objects that permit the movement of charge easily are known as conductors.

Objects that prevent the movement of charge are known as insulators.

Objects that allow the movement of charge with significant loss of energy are known as resistors.

Polarization

When a neutral object is placed near a charged object, electrons may move within the object to form two poles.

  • The negative pole will be the region where electrons are attracted to.
  • The positive pole will be the region where electrons are repeled from.
  • The pole closest to the charged object will be the one that has the opposite sign.
  • A polarized object will attract to charges of either sign.
  • Some substances, including water, methanol, and ethanol, have a permanent polarization due to their molecular structure.

Electroscope

A metal-leaf electroscope is a device for detecting charge.

  • A charged object brought close to the top of the electroscope will polarize it.
  • The ball will have the opposite sign as the charge, and the leaves will have the same sign.
  • The leaves will repel each other due to having the same sign.

If the electroscope is given a net charge, the ball and leaves will all have the same sign as the charge spreads out as much as it can due to repulsion.

  • If the same sign charge is brought near the top, the leaves will become more charged and spread farther apart.
  • If the opposite sign charge is brought near the top, the leaves will become less charged and will become less separated.
  • The leaves will never have opposite signs, and therefore will never attract past vertical.

  1. A conductive object has a net negative charge. Three students had a disagreement about the distribution of the charge.

    Chris argued that the charge would be evenly distributed throughout the object (left illustration). Kelly argued that the charge would spread out over the exterior of the object only (centre illustration). Sam believed that the charge would be concentrated in the object’s centre (right illustration). Which student do you agree with? Justify your choice.

    2. Kelly’s prediction (centre illustration) is correct. The negative charge is due to an excess of electrons. Because the electrons repel each other (same charge), they will move as far apart from each other as they can, which means they will end up on the exterior of the conductor.

      4. Bacuse the positive pole is slighly closer to the negative charge, the attractive force will be a little stronger than the repulsive force on the negative pole.

  3. Two identical spheres are placed in contact. One of the spheres has a net negative charge. Both spheres are good enough conductors that it is possible for charge to flow between the two spheres.

    1. Explain what will happen in terms of movement of charges. Predict (and justify) how the net charge will be distributed between the two spheres after enough time has been allowed for the charged particles to reach their final locations.

      2. Since the excess electrons repel each other, they will spread out as far apart from each other as possible. This means some of the electrons will be pushed off of the charged object and onto the neutral one, making it negative too. If the spheres are identical in size, this means we will get exactly half of the net negative charge ending up on each sphere...

    2. Repeat for a net positive charge.

      3. In this case electrons will be pulled off of the neutral object as they are attracted to the positive one. Both objects will end up with a positive charge. If the spheres are the same size, the amount of charge must end up being the same on both spheres...

  4. Why does a neutral grain of dust become attracted to a negatively charged TV screen?

    5. The dust becomes polarized due to the presence of the charged TV screen. Since the oppositely charged side of the dust will be closer to the TV than the similarly charged side, the net force will be attractive.

  5. A metal sphere has a net charge of –2.50 nC when it is touched to a neutral cork ball. The cork acquires a charge of –0.42 nC.

    1. Which method of charging (friction, conduction, induction) produced the charge on the cork ball?

      2. Conduction

    2. How much net charge does the metal sphere have after contact with the cork?

      3. Since charge is conserved...

      q_{iM} + q_{iC} = q_{fM} + q_{fC}

      q_{fM} = q_{iM} + q_{iC} - q_{fC}

      q_{fM} = \rm (-2.50 + 0.00 - (-0.42))\ nC = -2.08\ nC

  6. A metal sphere “A” has a net charge of –4.20 nC. The sphere is placed in contact with a second, identical metal sphere “B”. When the spheres are separated, sphere “A” has a charge of –2.50 nC. What was the charge on sphere “B” (i) before, and (ii) after it was placed in contact with “A”? [Hint: When two identical metal spheres are in contact, they must have the same electric charge!]

    7. Since charge is conserved...

    q_{iA} + q_{iB} = q_{fA} + q_{fB}

    q_{iB} = q_{fA} + q_{fB} - q_{iA}

    q_{iB} = \rm (-2.50 + (-2.50) - (-4.20))\ nC = -0.80\ nC

  7. Four pith (cork) balls labelled A, B, C and D have unknown charges. Use the following observations to determine whether each pith ball is positive, negative or neutral:

    • An ebonite rod charged using fur repels pith ball C and attracts the other three pith balls.

      • Since an ebonite rod charged with fur becomes negative, C must also be negative as it repels. The other balls can be either positive or neutral.

    • There is no attraction or repulsion between B and D.

      • B and D must both be neutral since there would be a net force if one or both had a net charge.

    • Pith ball A attracts each of the other three pith balls.

      • A must be positive since it attracts the negative and the neutral objects.

  8. Three identical metal spheres initially have the following electric charges: Sphere A, +7.50 nC; Sphere B, –4.00 nC; and Sphere C, –3.50 nC. Sphere A is then placed in contact with B. These spheres are then separated, and B is placed in contact with C and then separated. What are the final charges of the three spheres?

    9. “A” contacts “B” (both will have the same final charge since they are identical)...

    q_{iA} + q_{iB} = q_{fA} + q_{fB} = 2q_{fA}

    q_{fA} = q_{fB} = \frac{q_{iA} + q_{iB}}{2}

    q_{fA} = q_{fB} = \rm \frac{(+7.50 + (-4.00))\ mC}{2} = +1.75\ mC

    Next, “B” contacts “C” (both will have the same final charge since they are identical)...

    q_{iB} + q_{iC} = q_{fB} + q_{fC} = 2q_{fB}

    q_{fB} = q_{fC} = \frac{q_{iB} + q_{iC}}{2}

    q_{fB} = q_{fC} = \rm \frac{(+1.75 + (-3.50))\ mC}{2} = -0.875\ mC

      6. Vector Diagram
