Lesson Notes

Data Analysis Review

Data Analysis

Once you have finished collecting your experimental data, the first step in analyzing the data is often to draw a scatter plot. If the graph appears linear, add a best-fit line.

Calculate the slope of the best-fit line using two points that are on the best-fit line and relatively far apart. The points you choose do not necessarily have to be data points:

k = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}

If the y-intercept is visible on the graph, you can simple record it. If it is not visible, solve for b using your slope and one of your two points:

y_1 = kx_1 + b

Finally, write the model equation in the form:

y = kx+b

...replacing x and y by the symbols for the variables on your graph, and k and b by your calculated slope and y-intercept, including standard SI units.

Non-Linear Data

Sometimes, a graph of our data will be curved rather than a straight line...

  • One way to obtain a model equation is to perform a non-linear (quadratic, power, exponential) regression using your graphing calculator or software (e.g. desmos.com, Google Sheets, or Microsoft Excel).
  • When we have reason to believe the relationship will be a power, there is a better way...

Transforming Power Data

Suppose our hypothesis is an equation (from our Data Sheet?) that predicts the relationship between manipulated and responding variables:

T =2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

  • Variables that appear under a radical sign are considered to have a fraction exponent:
    • square root = exponent 1/2
    • cube root = exponent 1/3

T = \frac{2\pi L^{\frac{1}{2}}}{g^{\frac{1}{2}}}

  • Variables that appear in the denominator are considered to have a negative exponent.
  • Any equation of the form y = ax^n is classified as a power relationship.
  • We can transform the manipulated variable by calculating L^{\frac{1}{2}}.
  • We do this because \frac{1}{2} is the exponent that appears with L in the equation!
  • The responding variable, T, does not have an exponent so it does not need to be transformed.
  • We are not concerned whether or not any controlled variables (g) have an exponent because the controlled variables are constant during the experiment.
  • Here is how we transform the first two points in the data table...

\rm{(0.250\ m)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 0.500\ m^{\frac{1}{2}}}

\rm{(0.500\ m)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 0.707\ m^{\frac{1}{2}}}

Model Equation

Now that the graph has been linearized, we can calculate its slope and y-intercept as with any other line.

b = \rm 0.0\ s

k = \rm \frac{(3.01 - 0.00)\ s}{(1.500 - 0.00)\ m^{\frac{1}{2}}} = 2.01\ s/m^{\frac{1}{2}}

  • When you write the model equation, y=kx+b, remember to include the power:

    T = {\rm (2.01\ s/m^{\frac{1}{2}})}\ L^{\frac{1}{2}}

  • The y-variable is T; what was graphed on the y-axis.
  • The x-variable is L^{\frac{1}{2}}; what was graphed on the x-axis.
  • You can substitute the model equation into the theoretical equation:

    T = \frac{2\pi L^{\frac{1}{2}}}{g^{\frac{1}{2}}} = {\rm (2.01\ s/m^\frac{1}{2})}\ L^{\frac{1}{2}}

  • You could then algebraically solve for g:

    \frac{2\pi}{g^{\frac{1}{2}}} = {\rm 2.01\ s/m^\frac{1}{2}}

    \frac{2\pi}{\rm 2.01\ s/m^\frac{1}{2}} = g^{\frac{1}{2}}

    g = \left(\frac{2\pi}{\rm 2.01\ s/m^\frac{1}{2}}\right)^2 = \rm 9.77\ m/s^2

Example 1

Students conducted an experiment on sound waves to test the equation v=f\lambda. They manipulated the frequency of the waves (f) and controlled the speed (v). The wavelength (\lambda) was the responding variable. How should they transform the data?

Isolate the responding variable:

\lambda = \frac{v}{f}

Since f appears in the denominator, use a negative power:

\lambda = vf^{-1}

Transform the data by calculating f^{-1} for each data point.

Example 2

Students conducted an experiment where the measured the repulsion force between two negatively charged objects as they manipulated the separation (distance) between the objects. Their Physics 30 Data Booklet contains a formula for electric force that is similar to Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation that you studied in Physics 20:

F_e = \frac{kq_1 q_2}{r^2}

In this equation, q_1 and q_2 represent the amount of charge on the objects, r represents the separation, and k is a constant

How should the students transform the data to obtain a linearized graph?

The manipulated variable r appears with an exponent of –2, so the students should transform the data by calculating r^{-2}. The exponent is negative because r^2 appears in the denominator of a fraction:

F_e = kq_1 q_2 r^{-2}

Review

    1. Explain how to draw a good best-fit line.
      2. The best-fit line should pass through the mean of the data points; i.e. the average of all of the x and all of the y coordinates. The slope of the line should be chosen so that the average distance of the data points to the line is minimized; there should be equal numbers of points above and below the best-fit line and those points should be distibuted as evenly as possible along the width of the graph.
    2. Explain how to calculate the slope and y-intercept of a best-fit line.
      1. Choose two points that are far apart and that lie on the best-fit line. These points do not have to be data points! Record the coordinates of the two points: (x_1, y_1) and (x_2, y_2).
      2. Calculate the slope, k, as “rise ÷ run” using the two points you chose:

        k = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}

      3. Calculate the y-intercept using the slope and one of your two points by solving for b:

        y_1 = kx_1 + b

        Note that if the y-intercept is visible on the graph, you can skip this calculation and simply measure b from the graph.
    3. Explain what a model equation is.
      4. The model equation is simply the equation of the best-fit line in the form:

      y = kx + b

      When writing the equation, you should replace x and y by the appropriate symbols for your manipulated and responding variables, and you should replace k and b by your calculated slope and intercept values in standard SI units.

      You can use your model equation to make predictions (interpolation or extrapolation) from the data.

  2. Explain how to transform (linearize) experimental data when the raw data is non-linear.
    3. When the raw data produces a curved graph, and you suspect the non-linearity occurs because of a power in the theoretical equation, you can transform the data by applying the predicted exponent to the raw data. For example, if the expected relationship is:

    g = \frac{GM}{r^2}

    The graph would be non-linear because the variable r appears with an exponent of –2. (Expressions that appear in the denominator are taken to be negative powers.)

    Transform the data by calculating r^{-2} for each data point and then graphing g versus r^{-2}. If the predicted equation is correct, the transformed data should be linear and you can add a best-fit line and determine its equation in the usual way.

    When writing the model equation as y = kx + b, remember that x now means r^{-2}, not just r!

    If you do not know what the expected exponent is, you can perform a power regression using a graphing calculator or graphing / statistics software.
