Electric Potential Difference

Work & Potential Energy

When a charged object is placed in an electric field, the electric force may do work on the charge by causing it to speed up or slow down.

Charged objects within an electric field have electric potential energy because the field has the “ability to do work” on the charge.

  • The work done by the field on a test charge is calculated using the work-energy theorem:

    \Delta E_k = -\Delta E_p = W = {\vec{\bf F}}_e\cdot\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}

  • A positive work indicates an increase in kinetic energy and a decrease in potential energy.
  • If the field is uniform, we can substitute {\vec{\bf F}}_e = q{\vec{\bf E}}:

    -\Delta E_p = W = q{\vec{\bf E}}\cdot\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}

Physicists define the electric potential difference, \Delta V, as the work done per unit of test charge:

-\Delta V = {W\over q} = {\vec{\bf E}}\cdot\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}

  • Do not confuse electric field (\vec{\bf E}) with energy (E).
  • This equation is just the work-energy theorem expressed ‘per Coulomb’ rather than as a total! I.e. potential difference is the work per Coulomb while the electric field is the force per Coulomb.
  • The unit of potential difference is J/C, which is called a volt. Potential difference is sometimes just called the ‘Voltage’.
  • When the field is not constant, potential diffference is the area under a graph of \vec{\bf E} vs \vec{\bf d}.

The new Physics 30 equations are:

\vec{\bf E} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_e}{q}

|\vec{\bf E}| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}

\Delta V = \frac{\Delta E}{q}

The equations from Physics 20 are:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t}

\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_i + \vec{\bf v}_f}{2}\ \Delta t

v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2 \vec{\bf a} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf v}_i \Delta t + \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf a} (\Delta t)^2

W_e = \vec{\bf F}_e \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a}

W = \Delta E = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2

Electron-volt

The electron-volt is defined as the amount of work done on an electron by a potential difference of 1 volt:

{\rm{eV = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 1\ V = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ J}}

  • The electron-volt is often used as a unit of energy when dealing with atomic scale problems.

Practice

  1. This photograph shows a 9-Volt battery and a 6-Volt battery.

    A battery is essentially one or more parallel plate capacitors submersed in a chemical solution that keeps the plates charged until the chemicals are depleted.

    If both batteries were connected to identical light bulbs, which would produce a brighter light? Which would keep the bulb lit for the longest amount of time? Justify your answer by referring to an appropriate equation.

    The 9-Volt battery produces electrons with 50% more energy (i.e. 9 J/C compared to 6 J/C) so it will make the light bulb brighter. The 6-Volt battery is much larger and presumably contains much more electrolyte, allowing it to provide much more total charge over its lifetime. Therefore, it will probably last longer.

    \Delta E = Q\Delta V

    1. Use your data sheet to look up the charge of a single electron. Calculate the amount of work done on an electron as it moves through a potential difference of 1.00 Volts. This amount of energy is known as an ‘electron-Volt’ (eV) and is sometimes used as an alternative unit to joules.

      2. \Delta E = q\Delta V = \rm (1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C)\cdot 1.00 V = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ J

    2. An electron with 13.6 eV of kinetic energy is slowed to 3.40 eV while moving 1.59 × 10–10 m. Determine the potential difference and average electric field over the electron’s motion.

      3. \Delta E = E_f - E_i = \rm (3.40 - 13.6)\ eV = -10.2\ eV

      \Delta V = \frac{\Delta E}{q} = \frac{\rm -10.2\ eV}{-1e} = \rm 10.2\ V

      |\vec{\bf E}| = \left|\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}\right| = \rm \frac{10.2\ V}{1.59\times 10^{-10}\ m} = 6.42\times 10^{10}\ N/C

  3. An electron accelerates as it passes through a region containing a uniform electric field of 4.20 × 103 N/C. The electron moves through a displacement of 1.50 mm parallel to the field. Calculate the work done on the electron and the potential difference between the electron’s initial and final positions.

    It’s simpler to calculate the potential difference first...

    \Delta V = \vec{\bf E}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm \pm 4.20\times 10^3\ N/C\cdot (\pm 1.50\times 10^{-3}\ m) = \pm 6.30\ V

    Next calculate the work...

    W = q\Delta V = \rm -1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot (\pm 6.30\ V) = \pm 1.01\times 10^{18}\ J = \pm 6.30\ eV

  4. A proton is decelerated from a speed of 3.76 × 106 m/s to 1.03 × 106 m/s by a uniform electric field.
    1. What is the potential difference between the proton’s initial and final positions?

      2. E_{ki} = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot (3.76\times 10^6\ m/s)^2 = 1.18\times 10^{-14}\ J

      E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot (1.03\times 10^6\ m/s)^2 = 8.86\times 10^{-16}\ J

      \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = \rm (8.86\times 10^{-16} - 1.18\times 10^{-14})\ J = -1.09\times 10^{-14}\ J

      \Delta V = \frac{\Delta E}{q} = \rm \frac{-1.09\times 10^{-14}\ J}{+1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = -6.82\times 10^4\ V

      Alternatively, rather than performing all the steps separately you could combine all of the equations into one...

      \Delta V = \frac{E_{kf} - E_{ki}}{q} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}m\cdot(v_f^2 - v_i^2)}{q}

    2. If the proton moves 6.25 cm parallel to \vec{\bf E} while decelerating, what is the average electric field?

      3. |\vec{\bf E}| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm \frac{-6.82\times 10^4\ V}{+6.25\times 10^{-2}\ m} = -1.09\times 10^6\ N/C

  5. Two horizontal conducting parallel plates are separated by 2.00 cm. An oxygen ion (O2–) is suspended between the plates such that the electric force balances the gravitational force.

    1. Which plate is positive, the top or bottom?

      2. Since the force of gravity is downward, the electric force must be upward to balance. Since the charge is negative, the top plate must be positive.

    2. What is the potential difference between the plates? (The mass of oxygen is 16 times the mass of a proton.)

      3. Since the electric and gravitational forces balance...

      F_e = F_g = mg = \rm 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 2.62\times 10^{-25}\ N

      \vec{\bf E} = \frac{\vec{\bf F_e}}{q} = \rm \frac{+2.62\times 10^{-25}\ N}{-2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = -8.19\times 10^{-7}\ N/C

      Since the electric force is up (balances gravity), and the test charge is negative, the direction of the field is down.

      \Delta V = \vec{\bf E}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm -8.19\times 10^{-7}\ N/C\cdot (\pm 0.0200\ m) = \pm 1.64\times 10^{-8}\ V

  6. Two parallel conducting plates are separated by 5.80 mm and are maintained at a potential difference of 12.0 V.

    1. What is the average electric field between the plates?

      2. |\vec{\bf E}| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm \frac{\pm 12.0\ V}{5.80\times 10^{-3}\ m} = \pm 2.07\times 10^3\ N/C

    2. An object with a charge of +3.50 μC and a mass of 27.5 mg is injected into the region between the electrodes. If the object’s initial velocity is 1.03 m/s, how far does it travel before the electric field brings it to rest?

      3. Calculate the work done...

      W = \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki}

      W = {\rm 0.00\ J} - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2

      W = \rm -\frac{1}{2} (2.75\times 10^{-5}\ kg)(1.03\ m/s)^2 = -1.46\times 10^{-5}\ J

      Calculate the force...

      \vec{\bf F}_e = q\vec{\bf E} = \rm +3.50\times 10^{-6}\ C\cdot (-2.07\times 10^3\ N/C) = -7.24\times 10^{-3}\ N

      Calculate the displacement...

      W = \vec{\bf F}_e\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{W}{\vec{\bf F}_e} = \rm \frac{-1.46\times 10^{-5}\ J}{-7.24\times 10^{-3}\ N} = +2.01\times 10^{-3}\ m
