Magnetic Fields

Field of a Straight Wire

Suppose we placed several compasses around a long, straight wire.

A substantial amount of current through the wire would produce a magnetic field much stronger than Earth’s.

The compass needles would orient themselves to point tangentially along a circle centred on the wire.

  • The magnetic field generated by a long, straight wire has the shape of circles centred on the wire.
  • The directions of the magnetic field vectors are counterclockwise when we view the field so that the electrons are flowing away from us (⊗, as in the diagram above), and clockwise when viewed with the electrons flowing toward us (⊙).

First Hand Rule

Use the First Hand Rule to remember how the direction of \vec{\bf B} relates to the direction of the current:

  • Point your left thumb in the direction of the flow of electrons (or other negative sources).
  • Your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field loops surrounding the source current.
  • For positive sources, use your right hand instead.

Bar Magnet

The magnetic field of a bar magnet is called a dipolar magnetic field because it has two poles.

  • The magnetic field along the axis is a striaght line into the south pole and out of the north pole.
  • Inside the magnet, the field points from south to north, maintaining a continuous line with no convergences or divergences.
  • As we move off-axis, the field becomes loops, emerging from the north pole, looping around to the south pole, and re-entering the magnet.

Field of a Coil

We often use loops or coils of wire to produce magnetic fields.

  • The magnetic field of a coil is dipolar, just like a bar magnet.
  • One side of the coil is its “north pole” and the other side is “south”.
  • A coil which is much longer than its diameter is sometimes called a solenoid.
  • A coil which is much shorter than its diameter is sometimes called a Helmholtz coil.

Second Hand Rule

Use the Second Hand Rule to remember how the poles are related to the direction of the current:

  • Curl your left fingers in the direction of the flow of electrons (or other negative sources).
  • Your thumb will point toward the north pole of the electromagnet.
  • This is also the direction of \vec{\bf B} on the coil’s axis.
  • For currents due to positive sources (“conventional current”), use your right hand instead.
