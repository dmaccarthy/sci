Motion in a Uniform Magnetic Field

Motion of a Charged Particle

We saw in the electric fields chapter that moving test charges in a uniform electric field will experience a constant force.

  • Constant force produces constant acceleration.
  • The motion is uniform accelerated motion (1D) or projectile motion (2D).
  • The particle’s trajectory is a parabola.

When a test charge moves in a magnetic field, the magnitude of the force is:

|\vec{\bf F}_m| = q\vec{\bf v}_\perp|\vec{\bf B}|

The direction of the magnetic force is given by the Third Hand Rule.

  • If the magnetic field is uniform (and there are no other forces), the magnitude of the force will remain constant.
  • According to the Third Hand Rule, the direction of the force must change so that it is always perpendicular to \vec{\bf v}.
  • The resulting motion will be uniform circular motion if the initial velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field!

    Motion of a Positive Charge

Uniform Circular Motion

Newton’s 2nd Law determines the motion of the test charge:

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a}

  • We can substitute the appropriate equations for the magnetic force and centripetal acceleration:

    qvB = m\frac{v^2}{r}

  • We can solve the resulting equation to obtain the radius of the uniform circular:

    r = \frac{mv}{qB}

  • Note that this equation is not on your data sheet!
  • You need to remember which equations to combine for uniform circular motion due to a magnetic field.

Helical Motion

If the initial velocity is not perpendicular to \vec{\bf B}, the perpendicular component of the velocity will still execute uniform circular motion.

  • Simply replace v in the equations by:

    v_\perp = |\vec{\bf v}| \sin\theta

  • The parallel component will be unaffected by the field.
  • The resulting trajectory will have the shape of a helix centred on a magnetic field line.

  • The field line forms the axis of the helix.

Aurora Borealis

The aurora borealis (Northern Lights) and aurora australis (Southern Lights) are lights that appear in the sky at polar latitudes.

  • These lights are produced when charged particles from the Sun (known as the solar wind) collide with molecules in Earth’s upper atmosphere.
  • The reason these lights occur near the poles has to do with Earth’s and the Sun’s magnetic fields.
  • Magnetic field lines from Earth’s poles form large loops that connect up with the Sun’s magnetic field.
  • Solar wind particles move in helixes around these field lines from the Sun to Earth’s poles.

  • The magnetic field loops near Earth’s equator are much smaller: they loop back to Earth without ever connecting to the Sun’s field.
  • Since there are no magnetic field lines connecting the Sun to Earth’s equatorial regions, solar wind particles rarely reach Earth’s atmosphere in the tropics.

Linear Accelerator

A particle accelerator is a device used to accelerate subatomic particles (e.g. protons) to extremely high speeds.

  • One type of particle accelerator is a linear accelerator (linac). This consists of a series of electrodes alternating between positive and negative.

  • A proton (for example) would accelerate away from the first (+) electrode toward the (–) one.
  • At the instant when the proton passes through the negative plate, the polarity is reversed so that the proton will keep accelerating.
  • This process repeats with the polarity reversing every time the proton passes through an electrode.
  • A problem with linacs is that as the proton gains speed, the distance between the electrodes must be increased.
  • To reach extreme speeds, linacs must be several hundred metres long!

Cyclotron

In 1929, Ernest Lawrence invented the cyclotron.

  • Like a linac, a cyclotron used the electric field between two oppositely charged plates to accelerate the particles.
  • The cyclotron, however, also uses a magnetic field to move the particles along a circular trajectory, so that the same pair of electrodes can accelerate the particles repeatedly.

  • As the particles gain speed, the circumference of their trajectory increases proportionally.
  • This means that the effective distance between electrodes increases automatically, so that the timing for reversing the polarity of the plates remains constant!

Practice

  1. An electron moving with a speed of 5.20 × 106 m/s enters a region where the magnetic field strength is 12.9 mT. The velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field.
    1. Determine the magnetic force that the electron experiences.
    2. Determine the centripetal acceleration and the radius of the electron’s orbit if its velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field.
  2. A proton from the solar wind moves through Earth’s magnetosphere with a speed of 3.00 × 106 m/s. If the magnetic field strength is 4.00 × 10–5 T and makes a 20.0° angle with the proton’s velocity, what will the radius of the proton’s helical motion be?
    1. A proton executes uniform circular motion due to a magnetic field. If the magnetic field strength was doubled, by what factor would the proton’s orbital radius change?
    2. A proton executes uniform circular motion due to a magnetic field. If an alpha particle with the same speed as the proton were placed in the same magnetic field, by what factor would its orbital radius differ from the proton’s orbit?
  4. Calculate the period of circular motion for a proton moving in a cyclotron with a magnetic field strength of 0.750 T. Determine the speed of the proton when it leaves the cyclotron (r = 30.0 cm).

  5. A cyclotron has a radius of 42.0 cm and uses a magnetic field strength of 1.20 T.
    1. Calculate the cyclotron period for protons, i.e. the period of protons in circular motion within the cyclotron.
    2. Calculate the speed and the kinetic energy of a proton about to leave the cyclotron.
    3. If the potential difference between the plates was 2.50 kV, how many revolutions did the proton need to make before leaving the cyclotron? [Hint: Remember that the potential difference tells you the work done on the protons each time they pass through the accelerating plates.]
  6. A positive ion with a net charge of one elementary charge unit is accelerated from rest through a 11.0 kV potential difference. The ion then moves into a region where the magnetic field strength is 1.05 T and follows a circular trajectory with a radius of 25.0 mm. The magnetic field is perpendicular to the velocity.
    1. Explain why qV=\frac{1}{2}mv^2 and why qvB=\frac{mv^2}{r}?
    2. Eliminate v from these two equations and solve for m.
    3. What is the mass of the ion? What two elements could the ion be? [Hint: How many times heavier is the ion than a proton?]
    4. What is the ion’s speed?
  7. Derive an equation for the “cyclotron period”, the time for one circular orbit of a particle with charge q and mass m moving in a perpendicular, uniform magnetic field \vec{\bf B}. Show that this period does not depend on the speed of the particle or the radius of its trajectory.
