Magnetic Force

Magnetic Force

Gravitational and electric forces do not depend on the velocities of the test mass or charge.

  • The direction of these forces is simply the direction of the field unless the test charge is negative, in which case the force is directed opposite to the field.

Magnetic forces are more complicated...

  • The direction of the force depends on whether the test charge is positive or negative.
  • It also depends on the direction of the test charge’s velocity.

Directions of \va{B} and \va{F}_m with
Electron Flow (\va{v}) Out of the Page

Third Hand Rule

Use the Third Hand Rule to find the directions of the vectors.

  • With your thumb pointed in the direction of the test charge’s velocity, \va{v}, and your fingers extended in the direction of the magnetic field, \va{B}, your palm will be facing the direction of the magnetic force, \va{F}_m.
  • Use your left hand for negative test charges and your right hand for positive ones.
  • According to this rule, the direction of the magnetic force acting on a test charge will always be perpendicular to both the velocity of the test charge and the magnetic field (i.e. the direction that a compass needle would point.)
  • Test charges at rest experience no magnetic force.

Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

Moving test charges experience a magnetic force that increases with the speed of the test charge.

|\va{F}_m| = qv_\perp |\va{B}|

  • Contrast this equation to the simpler ones for gravity and electric forces:

    \va{F}_g = m\va{g}

    \va{F}_e = q\va{E}

  • The symbol \va{v}_\perp means the component of the velocity that is perpendicular to the magnetic field:

    \va{v}_\perp = |\va{v}| \sin\theta

  • \theta is the angle between \va{v} and \va{B}.
  • The parallel component of the velocity is not relevant to the force because the Third Hand Rule says there is no force when the velocity is parallel to \va{B}!

Magnetic Force on a Wire

We often need to know the magnetic force not just on a single test charge, but also on all of the charges moving through a wire.

  • The force for this situation is:

    |\va{F}_m| = IL |\va{B}_\perp|

  • I is the current flowing through the wire.
  • L is the length of wire experiencing (the perpendicular component of) the magnetic field.

  5. A wire carrying 1.25 A of current experiences a force of 3.25 mN when held perpendicular to the magnetic field between the poles of a horseshoe magnet. If the length of wire within the magnetic field is 5.20 cm, what is the average magnetic field strength? Include a diagram with the directions of \va{v}, \va{B} and \va{F}_m labelled.

    6. F_m = ILB

    B = \frac{F_m}{IL} = \rm \frac{3.25\times 10^{-3}\ N}{1.25\ A\cdot 0.0520\ m} = 0.0500\ T

  6. A wire with 720 mA of current (electron flow = east) passes through a magnetic field of 50.0 mT (down). A total length of 35.0 cm of wire is exposed to the magnetic field. Determine the total magnetic force (including direction) that the wire experiences. Include a diagram with the directions of \va{v}, \va{B} and \va{F}_m labelled.

    7. F_m = ILB = \rm 0.720\ A\cdot 0.350\ m\cdot 0.0500\ T = 0.0126\ N

    According to the 3rd Left Hand Rule, the direction of the magnetic force is (geographic!) south.

  7. An alpha particle (see formula sheet) moving with a speed of 9.20 × 104 m/s passes through a uniform 2.05 mT magnetic field and experiences a force of 3.50 × 10–17 N. What angle does the particle’s velocity make with the magnetic field?

    8. F_m = qvB\sin\theta

    \frac{F_m}{qvB} = \sin\theta

    \theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{F_m}{qvB}

    \theta = \rm \sin^{-1}\frac{3.50\times 10^{-17}\ N}{3.20\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 9.20\times 10^4\ m/s\cdot 2.05\times 10^{-3}\ T}

    \theta = \rm 35.4^\circ

  8. A wire experiences an upward magnetic force of 48.2 mN when held so that its current flows north. When the wire is turned so that its current flows east, the magnetic force is 60.5 mN downward. In what direction does the uniform, horizontal magnetic field point?

    9. Using the 3rd Left Hand Rule, there must be a component of the magnetic field directed east, to produce an upward force when the electron flow is north...

    F_{up} = ILB_{east} = \rm 48.2\ mN

    Using the rd Left Hand Rule, there must be a component of the magnetic field directed north, to produce a downward force when the electron flow is east...

    F_{down} = ILB_{north} = \rm 60.5\ mN

    If we divide these two equations, the factors IL will cancel, leaving...

    \frac{B_{north}}{B_{east}} = \rm \frac{60.5\ mN}{48.2\ mN} = 1.26

    Since B_{north} is the y-component and B_{east} is the x-component of \va{B}, the direction of \va{B} is...

    \theta = \tan^{-1}\frac{B_{north}}{B_{east}} = \tan^{-1} 1.26 = 51.5^\circ
