Nuclear Reactions

Nuclear Reactions

Radioactivity is an example of nuclear transmutation: one isotope being converted into another.

  • We can write a reaction equation for nuclear reactions just as we do for chemical ones.
  • For example, the alpha decay of uranium-238 can be written as:

    \rm{{_{92}^{238}}{U} \rightarrow {_{90}^{234}}{Th} + {_{2}^{4}}{He} + {_{0}^{0}}{\gamma}}

  • Alpha decay occurs when a nucleus loses two protons and two neutrons as an alpha particle (helium-4).
  • This reduces the nucleus’s atomic number by 2 and its atomic mass by 4.
  • The energy released by this reaction takes the form of kinetic energy (the \alpha is emitted at high speed) and the \gamma photon.

Conservation Laws

\rm{{_{92}^{238}}{U} \rightarrow {_{90}^{234}}{Th} + {_{2}^{4}}{He} + {_{0}^{0}}{\gamma}}

Nuclear reactions must obey a number of conservation laws.

  • The subscripts in the reaction equation indicate the charge of each particle.
  • Since charge is conserved, the total of the subscripts on each side of the equation must balance.
  • The superscripts indicate the total number of nucleons (protons and neutrons) which must also balance.
  • Nuclear reactions must conserve...
    1. Energy
    2. Momentum
    3. Electric Charge
    4. Nucleons
  • Mass is not a conserved quantity in nuclear reactions.
  • According to Einstein mass is a form of energy that can be transformed to or from other types of energy:

    E=mc^2

Neutrinos

An example of a beta decay reaction is:

\rm{{_{6}^{14}}{C} \rightarrow {_{7}^{14}}{N} + {_{-1}^{0}}{\beta} + {_{0}^{0}}{\overline\nu}}

  • The \beta particle is an electron and can also be written as {_{-1}^0}e.
  • We write the electron as having an “atomic number” of –1, because this is the electron’s charge, and total charge must be conserved.
  • The electron’s “mass number” is 0 because the electron does not count as a nucleon.
  • By balancing charge and nucleons, we can conclude that after \beta decay, the carbon-14 nucleus must have transmuted into nitrogen-14.
  • This means that one of the neutrons in C-14 has turned into a proton to make N-14.
  • The electron was created to conserve charge.

  • Scientists were surprised to discover that the kinetic energy of the particle (electron) does not account for all of the energy released by the reaction!
  • Also, the reaction does not produce any photons that could account for the missing energy.
  • Wolfgang Pauli proposed that the reaction must produce another particle that carries the missing energy.
  • Like the \gamma, this particle has no charge, and a mass (if any) that is much smaller than an electron’s.
  • Enrico Fermi named these particles neutrinos.
  • Because they could not be detected by any of the devices used to detect ionizing radiation, neutrinos must be non-ionizing and have very little interaction with matter; they are extremely penetrating but not dangerous.

Anti-Matter

If neutrons can turn into protons it should be possible for protons to turn into neutrons!

\rm{{_{11}^{22}}{Na} \rightarrow {_{10}^{22}}{Ne} + {_{1}^0}{\overline{e}} + {_0^0}{\nu}}

  • This process is called inverse beta decay or \beta+decay.
  • The regular \beta decay is often called \beta- decay
  • Based on the conservation laws, \beta+ decay must produce a positively-charged electron (!!!) which scientists call a positron.
  • Positrons are the antimatter equivalent of an electron.
  • Scientists now believe that all matter particles have a corresponding antimatter particle.
  • Regular \beta- decays produce an antimatter neutrino (anti-neutrino, \overline\nu) to “balance” the normal matter electron.
  • \beta+ decays produce a normal matter neutrino (\nu) to balance the antimatter positron.
  • The existence of positrons can be confirmed by the fact that they react readily with electrons:

    {_{-1}^0}{e} + {_{1}^0}{\overline{e}} \rightarrow 2\left({_0^0}\gamma\right)

  • A reaction like this in which a particle and its antiparticle react to produce energy is called pair annihilation.

  • Because momentum is conserved, the reaction must produce two photons with the same wavelength, moving in opposite directions.
  • Furthermore, energy conservation requires that each photon’s energy balance the energy of one of the particles:

    E = hf = mc^2

  • \gamma radiation is a form of energy released as a by-product of a nuclear reaction.
