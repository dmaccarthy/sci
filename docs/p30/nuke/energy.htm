Nuclear Energy

Mass & Energy

Albert Einstein proposed the idea of mass-energy equivalence.

  • According to Einstein, mass is a form of energy that can be transformed to or from other types of energy:

    E = m c^2

  • In chemical reactions, the energy released (or absorbed) in a reaction is typically on the order of \rm{10^5\ J/mol}.
  • This corresponds to a change in mass of about \rm{10^{-12}\ kg/mol} which is too small to notice. In chemical reactions, mass can be considered as a conserved quantity.
  • For nuclear reactions, the energy change is typically around \rm{10^{11}\ J/mol}, resulting in a noticeable mass change of \rm{10^{-6}\ kg/mol}.
  • As we’ve already seen, energy released may take the form of kinetic energy (e.g. of the \alpha, \beta-, and \beta+ particles), photons, or neutrinos.
  • Note that “heat” and “sound” cannot be produced directly, since these forms of energy involve the motion of molecules; nuclei exist on a much smaller distance scale than do molecules.
  • Since the mass of isotopes can be measured extremely accurately using a mass spectrometer, we can calculate the energy change in any nuclear reaction by comparing the masses of the products to the reactant(s).

Calculate the energy released by the \beta- decay of carbon-14.

{_{6}^{14}}{\rm C} \rightarrow {_{7}^{14}}{\rm N} + {_{-1}^{0}}e + {_{0}^{0}}{\overline\nu}

Mass Defect & Nuclear Binding Energy

Mass-energy calculations can be used to estimate the stability of various isotopes, using the rule-of-thumb that lower energy configurations tend to be more stable.

  • Mass defect is the difference between the actual mass of an isotope and the combined mass of the individual particles that comprise it.
  • Nuclear binding energy (NBE) is the energy equivalence of the mass defect using:

    \Delta E = \Delta\cdot m c^2

  • NBE represents the amount of energy needed to break the nucleus apart into individual nucleons; for example...

    {_2^4}{\rm {He}} \rightarrow 2\left({_1^1}{\rm H}\right) + 2\left({_0^1}n\right)

  • The relative stability of different isotopes can be estimated by comparing the nuclear binding energies per nucleon; i.e. the NBE divided by the mass number.
  • Energies of nuclear reaction are often expressed in megaelectronvolts.
  • \rm{MeV/c^2} is sometimes used as a unit of mass; it is defined as the mass equivalent of \rm{1\ MeV} of energy.
  • Another unit of mass is the atomic mass unit (u or amu), which is defined such that carbon-12 has a mass of exactly 12 u.
  • The atomic mass unit is equal to \rm{1.6605 \times 10^{-27}\ kg}, a fraction of a percent smaller than the proton or neutron mass.

  1. A certain nuclear reaction transforms 1.00% of the initial mass into other forms of energy. The energy released can be used to generate electricity. The entire process has an overall efficiency of 12.0%. What mass of reactants would be required each day to power a 500 MW electric generating station?

    2. Calculate the total energy generated in 24.0 hours...

    \Delta E_{useful} = P_{useful}\Delta t = \rm 5.00\times 10^8\ W\cdot 8.64\times 10^4\ s = 4.32\times 10^{13}\ J

    Taking the efficiency into account, calculate the amount of nuclear energy that must be released...

    \Delta E_{input} = \frac{\Delta E_{useful}}{\varepsilon} = \rm \frac{4.32\times 10^{13}\ J}{0.120} = 3.64\times 10^{14}\ J

    Convert the energy into a mass equivalence...

    \Delta m = \frac{\Delta E_{input}}{c^2} = \rm \frac{3.64\times 10^{14}\ J}{(3.00\times 10^8\ m/s)^2} = 4.00\times 10^{-3}\ kg

    Since the converted mass (4.00 grams) represents 1.00% of the reactant mass, the mass of the reactant would need to be 4.00 g / 0.0100 = 400 grams per day.
