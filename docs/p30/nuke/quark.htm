The Standard Model

Quarks & Leptons

Murray Gell-Mann proposed in 1963 that protons and neutrons are not fundamental particles; instead, they are made up of three smaller particles which Gell-Mann named quarks.

  • The strong nuclear force is a short-range, attractive force that acts between quarks.
  • Scattering experiments using high-energy particle accelerators later confirmed that when other particles collide with protons or neutrons, the nucleons behave as three “scattering centres” rather than just one.
  • Protons consist of two “up” quarks and one “down” quark.
  • Neutrons are two “downs” and one “up”.

  • The fundamental particles of matter now include four first generation particles: the up quark, the down quark, the electron, and the neutrino.
  • Each of these particles has a corresponding antiparticle.
  • Physicists discovered two additional generations of fundamental particles.
  • The higher generation particles have the same charge as their first generation counterparts, but have much higher mass-energies.

  • The electron, neutrino, their antiparticles, and their second and third generation counterparts are known collectively as leptons.
  • The first generation neutrino can also be called an electron-neutrino to distinguish it from the muon-neutrino and tau-neutrino.
  • Unlike quarks, leptons do not experience the strong nuclear force.

Mediating Particles

In the quantum field theory, fundamental particles exert forces on each other by exchanging mediating particles.

  • For example, all charged particles are constantly emitted and absorbing photons.
  • The photon is the mediating particle for the electromagnetic force.
  • The table below lists the fundamental mediating particles that are responsible for the four fundamental forces of nature...

  • The weak nuclear force is the one responsible for beta-type decays.
  • This force has been successfully “unified” with the electromagnetic force; physicists often refer to the “electroweak” force.
  • There is another fundamental particle called the Higgs boson that is responsible for other particles acquiring mass.

Other Particles

Fundamental particles can join together to form additional particles.

  • Three quark combinations are called baryons, which include protons and neutrons.
  • Combinations of one quark and one antiquark are called mesons.
  • Particles that experience the strong nuclear force (including all quarks, baryons, and mesons, but not leptons or mediating particles) are called hadrons.
  • Fermions include all quarks and leptons as well as combinations of an odd number of fermions (e.g. baryons).
  • Bosons include the mediating particles and combinations of an even number of fermions (e.g. mesons).
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: Fermions exist in unique quantum states.
  • This is why the electrons in an atom (with more than two electrons) cannot all be in the first energy level at the same time; electrons are fermions.
  • This restriction does not apply to bosons. For example, a laser can produce a coherent beam of light because photons are bosons; many photons can be created in the same quantum state.
