The nucleus of the Rutherford-Bohr atom consists of positively charged protons and neutral neutrons, both of which have masses of about \mathrm{1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg}.

  • The number of protons is called the atomic number; this determines which element a nuclide is.
  • The total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons) is called the mass number.
  • Most elements have more than one isotope; carbon always has 6 protons but can have 6, 7, or 8 neutrons.
  • In isotope notation, the atomic number (charge) is written as a left-subscript and the mass number (nucleons) is written as a left-superscript.
  • In a nuclear reaction, the charge and nucleons must be conserved, in addition to energy and momentum:

    {_{6}^{14}}{\mathrm C} \rightarrow {_{7}^{14}}{\mathrm N} + {_{-1}^{0}}e + {_{0}^{0}}{\overline\nu}

The mass of each isotope can be measured very accurately using a mass spectrometer, which operates on the same principles as Thomson’s CRT experiment.

  • A beam is produced by subjecting the ions of the sample to an accelerating voltage:

    qV_{acc} = W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

  • If the ions do not begin from rest or do not all experiences the same potential difference, we can use a velocity selector (balanced electric and magnetic forces) to select only those ions with the desired speed:

    qvB = F_m = F_e = q|\vec{\mathbf E}|

    v = \frac{|\vec{\mathbf E}|}{B}

  • In the detector, a uniform magnetic field makes the ions move in uniform circular motion:

    qvB = F_m = ma = m\frac{v^2}{r}

Radioactivity refers to the natural emission of ionizing radiation by certain isotopes.

  • Alpha particles are helium-4 nuclei emitted by some large isotopes:

    {_{92}^{238}}{\mathrm U} \rightarrow {_{90}^{234}}\mathrm{Th} + {_2^4}\mathrm{He}

  • Alpha particles interact highly with matter and are very damaging, but have low pentrating power.
  • Beta particles are high-speed electrons emitted when a neutron turns into a proton, such as in the \beta- decay of carbon-14 shown above.
  • Gamma radiation are photons that carry away some of the energy released by a nuclear reaction. They have the most penetrating power as they interact the least with matter.
  • The \alpha, \beta, and \gamma can be distinguished by their charge (different deflection in an electric or magnetic field) and their penetrating power.
  • The ability to ionize matter provides several methods of detecting these particles: photographic film, photomultiplier tubes, scintillation, cloud or bubble chambers, or a Geiger-Mueller tube.

Radioactive decay is random; it follows an exponential probability distribution with a half-life that is characteristic of each isotope:

N = N_0\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n

n = \frac{t}{T_{1/2}}

  • Half-life is defined as the time over which the probability of any individual nucleus decaying is 50%. For a large sample size, the amount remaining after one half-life will be very close to one-half of the initial amount.
  • N is any measure of the amount of the radioactive isotope present, such as the number or mass of these atoms, the percentage remaining, or the activity (measured in becquerels, Bq).
  • From a graph of N vs. t, find the point on the graph where the quantity remaining is half the original amount; this allows you to read the half-life from the t-axis.
  • Radiocarbon dating is used to determine the age of dead material (wood, bone, leather, etc.) based on how much radioactive carbon-14 remains.

Einstein proposed the idea of mass-energy equivalence:

E=mc^2

  • Mass is a form of energy that can be converted to (or from) other energy forms.
  • The energy released in a nuclear reaction is large enough that there is a measurable change in the mass!
  • By comparing the mass of the products to the reactants, we can calculate the energy released by a reaction.
  • The mass defect is the difference between the mass of a nuclide and its individual nucleons:

    {_{92}^{235}}{\mathrm U} \rightarrow 92\left({_1^1}{\mathrm H}\right) + 143\left({_0^1}n\right)

  • Multiplying the mass defect by c^2 gives the nuclear binding energy, the energy needed to disassemble the nucleus.
  • A larger NBE per nucleon indicates a more stable isotope.
  • Iron-56 is the most stable isotope based on the greatest NBE per nucleon.

Nuclei larger than iron-56 tend to undergo nuclear fission, reactions where a nucleus breaks into two or more smaller nuclei.

  • With some isotopes, fission can be induced by neutron bombardment:

    {_{92}^{235}}{\mathrm U} + {_0^1}n \rightarrow {_{92}^{236}}{\mathrm U} \rightarrow {_{56}^{141}}{\mathrm {Ba}} + {_{36}^{92}}{\mathrm {Kr}} + 3\left({_0^1}n\right)

  • If the nuclear fuel exceeds critical mass, each neutron absorbed has a likelihood of emitting more than one additional neutron that will itself be absorbed. This results in a chain reaction.
  • We can reduce the critical mass by enriching the fuel to have a higher proportion of fissile nuclei.
  • We can also enhance the reaction by moderating the neutrons (for example by allowing them to collide with deuterium nuclei) so they have the optimal speed for creating a chain reaction.
  • Control rods made of a material that absorbs neutrons can be used to control the rate of reaction.
  • Pressurized heavy water can be used as both a moderator and as a coolant to collect energy from the reactor as heat.
  • The heat can be used to generate steam that turns a turbine connected to an electrical generator.

Nuclei smaller than iron-56 tend to undergo nuclear fusion, joining together to form larger nuclei.

  • Because both reacting nuclei are positively charged, it is very difficult to overcome the long-range electric repulsion to move the nuclei close enough for the short-range strong nuclear force to kick in and allow the fusion to occur.
  • Fusion requires extremely high temperatures and pressures such as found in the core of the Sun and other stars.

Protons and neutrons are not fundamental particles. According to the standard model of particle physics, each is composed of three smaller particles called quarks.

  • Quarks are fundamental fermions that experience the strong nuclear force.
  • A proton consists of two “up” quarks and one “down” quark.
  • A neutron consists of one “up” quark and two “down” quarks.
  • Fundamental fermions that do not experience the strong nuclear force (electrons and neutrinos) are called leptons.
  • Neutrinos have extremely small masses, are very penetrating, and interact minimally with matter (they are non-ionizing).
  • There are second and third generation quarks and leptons which have higher energies than the first generation ones.
  • Fermions obey the Pauli Exclusion Principle: each fermion must have a unique quantum state. This is why you cannot put an unlimitted number of electrons in an atom’s lowest energy level.
  • Particles that do not follow the Exclusion Principle are called bosons.
  • Several fundamental bosons are responsible for all (except maybe gravity?) interactions (forces) that occur in nature...
    • Photons are responsible for electromagnetic forces.
    • Gluons are responsible for the strong nuclear force.
    • The Z and W^\pm bosons are responsible for the weak nuclear force which causes \beta- and \beta+ decays.
    • Gravitons (hypothesized but not yet observed) may be responsible for gravitational forces.
    • The Higgs boson is responsible for all other particles having mass.

For each of the twelve fundamental fermions, there is a corresponding anti-particle.

  • The electron’s anti-particle is called the positron.
  • Positrons have the same mass but opposite sign charge as a normal electron.
  • Positrons are emitted in \beta+ decay where a proton turns into a neutron.
  • Positrons and electrons can undergo pair annihilation where all of their mass-energy is transformed into photons:

    {_{-1}^0}{e} + {_{1}^0}{\overline{e}} \rightarrow 2\left({_0^0}\gamma\right)

  • If the electron/positron momenta are much smaller than the momentum of each photon, the photons must have the same wavelength and opposite directions so that their combined momentum is close to zero.
