Diffraction

Single Slit Diffraction

In Young’s double-slit experiment, we saw that interference of light passing through two slits separated by a distance d, could be described by an equation:

\sin\theta = \frac{n\lambda}{d}

  • The angles where the interference maxima occur can be found by solving for \theta when n is a whole number (0, 1, 2, ...)
  • The interference minima occur where n is a half number (0.5, 1.5, 2.5, ...)
  • Graphing the relative intensity of the light against n produces the graph shown below in red...

Another experiment we could do, would be to look at the interference produced by light passing through a single, wide slit of width d.

  • Interference happens, according to Huygens’ Principle, because each individual point along the opening acts as a source of wavelets, and the wavelets from each point overlap with the wavelets from all other points.

  • The intensity of the interference pattern produced by the single wide slit is graphed above in blue.
  • The most notable difference, is that the first, second, and higher order maxima are much less intense than the central maximum.
  • Nearly all of the wave’s energy occurs within the central maximum.
  • Except for n = 0, which is a maximum for both patterns, the other maxima and minima are shifted by an additional 0.5...

Scientists use the term diffraction to describe the “spreading out” of waves as a result of interference as they pass by obstacles or through openings in a barrier.

  • For a double-slit experiment where the slits have a finite width, both interference patterns occur: dots due to the double-slit with the intensity modulated by the wide slit diffraction.

  • In the photo above, the first-order wide-slit minimum (bottom numbers) coincides with the 7th-order double-slit maximum (top numbers). This indicates that the width of the slits is about 1/7th of the slit separation.

Example

Estimate how much the following waves “spread out” as they pass through an 80.0 cm wide door...

A “treble” sound wave with \lambda = 20.0\ \mathrm{cm}

Calculate the location of the first minimum at n = 1...

\theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{\lambda}{d} = \sin^{-1}\mathrm{\frac{20.0\ cm}{80.0\ cm}} = 14.5^\circ

Nearly all of the diffracted wave’s energy would be found within 14.5° of centre, with very low intensity at larger angles.

A mid-range sound wave with \lambda = 60.0\ \mathrm{cm}

\theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{\lambda}{d} = \sin^{-1}\mathrm{\frac{60.0\ cm}{80.0\ cm}} = 48.6^\circ

A “bass” sound wave with \lambda = 2.00\ \mathrm{m}

\theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{\lambda}{d} = \sin^{-1}\mathrm{\frac{200\ cm}{80.0\ cm}} = \mathrm{No\ Solution!}

  • When the wavelength is larger than the opening, there is no solution for the first minimum at n=1; this means the wave diffracts (spreads out) completely!
  • Larger wavelengths are better at diffracting than smaller wavelengths.
  • This is why we can hear the bass better than the treble when music is playing in a different room.
  • AM radio (longer \lambda) travels better than FM.

A light wave with \lambda = 500\ \mathrm{nm}

\theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{\lambda}{d} = \sin^{-1}\mathrm{\frac{5.00\times 10^{-7}\ m}{0.800\ m}} = (3.58\times 10^{-5})^\circ

  • The wavelength of visible light is so small that it essentially travels in stright lines with negligible diffraction, unless the opening is extremely narrow.
  • When scientists assume light moves in straight lines without diffraction, we call this the ray model.

Resolution

When light from a point source passes through an opening (like the pupil of your eye or the lens of a telescope), diffraction causes the light to spread out.

  • When two point sources are far enough apart, the light will strike your retina in such a way that you can resolve two separate sources.
  • When the sources get too close together, the diffraction patterns will overlap and you will no longer be able to resolve the sources.

  • We can make a rough estimate of how far apart the sources must be to resolve them:

    \theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{\lambda}{d}

  • A more accurate equation for resolving two sources, that accounts for the circular shape of the lens, is called the Rayleigh criterion:

    \theta \approx \left(1.22\ \frac{\lambda}{d}\right) \frac{180^\circ}{\pi}

  • Astronomers prefer very large telescopes (big d) because that lets them resolve smaller angles.
