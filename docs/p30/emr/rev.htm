Faraday’s Law states that a changing magnetic field produces an electric field. Maxwell’s Law proposes that the reverse is also true: a changing electric field generates a magnetic field.

  • The consequence of Maxwell’s Law is electromagnetic radiation (EMR).
  • Once the initial field starts to change (because of non-steady currents / accelerating charges), \vec{\mathbf E} and \vec{\mathbf B} will generate each other and propagate as EMR.
  • Using some advanced math, Maxwell showed that EMR behaves as waves that travel at a speed of c = 3.00\times 10^8\ \mathrm{m/s}.
  • The wave comprises electric and magnetic fields that oscillate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.

Visible light is an EM wave because:

  • Light travels at the same speed predicted by Maxwell, as measured by Rømer & Huygens, Fizeau, and especially Michelson’s experiment with an N-sided rotating mirror:

    c = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \frac{2L}{\frac{1}{N} T}

  • Light travels slower through a medium than it does in vacuum depending on the medium’s index of refraction, n:

    v=\frac{c}{n}

  • Light exhibits an interference pattern in Young’s double-slit experiment.
  • The interference pattern can be explained by Huygen’s principle, and leads to the result that the maxima (anti-nodes) and minima (nodes) are located at angles \theta from the central maximum, where:

    \sin\theta=\frac{n\lambda}{d}

  • d is the distance between the two slits.
  • Whole number values of n identify the maxima and half numbers (0.5, 1.5, etc.) identify the minima.
  • We can use this result to measure the wavelength of the light observed through a diffraction grating, and to separate light into its individual wavelengths in a spectroscope:

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n}

Diffraction is the phenomenon of waves bending or spreading out once they pass through an opening in a barrier.

  • Each point on the opening must be treated as a source, according to Huygen’s principle.
  • The interfrence pattern has maxima and minima described by the same equation as the double-slit experiment, except that the minima occur at the natural numbers (1, 2, 3, etc.) and the maxima occur at 0, 1.5, 2.5, etc.
  • Nearly all of the diffracted wave’s energy falls within the central maxima, as the other maxima have much smaller amplitudes.
  • Wavelengths larger than the size of the opening diffract completely.
  • Wavelengths much smaller than the opening hardly diffract at all.
  • Because of its small wavelength, visible light can be treated as moving in straight lines (the ray model) except when passing through openings that are extremely small.
  • Diffraction limits the ability of optical devices (microscopes, telescopes, your eye) to resolve two sources that are close together, as their diffraction patterns will overlap.

When a wave reaches a boundary between two media, Huygen’s principle states that the points along the boundary should be treated as sources.

  • Part of the wave’s energy will be reflected back into the original medium, and part will be transmitted or refracted into the new medium.
  • The reflected and refracted waves must have the same frequency.
  • The wavelength and direction of the wave will change depending on the speed of the wave in the two media according to Snell’s Law:

    \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_2} = \frac{v_1}{v_2} = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2}

  • Angles of incidence and refraction are measured between the normal to the boundary and the direction of the wave’s propagation.
  • When a wave passes from a slower to a faster medium, there will be a critical angle for \theta_1 at which \theta_2 = 90^\circ.
  • When the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, total internal reflection occurs; the wave is completed reflected and no refracted wave exists.
