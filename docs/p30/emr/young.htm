Huygens’ Principle

What is the nature of light?

  • Sir Isaac Newton believed that light was composed of corpuscles (particles).
  • Newton’s view was opposed by Christiaan Huygens who believed that light was a wave.
  • Huygens was one of the first scientists to study waves systematically. He developed what is now known as Huygens’ Principle...
  • Each point on a wave can be considered as a point source producing circular (for 2D) or spherical (for 3D) wavelets.
  • The future shape of the wave may be determined by adding up all of these wavelets.
  • When waves overlap to produce a larger wave (crest+crest or trough+trough) we call this constructive interference.
  • When waves overlap to produce a smaller wave (crest+trough) we call this destructive interference.
  • The simplest example of Huygens’ Principle is a wave (e.g. water waves) passing through a very narrow opening in a barrier.
  • The opening can be treated as a single point source: the waves should spread out in a semi-circular pattern on the other side of the barrier.

  • The grey arrows represent the direction of energy flow.

Two-Source Interference

When there are two narrow openings in the barrier, the wavelets from one opening will overlap and interfere with the wavelets from the other opening.

  • Constructive interference will occur wherever two crests (blue) or two troughs (red) overlap.
  • Locations of maximum constructive interference are called interference maxima or anti-nodes.
  • Destructive interference will occur where a crest overlaps a trough.
  • Locations of complete destructive interference are called interference minima or nodes.
  • The result of two-source interference will be an alternating pattern of maxima and minima.

Geometry of 2-Source Interference

All points along the central maximum are the same distance from both sources.

  • These points will always experience constructive inteference.
  • Destructive interference will occur at points that are one-half of a wavelength closer to one source than the other, because one wave will be exactly \frac{1}{2}\lambda ahead of the other.
  • When the path difference is exactly \lambda or an exact multiple (n\lambda, if n is a whole number) another maximum will occur.
  • We can derive an equation for the locations of the maxima and minima from the diagram below.

  • Points S_1 and S_2 are the sources and d is the distance between them.
  • Point P is an arbitrary point far away from the sources.
  • Point Q is the same distance from P as S_2, so that the triangle PQS_2 is isoceles.
  • The segment S_1Q is the path difference, n\lambda.
  • When P is very far away (many times larger than d), the angle at P will be very close to zero and the angles at Q and S_2 will be very close to 90º.
  • We can approximate the triangle QS_1S_2 as a right triangle since the angle at Q is about 90º:

    \sin\theta = \frac{n\lambda}{d}

Example

Water waves with a wavelength of 2.00 metres pass through two openings separated by 5.00 metres. Find the locations of all the maxima.

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{n\lambda}{d}\right)

The central maximum (n=0) is always located at \theta = 0^\circ.

The first-order maximum (n=1) will be at:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{1\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{23.6^\circ}

The second-order maximum (n=2) will be at:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{2\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{53.1^\circ}

There are no higher-order maxima; if we attempt to set n=3, the expression in brackets will be greater than 1:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{3\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{No\ Solution!}

Because the \sin function has a maximum value of 1, there will only be solutions for:

n \le \frac{d}{\lambda}

Find the locations of all the minima.

The first-order minimum (n=0.5) will be at:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{0.5\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{11.5^\circ}

The second-order minimum (n=1.5) will be at:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{1.5\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{36.9^\circ}

The third-order minimum (n=2.5) will be at:

\theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathrm{2.5\cdot 2.00\ m}}{\mathrm{5.00\ m}}\right) = \mathrm{90.0^\circ}

Young’s Experiment

To settle the dispute between Newton and Huygens, Thomas Young conducted his double-slit experiment in 1801.

  • Light will be passed through a double-slit (two narrow openings set close together).
  • If light is a wave, like Huygens believed, an interference pattern of alternating bright (maxima) and dark (minima) spots should be produced.
  • If light is made of particles, like Newton believed, there should be two bright spots produced corresponding to the two slits.
  • The results show an interfernce pattern, supporting Huygens’ belief that light is a wave.

  • The variation in brightness is due to the fact that the slits are not points (as we assumed) but rather have a finite width.
  • An additional benefit of Young’s experiment was that it provided a method of separating light into different colours (wavelengths).
  • Furthermore, the equation can be rearranged to calculate the wavelength of light:

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n}

  • It is through measurements based on Young’s experiment that we know visible light has wavelengths ranging from just under 400 nm (violet) to around 750 nm (red).

Spectroscopy

A device that uses this phenomenon to separate and measure wavelengths is called a spectroscope.

  • Spectroscopes typically use a diffraction grating with several hundred evenly spaced slits rather than just two.
  • A diffraction grating produces an interference pattern that obeys the same equation as the double-slit, but the maxima are better-defined.
  • A diffraction grating is often described by the number of lines per mm; for example, 300 lines per mm means:

    d = \frac{1}{300}\ \mathrm{mm} = 3.33\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m}

  • Spectroscopy is the branch of science that deals with the analysis of light sources.
  • It is used in chemistry as a method of identifying substances (since the pattern of light that each substance emits or absorbs is unique).
  • It is also used in astronomy where the objects of study are often too far away to collect any data other than the EMR that they emit, absorb, or reflect.

Practice

  1. Green light is observed through a diffraction grating with 500 lines per mm. The second-order interference maximum is observed at 30.0°.
    1. Calculate the wavelength and frequency of the green light wave.

      2. d = \rm \frac{1}{500} \ mm = 2.00\times 10^{-6}\ m

      \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.00\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 30.0^\circ}{2} = 5.00\times 10^{-7}\ m = 500\ nm

      f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{5.00\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 6.00\times 10^{14}\ Hz

    2. Predict the locations of the first- and third-order maxima.

      3. d\sin\theta = n\lambda

      \sin\theta = \frac{n\lambda}{d}

      \theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{n\lambda}{d}

      \theta_1 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1\cdot 5.00\times 10^{-7}\ m}{2.00\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = 14.5^\circ

      \theta_3 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{3\cdot 5.00\times 10^{-7}\ m}{2.00\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = 48.6^\circ

  2. Determine the angle of the first- and second-order maxima for light with \lambda = 492 nm passing through a diffraction grating with 700 lines per mm.

    3. d = \rm \frac{1}{700} \ mm = 1.43\times 10^{-6}\ m

    The algebra is the same as the previous question...

    \theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{n\lambda}{d}

    \theta_1 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1\cdot 4.92\times 10^{-7}\ m}{1.43\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = 20.1^\circ

    \theta_2 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{2\cdot 4.92\times 10^{-7}\ m}{1.43\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = 43.5^\circ

  3. Light passes through a green filter and then through a diffraction grating with 250 lines per mm. A first-order maximum is observed at an angle of 7.24°.
    1. Calculate the wavelength of the green light.

      2. d = \rm \frac{1}{250} \ mm = 4.00\times 10^{-6}\ m

      \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{4.00\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 7.24^\circ}{1} = 5.04\times 10^{-7}\ m = 504\ nm

    2. Predict the angle at which the second-order maximum will be located.

      3. \theta = \sin^{-1}\frac{n\lambda}{d}

      \theta_2 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{2\cdot 5.04\times 10^{-7}\ m}{4.00\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = 14.6^\circ

    3. [Attempt to...] Calculate the angle at which the 8th-order maximum will be located. Can you explain your result? [Hint: Can the path difference ever by more than d? Compare 8\lambda to d.]

      4. \theta_8 = \rm \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{8\cdot 5.04\times 10^{-7}\ m}{4.00\times 10^{-6}\ m}\right) = \sin^{-1}(1.008) = Undefined!

      The \sin^{-1} function does not have a solution when the argument exceeds 1; there is only a solution when:

      n\lambda < d

      n < \frac{d}{\lambda}

  4. Light with \lambda = 650 nm produces a first-order maximum at an angle of 12.6°. What is the slit spacing of the diffraction grating used?

    5. \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n}

    d = \frac{n\lambda}{\sin\theta} = \rm \frac{1\cdot 6.50\times 10^{-7}\ m}{\sin 12.6^\circ} = 2.98\times 10^{-6}\ m

  5. A spectroscope is a device that uses a diffraction grating to separate light into different wavelengths. When light from a hydrogen gas discharge tube passes through a spectroscope, different colours of light are observed to produce first-order maxima at angles of 9.2°, 9.5°, 10.0°, 11.2° and 15.2°. The spectroscope uses a grating with 400 lines per mm. Calculate the wavelengths and frequencies of these five observed colours of light.

    6. d = \rm \frac{1}{400} \ mm = 2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m

    For 9.2°...

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 9.2^\circ}{1} = 4.0\times 10^{-7}\ m

    f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{4.0\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 7.5\times 10^{14}\ Hz

    For 9.5°...

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 9.5^\circ}{1} = 4.1\times 10^{-7}\ m

    f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{4.1\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 7.3\times 10^{14}\ Hz

    For 10.0°...

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 10.0^\circ}{1} = 434\ nm

    f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{4.34\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 6.91\times 10^{14}\ Hz

    For 11.2°...

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 11.2^\circ}{1} = 486\ nm

    f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{4.86\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 6.18\times 10^{14}\ Hz

    For 15.2°...

    \lambda = \frac{d\sin\theta}{n} = \rm \frac{2.50\times 10^{-6}\ m\cdot \sin 15.2^\circ}{1} = 655\ nm

    f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{6.55\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 4.58\times 10^{14}\ Hz
