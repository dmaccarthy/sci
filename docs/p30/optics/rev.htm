Notes

Because reflection and refraction both change the direction of a light ray, image formation can occur.

  • Images are formed where the reflected or refracted rays appear to originate from.
  • Real images are produced where the reflected/refracted rays converge; real images can be projected, for example onto a screen or a digital camera sensor.
  • Virtual images, which cannot be projected, are produced when the reflected/refracted rays diverge; we can locate the image by extending these rays backwards to locate their apparent source.
  • Images can also be classified as enlarged/reduced and upright/inverted.

Curved mirrors and lenses have a focal point; a point along the principal axis through which all paraxial incident rays are focussed.

  • Curved mirrors are usually shaped as a parabaloid, which produces a sharp focus.
  • Spherical mirrors exhibit spherical aberration; the focus is spread out rather than at a single point.
  • Chromatic aberration refers to the fact that in lenses the index of refraction may depend on the frequency of the light, causing different colours to have different focal points.

Principal rays are rays that we can draw without having to measure any angles, that allow us to find the location and properties of an image.

  1. Draw an incident ray from the object parallel to the principal axis. The reflected/refracted ray will pass througha or away fromb the focal point.
  2. Draw an incident ray from the object in line with the focusc. The reflected/refracted ray will propagate parallel to the principal axis.
  3. Draw an incident ray from the object in line with the centre of curvatured or the centre of the lens itself. The ray will reflect straight back on itself (mirrors) or propagate with no change of direction (lenses).

a - Applies to concave mirrors and converging lenses (f>0).
b - Applies to convex mirrors and diverging lenses (f<0).
c - Use the secondary focus for lenses.
d - For mirrors, the centre of curvature is at double the focal length.

For both mirrors and lenses, we can calculate the location of the image and determine whether it is real or virtual by solving:

\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}

The magnification tells us whether the image is upright or inverted and enlarged or reduced:

m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o}
