Lesson Notes

Ray Diagrams

Ray Model

Because light has a very small wavelength, in most situations it does not diffract significantly.

  • The ray model of light assumes that light can be treated as travelling in straight lines, except where it reflects or refracts from a boundary.
  • When light rays change direction due to reflection (mirror) or refraction (lens), light may appear to originate from a place other than its actual origin.

Image Formation

The point where the reflected/refracted light appears to originate is the image of the object where the light actually originates.


  • This ray diagram shows refraction of light from a submerged object at the surface of the pond.
  • The rays shown in blue are the incident rays; the rays that travel from the object to the reflective or refractive surface.
  • The rays shown in red are the refracted rays that change direction at the surface according to Snell’s Law.
  • To an observer outside the water, the refracted rays appear to be coming from a point that is shallower than the object actually is!
  • The apparent source of the refracted rays is the location of the image.
  • The dashed grey lines that extend the refracted rays back to the image are called virtual rays. The image is called a virtual image.
  • Sometimes the reflected/refracted rays actually cross without having to be extended backwards. An image formed by real reflected/refracted rays is called a real image.
  • Real images can be projected onto a screen (e.g. as in a movie projector or a camera.) Virtual images cannot, because there are no actual light rays there.
  • When all the reflected/refracted rays from a point object converge to form an image at a single point, the image will be sharp.
  • A fuzzy image is produced when the rays from a single point converge over a region larger than a single point.
  • A ray diagram is an illustration of the incident and reflected/refracted rays that we use to locate the image.
  • To draw a ray diagram, you will need a protractor to ensure that the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence for reflection, while the angle of refraction must be calculated from the Snell’s Law formula.
