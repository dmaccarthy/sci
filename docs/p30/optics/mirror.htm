Lesson Notes

Mirror Equation

Derivation

A principal ray diagram for a curved mirror results in several pairs of similar triangles.

  • The ratio of the heights of the similar pairs is equal to the ratio of the bases.

    \frac{-h_i}{h_o} = \color{green}{\frac{d_i}{d_o}} = \color{#0065fe}{\frac{f}{d_o-f}} = \color{red}{\frac{d_i-f}{f}}

  • By combining (“cross multiplying”) the blue and green parts of the equation:

    d_o f = d_i d_o - d_i f

  • If we now divide this equation by the product of all three variables, fd_od_i:

    \frac{d_o f}{fd_o d_i} = \frac{d_i d_o}{fd_o d_i} - \frac{d_i f}{fd_o d_i}

    \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o}

  • The remaining parts of the original equation (black and green) tell us the magnification:

    m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o}

Example 1

An object is placed 5.0 cm from a concave mirror with a focal length of 3.0 cm. Find the location and properties of the image.

Given:

  • f = 3.0\ \mathrm{cm}
  • d_o = 5.0\ \mathrm{cm}

Solve for d_i:

\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{3.0\ \mathrm{cm}} - \frac{1}{5.0\ \mathrm{cm}} = \frac{2}{15}\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}

Remember that this is the reciprocal of d_i:

d_i = \frac{15}{2}\ \mathrm{cm}= 7.5\ \mathrm{cm}

Calculate m:

m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{7.5\ \mathrm{cm}}{5.0\ \mathrm{cm}} = -1.5

The image is real, inverted, and enlarged.

Example 2

An object is placed 5.0 cm from a convex mirror with a focal length of 3.0 cm. Find the location and properties of the image.

Given:

  • f = -3.0\ \mathrm{cm}
  • d_o = 5.0\ \mathrm{cm}

Solve for d_i:

\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-3.0\ \mathrm{cm}} - \frac{1}{5.0\ \mathrm{cm}} = \frac{-8}{15}\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}

d_i = \frac{15}{-8}\ \mathrm{cm}= -1.9\ \mathrm{cm}

Calculate m:

m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-1.9\ \mathrm{cm}}{5.0\ \mathrm{cm}} = 0.38

The image is virtual, upright, and reduced.

Practice

    1. An object is placed 8.00 cm in front of a concave mirror with a focal length of 5.00 cm. Predict the location and properties of the image.
    2. Repeat for a convex mirror.
  2. Use the mirror equation to predict the location and properties (real/virtual, upright/inverted, magnification) of the image. Draw an accurate principal ray diagram to verify the prediction.
    1. f = 6.0 cm, d_o = 12.0 cm.
    2. f = –6.0 cm, d_o = 12.0 cm.
    3. f = 5.0 cm, d_o = 3.0 cm.
  3. An object placed 2.00 cm from a mirror produces a virtual image 2.73 cm from the mirror.
    1. Calculate the focal length of the mirror. Is the mirror concave or convex?
    2. Draw an accurate principal ray diagram to verify your calculations.
  4. An object placed 5.00 cm from a mirror produces a real image with a magnification of –0.375.
    1. Calculate the location of the image.
    2. Calculate the focal length of the mirror. Is the mirror concave or convex?
    3. Draw an accurate principal ray diagram to verify your calculations.
  5. A convex mirror has a focal length of 6.00 cm.
    1. To obtain a virtual image 1.50 cm from the mirror, where would one have to place the object?
    2. Calculate the image’s magnification.
    3. Draw an accurate principal ray diagram to verify your calculations.

  6. A concave mirror with f = 37.5 cm is to be used to project the image of a glowing object (e.g. a candle) onto a screen as illustrated.

    The candle is 2.00 m from the screen (i.e. d_i - d_o = 2.00 m.) How far from the candle should the mirror be placed (d_o) to obtain an image that is “in focus”? Use the mirror equation to solve the problem and verify your solution by drawing an accurate principal ray diagram.
