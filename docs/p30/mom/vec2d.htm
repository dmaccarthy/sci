2D Vectors Review

2D Vectors

Vectors are quantities that involve a direction, such as displacement, velocity, acceleration, and force.

  • We can illustrate vectors using a vector diagram, in which each vector is drawn as an arrow illustrating the direction and the magnitude (size) of the vector.
  • A vector described by its magnitude and direction is said to be in polar form:

    \vec{\bf F} = \rm{8.0\ N\ [30°]}

  • Directions are measured as angles measured counterclockwise from the +x-axis.
  • Negative angles indicate clockwise from the +x-axis.
  • The components of a vector describe “how much” of the vector is in each of the x and y directions.
  • We can use trigonometry to calculate the components of a vector from its magnitude and direction:

    {\vec{\bf{F}}}_x=\left|\vec{\bf{F}}\right|\cos{\theta}

    {\vec{\bf{F}}}_y=\left|\vec{\bf{F}}\right|\sin{\theta}

  • Describing a vector by its components is called the Cartesian form of the vector:

    \vec{\bf F} = \rm{(6.9, 4.0)\ N}

  • We can also use trigonometry to convert a vector from Cartesian to polar form:

    \left|\vec{\bf{F}}\right|=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}

    \tan{\theta}=\frac{{\vec{\bf{F}}}_y}{{\vec{\bf{F}}}_x}

  • When solving for \theta using \tan^{-1}, you will get only one of two possible solutions; when the x-component is negative, you will have to add 180° to get the correct answer.
  • Compass directions should be converted to conventional angles...
East0°
North90°
West180°
South270°

Vector Diagrams

We can illustrate addition of two or more 2D vectors by drawing a tip-to-tail vector diagram.

  • Example: A speedboat has a velocity of 20.0 m/s [120°] relative to the water. There is a current of 5.00 m/s [200°]. Determine the resultant velocity.

  • To subtract a vector, just add its negative!

Vector Arithmetic

To add or subtract vectors more precisely than is possible with a diagram, convert all vectors to Cartesian form...

\vec{\bf v}_{1x} = |\vec{\bf v}_1| \cos\theta_1 = \rm{20.0\ m/s\cdot\cos 120° = -10.0\ m/s}

\vec{\bf v}_{1y} = |\vec{\bf v}_1| \sin\theta_1 = \rm{20.0\ m/s\cdot\sin 120° = +17.3\ m/s}

\vec{\bf v}_{2x} = |\vec{\bf v}_2| \cos\theta_2 = \rm{5.00\ m/s\cdot\cos 200° = -4.70\ m/s}

\vec{\bf v}_{2y} = |\vec{\bf v}_2| \sin\theta_2 = \rm{5.00\ m/s\cdot\sin 200° = -1.71\ m/s}

Then perform the calculations separately on the x- and y-components...

\vec{\bf v}_x = \vec{\bf v}_{1x} + \vec{\bf v}_{2x}= \rm{(-10.0 + (-4.70))\ m/s = -14.7\ m/s}

\vec{\bf v}_y = \vec{\bf v}_{1y} + \vec{\bf v}_{2y}= \rm{(+17.3 + (-1.71))\ m/s = +15.6\ m/s}

  • If the resultant vector needs to be in polar form, just convert it using the Pythagoras and \tan^{-1} formulas.

  5. As an object slides down a ramp, it experiences a force of gravity equal to \vec{\bf F}_g = 52.0 N [–90.0º]. Measurements determine that the “contact force” of the ramp pushing on the object (i.e. the normal and friction forces combined) has a magnitude of |\vec{\bf F}_{contact}| = 45.0 N and the magnitude of the net (resultant) force is 18.5 N. Determine the direction of the contact force and of the net force. [Hint: You may find the compass from your geometry set useful!]

    6. Draw a sketch of the vector diagram first. We know that the two forces and the resultant vector form a triangle. We know all three sides of the triangle (52.0, 45.0, and 18.5 N), so we can use the Law of Cosines to find the direction of the contact force:

    c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos\theta

    2ab\cos\theta = a^2 + b^2 - c^2

    \cos\theta = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}

    \theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}\right)

    If \theta is the angle between gravity and the contact force, then we must take c = \rm 18.5\ N since the resultant force is the side opposite to \theta:

    \theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{52.0^2 + 45.0^2 - 18.5^2}{2\cdot 52.0\cdot 45.0}\right) = 20.4^\circ

    Since gravity points along the negative y-axis, the direction of the contact force from the positive x-axis is:

    90^\circ - \theta = (90 - 20.4)^\circ = 69.6^\circ

    Now that we know the magnitude and direction of both forces, we can add them using components and then draw the vector diagram accurately!
