2D Vectors Review

2D Vectors

Vectors are quantities that involve a direction, such as displacement, velocity, acceleration, and force.

  • We can illustrate vectors using a vector diagram, in which each vector is drawn as an arrow illustrating the direction and the magnitude (size) of the vector.
  • A vector described by its magnitude and direction is said to be in polar form:

    \vec{\mathbf F} = \mathrm{8.0\ N\ [30°]}

  • Directions are measured as angles measured counterclockwise from the +x-axis.
  • Negative angles indicate clockwise from the +x-axis.
  • The components of a vector describe “how much” of the vector is in each of the x and y directions.
  • We can use trigonometry to calculate the components of a vector from its magnitude and direction:

    {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_x=\left|\vec{\mathbf{F}}\right|\cos{\theta}

    {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_y=\left|\vec{\mathbf{F}}\right|\sin{\theta}

  • Describing a vector by its components is called the Cartesian form of the vector:

    \vec{\mathbf F} = \mathrm{(6.9, 4.0)\ N}

  • We can also use trigonometry to convert a vector from Cartesian to polar form:

    \left|\vec{\mathbf{F}}\right|=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}

    \tan{\theta}=\frac{{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_y}{{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_x}

  • When solving for \theta using \tan^{-1}, you will get only one of two possible solutions; when the x-component is negative, you will have to add 180° to get the correct answer.
  • Compass directions should be converted to conventional angles...
East0°
North90°
West180°
South270°

Vector Diagrams

We can illustrate addition of two or more 2D vectors by drawing a tip-to-tail vector diagram.

  • Example: A speedboat has a velocity of 20.0 m/s [120°] relative to the water. There is a current of 5.00 m/s [200°]. Determine the resultant velocity.

  • To subtract a vector, just add its negative!

Vector Arithmetic

To add or subtract vectors more precisely than is possible with a diagram, convert all vectors to Cartesian form...

\vec{\mathbf v}_{1x} = |\vec{\mathbf v}_1| \cos\theta_1 = \mathrm{20.0\ m/s\cdot\cos 120° = -10.0\ m/s}

\vec{\mathbf v}_{1y} = |\vec{\mathbf v}_1| \sin\theta_1 = \mathrm{20.0\ m/s\cdot\sin 120° = +17.3\ m/s}

\vec{\mathbf v}_{2x} = |\vec{\mathbf v}_2| \cos\theta_2 = \mathrm{5.00\ m/s\cdot\cos 200° = -4.70\ m/s}

\vec{\mathbf v}_{2y} = |\vec{\mathbf v}_2| \sin\theta_2 = \mathrm{5.00\ m/s\cdot\sin 200° = -1.71\ m/s}

Then perform the calculations separately on the x- and y-components...

\vec{\mathbf v}_x = \vec{\mathbf v}_{1x} + \vec{\mathbf v}_{2x}= \mathrm{(-10.0 + (-4.70))\ m/s = -14.7\ m/s}

\vec{\mathbf v}_y = \vec{\mathbf v}_{1y} + \vec{\mathbf v}_{2y}= \mathrm{(+17.3 + (-1.71))\ m/s = +15.6\ m/s}

  • If the resultant vector needs to be in polar form, just convert it using the Pythagoras and \tan^{-1} formulas.

  1. A cyclist rides 14.2 km due north, then turns due northeast (i.e. N 45.0° E) and rides another 10.8 km. Find the resultant displacement using the graphical method (i.e. by drawing an accurate, labelled “tip-to-tail” vector diagram) and using the component method. Express the answer in both Cartesian and polar form.

    2. Solution

  2. A spacecraft moving near the Moon experiences three forces: the thrust of the rocket engines is \vec{\bf F}_a = 120.0 N [+85.2º], the force of the Moon’s gravity is \vec{\bf F}_{gM} = 85.0 N [–90.0º], and the force of Earth’s gravity is \vec{\bf F}_{gE} = 25.0 N [+235.0º]. Determine the net force (i.e. the resultant vector obtained by adding these three force vectors) both graphically and by the component method.

    3. Solution

  3. As an object slides down a ramp, it experiences a force of gravity equal to \vec{\bf F}_g = 52.0 N [–90.0º]. Measurements determine that the “contact force” of the ramp pushing on the object (i.e. the normal and friction forces combined) has a magnitude of |\vec{\bf F}_{contact}| = 45.0 N and the magnitude of the net (resultant) force is 18.5 N. Determine the direction of the contact force and of the net force. [Hint: You may find the compass from your geometry set useful!]

    4. Draw a sketch of the vector diagram first. We know that the two forces and the resultant vector form a triangle. We know all three sides of the triangle (52.0, 45.0, and 18.5 N), so we can use the Law of Cosines to find the direction of the contact force:

    c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos\theta

    2ab\cos\theta = a^2 + b^2 - c^2

    \cos\theta = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}

    \theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}\right)

    If \theta is the angle between gravity and the contact force, then we must take c = \rm 18.5\ N since the resultant force is the side opposite to \theta:

    \theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{52.0^2 + 45.0^2 - 18.5^2}{2\cdot 52.0\cdot 45.0}\right) = 20.4^\circ

    Since gravity points along the negative y-axis, the direction of the contact force from the positive x-axis is:

    90^\circ - \theta = (90 - 20.4)^\circ = 69.6^\circ

    Now that we know the magnitude and direction of both forces, we can add them using components and then draw the vector diagram accurately!

Review

  1. Define the magnitude of a vector.
    2. The magnitude is the size of the vector without including the direction.
  2. Explain how to describe the direction of a 2D vector.
    3. Directions in 2D are described as angles measured from the positive x-axis. Angles measured counter-clockwise are positive and negative angles are measured clockwise.
  3. Define polar and Cartesian form for 2D vectors and explain how to convert between the two forms.

    4. The polar form describes a 2D vector as a magnitude and direction. For example, \vec{\bf F} = 25.0 N [+60.0°] means a force that is 25.0-newtons strong directed 60.0° counter-clockwise from the direction parallel to the positive x-axis.

    The Cartesian form decribes the vector as separate x- and y -components. For example, \vec{\bf F} = (+12.5, +21.7) N means a force that pulls with 12.5 newtons parallel to the x-axis and simultaneously with 21.7 newtons parallel to the y-axis.
