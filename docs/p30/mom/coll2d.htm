Lesson Notes

Collisions in 2D

Momentum & Impulse Vectors

Momentum and impulse are vector quantities.

  • Momentum will always have the same direction as the velocity:

    {\vec{\bf p}} = m{\vec{\bf v}}

  • Impulse will always have the same direction as the force:

    \Delta{\vec{\bf p}} = {\vec{\bf F}}\Delta t

When calculating impulse as the change in momentum...

\Delta{\vec{\bf p}} = {\vec{\bf p}}_f - {\vec{\bf p}}_i

...or calculating the total momentum to use the Law of Momentum Conservation...

\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}}_f = \Sigma{\vec{\bf p}}_i

...we need to remember that in 2D or 3D situations, vector addition and subtraction is different than adding and subtracting scalar quantities.

Example 1

A 1500 kg car moving at 18.0 m/s [north] and a 1200 kg car moving at 20.0 m/s [west] collide in an intersection. What is the total momentum of the system? Calculate the final velocity of the cars if they stick together after the collision.

First calculate the initial momenta:

{\vec{\bf p}}_{iA} = m_A {\vec{\bf v}}_{iA} = {\rm{1500\ kg\cdot 18.0\ m/s\ [north] = 27000\ N\cdot s\ [north]}}

{\vec{\bf p}}_{iB} = m_B {\vec{\bf v}}_{iB} = {\rm{1200\ kg\cdot 20.0\ m/s\ [west] = 24000\ N\cdot s\ [west]}}

To add the 2D vectors, draw them tip-to-tail starting at the origin.

  • The resultant vector goes from the origin to the tip of the last vector.
  • Because the triangle contains a right angle, we can solve for the missing sides and/or angles using trigonometry.

|\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}}| = \sqrt{27000^2 + 24000^2}\ {\rm{N\cdot s}} = {\rm{36125\ N\cdot s}}

After the collision, we can treat the two masses as a single 2700-kilogram mass because because they stick together:

v_f = \frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}}{m_A+m_B} = \rm \frac{36125\ N\cdot s}{2700\ kg} = 13.4\ m/s

\theta = \tan^{-1}\left({\rm{24000\ N\cdot s\over 27000\ N\cdot s}}\right) = 41.6^\circ

  • Note that this is 41.6° west of north, or 131.6° from the +x-axis.

Example 3

During a billiards game, the 170-gram cue ball moving at 2.50 m/s collides with a stationary ball of mass 150 grams. After the collision, the cue ball moves at an angle of +35.0° to its original direction, while the second ball moves at –55.0°. Find the final speeds of the two billiard balls.

Since only one ball is initially in motion, its momentum is the total momentum of the system:

\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}} = m_A {\vec{\bf v}}_{iA} = {\rm{0.170\ kg\cdot 2.50\ m/s\ [0^\circ] = 0.425\ N\cdot s\ [0^\circ]}}

  • Draw the two final momentum vectors tip-to-tail.
  • The resultant vector must equal the initial momentum, since momentum must be conserved.
  • You can solve for the missing sides or angles using trigonometry when the vector diagram is a triangle.
  • Since the two known angles add up to 90°, the third angle must be 90°.
  • Since it is a right-angle triangle, you can use \sin or \cos to find the missing sides:

    p_A = \rm{0.425\ N\cdot s}\times \cos(35.0^\circ) = \rm{0.348\ N\cdot s}

    p_B = \rm{0.425\ N\cdot s}\times \sin(35.0^\circ) = \rm{0.244\ N\cdot s}

  • Remember to divide the momenta by the masses to get the speeds:

    v_A = \frac{p_A}{m_A} = \rm{\frac{0.348\ N\cdot s}{0.170\ kg}} = \rm{2.05\ m/s}

    v_B = \frac{p_B}{m_B} = \rm{\frac{0.244\ N\cdot s}{0.150\ kg}} = \rm{1.63\ m/s}

  • When the triangle does not have a right angle, you must use the Law of Sines or Law of Cosines.
  • Alternatively, you can write the momentum conservation equation separately for the x- and y-components:

\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}}_x = p_A\cos(35.0^\circ) + p_B\cos(-55.0^\circ)= \rm{0.425\ N\cdot s}

\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}}_y = p_A\sin(35.0^\circ) + p_B\sin(-55.0^\circ)= \rm{0.00\ N\cdot s}

  • This is more difficult to solve because it is a system of two equations with two unknowns.

  1. Two Physics 20 students are riding in the bumper cars during the class field trip to Galaxyland. One student is driving her car with a velocity of 2.00 m/s [0.00º]. The combined mass of the student and bumper car is 100 kg. She crashes her car into a classmate whose car has a mass (including driver) of 125 kg and a velocity of 1.40 m/s [+90.0º].
    1. Determine the total momentum of the system (i.e. the combined momentum of the two cars).

      2. \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{iA} &= m_A\vec{\bf p}_{iA} = \rm 100\ kg\cdot 2.00\ m/s\ [0.00^\circ] \\[8pt] &= \rm {200\ N\cdot s\ [0.00^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(+200, 0.00) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{iB} &= m_B\vec{\bf p}_{iB} = \rm 125\ kg\cdot 1.40\ m/s\ [0.00^\circ] \\[8pt] &= \rm {175\ N\cdot s\ [0.00^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(0.00, +175) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \Sigma\vec{\bf p} &= \vec{\bf p}_{iA} + \vec{\bf p}_{iB} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(+200, 0.00) \ N\cdot s + (0.00, +175) \ N\cdot s} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(+200, +175) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      Vector Diagram

      If we want to express the total momentum in polar form:

      \begin{align} \left|\Sigma\vec{\bf p}\right| &= \sqrt{(\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_x)^2 + (\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_y)^2} \\[8pt] &= \rm \sqrt{200^2 + 175^2}\ N\cdot s \\[8pt] & = \rm {266\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \theta &= \tan^{-1}\frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_y}{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_x} \\[10pt] &= \rm {\tan^{-1}\frac{+175 \ N\cdot s}{+200 \ N\cdot s}} \\[10pt] &= \rm {+41.2^\circ} \end{align}

    2. If the two bumper cars were to stick together after colliding so that they had the same final velocity, what would that velocity be if the total momentum is conserved?

      3. Because the two cars stick together, we can treat them as a single mass:

      m = m_A + m_B = \rm (100 + 125)\ kg = 225\ kg

      \vec{\bf v}_f = \frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}}{m} = \rm {\frac{266\ N\cdot s\ [+41.2^\circ]}{225\ kg}} = \rm {1.18\ m/s\ [+41.2^\circ]}

  2. A 1250 kg car moving at 75.0 km/h [south] collides with a 1000 kg car at 55.0 km/h [west]. The cars become entangled and remain attached after the collision.
    1. Determine the final velocity of the cars.

      2. First calculate the initial momentum of each car, remembering to convert km/h to m/s:

      \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{iA} &= m_A\vec{\bf p}_{iA} = \rm 1250\ kg\cdot 20.8\ m/s\ [-90.0^\circ] \\[8pt] &= \rm {26042\ N\cdot s\ [-90.0^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(0.00, -26042) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{iB} &= m_B\vec{\bf p}_{iB} = \rm 1000\ kg\cdot 15.3\ m/s\ [180.0^\circ] \\[8pt] &= \rm {15278\ N\cdot s\ [180.0^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(-15278, 0.00) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      Vector Diagram

      Now calculate the total momentum and convert to Cartesian form:

      \begin{align} \Sigma\vec{\bf p} &= \vec{\bf p}_{iA} + \vec{\bf p}_{iB} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(0.00, -26042) \ N\cdot s + (-15278, 0.00) \ N\cdot s} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(-15278, -26042) \ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \left|\Sigma\vec{\bf p}\right| &= \sqrt{(\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_x)^2 + (\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_y)^2} \\[8pt] &= \rm \sqrt{15278^2 + 26042^2}\ N\cdot s \\[8pt] & = \rm {30192\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \theta &= \tan^{-1}\frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_y}{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}_x} \\[10pt] &= \rm {\tan^{-1}\frac{-26042 \ N\cdot s}{-15278 \ N\cdot s} + 180^\circ} \\[10pt] &= \rm {+239.6^\circ} \end{align}

      We can treat the cars as a single mass after they become entangled:

      m = m_A + m_B = \rm (1250 + 1000)\ kg = 2250\ kg

      \vec{\bf v}_f = \frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}}{m} = \rm {\frac{30192\ N\cdot s\ [+239.6^\circ]}{2250\ kg}} = \rm {13.4\ m/s\ [+239.6^\circ]}

    2. Calculate the change (if any) in mechanical energy and classify the collision as elastic, inelastic, or superelastic.

      3. Calculate the initial mechanical energy:

      \begin{align} E_i &= \frac{1}{2}m_{iA}{v_{iA}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_{iB}{v_{iB}}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (1250\ kg) (20.8\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (1000\ kg) (15.3\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {3.88\times 10^5\ J} \end{align}

      Calculate the final mechanical energy:

      \begin{align} E_i &= \frac{1}{2}m{v_f}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (2250\ kg) (13.4\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {2.03\times 10^5\ J} \end{align}

      The collision is inelastic because there is a loss of mechanical energy.

  3. At a construction site, workers have decided to use explosives to remove a 2.00 × 103 kg boulder. After the explosion, the boulder has been blown into three pieces: fragment “A” has a mass of 860 kg and moves west at 26.0 m/s and fragment “B” has a mass of 534 kg and moves south at 32.0 m/s. Determine the final velocity of the third fragment, “C”. Include a labelled vector diagram. [Hint: The boulder was not moving before the explosion! What does this tell you about the total momentum of the system?]

    4. Since the boulder is initially at rest, the total momentum of the system is zero.

    Calculate the final momentum of the two pieces for which the velocity is given and convert to Cartesian form:

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{fA} &= m_A\vec{\bf v}_{fA} \\[8pt] &= \rm {860\ kg\cdot 26.0\ m/s\ [180^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {22360\ N\cdot s\ [180^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(-22360, 0.00)\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{fB} &= m_B\vec{\bf v}_{fB} \\[8pt] &= \rm {534\ kg\cdot 32.0\ m/s\ [-90.0^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {17088\ N\cdot s\ [-90.0^\circ]} \\[8pt] &= \rm {(0.00, -17088)\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

    The third piece must have the opposite momentum to the first two so that the total is zero:

    \vec{\bf p}_{fC} = \rm (+22360, +17088)\ N\cdot s

    |\vec{\bf p}_{fC}| = \rm \sqrt{22360^2 + 17088^2}\ N\cdot s = 28142\ N\cdot s

    \theta = \rm \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{+17088\ N\cdot s}{+22360\ N\cdot s}\right) = 37.4^\circ

    v_{fC} = \frac{p_{fC}}{m_C} = \rm \frac{28142\ N\cdot s}{606\ kg} = 46.4\ m/s

  5. Physicists use a particle accelerator to produce a 2D collision between a proton initially at rest and a particle with three times the mass of a proton moving at 6.00 × 107 m/s [0.00°]. As a result of the collision, the heavier particle’s velocity changes to 1.32 × 107 m/s [+32.0°]. Calculate the velocity of the proton after the collision. [Hint: Before attempting to calculate the proton’s velocity, determine its momentum.]

    6. The initial momentum is just the heavy particle since the proton is at rest:

    \begin{align} \Sigma\vec{\bf p} &= m_H\vec{\bf v}_{iH} \\[6pt] &= \rm {5.01\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot 6.00\times 10^7\ m/s\ [0.00^\circ]} \\[6pt] &= \rm {3.006\times 10^{-19}\ N\cdot s\ [0.00^\circ]} \\[6pt] &= \rm {(+3.0060, 0.00) \times 10^{-19}\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

    After the collision, the heavier particle momentum is:

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{fH} &= m_H\vec{\bf v}_{fH} \\[6pt] &= \rm {5.01\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot 1.32\times 10^7\ m/s\ [+32.0^\circ]} \\[6pt] &= \rm {6.6132\times 10^{-20}\ N\cdot s\ [+32.0^\circ]} \\[6pt] &= \rm {(+5.6083, +3.5045) \times 10^{-20}\ N\cdot s} \end{align}

    To find the proton’s momentum, subtract the heavier particle’s from the total:

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf p}_{fP} &= \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - \vec{\bf p}_{fH} \\[6pt] &= \rm {(+30.060 - 5.6083, 0.00 - 3.5045)\times 10^{-20}\ }N\cdot s \\[6pt] &= \rm {(+24.4517, -3.5045) \times 10^{-20}\ N\cdot s} \\[6pt] &= \rm {2.47\times 10^{-19}\ N\cdot s\ [-8.20^\circ]} \end{align}

    Vector Diagram

    To get the velocity, divide the proton’s momentum by its mass:

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf v}_{fP} &= \frac{\vec{\bf p}_{fP}}{m_P} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{2.47\times 10^{-19}\ N\cdot s\ [-8.20^\circ]}{1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{1.48\times 10^8\ m/s\ [-8.20^\circ]} \end{align}
