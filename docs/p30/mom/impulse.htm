Lesson Notes

Impulse

Impulse

A change in momentum is called impulse:

\Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}} = {\vec{\mathbf p}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf p}}_i

  • Impulse is the vector analog of work (change in kinetic energy):

    \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki}

  • As with work, impulse is caused by unbalanced forces.

Newton’s 2nd Law

Newton’s 2nd Law can be rearranged to show the relationship between force and impulse:

{\vec{\mathbf F}} = m{\vec{\mathbf a}} = m {\Delta\vec{\mathbf v}\over \Delta t}

{\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t = m \Delta\vec{\mathbf v}

{\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t = \Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}}

  • We can see from these equations that the unit for impulse and momentum is \color{red}{\mathrm{N\cdot s}} or \color{red}{\mathrm{kg\cdot m/s}}.
  • This equation is analogous to the work-energy theorem:

    \Delta E_k = {\vec{\mathbf F}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}

  • For forces that are not constant, impulse is the area under a graph of \vec{\mathbf F} versus t.
  • Work is the area under a graph of \vec{\mathbf F} versus \vec{\mathbf d}.

Newton’s 3rd Law

Newton’s 3rd Law states that when two objects interact, they will exert equal and opposite forces on each other.

\Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}} = {\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t

  • This means that the impulse (change in momentum) experienced by the objects will also be equal and opposite.
  • In other words, the momentum “gained” by one object will equal the momentum “lost” by the other.
  • Impulse represents a transfer of momentum between two objects.

Impulse is a Vector

Since momentum, velocity and force are all vectors, impulse is also a vector.

  • For 2D motion, use components (or trigonometry) to add or subtract vectors.
  • Draw a vector diagram to accompany your calculations.

  5. Use the concepts of momentum and impulse to explain why an air bag reduces the likelihood of severe injury during an automobile collision.

    6. Since the passenger eventually comes to rest, his or her final momentum is zero and the impulse is:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_f - \vec{\bf p}_i = 0 - \vec{\bf p}_i = -\vec{\bf p}_i

    Solve for the force:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf F}\Delta t

    \vec{\bf F} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf p}}{\Delta t} = \frac{-\vec{\bf p}_i}{\Delta t}

    The airbag slows the passenger over a longer time (compared to the sudden impact of the windshield) and therefore reduces the force, making severe injury less likely.

Review

  1. Define impulse and explain how it is related to momentum and force.
    2. Impulse is the change in momentum. A force is what causes the object’s impulse to change:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_f - \vec{\bf p}_i = \vec{\bf F} \Delta t

  2. A student slowly lifts a weight at constant speed. How is it possible that the student is doing work (\vec{\bf F}\cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}) and producing impulse (\vec{\bf F} \Delta t) but the object’s kinetic energy and momentum are not changing?
    3. Because the velocity is constant, the net force must be zero according to Newton’s Laws: i.e. the applied force is balanced by gravity. Although the work and impulse equations give non-zero results when calculated using the applied force, an equal but opposite answer would result when calculating the work and impulse due to gravity. Thus the net work and impulse on the object is zero.
  3. Are work and impulse essentially the same as force? Consider this scenario: a weightlifter holds a heavy mass above his or her head. Does the weightlifter exert a lot of force? Is he or she doing a lot of work or producing a lot of impulse?
    4. Impulse and work are caused by forces. However,exerting a force does not necessarily result in work or impulse since there may be other forces present: it is the net force that determines the work and impulse done on the object.
    1. A 3500 kg rail car moving at 15.0 km/h collides with another rail car and slows to 8.00 km/h as a result. How much impulse did the first rail car experience?

      2. Calculate the initial and final momenta, remember to change km/h to the standard SI unit, m/s:

      \vec{\bf p}_i = m\vec{\bf v}_i = \rm 3500\ kg\cdot (+4.17\ m/s) = +14583\ kg\cdot m/s

      \vec{\bf p}_f = m\vec{\bf v}_f = \rm 3500\ kg\cdot (+2.22\ m/s) = +7778\ kg\cdot m/s

      The impulse is the change in momentum:

      \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_f - \vec{\bf p}_i = \rm +7778\ kg\cdot m/s - (+14583\ kg\cdot m/s) = -6805\ kg\cdot m/s

      Rounded to 3 significant digits, the impulse is \rm -6.81\times 10^3\ kg\cdot m/s.

    2. If the collision lasted for 1.25 seconds, what was the average force that the two rail cars exerted on each other?

      3. For the first rail car:

      \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf F}\Delta t

      \vec{\bf F} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf p}}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{-6805\ kg\cdot m/s}{1.25\ s} = -5.44\times 10^3\ N

      The second rail car experiences the same magnitude of force but in the opposite direction (\rm +5.44\times 10^3\ N) according to Newton’s 3rd Law.
