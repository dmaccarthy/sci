Notes

Momentum (\vec{\bf p}) is the vector analog of kinetic energy:

\vec{\bf p}=m\vec{\bf v}

  • Like E_k, momentum measures “quantity of motion”, but while energy is a scalar, momentum includes a direction.
  • Like energy, momentum is a conserved quantity.
  • Unlike energy, momentum does not come in different forms!
  • Momentum is measured in \rm{kg\cdot m/s} or \rm{N\cdot s}.

The change in an object’s momentum is called impulse.

  • According to Newton’s 2nd Law, forces cause an object to experience a change in momentum:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = m\Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf F} \Delta t

  • If the force is not constant, you can calculate impulse as the area under a graph of \vec{\bf F} vs. t.
  • According to Newton’s 3rd Law, the impulse that two interacting objects exert on each other will be equal and opposite.
  • The total momentum of a system will be constant, as long as there are no unbalanced, external forces.

Momentum conservation is typically used to solve collisions.

  • Often, a system loses mechanical energy in the form of sound or thermal energy as a result of a collision.
  • Collisions in which there is a loss of mechanical energy are classified as inelastic.
  • Collisions in which the mechanical energy remains constant are classified as elastic.
  • When two colliding objects stick together, the collision will be completely inelastic (maximum loss of mechanical energy).

Collisions involving motion in both the x- and y-directions are called 2D collsions.

  • Draw a (tip-to-tail) vector diagram to add momentums; do not just add the magnitudes!
  • The total initial momentum (resultant vector) must be the same as the total final momentum.
  • Sometimes, you can use trigonometry to find missing angles or magnitudes on your vector diagram.
  • The Pythagoras and “SOH CAH TOA” equations only apply to triangles containing a right angle.
  • For triangles that may not contain a right angle, you can use the Law of Sines or Law of Cosines.
  • Alternatively, you can resolve each momentum vector into x- and y-components, and then solve the x- and y-equations independently; the total x-momentum and the total y-momentum must both be conserved!
