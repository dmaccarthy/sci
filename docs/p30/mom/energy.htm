  1. A 70.0 kg student rides a 20.0 kg bike with a speed of 12.0 m/s.
    1. How much combined kinetic energy does the bike/rider system have?

      2. Use the kinetic energy with the combined mass of the students and bike...

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(90.0\ kg)(12.0\ m/s)^2 = 6.48\times 10^3\ J

    2. The cyclist approaches a hill. How high up the hill could the bicycle reach if no mechanical energy is added or removed from the system?

      3. The kinetic energy that the student starts with becomes gravitational potential energy as they go up the hill...

      E_g = mgh

      h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm \frac{6.48\times 10^3\ J}{90.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 7.34\ m

  2. A 2.20 kg toy rocket has 600 J of kinetic energy and 800 J of gravitational potential energy. Determine the rocket’s (i) mechanical energy, (ii) height, and (iii) speed.

    3. The mechanical energy is the total of the kinetic plus potential energy...

    \Sigma E = E_k+E_g = \rm 600\ J + 800\ J = 1400\ J = 1.40\times 10^3\ J

    Use the gravitational potential energy to find the height; the algebra is the same as #1b...

    h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm \frac{800\ J}{2.20\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 37.1\ m

    Use the kinetic energy to find the speed:

    E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

    \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

    v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 600\ J}{2.20\ kg}} = 23.4\ m/s

  3. A quarterback releases a 675 g football from an initial height of 2.00 m with a speed of 18.5 m/s.
    1. Calculate the football’s: (i) kinetic energy, (ii) potential energy, and (iii) mechanical energy.

      2. Convert grams to kg before calculating the kinetic and gravitational potential energies...

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(0.675\ kg)(18.5\ m/s)^2 = 116\ J

      E_g = mgh = \rm 0.675\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 2.00\ m = 13.2\ J

      The mechanical energy is the kinetic and potantial energy added together...

      \Sigma E = E_k+E_g = \rm 116\ J + 13.2\ J = 129\ J

      Bar Chart

    2. Neglecting the effects of air resistance, what will the football’s speed be when it lands on the ground?

      3. When the ball reaches the ground, the gravitational potential energy is zero, so all of the mechanical energy is now kinetic. Re-arrange the kinetic energy equation to isolate the speed...

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

      \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 129\ J}{0.675\ kg}} = 19.5\ m/s

  4. A curling rock is moving with 40.0 joules of kinetic energy. The rock experiences a friction of force of 1.20 newtons in the direction opposite to the motion.
    1. How much work does the friction force do while the curling rock moves 5.00 metres?

      2. W_f=\vec{\bf F}_f\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm -1.20\ N\cdot 5.00\ m = -6.00\ J

    2. What will the rock’s kinetic energy be at the end of the 5.00-metre motion?

      3. \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = W

      E_{kf} = W + E_{ki} = \rm -6.00\ J + 40.0\ J = 34.0\ J

Lesson Notes

Work & Energy Review

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical forms of energy (measured in joules) include kinetic, gravitational potential, elastic potential and rotational energy.

  • If a system contains more than one mechanical form of energy, “mechanical energy” means the total of these.
  • Other (non-mechanical) forms of energy include thermal energy, sound, chemical, nuclear, electrical and electromagnetic energy.

Kinetic Energy

E_k = {1\over 2}mv^2

  • m is the mass measured in kilograms.
  • v is the speed measured in m/s.

Gravitational Potential Energy

E_g = mgh

  • h is the height (relative to the origin) measured in metres.
  • g is the strength of gravity in m/s2.
  • Near the surface of Earth, g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2}.

Elastic Potential Energy

E_{elas} = {1\over 2}k(\Delta L)^2

  • \Delta L is the distance in metres by which the object is stretch or compressed.
  • k is the “elastic constant” measured in N/m.

Rotational Energy

The equation for rotational energy depends on the shape (e.g. sphere or cylinder) and mass distribution (e.g. solid or hollow)

Energy Conservation

Law of Energy Conservation: Energy can be transformed from one form to another, but the total energy always remains constant.

\Sigma E_i = \Sigma E_f

  • Loss of energy must always be balanced by a gain of a different form of energy.
  • On an energy bar chart, the total of all bars will always be constant.

  • Any energy that is going to be added to a system must be included with the initial energy.
  • Any energy that is removed from a system must be included with the final energy.

Energy Transformations

Mechanical energy transformations are caused by forces (measured in newtons).

  • The amount of energy that a force transforms is called the work.

    W = \vec{\mathbf F}_\parallel \cdot \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}

  • When more than one force does work on an object, the change in kinetic energy is equal to the total work done by all forces.

    \Delta E_k = \Sigma W

  • Power (measured in watts) is the rate at which a force does work.

    P = {\Delta E\over \Delta t}

  • Efficiency is the ratio of the useful energy output compared to the energy input.

    \epsilon = {\Delta E_{useful}\over |\Delta E_{input}|}

Practice

  1. How much elastic potential energy is available in a spring with k = 85.0 N/m that has been compressed 7.00 cm?

    2. Use the elastic potential energy equation. Remember to change cm to metres...

    E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(85.0\ N/m)(0.0700\ m)^2 = 0.208\ J

    1. Students release a 200 g ball from the top of a 25.0 m tall building. The ball is dropped from rest. Neglecting air resistance, determine the speed with which the ball will strike the ground.

      2. The mechanical energy is gravitational potential initially. Remember to change grams to kg...

      E_g = mgh = \rm 0.200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 25.0\ m = 49.1\ J

      When the ball reaches the ground, the gravitational potential energy is zero, so all of the mechanical energy has become kinetic. Algebra is the same as #3b of the “Remember?” section...

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 49.1\ J}{0.200\ kg}} = 22.1\ m/s

    2. How fast will the ball be traveling when it is halfway to the ground?

      3. Calculate the gravitational potential energy when the height is half the initial height...

      E_g = mgh = \rm 0.200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 12.5\ m = 24.5\ J

      Since total energy is conseved, kinetic energy is the difference between the total and the potential energy...

      E_k = \Sigma E - E_g = \rm 49.1\ J - 24.5\ J = 24.5\ J

      Re-do the calculation from (a) using this new kinetic energy...

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 24.5\ J}{0.200\ kg}} = 15.7\ m/s

    3. Would your answers for (a) and (b) change if the students used a ball with a different mass? Explain.

      4. No! Since gravitational potential energy is transformed (partly) into kinetic energy, we can write energy conservation as...

      mgh_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mgh_f

      Since all three terms contain a factor m, the mass cancels!

      gh_i = \frac{1}{2}v_f^2 + gh_f

      Isolating v_f gives...

      gh_i - gh_f = \frac{1}{2}v_f^2

      2g(h_i - h_f) = v_f^2

      v_f = \sqrt{2g(h_i - h_f)}

      We can get the answers from (a) and (b) by substituting h_i = 25.0 m and h_f = 0.00 m (a) or 12.5 m (b).

    4. Would your answers change if the effects of air resistance were included? Explain.
      5. Yes! Air resistance transforms some of the mechanical energy into waste (sound and/or thermal energy). This means that the final kinetic energy would be less than calculated above and the ball would have a slower speed.
    1. A 12.5 kg mass is slid up a frictionless 10.0° incline. The mass is given an initial speed of 2.50 m/s at the bottom of the incline and then released. What height does the mass reach before gravity brings it to a stop?

      2. At the bottom of the incline, there is only kinetic energy...

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (12.5\ kg) (2.50\ m/s)^2 = 39.1\ J

      At the highest position up the ramp, the energy has been transformed into only gravitational potential...

      E_p = mgh

      h = \frac{E_p}{mg} = \rm \frac{39.1\ J}{12.5\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.319\ m

    2. Repeat the question assuming a constant friction force of 5.00 newtons.

      3. Friction does work, causing some of the energy to be removed from the system...

      E_{pf} = E_i + W_f

      E_{pf} - W_f = E_i

      mgh_f - \vec{\bf F_f}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d} = E_i

      Because the height (opposite side) and displacement (hypotenuse) are related by trigonometry, we can sbstitute...

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{h_f}{\sin 10.0^\circ}

      ...to get...

      mgh_f - \vec{\bf F_f}\cdot \frac{h_f}{\sin 10.0^\circ} = E_i

      h_f \left(mg - \frac{\vec{\bf F_f}}{\sin 10.0^\circ}\right) = E_i

      h_f = \frac{E_i}{mg - \frac{\vec{\bf F_f}}{\sin 10.0^\circ}}

      h_f = \rm \frac{39.1\ J}{12.5\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 - \frac{-5.00\ N}{\sin 10.0^\circ}} = 0.258\ m

      Note that the force of friction is negative because it acts in the opposite direction to the displacement. If you use a positive friction force the final height would be greater than it was with no friction, which would violate energy conservation.

      We can check our answer by calculating...

      E_{pf} = mgh_f = \rm 12.5\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.258\ m = 31.6\ J

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{h_f}{\sin 10.0^\circ} = \rm \frac{0.258\ m}{\sin 10.0^\circ} = 1.49\ m

      W_f = \vec{\bf F_f}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm -5.00\ N\cdot 1.49\ m = -7.43\ J

      We can verify that the work done by friction is equal to change in energy. Note that there may be a small error if you use the answers rounded to three significant digits.

      \Delta E = E_f - E_i = \rm 31.63\ J - 39.06\ J = -7.43\ J

    1. One student claimed that since a standard rope doesn’t stretch (much), its “spring constant” (k) is equal to (practically) zero. Another student disagreed, saying that if the rope doesn’t stretch, its spring constant is (practically) infinite! Which student is correct? Justify your answer.

      2. If we suppose that the rope stretches slightly, then the elastic potential energy is...

      E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2

      If we isolate k, we get...

      k = \frac{2E_p}{x^2}

      Since x will be “small” no matter how much work is done in stretching the rope (at least up to the point where the rope breaks!), the value of k must be very big; the closer x gets to zero, the closer k gets to infinity!

    2. Why is an elastic cord used for “bungee jumping” rather than a regular rope? Justify your answer by referring to the work-energy theorem.

      3. The work-energy theorem energy states that...

      \Delta E_k = \vec{\bf F}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}

      This means the force is...

      \vec{\bf F} = \frac{\Delta E_k}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

      Less force is exerted when the work is done over a larger displacement. Since a standard rope stretches through a very small displacement, the force would be very large and would likely cause injuries. A bungee extends the work over a much larger displacement, resulting in a much smaller force.

Review

  1. Define: energy, mechanical energy, work.
    2. Energy is the ability to “do work” or “create motion”. Mechanical energy refers to forms of energy that are related to an objects motion (kinetic, rotational, gravitational potential, elastic potential). Work is the amount of kinetic energy transformed due to a force acting on an object.
  2. State the formulas for kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy, and elastic potential energy.

    3. E_k = {1\over 2}mv^2

    E_g = mgh

    E_{elas} = {1\over 2}k(\Delta L)^2

  3. Explain the Law of Energy Conservation.
    • Energy cannot be created or destroyed, but it can be transformed from one form to another and transferred from one place/object to another.
    • A gain in one type of energy will always be balanced by a loss of an equal amount of another type of energy.
    • The total amount of all forms of energy for a system and its surroundings will always remain constant.
  4. In the pulley system illustrated below, m_1 = 12.0 kg, m_2 = 8.00 kg, and h = 1.50 m. Assuming the system is released from rest and there is no friction or air resistance, determine the speed of the system just before m_1 contacts the ground.

    5. The initial energy of the system is the gravitational potential energy of m_1...

    E_i = m_1gh = \rm{12.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.50\ m = 177\ J}

    When m_1 reaches the ground, m_2 now has gravitational potential energy...

    E_{g2} = m_2gh = \rm{8.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.50\ m = 118\ J}

    The difference between the initial and final potential energies has been transformed into kinetic energy...

    E_k = E_i - E_{g2} = \rm{177\ J - 117\ J = 58.9\ J}

    This is the combined kinetic energy of both masses!

    E_k = \frac{1}{2} m_1v^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2v^2

    Since the masses are attached, they have the same speed, so you can factor out the v^2...

    E_k = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2)v^2

    v^2 = \frac{2E_k}{m_1 + m_2}

    v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m_1 + m_2}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 58.9\ J}{12.0\ kg + 8.00\ kg}} = 2.43\ m/s}

    Now we can calculate the kinetic energies of the masses separately...

    E_{k1} = \frac{1}{2} m_1v^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (12.0\ kg)(2.43\ m/s)^2 = 35.3\ J}

    E_{k2} = \frac{1}{2} m_2v^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (8.00\ kg)(2.43\ m/s)^2 = 23.5\ J}
