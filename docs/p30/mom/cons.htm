Formative

    1. A 3500 kg rail car moving at 15.0 km/h collides with another rail car and slows to 8.00 km/h as a result. How much impulse did the first rail car experience?

      2. Calculate the initial and final momenta, remember to change km/h to the standard SI unit, m/s:

      \vec{\bf p}_i = m\vec{\bf v}_i = \rm 3500\ kg\cdot (+4.17\ m/s) = +14583\ kg\cdot m/s

      \vec{\bf p}_f = m\vec{\bf v}_f = \rm 3500\ kg\cdot (+2.22\ m/s) = +7778\ kg\cdot m/s

      The impulse is the change in momentum:

      \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_f - \vec{\bf p}_i = \rm +7778\ kg\cdot m/s - (+14583\ kg\cdot m/s) = -6805\ kg\cdot m/s

      Rounded to 3 significant digits, the impulse is \rm -6.81\times 10^3\ kg\cdot m/s.

    2. If the collision lasted for 1.25 seconds, what was the average force that the two rail cars exerted on each other?

      3. For the first rail car:

      \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf F}\Delta t

      \vec{\bf F} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf p}}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{-6805\ kg\cdot m/s}{1.25\ s} = -5.44\times 10^3\ N

      The second rail car experiences the same magnitude of force but in the opposite direction (\rm +5.44\times 10^3\ N) according to Newton’s 3rd Law.

    3. If the second rail car has a mass of 2000 kg and was initially at rest, what would its velocity be immediately after the collision?

      4. Because it was initially at rest, its final momentum would equal the impulse (momentum gained) which is the opposite of the impulse experienced by the first car:

      \vec{\bf p}_f = \rm +6805\ kg\cdot m/s

      \vec{\bf v}_f = \frac{\vec{\bf p}_f}{m} = \rm \frac{+6805\ kg\cdot m/s}{2000\ kg} = +3.470\ m/s

  2. Use the concepts of momentum and impulse to explain why an air bag reduces the likelihood of severe injury during an automobile collision.

    3. Since the passenger eventually comes to rest, his or her final momentum is zero and the impulse is:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_f - \vec{\bf p}_i = 0 - \vec{\bf p}_i = -\vec{\bf p}_i

    Solve for the force:

    \Delta\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf F}\Delta t

    \vec{\bf F} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf p}}{\Delta t} = \frac{-\vec{\bf p}_i}{\Delta t}

    The airbag slows the passenger over a longer time (compared to the sudden impact of the windshield) and therefore reduces the force, making severe injury less likely.

Lesson Notes

Momentum Conservation

Momentum Conservation

We saw in the previous lesson that impulse is a transfer of momentum.

  • Due to Newton’s 3rd Law, the change in one object’s momentum will always be balanced by an equal and opposite change to the other object.
  • The total momentum will always be conserved!

    \Sigma{\vec{\bf p}_f} = \Sigma{\vec{\bf p}_i}

Problem Solving

\Sigma{\vec{\bf p}_f} = \Sigma{\vec{\bf p}_i}

Momentum conservation is most useful for isolated systems and systems where external forces are balanced.

  • Frictionless collisions, rocket propulsion, and explosions are some of the most common uses for momentum conservation.
  • Forces that objects within the system exert on each other are internal forces. Internal forces do not change the total momentum of the system.
  • Forces that an object within the system experiences due to the environment (e.g. Earth) are external forces.
  • Unbalanced external forces will transfer momentum into or out of the system (e.g. to or from the Earth!)
  • Always draw a free body diagram when there is more than one force involved.

Example 1

A bumper car of mass 200 kg (including the rider) moves at +3.00 m/s when it collides head-on with a second bumper car of mass 150 kg and velocity −2.50 m/s. After the collision the first car has slowed to +0.500 m/s.

  • Calculate the final velocity of the second bumper car.

Use {\vec {\bf p}} = m{\vec {\bf v}} to calculate all initial and final momenta for which both the mass and velocity are known...

{\vec{\bf p}_{Ai}} = \rm{200\ kg\ (+3.00\ m/s) = +600\ N\cdot s}

{\vec{\bf p}_{Bi}} = \rm{150\ kg\ (-2.50\ m/s) = -375\ N\cdot s}

{\vec{\bf p}_{Af}} = \rm{200\ kg\ (+0.500\ m/s) = +100\ N\cdot s}

N·s Initial Final
\vec{\bf p}_A +600 +100
\vec{\bf p}_B –375 +125
\Sigma\vec{\bf p} +225

Calculate the total momentum of the bumper cars using the initial momenta...

\Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \rm{(+600 + (-375))\ N\cdot s = +225\ N\cdot s}

Use the fact that total momentum is conserved to find the remaining final momentum...

\Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_{Bf} + \vec{\bf p}_{Af}

{\vec{\bf p}_{Bf}} = \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - {\vec{\bf p}_{Af}} = \rm{(+225 - 100)\ N\cdot s = +125\ N\cdot s}

Use the momentum to find the final velocity of “B”:

{\vec {\bf v}_{Bf}} = {{\vec {\bf p}_{Bf}}\over m_B} = \rm{+125\ N\cdot s\over 150\ kg} = +0.833\ m/s

Summary

  1. Explain the Law of Momentum Conservation and how it relates to Newton’s Laws of Motion.

Practice

    1. As a book slides across the floor, it loses both momentum and kinetic energy. What causes these losses? Where does the lost momentum and the lost kinetic energy go?

      2. The friction force causes the changes in energy and momentum. The book’s kinetic energy is transformed into sound and thermal energy, while the momentum is transferred to the Earth.

    2. As a ball falls, it gains kinetic energy and loses momentum (i.e. it gains momentum in the negative direction). What causes these changes? Where does the lost momentum go? Where did the kinetic energy come from?

      3. The gravity force causes the changes in energy and momentum. The ball gains kinetic energy as it loses gravitational potential energy. The ball’s change in momentum is balanced by an opposite impulse experienced by the Earth.

  2. A 1160 kg car moving at 60.0 km/h collides with a 1480 kg car at rest. If the collision brings the first car to a stop, with what velocity will the second car be pushed forward? What assumptions are you making in solving this problem?

    3. We are assuming that the system’s momentum remains constant, which will only be true if friction and air resistance are absent, and the collision occurs on flat ground so the gravity and normal forces balance.

    Calculate the momentum of the system before the collision. This is just the momentum of car “A” since car “B” is at rest. Remember to change km/h to m/s:

    \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = m_A\vec{\bf v}_{iA} = \rm 1160\ kg\cdot (+16.7\ m/s) = +19333\ kg\cdot m/s

    After the collision, car “A” is at rest, so all of the momentum is transferred to car “B”.

    \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = m_B\vec{\bf v}_{fB}

    \vec{\bf v}_{fB} = \frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}}{m_B} = \rm \frac{+19333\ kg\cdot m/s}{1480\ kg} = +13.1\ m/s = +47.0\ km/h

  3. An asteroid with a mass of 8.92 × 108 kg moving at 8.50 km/s collides head-on with a second asteroid of mass 3.15 × 108 kg with a speed of 9.26 km/s in the opposite direction.
    1. If the two asteroids become stuck together in the collision, what will their final velocity be? Remember that velocity is a vector so you must include a direction!

      2. Calculate the total momentum of the system:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = m_A\vec{\bf v}_{iA} + m_B\vec{\bf v}_{iB}

      = \rm 8.92\times 10^8\ kg\cdot (+8500\ m/s) + 3.15\times 10^8\ kg\cdot (-9260\ m/s)

      = \rm +4.67\times 10^{12}\ kg\cdot m/s

      After the collision, the asteroids stick together and behave as a single mass:

      m = m_A + m_B = \rm (8.92\times 10^8 + 3.15\times 10^8)\ kg = 1.207\times 10^9\ kg

      Since momentum is conserved, the velocity is:

      \vec{\bf v}_f = \frac{\Sigma\vec{\bf p}}{m} = \rm \frac{+4.67\times 10^{12}\ kg\cdot m/s}{1.207\times 10^9\ kg}

      \vec{\bf v}_f = \rm +3865\ m/s = +3.87\ km/s

    2. What impulse did the first asteroid experience? What impulse did the second asteroid experience?

      3. Calculate the impulse (change in momentum) of the first asteroid:

      \Delta\vec{\bf p}_A = m_A\Delta\vec{\bf v}_A

      \Delta\vec{\bf p}_A = \rm 8.92\times 10^8\ kg\cdot (+3865 - (+8500))\ m/s

      \Delta\vec{\bf p}_A = \rm -4.13\times 10^{12}\ kg\cdot m/s

      According to Newtons 3rd Law, the impulse experienced by the second asteroid must be \rm +4.13\times 10^{12}\ kg\cdot m/s.

    3. If the collision lasted 0.250 s, what was the average force of the impact?

      4. For the first asteroid:

      \vec{\bf F}_A = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf p}_A}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{-4.13\times 10^{12}\ kg\cdot m/s}{0.250\ s} = -1.65\times 10^{13}\ N

      The average force experienced by the second asteroid is \rm +1.65\times 10^{13}\ N.

    4. Calculate the percentage change in the mechanical energy of the two-asteroid system.

      5. The system’s energy before the collision is:

      \Sigma E_i = \frac{1}{2} m_A{v_{iA}}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_B{v_{iB}}^2

      = \rm \frac{1}{2} (8.92\times 10^8\ kg) (8500\ m/s)^2 + (3.15\times 10^8\ kg) (9260\ m/s)^2

      = \rm 4.57\times 10^{16}\ J

      The system’s energy after the collision is:

      \Sigma E_f = \frac{1}{2} (m_A + m_B){v_f}^2

      \rm = \frac{1}{2} (8.92\times 10^8 + 3.15\times 10^8)\ kg\cdot (3865\ m/s)^2

      \rm = 9.02\times 10^{15}\ J

      The percent change is:

      \frac{\Sigma E_f - \Sigma E_i}{\Sigma E_i} = \rm \frac{9.02\times 10^{15}\ J - 4.57\times 10^{16}\ J}{4.57\times 10^{16}\ J} = -0.803 = -80.3\%

  4. A 560 kg roller coaster starts at rest from a height of 10.0 m above the ground and then accelerates down a hill to a final height of 2.50 m above the ground. Assume there is no friction or air resistance.
    1. As the roller coaster moves, is its mechanical energy conserved? Is its momentum conserved? Explain.

      2. The unbalanced force causing the acceleration is the sum of the gravity and normal forces. Since these are external forces that act between the system (roller coaster) and its environment (Earth), there is a transfer of momentum between the system and the Earth; thus the momentum of the system is not conserved. Mechanical energy is conserved because gravity transforms energy between kinetic and potential forms (without affecting the total mechanical energy) while the normal force does not transform any energy since it acts in a direction perpendicular to the motion.

    2. Determine the final speed of the roller coaster. Use your answer to (a) to decide whether to use energy conservation or momentum conservation.

      3. Calculate the initial energy of the system:

      \Sigma E_i = E_{gi} = mgh_i = \rm 560\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 10.0\ m = 54936\ J

      Calculate the final gravitational potential energy of the system:

      E_{gf} = mgh_f = \rm 560\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 2.50\ m = 13734\ J

      Use energy conservation to find the final kinetic energy:

      \Sigma E_f = E_{gf} + E_{kf}

      E_{kf} = \Sigma E_f - E_{gf} = \rm (54936 - 13734)\ J = 41202\ J

      No use the kinetic energy to calculate the speed. The algebra is the same as for previous questions:

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 41202\ J}{560\ kg}} = 12.1\ m/s

  5. During a roller derby event two teammates, Randy and Sandy, are initially moving together with a velocity of +7.50 m/s. Randy, who has a mass of 60.0 kg, gives 48.0-kilogram Sandy a push in the positive direction causing Sandy to speed up. According to Newton’s 3rd Law, Randy will experience a negative force and will slow down. If Randy’s final velocity is +3.80 m/s, what will Sandy’s final velocity be?

    6. Calculate the initial momentum of the system:

    \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = (m_R+m_S)\vec{\bf v}_i = \rm (60.0 + 48.0)\ kg\cdot (+7.50\ m/s) = +810\ kg\cdot m/s

    Randy’s final momentum is:

    \vec{\bf p}_{fR} = m_R\vec{\bf v}_{fR} = \rm 60.0\ kg\cdot (+3.80\ m/s) = +228\ kg\cdot m/s

    Because total momentum is conserved, Sandy’s final momentum is:

    \vec{\bf p}_{fS} = \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - \vec{\bf p}_{fR} = \rm (+810 - (+228))\ kg\cdot m/s = +582\ kg\cdot m/s

    We can use this momentum to calculate Sandy’s velocity:

    \vec{\bf v}_{fS} = \frac{\vec{\bf p}_{fS}}{m_S} = \rm {\frac{+582\ kg\cdot m/s}{48.0\ kg}} = \rm {+12.1\ m/s}
