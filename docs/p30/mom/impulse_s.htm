Physics 30

Impulse

A change in momentum is called impulse:

\Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}} = {\vec{\mathbf p}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf p}}_i

  • Impulse is the vector analog of work (change in kinetic energy):

    \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki}

  • As with work, impulse is caused by unbalanced forces.

Newton’s 2nd Law

Newton’s 2nd Law can be rearranged to show the relationship between force and impulse:

{\vec{\mathbf F}} = m{\vec{\mathbf a}} = m {\Delta\vec{\mathbf v}\over \Delta t}

{\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t = m \Delta\vec{\mathbf v}

{\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t = \Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}}

  • We can see from these equations that the unit for impulse and momentum is \color{red}{\mathrm{N\cdot s}} or \color{red}{\mathrm{kg\cdot m/s}}.
  • This equation is analogous to the work-energy theorem:

    \Delta E_k = {\vec{\mathbf F}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}

  • For forces that are not constant, impulse is the area under a graph of \vec{\mathbf F} versus t.
  • Work is the area under a graph of \vec{\mathbf F} versus \vec{\mathbf d}.

Newton’s 3rd Law

Newton’s 3rd Law states that when two objects interact, they will exert equal and opposite forces on each other.

\Delta{\vec{\mathbf p}} = {\vec{\mathbf F}}\Delta t

  • This means that the impulse (change in momentum) experienced by the objects will also be equal and opposite.
  • In other words, the momentum “gained” by one object will equal the momentum “lost” by the other.
  • Impulse represents a transfer of momentum between two objects.

Impulse is a Vector

Since momentum, velocity and force are all vectors, impulse is also a vector.

  • For 2D motion, use components (or trigonometry) to add or subtract vectors.
  • Draw a vector diagram to accompany your calculations.
