Momentum

An object’s momentum is defined by the equation:

{\vec {\bf p}} = m{\vec {\bf v}}

  • {\vec{\bf p}} is the momentum.
  • m is the mass in kilograms.
  • {\vec{\bf v}} is the velocity in m/s.
  • The SI unit of momentum is kg·m/s.
  • Alternatively, the unit of momentum can be written as N·s.

Momentum in 2D

Because momentum is a vector, extra care is needed when dealing with 2D situations.

Example: A system contains two objects with momentums...

{\vec {\bf p}}_1 = 20\ {\rm {N\cdot s}}\ [15°]

{\vec {\bf p}}_2 = 30\ {\rm {N\cdot s}}\ [75°]

Calculate the total momentum of the system.

  • Use a vector diagram (“tip-to-tail”) when adding two or more momentum vectors.
  • Perform calculations on the {\vec {\bf p}}_x and {\vec {\bf p}}_y components separately.

Practice

  1. Evelyn has a mass of 36.0 kg. While playing soccer, she runs down the field with a velocity of 4.20 m/s [south]. Calculate Evelyn’s kinetic energy and her momentum.

    2. Calculate kinetic energy; the direction of the velocity does not matter:

    E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (36.0\ kg) (4.20\ m/s)^2 = 318\ J

    Calculate momentum, taking north as positive and south as negative:

    \vec{\bf p} = m\vec{\bf v} = \rm 36.0\ kg\cdot (-4.20\ m/s) = -151 kg\cdot m/s

  2. Calculate the velocity and the kinetic energy of a 25.0 kg mass that has a momentum of 370 kg·m/s [north].

    3. Use the momentum to calculate the velocity:

    \vec{\bf p} = m\vec{\bf v}

    \vec{\bf v} = \frac{\vec{\bf p}}{m} = \rm \frac{+370\ kg\cdot m/s}{25.0\ kg} = +14.8\ m/s

    Use the velocity to calculate the kinetic energy:

    E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}\ (25.0\ kg) (14.8\ m/s)^2 = 2.74\times 10^3\ J

  3. A lacrosse ball has 75.0 J of kinetic energy and is moving with a speed of 15.0 m/s. Determine (i) the ball’s mass and (ii) the magnitude of its momentum.

    4. Use the kinetic energy to calculate the mass:

    E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

    2 E_k =mv^2

    m = \frac{2 E_k}{v^2} = \rm \frac{2\cdot 75.0\ J}{(15.0\ m/s)^2} = 0.667\ kg

    Calculate the momentum:

    p = mv = \rm (0.667\ kg)(15.0\ m/s) = 10.0\ kg\cdot m/s

    1. Two objects have the same amount of momentum, but object “A” has a mass m while object “B” has a mass 2m. Which object has more kinetic energy? By what factor?

      2. Let’s call the momentum of each object \vec{\bf p}, and the masses m_A = m and m_B = 2m. The speeds of the two masses are:

      v_A = \frac{p_A}{m_A} = \frac{p}{m}

      v_B = \frac{p_B}{m_B} = \frac{p}{2m}

      The kinetic energies are:

      E_{kA} = \frac{1}{2}m_A{v_A}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m{\left(\frac{p}{m}\right)}^2 = \frac{mp^2}{2m^2} = \frac{p^2}{2m}

      E_{kB} = \frac{1}{2}m_B{v_B}^2 = \frac{1}{\bcancel 2} ({\bcancel 2}m) {\left(\frac{p}{2m}\right)}^2 = \frac{mp^2}{4m^2} = \frac{p^2}{4m}

      Mass B, the larger one, has only half as much kinetic energy.

    2. Two objects have the same amount of kinetic energy, but object “A” has a mass m while object “B” has a mass 2m. Which object has more momentum? By what factor?

      3. Let’s call the kinetic energy of each object E_k, and the masses m_A = m and m_B = 2m. Calculate the speed of mass “A” by rearranging the kinetic energy equation:

      E_k = \frac{1}{2} m_A{v_A}^2

      \frac{2E_k}{m_A} = {v_A}^2

      v_A = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m_A}} = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}}

      The momentum is:

      p_A = m_Av_A = m\sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \sqrt{2mE_k}

      The algebra is the same for mass “B”, the only difference being that the mass is m_B = 2m:

      v_B = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m_B}} = \sqrt{\frac{{\bcancel 2}E_k}{{\bcancel 2}m}} = \sqrt{\frac{E_k}{m}}

      p_B = m_Bv_B = (2m) \sqrt{\frac{E_k}{m}} = 2\sqrt{mE_k}

      The ratio of the momenta is:

      \frac{p_B}{p_A} = \frac{2\sqrt{\bcancel{mE_k}}}{\sqrt{2\bcancel{mE_k}}} = \sqrt{2}

      Mass “B” has more momentum than “A” by a factor of \sqrt{2}.

Review

  1. Define momentum.

    2. Momentum is a vector measure of the “quantity of motion” a mass has defined by the equation:

    \vec{\bf p} = m\vec{\bf v}

  2. Compare and contrast the concept of momentum to kinetic energy.
    3. See the “Kinetic Energy vs. Momentum” section of the Lesson Notes above.
  3. Explain why calculating the total momentum of a system containing more than object is more difficult when the objects are moving in 2D or 3D compared to 1D motion.
    4. Because momentum is a vector, the x-, y-, and z-components of the momentum vector must be added separately. Two masses that each have 1.0 kg·m/s of momentum will not have a combined momentum of 2.0 kg·m/s when the directions of the vectors are not the same!
    1. James drives a bumper car at 3.00 m/s [east]. The combined mass of James and his bumper car is 185 kg. Shannon, who is driving a second bumper car with a total mass of 160 kg, chases after James with a velocity of 4.25 m/s [east]. Calculate the total momentum and the total mechanical energy of the system.

      2. \vec{\bf p}_J = m_J\vec{\bf v}_J = \rm (185\ kg) (+3.00\ m/s) = +555\ kg\cdot m/s

      \vec{\bf p}_S = m_S\vec{\bf v}_S = \rm (160\ kg) (+4.25\ m/s) = +680\ kg\cdot m/s

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_J + \vec{\bf p}_S = \rm +555\ kg\cdot m/s + 680\ kg\cdot m/s = 1235\ kg\cdot m/s

      E_{kJ} = \frac{1}{2} m_J{v_J}^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (185\ kg) (3.00\ m/s)^2 = 833\ J

      E_{kS} = \frac{1}{2} m_S{v_S}^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (160\ kg) (4.25\ m/s)^2 = 1445\ J

      \Sigma E_k = E_{kJ} + E_{kS} = \rm 833\ J + 1445\ J = 2278\ J

    2. Repeat your calculations for a head-on collision in which Shannon’s bumper car is traveling west, but all other information remains unchanged.

      3. All of the calculations are the same as above, except that Shannon’s momentum is \rm -680\ kg\cdot m/s. This makes the total momentum:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_J + \vec{\bf p}_S = \rm +555\ kg\cdot m/s + (-680\ kg\cdot m/s) = -125\ kg\cdot m/s

      Because kinetic energy is a scalar, the direction does not matter and the answers are identical to (a).
