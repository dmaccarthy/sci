Elastic & Inelatic Collisions

When a system experiences no unbalanced, external forces, the system’s total momentum must remain constant.

Internal forces do not affect the total momentum; however, they can still transform kinetic energy to or from other forms...

A system’s kinetic energy will not necessarily remain constant during a collision or other situations where the momentum does!

Whether the kinetic energy changes, and by how much, depends on the nature of the forces.

A collision in which the kinetic energy remains constant is called an elastic collision.

  • When two sponge or rubber objects collide, their kinetic energy is temporarily transformed into elastic potential energy as the objects compress.
  • The kinetic energy is restored when the objects return to their original shapes.
  • Almost no sound or thermal energy is produced; kinetic energy is (nearly) conserved.
  • Although we use the term “elastic”, other types of force can also result in collisions with no change of kinetic energy.

A collision in which there is a loss of kinetic energy is called an inelastic collision.

  • When two colliding objects stick together after the collision, the collision will always be inelastic.
  • In fact, this type of collision results in a maximum loss of kinetic energy (more is lost when they stick together than when they don’t.)

A scenario when kinetic energy increase is sometimes called superelastic.

  • This is only possible in explosions, and other situations where there is a source of energy (e.g. chemical) available to increase the kinetic energy.

  2. Physicists use a particle accelerator to produce a 1D collision between a proton (m_p = 1.67 × 10–27 kg) initially at rest and a particle of unknown mass moving at +6.00 × 107 m/s. As a result of the collision, the unknown particle is slowed to a velocity of +3.59 × 107 m/s while the proton is accelerated to a velocity of +9.59 × 107 m/s.
    1. What is the mass of the unknown particle?

      2. Since the proton is initially at rest, the total momentum is equal to the momentum of the unknown particle before the collision:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_{iU} = m_U\vec{\bf v}_{iU}

      After collision, both particles are in motion:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_{fP} + \vec{\bf p}_{fU} = m_P\vec{\bf v}_{fP} + m_U\vec{\bf v}_{fU}

      Without knowing the mass of the unkonwn particle we cannot calculate the momentum, but we can equate the initial and final momentum becuase momentum is conserved:

      m_U\vec{\bf v}_{iU} = m_P\vec{\bf v}_{fP} + m_U\vec{\bf v}_{fU}

      We can now re-arrange to find the mass of the unknown particle:

      \begin{align} m_U\vec{\bf v}_{iU} - m_U\vec{\bf v}_{fU} &= m_P\vec{\bf v}_{fP} \\[10pt] m_U \left(\vec{\bf v}_{iU} - \vec{\bf v}_{fU} \right) &= m_P\vec{\bf v}_{fP} \\[10pt] m_U &= \frac{m_P\vec{\bf v}_{fP}}{\vec{\bf v}_{iU} - \vec{\bf v}_{fU}} \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg\cdot (+9.59\times 10^7\ m/s)}{+6.00\times 10^7\ m/s - (+3.59\times 10^7\ m/s)}} \\[10pt] &= \rm {6.65\times 10^{-27}\ kg} \end{align}

    2. Calculate the change (if any) in mechanical energy and classify the collision as elastic, inelastic, or superelastic.

      3. Calculate the initial mechanical energy:

      \begin{align}E_i &= \frac{1}{2}m_U{v_{iU}}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (6.65\times 10^{-27}\ kg) (6.00\times 10^7\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {1.20\times 10^{-11}\ J} \end{align}

      Calculate the final mechanical energy:

      \begin{align}E_f &= \frac{1}{2}m_P{v_{fP}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_U{v_{fU}}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg) (9.59\times 10^7\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (6.65\times 10^{-27}\ kg) (3.59\times 10^7\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {1.20\times 10^{-11}\ J} \end{align}

      The collision appears to be elastic because the mechanical energy does not change.

  3. In a game of billiards, a 350-gram ball moving with a velocity of +2.25 m/s collides with a 300-gram ball moving in the exact opposite direction with a velocity of –0.400 m/s. After the collision, the heavier ball’s velocity is +0.900 m/s.
    1. What is the final velocity of the lighter ball?

      2. Calculate the total momentum before the collision:

      \begin{align} \Sigma\vec{\bf p} &= \vec{\bf p}_{iA} + \vec{\bf p}_{iB} \\[10pt] &= m_A\vec{\bf v}_{iA} + m_B\vec{\bf v}_{iB} \\[10pt] &= \rm {0.350\ kg\cdot (+2.25\ m/s) + 0.300\ kg\cdot (-0.400\ m/s)} \\[10pt] &= \rm {+0.668\ kg\cdot m/s} \end{align}

      After the collision, the momentum of “A” is:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fA} = m_A\vec{\bf v}_{fA} = \rm 0.350\ kg\cdot (+0.900\ m/s) = +0.315\ kg\cdot m/s

      Because total momentum is conserved, the final momentum of “B” is:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fB} = \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - \vec{\bf p}_{fA} = \rm (+0.668 - (+0.315))\ kg\cdot m/s = +0.473\ kg\cdot m/s

      The velocity is:

      \vec{\bf v}_{fB} = \frac{\vec{\bf p}_{fB}}{m_B} = \rm \frac{+0.473\ kg\cdot m/s}{0.300\ kg} = +1.18\ m/s

    2. Calculate the change (if any) in mechanical energy and classify the collision as elastic, inelastic, or superelastic.

      3. Calculate the initial mechanical energy:

      \begin{align}E_i &= \frac{1}{2}m_A{v_{iA}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_B{v_{iB}}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (0.350\ kg) (2.25\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (0.300\ kg) (0.400\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {0.910\ J} \end{align}

      Calculate the final mechanical energy:

      \begin{align}E_f &= \frac{1}{2}m_A{v_{fA}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_B{v_{fB}}^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (0.350\ kg) (0.900\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (0.300\ kg) (1.18\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm {0.351\ J} \end{align}

      The collision is inelastic because the system lost 61.4% of its initial mechanical energy.

Review

  1. Define elastic and inelastic collisions.
    2. Elastic means that mechanical energy as well as momentum is conserved. Inelastic means that only momentum is conserved as some of the mechanical energy is transformed into non-mechanical forms such as sound or thermal energy.
  2. What does it mean to say that a collision in which the objects remain attached after the collison is “completely” inelastic.
    3. The loss of mechanical energy is greatest (while momentum is conserved) when the two masses stick together.
  3. A toy train set includes a locomotive of mass of 750 grams and a passenger car of mass 400 grams. The locomotive has an initial velocity of –20.0 cm/s as it backs into the passenger car which is initially at rest. The locomotive and the passenger car do not join together successfully, and the passenger car moves off with a final velocity of –15.0 cm/s. Calculate:
    1. the final velocity of the locomotive.

      2. Because the passenger car is at rest, the locomotive’s initial momentum is the total system momentum:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_{iL} = m_L\vec{\bf p}_{iL} = \rm 0.750\ kg\cdot (-0.200\ m/s) = -0.150\ kg\cdot m/s

      Now calculate the momentum of the passenger car after the collision:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fP} = m_P\vec{\bf p}_{fP} = \rm 0.400\ kg\cdot (-0.150\ m/s) = -0.0600\ kg\cdot m/s

      The total momentum remains constant, so the final momentum of the locomotive is:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fL} = \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - \vec{\bf p}_{fP} = \rm (-0.150 - (-0.060))\ kg\cdot m/s = -0.090\ kg\cdot m/s

      Finally, calculate the locomotive’s final velocity:

      \vec{\bf v}_{fL} = \frac{\vec{\bf p}_{fL}}{m_L} = \rm \frac{-0.090\ kg\cdot m/s}{0.750\ kg} = -0.120\ m/s

    2. the change in mechanical energy of the system.

      3. Since the passenger car is initially at rest, the initial mechanical energy of the system is:

      \Sigma E_i = \frac{1}{2}m_L v_{iL}^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.750\ kg) (0.200\ m/s)^2 = 0.0150\ J

      The final mechanical energy is:

      \begin{align}\Sigma E_f &= \frac{1}{2}m_L v_{fL}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_P v_{fP}^2 \\[12pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (0.750\ kg) (0.120\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (0.400\ kg) (0.150\ m/s)^2} \\[12pt] &= \rm {0.00990\ J} \end{align}

      The change in energy is:

      \Delta E = \Sigma E_f - \Sigma E_i = \rm (0.00990 - 0.0150)\ J = -0.0051\ J

    3. Classify the collisions as elastic, partly or completely inelastic, or superelastic.

      4. Since there is a loss of mechanical energy but the masses do not join together, the collision was partly inelastic.
