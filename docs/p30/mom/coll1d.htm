Elastic & Inelatic Collisions

When a system experiences no unbalanced, external forces, the system’s total momentum must remain constant.

Internal forces do not affect the total momentum; however, they can still transform kinetic energy to or from other forms...

A system’s kinetic energy will not necessarily remain constant during a collision or other situations where the momentum does!

Whether the kinetic energy changes, and by how much, depends on the nature of the forces.

A collision in which the kinetic energy remains constant is called an elastic collision.

  • When two sponge or rubber objects collide, their kinetic energy is temporarily transformed into elastic potential energy as the objects compress.
  • The kinetic energy is restored when the objects return to their original shapes.
  • Almost no sound or thermal energy is produced; kinetic energy is (nearly) conserved.
  • Although we use the term “elastic”, other types of force can also result in collisions with no change of kinetic energy.

A collision in which there is a loss of kinetic energy is called an inelastic collision.

  • When two colliding objects stick together after the collision, the collision will always be inelastic.
  • In fact, this type of collision results in a maximum loss of kinetic energy (more is lost when they stick together than when they don’t.)

A scenario when kinetic energy increase is sometimes called superelastic.

  • This is only possible in explosions, and other situations where there is a source of energy (e.g. chemical) available to increase the kinetic energy.

Review

  1. A toy train set includes a locomotive of mass of 750 grams and a passenger car of mass 400 grams. The locomotive has an initial velocity of –20.0 cm/s as it backs into the passenger car which is initially at rest. The locomotive and the passenger car do not join together successfully, and the passenger car moves off with a final velocity of –15.0 cm/s. Calculate:
    1. the final velocity of the locomotive.

      2. Because the passenger car is at rest, the locomotive’s initial momentum is the total system momentum:

      \Sigma\vec{\bf p} = \vec{\bf p}_{iL} = m_L\vec{\bf p}_{iL} = \rm 0.750\ kg\cdot (-0.200\ m/s) = -0.150\ kg\cdot m/s

      Now calculate the momentum of the passenger car after the collision:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fP} = m_P\vec{\bf p}_{fP} = \rm 0.400\ kg\cdot (-0.150\ m/s) = -0.0600\ kg\cdot m/s

      The total momentum remains constant, so the final momentum of the locomotive is:

      \vec{\bf p}_{fL} = \Sigma\vec{\bf p} - \vec{\bf p}_{fP} = \rm (-0.150 - (-0.060))\ kg\cdot m/s = -0.090\ kg\cdot m/s

      Finally, calculate the locomotive’s final velocity:

      \vec{\bf v}_{fL} = \frac{\vec{\bf p}_{fL}}{m_L} = \rm \frac{-0.090\ kg\cdot m/s}{0.750\ kg} = -0.120\ m/s

    2. the change in mechanical energy of the system.

      3. Since the passenger car is initially at rest, the initial mechanical energy of the system is:

      \Sigma E_i = \frac{1}{2}m_L v_{iL}^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.750\ kg) (0.200\ m/s)^2 = 0.0150\ J

      The final mechanical energy is:

      \begin{align}\Sigma E_f &= \frac{1}{2}m_L v_{fL}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_P v_{fP}^2 \\[12pt] &= \rm {\frac{1}{2} (0.750\ kg) (0.120\ m/s)^2 + \frac{1}{2} (0.400\ kg) (0.150\ m/s)^2} \\[12pt] &= \rm {0.00990\ J} \end{align}

      The change in energy is:

      \Delta E = \Sigma E_f - \Sigma E_i = \rm (0.00990 - 0.0150)\ J = -0.0051\ J

    3. Classify the collisions as elastic, partly or completely inelastic, or superelastic.

      4. Since there is a loss of mechanical energy but the masses do not join together, the collision was partly inelastic.
