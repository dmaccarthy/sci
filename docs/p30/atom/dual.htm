Wave-Particle Duality

Wave-Particle Duality

We saw earlier that EMR exhibits wave-particle duality.

  • EM waves behave according to Maxwell’s model and Huygen’s Principle.
  • Wave-like behaviour includes interference phenomena like diffraction.
  • EM particles (photons) behave according to Einstein’s model.
  • Particle-like behaviour includes the photoelectric and Compton effects.
  • Shorter wavelengths tends to be more particle-like, for example by diffracting less.
  • Because x-rays have such short wavelengths, they can usually be treated as particles.
  • To make an x-ray behave as a wave, we would need to use a “diffraction grating” with a slit spacing comparable to the size of a single atom.
  • Crystals are regular arrangements of atoms that can be used like a diffraction grating but produce more complicated 3D interference patterns.

De Broglie’s Hypothesis

De Broglie proposed that wave-particle duality applies to matter (electrons, protons, neutrons) as well as to EMR!

  • Photons obey the relations:

    E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = cp

  • If we cancel the speed of light:

    \frac{h}{\lambda} = p

    \lambda = \frac{h}{p}

  • This is called the de Broglie wavelength and de Broglie suggested that it applies to all particles, not just photons.

Example 1

Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of an electron in a cathode ray beam after being accelerated by a 1000-volt potential difference.

The potential difference tells us the kinetic energy, and therefore the speed of the electrons:

q\Delta V = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

v = \sqrt{\frac{2q\Delta V}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C \cdot 1000\ V}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}}}

v = \rm{1.87\times 10^7\ m/s}

Now calculate the wavelength:

\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} = \rm \frac{6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot 1.87\times 10^7\ m/s}

\lambda = \rm 3.88\times 10^{-11}\ m

This is very short, similar to the wavelength of an x-ray and the size of an atom. Electrons tend to behave as particles on everyday scales but as waves on atomic scales.

Electrons as Waves

A regular arrangement of atoms (i.e. a crystal) can be used as a 3D diffraction grating to produce an interference pattern from very short waves like x-rays.

  • William and Lawrence Bragg had developed this technique (using x-rays) to study the structure of crystals.
  • In 1927, Davisson and Germer were able to demonstrate that an electron beam incident on a nickel crystal produced an interference pattern too!

  • Interference indicates the electrons are acting like waves.
  • More recent experiments have demonstrated that electrons exhibit the same behaviour as light in a double-slit experiment, provided the slit spacing is small enough.
  • Everyday objects (people, cars, basketballs, planets) can safely be treated as particles all the time because their de Broglie wavelengths are very much shorter than the size of the objects themselves.

Quantum Physics

The branch of physics known as quantum mechanics attempts to resolve the apparent contradiction of wave-particle duality.

  • In the standard interpretation (first suggested by Max Born), a particle’s “position” is not an exact location, but a probability distribution.
  • The “wave” associated with any particle determines its probability distribution.
  • This interpretation of wave-particle duality is born out by the quantum version of Young’s double-slit experiment.
  • When the intensity of the light is reduced to a single photon at a time, the location where the photon hits the screen is random, but obeys a probability distribution described by the interference pattern observed in Young’s original (many photons at once!) experiment.
  • This probabilistic way of looking at mechanics applies to other variables (e.g. energy and momentum) as well as position.
  • The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle states that as a particle’s location becomes less uncertain, its momentum becomes more uncertain.

Practice

  1. An electron is accelerated from rest by a potential difference of 1.25 kV.
    1. Determine the electron’s speed.

      2. The work done by the potential difference determines the electron kinetic energy...

      q\Delta V = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2

      \frac{2q\Delta V}{m} = v_f^2

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2q\Delta V}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 1250\ V}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}} = 2.10\times 10^7\ m/s

    2. What is its de Broglie wavelength?

      3. \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} = \rm \frac{6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot 2.10\times 10^7\ m/s} = 3.47\times 10^{-11}\ m

    3. Would the electron behave more like a particle or a wave when it is incident on a crystal with an atom spacing of 2.37 × 10–10 m?

      4. Since the wavelength is only slightly smaller than the spacing, the electron would behave as a wave.

  2. Neutrons exhibit diffraction from a crystal just as electrons and x-rays do. Estimate the speed at which the neutron’s behaviour would change from wave-like to particle-like when it is incident on a crystal with an atom spacing of 4.25 × 10–10 m.

    3. \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}

    \lambda mv = h

    v = \frac{h}{\lambda m} = \rm \frac{6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s}{4.25\times 10^{-10}\ m \cdot 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg} = 934\ m/s

  3. Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of a 182 g billiard ball moving at 75.0 cm/s. Why is it unlikely that a billiard ball would behave as a wave?

    4. \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} = \rm \frac{6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s}{0.182 kg\cdot 0.750\ m/s} = 4.86\times 10^{-33}\ m

    Since this is 1019 times smaller than a single nucleus, a billiard ball will never be part of a system this small!

    1. Calculate the speed of an electron that has a wavelength equal to the wavelength of a 2.50 × 1017 Hz UV wave.

      2. For the UV photon...

      \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{2.50\times 10^{17}\ Hz} = 1.20\times 10^{-9}\ m

      For the electron...

      v = \frac{h}{\lambda m} = \rm \frac{6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s}{1.20\times 10^{-9}\ m \cdot 9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg} = 6.06\times 10^5\ m/s

    2. Calculate the energy of the electron.

      3. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}\cdot 9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot (6.06\times 10^5\ m/s)^2 = 1.68\times 10^{-19}\ J

    3. Compare the photon and the electron in terms of (i) speed, (ii) momentum, (iii) energy.

      4. The electron’s speed (6.06 × 105 m/s) is only 1/500th of the speed of light.

      Because they have the same deBroglie wavelength, their momenta are equal!

      The photon energy is...

      E = hf = \rm 6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s\cdot 2.50\times 10^{17}\ Hz = 1.66\times 10^{-16}\ J

      This is nearly 1000 times more than the electron. Since the photon has less mass (i.e. none) compared to the electron, it needs much more energy to have the same momentum!
