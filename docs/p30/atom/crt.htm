Lesson Notes

Thomson’s CRT Experiment

Cathode Ray Tube

A cathode ray tube is a device that uses a strong electric field to produce a beam of electrons.

  • Although the electrons themselves are not visible, coating the tube with a fluorescent material or filling the tube with certain low pressure gases will cause a glow to appear that lets us “see” the electron beam.
  • The nature of the beam was unknown when scientists started using CRTs in the late 19th century.
  • Experimentation showed that the beam originated at the cathode (negative electrode), resulting in the name cathode ray.
  • By applying electric and magnetic fields, scientists determined that the cathode ray particles were negatively charged.

Speed of CRT Particles

J.J. Thomson conducted an experiment in 1897 that enabled him to determine the speed of the cathode ray particles, and the ratio of the particles’ charge to mass.

  • Thomson placed a second pair of electrodes in the CRT to produce an electric field perpendicular to the beam’s direction.

  • The effect of this field was to deflect the beam toward the positive electrode.
  • Thomson then applied a uniform magnetic field to the same region of the CRT to produce a magnetic force in the opposite direction to the electric force.
  • Thomson next adjusted the relative strengths of the two fields until the forces balanced, causing the beam to remain undeflected:

    F_e = F_m

    q|\va{E}_{def}| = qvB

    v = \frac{|\va{E}_{def}|}{B}

  • Thomson determined the speed of the particles by dividing the field strengths.
  • The subscript “def” distinguishes the deflecting electric field from the accelerating electric field. Both fields can be calculated using the respective voltages and distances between electrodes:

    |\va{E}| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}

Charge-to-Mass Ratio

Once the particle speed was known, Thomson turned off one of the fields and left the other on.

  • Since the forces are no longer balanced, the CRT particles have an acceleration.
  • A uniform magnetic field causes uniform circular motion:

    F_m = ma_c

    qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}

    \frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br}

  • By measuring the radius of the particles’ trajectory in a uniform magnetic field, Thomson could determine the ratio of the charge to mass.
  • Thomson found that the q/m ratio was the same no matter which material was used for the cathode; all electrons are the same!
  • Millikan had not yet performed his oil drop experiment, so the charge of the particles was unknown.
  • Once Millikan’s experiment was complete, the results of the two experiments could be combined to find the mass of the electron.
  • Thomson then reversed the direction of the accelerating field and introduced positive ions, to create a beam of positive particles.
  • The results showed that positive ions have a much smaller q/m ratio (i.e. a much larger mass) than the negative particles.
  • The q/m ratio is different for different ions.
  • Thomson also measured the direction of the beam when it was deflected using an electric field and no magnetic field.
  • The motion of the particles in a uniform electric field is projectile motion.
  • Although the calculations are more difficult, the q/m ratio obtained this way is the same as for the uniform circular motion due to the uniform magnetic field.

Projectile Motion

Alternatively, the second part of the experiment can be done with a uniform electric field instead of a magnetic field.

  • A uniform electric field causes projectile (parabolic) motion instead of uniform circular motion.
  • The electric field acts in the y-direction and causes the acceleration:

    \va{a}_y = \frac{\va{F}_e}{m} = \frac{q\va{E}_{def}}{m}

  • The y-velocity is then:

    \va{v}_y = \va{v}_{iy} + \va{a}_y \Delta t = \frac{q\va{E}_{def}}{m} \Delta t

  • Because there is no force in the x-direction, the x-motion is uniform:

    \va{v}_x = \frac{\Delta\va{d}_x}{\Delta t}

  • If we multiply the magnitudes of the two equations the time will cancel:

    v_y v_x = \frac{q}{m} |\va{E}_{def}| \Delta d_x

    \frac{q}{m} = \frac{v_y v_x}{|\va{E}_{def}| \Delta d_x}

  • We can calculate the q/m ratio using the initial speed of the particles as v_x and using trigonometry to calculate v_y = v_x\tan\theta.
  • Thus Thomson needed to measure the direction of the beam’s motion (\theta) after travelling a known horizontal distance \Delta d_x.
  • The results of the experiment — the calculated q/m — when performed with a uniform electric field are the same as when performed with a uniform magnetic field.

Practice

  1. A cathode ray is produced by a high voltage applied between the cathode and anode. The ray passes through a pair of deflecting plates separated by 3.20 mm. A uniform, perpendicular magnetic field of 36.5 milliteslas is applied to the region between the deflecting plates. The experimenter then adjusts the voltage of the deflecting plates until the cathode ray appears undeflected. This occurs when the deflecting voltage is 2.93 × 103 V.
    1. Is the magnetic field directed into or out of the page if the deflecting voltage has the polarity illustrated in the first diagram of the notes above?

      2. Since the top plate is positive, the electric force on the electrons is directed upward. The magnetic force must be down to balance it. According to the 3rd Left Hand Rule, the direction of \va B is into the page.

    2. What is the speed of the particles in the cathode ray?

      3. The deflecting electric field strength is...

      |\va E| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va d} = \rm \frac{2.93\times 10^3\ V}{3.20\times 10^{-3}\ m} = 9.16\times 10^5\ N/C

      Since the electric and magnetic forces balance...

      F_m = F_e

      qvB = q|{\va E}|

      vB = |{\va E}|

      v = \frac{|{\va E}|}{B} = \rm \frac{9.16\times 10^5\ N/C}{0.0365\ T} = 2.51\times 10^7\ m/s

    3. When the deflecting voltage is turned off, the particles of the cathode ray travel in a circle with radius 3.90 mm. Calculate the charge-to-mass ratio and the mass of the cathode rays.

      4. Using Newton’s 2nd Law with only the magnetic force...

      F_m = ma

      qvB = m\frac{v^2}{r}

      qB = m\frac{v}{r}

      \frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \rm \frac{2.51\times 10^7\ m/s}{0.0365\ T\cdot 3.90\times 10^{-3}\ m} = 1.76\times 10^{11}\ C/kg

      Isolate m and use the elementary charge for q...

      m = \rm \frac{1.76\times 10^{11}\ C/kg}{1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 9.07\times 10^{-31}\ kg

    4. What is the value of the accelerating voltage needed to produce the beam of cathode ray particles?

      5. \Delta V = \frac{\Delta E}{q} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2}{q}

      Since the electrons are initially at rest before being accelerated...

      \Delta V = \frac{mv_f^2}{2q} = \rm \frac{9.07\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot (2.51\times 10^7\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 1.79\times 10^3\ V

    1. A modified CRT design is used to measure the q/m ratio for positive ions. The “electron gun” is aimed at hydrogen gas, causing it to become ionized. The accelerating and deflecting voltages are reversed so that the positive particles are accelerated into a ray. Using the same 36.5 mT magnetic field and 3.20 mm deflecting plate separation, the experimenter finds that a deflecting voltage of 68.3 V is required for the particles to remain undeflected and that the particles travel with a radius of 16.7 cm when the deflecting voltage is turned off. Find the q/m ratio for these positive particles.

      2. The calculations are identical to #1...

      |\va E| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va d} = \rm \frac{68.3\ V}{3.20\times 10^{-3}\ m} = 2.13\times 10^4\ N/C

      v = \frac{|{\va E}|}{B} = \rm \frac{2.13\times 10^4\ N/C}{0.0365\ T} = 5.85\times 10^5\ m/s

      \frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \rm \frac{5.85\times 10^5\ m/s}{0.0365\ T\cdot 0.167\ m} = 9.59\times 10^7\ C/kg

    2. Determine the mass of protons using this result. How many times more massive is the proton than the electron?

      3. m = {\rm \frac{9.59\times 10^7\ C/kg}{1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 1.67\times 10^{-27}\ kg = 1.83\times 10^3}\ m_e

  3. In a Thomson-style experiment, the cathode ray is undeflected when the magnetic field is 0.500 mT and the deflecting voltage is 1060 V. (The deflecting electrodes are 8.00 cm apart and 12.0 cm long.) When the magnetic field is turn off, the cathode ray follows a parabola that corresponds to a deflection of 21.7º at the far end of the deflecting electrodes. Use the data to determine the (a) horizontal and vertical components of the electrons’ velocity, (b) charge-to-mass ratio, and (c) accelerating voltage.

    4. Stage 1 — Forces Balanced

    |\va E| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va d} = \rm \frac{1060\ V}{0.0800\ m} = 1.33\times 10^4\ N/C

    v_x = \frac{|{\va E}|}{B} = \rm \frac{1.33\times 10^4\ N/C}{5.00\times 10^{-4}\ T} = 2.65\times 10^7\ m/s

    Stage 2 — Projectile Motion

    Use the angle to calculate the y-component of the final velocity...

    \tan\theta = \frac{\va{v}_{fy}}{\va{v}_x}

    Use the x-motion to find the time...

    \va{v}_{fy} = \va{v}_x\cdot\tan\theta = \rm 2.65\times 10^7\ m/s\cdot \tan 21.7^\circ = 1.05\times 10^7\ m/s

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta\va d_x}{\va{v}_x} = \rm \frac{0.120\ m}{2.65\times 10^7\ m/s} = 4.53\times 10^{-9}\ s

    Calculate the y-acceleration...

    \va{a}_y = \frac{\va{v}_{fy} - \va{v}_{iy}}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{(1.05\times 10^7 - 0)\ m/s}{4.53\times 10^{-9}\ s} = 2.32\times 10^{15}\ m/s^2

    Use Newton’s 2nd Law to determine q/m...

    \va{F}_e = q\va{E} = m\va{a}_y

    \frac{q}{m} = \frac{\va{a}_y}{\va E} = \rm \frac{2.32\times 10^{15}\ m/s^2}{1.33\times 10^4\ N/C} = 1.75\times 10^{11}\ C/kg

    Calculate the accelerating voltage based on the work done on the electrons by the electric field...

    q\Delta V = \Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2

    Since the initial velocity is zero...

    \Delta V = \frac{mv_f^2}{2q} = \rm\frac{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot (2.65\times 10^7\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 2.01\times 10^3\ V

  4. An experimenter has produced a beam that contains numerous charged particles moving with different speeds. She wants to use electric and magnetic fields to select only those particles with a speed of 9.17 × 104 m/s.
    1. If the magnetic field strength is 1.34 T, what electric field is needed?

      2. F_e = F_m

      q|\va{E}| = qvB

      |\va{E}| = vB = \rm 9.17\times 10^4\ m/s\cdot 1.34\ T = 1.23\times 10^5\ N/C

    2. If the electric field is created by parallel conducting plates separated by 89.0 mm, what voltage should be maintained between the plates?

      3. \Delta V = \va{E}\cdot \Delta\va{d} = \rm 1.23\times 10^5\ N/C \cdot 0.0890\ m = 1.09\times 10^3\ V

    1. A charged particle enters a region where there is a uniform magnetic field of 0.920 T and there is an adjustable electric field created by two parallel conducting plates that are 1.15 cm apart. Both fields are perpendicular to the particle’s velocity. An experimenter finds that the particle moves in a straight line when the voltage between the deflecting plates is 8.04 kV. What is the particle’s speed?

      2. The calculations are identical to #1...

      |\va E| = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va d} = \rm \frac{8040\ V}{0.0115\ m} = 6.99\times 10^5\ N/C

      v = \frac{|{\va E}|}{B} = \rm \frac{6.99\times 10^5\ N/C}{0.920\ T} = 7.60\times 10^5\ m/s

    2. If the particle next passes into a region with a uniform magnetic field of 0.750 T, it follows a circular orbit with radius 2.11 cm, calculate the particle’s q/m ratio.

      3. \frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \rm \frac{7.60\times 10^5\ m/s}{0.750\ T\cdot 0.0211\ m} = 4.80\times 10^7\ C/kg

    3. Assuming that the charge of the particle is equal to one elementary charge, what is the particle’s mass?

      4. m = {\rm \frac{4.80\times 10^7\ C/kg}{1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C} = 3.33\times 10^{-27}\ kg} = 2m_p

  6. A cathode ray tube uses an accelerating voltage of 14.0 kV.
    1. Calculate the speed of the electrons in the cathode ray once they leave the accelerating region, assuming they start from rest.

      2. The work done by the electric field equals the kinetic energy gained by the electrons...

      q\Delta V = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2

      \frac{2q\Delta V}{m} = v_f^2

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2q\Delta V}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 1.40\times 10^4\ V}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}} = 7.01\times 10^7\ m/s

    2. The vertical deflecting plates are 2.00 cm long. Determine the time it takes for the electrons to pass through the deflecting region (by using your answer to (a)).

      3. \Delta t = \frac{\Delta\va{d}_x}{\va{v}_x} = \rm \frac{0.0200\ m}{7.01\times 10^7\ m/s} = 2.85\times 10^{-10}\ s

    3. Determine the acceleration of the electrons between the deflecting electrodes if their potential difference is 42.0 kV and the electrodes are separated by 65.0 mm.

      4. \va{a} = \frac{\va F}{m} = \frac{q\va E}{m} = \frac{q}{m}\left(\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta\va{d}}\right)

      \va{a} = \rm \frac{1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}\left(\frac{4.20\times 10^4\ V}{0.0650\ m}\right) = 1.13\times 10^{17}\ m/s^2

    4. Determine the vertical speed of the electrons leaving the deflecting region (by using your answers to (b) and (c)).

      5. \va{v}_{fy} = \cancel{\va{v}_{iy}} + \va{a}_y\Delta t = \rm 1.13\times 10^{17}\ m/s^2\cdot 2.85\times 10^{-10}\ s = 3.24\times 10^7\ m/s

    5. By what angle do the electrons’ velocity change due to the deflecting plates. [Hint: You know both the vertical and horizontal components of the final velocity.]

      6. \tan\theta = \frac{\va{v}_y}{\va{v}_x}

      \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\va{v}_y}{\va{v}_x}\right) = \rm \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3.24\times 10^7\ m/s}{7.01\times 10^7\ m/s}\right) = 24.8^\circ
