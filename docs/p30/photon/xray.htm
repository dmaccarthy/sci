X-Rays

Discovery of X-Rays

Wilhelm Röntgen discovered x-rays by accident in 1895 while using a device called a cathode ray tube (CRT), which creates a beam of electrons inside a glass tube.

Cathode Ray Tube

  • The electron beam is supposed to create fluorescence (a glow of visible light) when the electrons strike the screen at the end of the CRT.
  • Röntgen noticed that fluorescence occurred not just on the inside of the tube, but also on a screen placed outside of the tube, even when the entire CRT apparatus was covered!
  • Röntgen concluded that the CRT produced invisible rays (which he termed x-rays) that could penetrate the covering of the CRT and cause the fluorescence on the outside screen.
  • Further experiments showed that x-rays could also penetrate soft tissue and expose photographic film, allowing them to be used to take pictures of bones.

Production of X-Rays

X-Rays are a form of EMR, which we know is produced by accelerating charges.

  • In Röntgen’s lab, it was the electron beam striking the end of the cathode ray tube that caused a sudden deceleration of the electrons, resulting in the emission of EMR (photons!)
  • This method of producing x-rays, by accelerating electrons to high speeds and then colliding them with a target, is called bremsstrahlung, which is German for “braking radiation.”
  • In a modern x-ray machine such as you would find in a hospital or dental clinic, the beam of electrons is directed at a metal target.
  • A coolant is pumped past the metal to keep it cool, as up to 99% of the electron beam energy is converted to heat and only 1% to x-ray photons.
  • The metal target may also be rotated to reduce the wear it experiences from the electron beam striking it.

X-Ray Spectrum

The spectrum for x-rays (a graph of intensity versus wavelength or frequency) is different than for thermal EMR sources.

  • Thermal radiation comes from collisions between molecules with random speeds.
  • Bremsstrahlung x-rays are emitted by electrons that have nearly uniform (i.e. non-random) speeds.
  • The highest energy photon that an electron beam can emit would have the same energy as the electron right before it collides, resulting in a cutoff for the spectrum, labelled as \lambda_{min} on the graph above.
  • This energy in turn is determined by the potential difference used to produce the electron beam:

    hf_{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{min}} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2= qV_{acc}

  • Note that in this equation, f and \lambda describe the x-ray photon, while m, v and q are properties of the electrons whose energy is used to produce the photons.
  • V_{acc} is the potential difference used to accelerate the electrons, often called the accelerating voltage.
  • For example, if the beam of electrons is created using a potential difference of 2000 volts, the electrons would have a kinetic energy of 2000 electronvolts and could only emit photons with energies up to 2000 eV.
  • An x-ray spectrum also includes characteristic x-rays, spikes at wavelengths that are characteristic to the material being used as a target.
  • These spikes represent photons produced indirectly when the electron’s kinetic energy is transferred first to an atom in the target, and the atom then emits a photon.
  • The energies of these photons are determined by the energy levels of the target atoms.

Practice

  1. An electron is accelerated from rest by a potential difference of 20.0 kV.
    1. Calculate the final speed of the electron.

      2. Since the electron starts from rest (E_{ki} = 0 J), the final kinetic energy is equal to the work done by the accelerating electric field...

      \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = q\Delta V

      v^2 = \frac{2q\Delta V}{m}

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2q\Delta V}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ C\cdot 2.00\times 10^4\ V}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}} = 8.38\times 10^7\ m/s

    2. If the electron then collides with a massive particle (e.g. a nucleus) and all of the electron’s energy is radiated as a single photon, what would the photon’s frequency and wavelength be?

      3. The electron’s energy, which is used to produce the photon, is...

      E_k = q\Delta V = 1e\cdot \rm 2.00\times 10^4\ V = 2.00\times 10^4\ eV = 3.20\times 10^{-15}\ J

      The photon frequency and wavelength are..

      f = \frac{E}{h} = \rm \frac{2.00\times 10^4\ eV}{4.14\times 10^{-15}\ eV\cdot s} = 4.83\times 10^{18}\ Hz

      \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \rm \frac{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s}{4.83\times 10^{18}\ Hz} = 6.22\times 10^{-11}\ m

    3. Compare the original momentum of the electron to the momentum of the photon emitted. Are these results consistent with the law of momentum conservation? Explain.

      4. Calculate the photon momentum...

      p = \frac{E}{c} = \rm \frac{3.20\times 10^{-15}\ J}{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s} = 1.07\times 10^{-23}\ N\cdot s

      Calculate the electron momentum...

      p = mv = \rm 9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot 8.38\times 10^7\ m/s\ = 7.63\times 10^{-23}\ N\cdot s

      Most of the electron’s momentum is actually transferred to the nucleus that the electron collides with. Because of its very large mass, the nucleus acquires a very small velocity, and therefore a negligible amount of kinetic energy.

    4. If the colliding electron produced several photons, would the photons have a higher, lower, or the same frequency as in (b)? Explain.

      5. Because total energy must be conserved, the energy of each photon must be less than the total. Thus the photons would all have frequencies lower than 4.83 × 1018 Hz.

    1. What is the shortest wavelength EMR that could be emitted during a collision by an electron moving with an initial speed of 2.35 × 106 m/s? [Hint: Calculate the electron’s kinetic energy first.]

      2. The total energy available is...

      E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg)(2.35\times 10^6\ m/s)^2 = 2.52\times 10^{-18}\ J

      Assuming that the photon acquires all of this energy, the photon wavelength is...

      \lambda = \frac{hc}{E} = \rm \frac{(6.63\times 10^{-34}\ J\cdot s) (3.00\times 10^8\ m/s)}{2.52\times 10^{-18}\ J} = 7.91\times 10^{-8}\ m

    2. Calculate and compare the electron’s initial momentum to the photon’s final momentum. Where did the “lost” momentum go?

      3. Calculate the photon momentum...

      p = \frac{E}{c} = \rm \frac{2.52\times 10^{-18}\ J}{3.00\times 10^8\ m/s} = 8.38\times 10^{-27}\ N\cdot s

      Calculate the electron momentum...

      p = mv = \rm 9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg\cdot 2.35\times 10^6\ m/s\ = 2.14\times 10^{-24}\ N\cdot s

      Most of the electron’s momentum is actually transferred to the object that the electron collides with. Because the object is likely much more massive than the electron, it acquires a very small velocity, and therefore a negligible amount of kinetic energy.

      2. Calculate the maximum photon energy, which is equal to the electron kinetic energy...

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 2.18\times 10^{-14}\ J}{9.11\times 10^{-31}\ kg}} = 2.19\times 10^8\ m/s

      Note that this speed is only approximate; since it is more than 2/3 of the speed of light, the Newtonian kinetic energy formula (\frac{1}{2}mv^2) is no longer valid.
