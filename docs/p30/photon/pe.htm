Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect (discovered by Heinrich Hertz) is the emission of electrons from a metal that has been exposed to EMR.

  • Electrons emitted this way are called photoelectrons.
  • There is no difference whatsoever between a photoelectron and any other electron!
  • Interestingly, there is a sharp threshold frequency (that depends on the metal), below which the photoelectric effect does not occur.
  • For zinc, EMR below 1.05 \times 10^{15}\ \rm{Hz} will not result in the release of photoelectrons, no matter how intense the radiation is.
  • EMR above 1.05 \times 10^{15}\ \rm{Hz} will release photoelectrons, even at low intensities (although the number of photoelectrons will be low when the EMR intensity is low.)
  • Maxwell’s wave theory of EMR cannot explain the photoelectric effect.
  • However, the explanation using Einstein’s photon theory is simple: photons below the threshold frequency do not have enough energy to knock an electron out of its orbit.
  • The energy required to free electrons from the least tightly bound orbit is called the work function. The work function, W, equals the energy of photons at the threshold frequency, f_0:

    W = hf_0

  • If we expose a metal to photons of a frequency higher than the threshold frequency (f > f_0), some of the extra energy may appear as the photoelectron’s kinetic energy.
  • We can use conservation of energy to write a relation:

    hf = E_k^{max} + W

  • The kinetic energy may be less that E_k^{max} if the electron loses some energy through collisions or radiation as it leaves the metal, or if it comes from a lower energy level.

Millikan’s Experiment

This relationship can be tested experimentally, to confirm that the photon energy is E=hf as predicted by Einstein, and to directly measure Planck’s constant.

  • Robert A. Millikan (who also performed the oil drop experiment) designed an experiment for the photoelectric effect.
  • The frequency of the light source is the manipulated variable.
  • The intensity of the light and the electrode metal (work function, threshhold frequency) are controlled variables.
  • The maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons is the responding variable.

  • The photoelectrode is connected to a second electrode through an ammeter. When the ammeter indicates a current, we know that photoelectrons are being emitted.
  • A potential difference is applied to repel the photoelectrons. This reduces the current by preventing the slower electrons from reaching the anode.
  • As the voltage is increased, fewer electrons reach the anode.
  • When the current reaches zero, the voltage is strongh enough to repel even the fastest photoelectrons.
  • We can calculate E_k^{max} from the stopping voltage:

    E_k^{max} = qV_{stop}

  • Rearranging the energy conservation equation predicts that a graph of E_k^{max} vs. f will be linear, with a slope of h, a y-intercept of -W, and an x-intercept of f_0:

    E_k^{max} = hf - W = h(f-f_0)

  • Millikan’s data showed that this was true, and that the value of Planck’s constant was the same as Planck calculated from the blackbody radiation spectrum!
  • The photoelectric effect confirms that photons exist and have the expected energy:

    E=hf

  • If we repeat the experiment with a different electrode, the slope of the graph will remain the same (h) but the intercepts will change since the work function depends on the metal.

Example

The stopping voltage is measured to be 1.02 volts when a photoelectrode is exposed to light at 520 nm. Find the work function and threshhold frequency.

E_k^{max} = qV_{stop} = 1.02\ \rm{eV}

hf = E_k^{max} + W

W = hf - E_k^{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - E_k^{max}

W = \rm{\frac{(4.14\times 10^{-15}\ eV\cdot s) (3.00\times 10^8\ m/s)}{5.20\times 10^{-7}\ m} - 1.02\ eV}

W = \rm{2.39\ eV - 1.02\ eV = 1.37\ eV}

This means that the photon has 2.39 eV of energy to transfer to the electron. The electron needs to use at least 1.37 eV to escape from the metal, leaving a maximum of 1.02 eV of kinetic energy.

f_0 = \frac{W}{h} = \rm{\frac{1.37\ eV}{4.14\times 10^{-15}\ eV\cdot s} = 3.31\times 10^{14}\ Hz}

If the source was replaced by light with wavelength 410 nm, what would the new stopping voltage become?

E_k^{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - W

E_k^{max} = \rm{\frac{(4.14\times 10^{-15}\ eV\cdot s) (3.00\times 10^8\ m/s)}{4.10\times 10^{-7}\ m} - 1.37\ eV}

E_k^{max} = \rm{3.03\ eV - 1.37\ eV = 1.66\ eV}

V_{stop} = \frac{E_k^{max}}{q} = \rm{1.66\ V}

Because the shorter wavelength photons have more energy, the photoelectrons escape with more kinetic energy, so the stopping voltage is higher.

Applications

The photoelectric effect gives us a method for detecting the presence of photons above the threshhold frequency.

This has many applications, including:

  • photovoltaic cells
  • infrared remote controls / detectors
  • digital cameras / scanners / photocopiers
  • night vision goggles / thermal imaging
  • remote sensing (elevator doors, robots, satellites)
  • smoke detectors
