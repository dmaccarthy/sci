Thermal (Blackbody) Radiation

Thermal Emission

Maxwell’s theory states that EM waves are produced when charged objects accelerate.

  • Stationery charges produce an electric field but no magnetic field.
  • Steady currents produce steady electric and magnetic fields.
  • Non-steady currents (i.e. accelerating charges) are needed to produce EM waves.
  • Collisions between molecules in dense materials (solids and liquids) are a form of acceleration that emits EM waves.
  • The EMR emitted by colliding molecules is called thermal radiation (or thermal EMR).

Blackbodies

A blackbody is an idealized (100% efficient!) emitter and absorber of EMR.

  • We call such an object a blackbody because it doesn’t reflect any EMR at any wavelength, and thus appears perfectly black when it’s too cold to emit its own visible light.
  • Viewed through a diffraction grating, the spectrum of a thermal EMR source appears as a continuous range of wavelengths.

Light Bulb EM Spectrum

  • The emission spectrum of the Sun seen from space approximates a blackbody.
  • Earth’s atmosphere absorbs some wavelengths more than others, so the solar spectrum is no longer a blackbody when it reaches the ground.

Effect of Temperature

  • If we graph the intensity of the EMR as a function of the wavelength (or frequency) we obtain a graph with a characteristic shape.

T_1 = kelvin     T_2 = kelvin

  • The temperature of the blackbody affects both the horizontal and the vertical position of the graph’s “peak”.
  • Wien’s Law: As the temperature of a blackbody increases, the wavelength of the peak gets shorter.

    \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T}

  • For example, objects at room temperature radiate in the infrared.
  • If an object is heated enough, it will begin to emit visible light, first in the red part of the spectrum, then orange, yellow, etc.
  • Stefan-Boltzmann Law: As the temperature of a blackbody increases, the total amount of EMR increases drastically.

    \frac{P}{A} = \epsilon\sigma T^4

  • The constant \sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{\frac{W}{m^2\cdot K^4}} is called the Stefan-Boltzmann constant.
  • The symbol \epsilon is called the emissivity. It measures how closely an object resembles a blackbody.
  • For an ideal blackbody \epsilon=1, whereas a perfectly reflective object has \epsilon=0.
