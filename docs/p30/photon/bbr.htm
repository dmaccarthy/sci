Lesson Notes

Thermal (Blackbody) Radiation

Thermal Emission

Maxwell’s theory states that EM waves are produced when charged objects accelerate.

  • Stationery charges produce an electric field but no magnetic field.
  • Steady currents produce steady electric and magnetic fields.
  • Non-steady currents (i.e. accelerating charges) are needed to produce EM waves.
  • Collisions between molecules in dense materials (solids and liquids) are a form of acceleration that emits EM waves.
  • The EMR emitted by colliding molecules is called thermal radiation (or thermal EMR).

Blackbodies

A blackbody is an idealized (100% efficient!) emitter and absorber of EMR.

  • We call such an object a blackbody because it doesn’t reflect any EMR at any wavelength, and thus appears perfectly black when it’s too cold to emit its own visible light.
  • Viewed through a diffraction grating, the spectrum of a thermal EMR source appears as a continuous range of wavelengths.

Light Bulb EM Spectrum

  • The emission spectrum of the Sun seen from space approximates a blackbody.
  • Earth’s atmosphere absorbs some wavelengths more than others, so the solar spectrum is no longer a blackbody when it reaches the ground.

Effect of Temperature

  • If we graph the intensity of the EMR as a function of the wavelength (or frequency) we obtain a graph with a characteristic shape.

T_1 = kelvin     T_2 = kelvin

  • The temperature of the blackbody affects both the horizontal and the vertical position of the graph’s “peak”.
  • Wien’s Law: As the temperature of a blackbody increases, the wavelength of the peak gets shorter.

    \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T}

  • For example, objects at room temperature radiate in the infrared.
  • If an object is heated enough, it will begin to emit visible light, first in the red part of the spectrum, then orange, yellow, etc.
  • Stefan-Boltzmann Law: As the temperature of a blackbody increases, the total amount of EMR increases drastically.

    \frac{P}{A} = \varepsilon\sigma T^4

  • The constant \sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{\frac{W}{m^2\cdot K^4}} is called the Stefan-Boltzmann constant.
  • The symbol \varepsilon is called the emissivity. It measures how closely an object resembles a blackbody.
  • For an ideal blackbody \varepsilon=1, whereas a perfectly reflective object has \varepsilon=0.

Practice

  1. Compare and contrast the thermal EMR spectrum of a blackbody at room temperature (about 300 K) to that of a blackbody at 1200 K.

    2. The two spectra have the same shape, but the 1200 K spectrum (as a function of \lambda) is narrower (the peak occurs at one-quarter of the wavelength) and taller by a factor of 1024 (45). The total area of the graph (which represents the total power radiated) is 256 times larger (44) for the higher temperature.

  2. A Physics student argued that since an object at 25000 K has its peak intensity in the ultraviolet, while a 6000 K object peaks in the visible, the 25000 K object emits less visible EMR than the same object at 6000 K. Evaluate this argument.

    3. With higher temperature, the graph gets narrower but it also gets taller. For the 25000 K object, the visible portion of the spectrum is farther from the peak (less visible EMR is emitted as a percentage of the total) but the graph is so much taller that the visible EMR emitted in total is larger.

  3. As it’s heated, an object will glow red, then orange, yellow, white, and blue. Why does an incandescent object never appear green?

    4. When the peak of the spectrum is green, there is only slightly less intensity at red and violet. Thus the EMR is a mixture of all colours in nearly equal proportions, which appears to the eye as white.

  4. The Sun has an “effective temperature” of 5772 K and its EM spectrum peaks at about 502 nm. Use this information to determine the value of the constant b in the Wien’s Law equation. Use the result to calculate the wavelength of peak intensity for a blackbody at:
    1. room temperature (20° C or 293 K)

      2. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T}

      b = \lambda_{peak}T = \rm 5.02\times 10^{-7}\ m\cdot 5772\ K = 2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m

      \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{293\ K} = 9.89\ \mu m

    2. body temperature (37° C or 310 K)

      3. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{310\ K} = 9.35\ \mu m

    3. 10000 K (e.g. a hot star)

      4. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{10000\ K} = 290\ nm

    4. 2.7 K (e.g. intergalactic space)

      5. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{2.7\ K} = 1.1\ mm

    5. 107 K (e.g. near a black hole)

      6. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{10^7\ K} = 0.290\ nm

  5. Determine the temperature of an object that emits thermal EMR most intensely at:
    1. 300 nm

      2. \lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T}

      T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{3.00\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 9.66\times 10^3\ K

    2. 21.0 cm

      3. T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{0.210\ m} = 0.0138\ K

    3. 620 nm

      4. T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{6.20\times 10^{-7}\ m} = 4.67\times 10^3\ K

    4. 3.50 μm

      5. T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{3.50\times 10^{-6}\ m} = 828\ K

    5. 1.00 m

      6. T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{1.00\ m} = 2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K

    6. 1.00 km

      7. T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{peak}} = \rm \frac{2.90\times 10^{-3}\ K\cdot m}{1000\ m} = 2.90\times 10^{-6}\ K

  6. Determine the temperature at which a sphere with a 12.5 cm diameter would emit 1500 W of EMR. Assume the sphere radiates with 65.0% of the efficiency of an ideal blackbody.

    7. Calculate the surface area...

    A = 4\pi r^2 = \rm 4\pi (0.0625\ m)^2 = 0.0491\ m^2

    Solve for T in the Stefan-Boltzmann Law...

    \frac{P}{A} = \varepsilon\sigma T^4

    \frac{P}{A\varepsilon\sigma} = T^4

    T = \left(\frac{P}{A\varepsilon\sigma}\right)^{1/4} = \rm \left(\frac{1500\ W}{0.0491\ m^2\cdot 0.650\cdot 5.67\times 10^{-8}\ W/(m^2\cdot K^4)}\right)^{1/4} = 954\ K

  7. Treating the Earth and the Sun both as blackbodies, calculate the Earth’s “equilibrium temperature”: the temperature at which the radiation received from the Sun just balances the radiation that Earth emits. Take the Sun’s temperature to be 5772 K and its radius to be 6.96 × 108 m. The Earth’s radius is 6370 km and it orbits the Sun with a radius of 1.50 × 1011 m.

    8. Calculate the surface area of the Sun...

    A_{Sun} = 4\pi R_{Sun}^2 = \rm 4\pi (6.96\times 10^8\ m)^2 = 6.09\times 10^{18}\ m^2

    Calculate the total power radiated by the Sun...

    P = \sigma T_{Sun}^4 A_{Sun} = \rm 5.67\times 10^{-8}\ W/(m^2\cdot K^4) \cdot (5772\ K)^4\cdot 6.09\times 10^{18}\ m^2 = 3.83\times 10^{26}\ W

    Only a tiny fraction of this radiation actually reaches Earth. We divide the total power by the surface area over which the radiation spreads, and then multiply by Earth’s cross-sectional area...

    P_{received} = P\frac{{\cancel\pi} R_{Earth}^2}{4{\cancel\pi} r^2} = \rm 3.83\times 10^{26}\ W\cdot \frac{(6.37\times 10^7\ m)^2}{4(1.50\times 10^{11}\ m)^2} = 1.73\times 10^{17}\ W

    Calculate the surface area of the Earth...

    A_{Earth} = 4\pi R_{Earth}^2 = \rm 4\pi (6.37\times 10^7\ m)^2 = 5.10\times 10^{14}\ m^2

    Solve for T in the Stefan-Boltzmann Law, assuming that Earth radiates the same power it receives. We have already done the algebra in question 6...

    T_{Earth} = \left(\frac{P_{received}}{A_{Earth}\sigma}\right)^{1/4} = \rm \left(\frac{1.73\times 10^{17}\ W}{5.10\times 10^{14}\ m^2\cdot 5.67\times 10^{-8}\ W/(m^2\cdot K^4)}\right)^{1/4} = 278\ K

  8. How can astronomers determine the temperature of a start just by observing the light that the star emits?

    9. Measure the wavelength at which the spectrum peaks, and then use Wien’s Law to calculate the temperature!

  9. Use a text or Internet source to find out how “night vision goggles” work.

    10. The goggles take a “photo” in the infrared part of the spectrum and redraw the image using visible colors. Objects that are hotter or colder than room temperature will show in a different color since they emit different wavelengths according to Wien’s Law.
