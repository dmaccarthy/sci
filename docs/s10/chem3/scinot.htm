Scientific Notation

Big Numbers

The radius of Earth’s orbit around the Sun is about:

  • We can avoid writing the zeroes by using powers of 10.
  • Each time we multiply (or divide) by 10, we move the decimal point in the number.
  • Count the number of places the decimal point is moved...

\rm{1.496\times 10^{11}\ m}

  • The exponent tells us that the decimal place has been moved by 11 positions.
  • In scientific notation, the decimal point is always placed after the first non-zero digit.
  • The normal way of writing numbers (without powers of 10) is called decimal notation.

Small Numbers

The electric charge of an electron is:

  • Because the number is very small, we have to move the decimal point in the opposite direction.
  • For small numbers, the power will have a negative exponent in scientific notation.

\rm{1.602\times 10^{-19}\ C}

Calculator Arithmetic

When using a scientific calculator or computer to perform arithmetic on numbers in scientific notation, there is an abbreviation usually represented by a button labelled EE.

1.496\times 10^{11} → 1.496{\small\rm E}11

  • Using the abbreviated notation can prevent “order of operations” mistakes.
  • If typing the power out in full, you will often need brackets for the correct order of operations.
  • Always use full scientific notation in written work.

Example

3.00\times 10^8 \over 6.33\times 10^{-7}

{3.00\times 10^8 \over 6.33\times 10^{-7}} = 4.74\times 10^{14}

Practice

  1. In a hydrogen atom, the electron orbits with a radius of

    \rm{0.000\ 000\ 000\ 052\ 9 m}

    Express this radius using scientific notation.

    2. \rm{5.29 \times 10^{-11}\ m}

  2. Earth has a mass of \rm{5.98\times 10^{24}\ kg}. Express this mass using decimal notation.

    3. \rm{5\ 980\ 000\ 000\ 000\ 000\ 000\ 000\ 000\ kg}

  3. In physics, the kinetic energy of a moving object (measured in joules, J) is calculated using the formula:

    E_k=\frac{1}{2}mv^2

    In this formula, m is the object’s mass in kilograms and v is the object’s speed in metres per second. Calculate the kinetic energy of a \rm 5.00\times 10^{12}\ kg asteroid hurtling towards Earth at \rm 8.20\times 10^4\ m/s. Show all steps!

    4. \begin{align} E_k &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{1}{2}(5.00\times 10^{12}\ kg) (8.20\times 10^4\ m/s)^2} \\[10pt] &= \rm{1.68\times 10^{22}\ J} \end{align}

    1. Calculate how many seconds there are in a year. Express your answer in scientific notation. (Hint: How many days are there in a year? How many hours in a day? How many seconds in an hour?)

      2. \rm 365.24\ days \times 24\ hours/day \times 60\ min/hour \times 60\ s/min = 3.16\times 10^7\ s

    2. In astronomy, a light-year is the distance that light travels in one year. This can be calculated using the formula:

      \Delta d=c\Delta t

      In this formula, c is the speed of light (\rm 3.00\times 10^8\ m/s) and \Delta t is the time in seconds. Use the formula to calculate how far a light-year is in metres.

      \begin{align} \Delta d &= \rm {(3.00\times 10^8\ m/s)\ (3.16\times 10^7\ s)} \\ &= \rm {9.47\times 10^{15}\ m} \end{align}

  5. In geometry, the surface area of a sphere is calculated using the formula:

    A=4\pi r^2

    where r is the radius of the sphere.
    1. Using Earth’s radius of 6.37 × 106 m, calculate the Earth’s surface area. Use the \pi button on your calculator (rather than typing in the value of \pi) to avoid unnecessary rounding errors.

      2. \begin{align} A &= 4\pi r^2 \\ &= 4\pi(\rm{6.37\times 10^6\ m})^2 \\ &= \rm{5.10\times 10^{14}\ m^2} \end{align}

    2. Earth’s atmosphere produces an average air pressure of 101 300 Pa at sea level. The symbol ‘Pa’ stands for pascals, the unit of pressure. Express this air pressure using scientific notation.

      3. \rm 1.013\times 10^5\ Pa

    3. The total weight of Earth’s atmosphere can be calculated by multiplying atmospheric pressure by Earth’s surface area. Calculate the weight (in newtons, N) of Earth’s atmosphere.

      4. \rm{(1.013\times 10^5\ Pa)\ (5.10\times 10^{14}\ m^2) = 5.17\times 10^{19}\ N}

    4. Carbon dioxide plays an important role in the greenhouse effect (which we will study in Unit 4). Carbon dioxide makes up only about 410 parts per million (0.041%) of the molecules in the atmosphere. Each carbon dioxide molecule is about 1.6 times as heavy as the molecules that make up most of the atmosphere, so CO2 represents about 0.066% of the atmosphere’s weight. Calculate the total weight. of CO2 in the atmosphere.

      5. \rm{\frac{0.066\%}{100\%}\ (5.17\times 10^{19}\ N) = 3.41\times 10^{16}\ N}

    5. On Earth, we can convert weight (measured in newtons) to mass (measured in kilograms) by dividing by 9.81 (the strength of Earth’s gravity). What is the total mass of all the CO2 in the atmosphere?

      6. \rm{\frac{3.41\times 10^{16}\ N}{9.81\ N/kg}} = \rm{3.48\times 10^{15}\ kg}

Review

  1. Explain what we mean by decimal notation and scientific notation.

    2. The normal way of writing numbers is called decimal notation. With scientific notation, we move the decimal place so that it immediately follows after the first non-zero digit. We then use a power of 10 top indicate how many places the decimal was moved. This allows us to omit any leading or trailing placeholder zeroes.

  2. Why is scientific notation useful?

    3. It allows us to write very large or very small numbers with fewer digits.

  3. Explain how to use the exponential “shorthand” when using a computer or scientific calculator. How does this shorthand make things easier?

    4. Use the “EE” button to indicate scientific notation. For example, to enter 1.23\times 10^4, type \rm 1.23{\small E}4
