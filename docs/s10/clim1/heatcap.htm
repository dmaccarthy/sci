Lesson Notes

Heat Capacity

Energy & Temperature

We learned about Kinetic-Molecular Theory in the Physics unit.

  • Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance.
  • We can increase the temperature of an object by adding energy (heat).
  • Thermal energy is the total kinetic energy all the particles in a substance.
  • A larger quantity of a substance will require more energy to heat.
  • For example, heating 200 grams of water from 20° to 30° C will require twice as much heat energy as heating 100 grams of water by the same temperature.

Heat Capacity

The ratio of heat added (Q) to the resulting change in temperature (\Delta T) is called the heat capacity:

{Q \over \Delta T} = mc

  • The variable c is called the specific heat capacity.
  • Specific heat capacity is the heat capacity of 1 gram of substance; the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram by 1° C.
  • This equation can be rearranged as:

Q = mc\Delta T

Example 1

Students perform an experiment where they use an electric heater to heat 100 grams of liquid water inside an insulated container. The water temperature increases from 20.0° to 30.0° C as 4186 joules of heat is added. What is the specific heat capacity of water?

  • m = \mathrm{100\ g}
  • \Delta T = \mathrm{30.0°\ C - 20.0°\ C = 10.0°\ C}
  • Q = \mathrm{4186\ J}

Q = mc\Delta T

{Q \over m\Delta T} = c

= \mathrm{{4186\ J \over 100\ g\cdot 10.0°\ C} = 4.19\ {J\over g\cdot °\ C}}

Liquid Water...

Liquid water has a specific heat capacity of 4.19 J/g·°C.

  • This means it takes 4.19 joules of heat energy to increase the temperatue of each gram of water by 1 °C.
  • 100 grams of water would need 100 times as much heat; the heat capacity would be 419 J/°C.
  • Heating the water by 10 °C would require 10 times more energy: 4190 J.

Specific Heat Capacity

Different substances have different specific heat capacities.

  • For example, copper has c = \mathrm{0.385\ J/g\cdot °C}, which is more than 10 times less than liquid water.
  • If all of the heat added went towards increasing the kinetic energy of the molecules, all substances would have the same specific heat capacity.
  • Intermolecular bonds (such as hydrogen bonds) represent a type of potential energy...
  • When the particles move faster, they move apart and “stretch” those bonds, increasing the potential energy.
  • Substances with strong hydrogen bonds (like water) require a lot more energy to heat; they have a much bigger specific heat capacity.
  • Intermolecular bonds are much different is solids, liquids, and gases. The specific heat capacity is different for different phases of matter.

Example 2

A 120 gram block of copper is initially at room temperature of 20.0 °C. An electric heater is used to add 5.00 kilojoules of heat to the copper. Find its final temperature.

  • m = \mathrm{120\ g}
  • T_i = \mathrm{20.0\ °C}
  • c = \mathrm{0.385\ J/g\cdot °C}
  • Q = \mathrm{5000\ J}

Q = mc\Delta T

{Q \over mc} = \Delta T

\Delta T = \mathrm{\frac{5000\ J}{120\ g\cdot 0.385\ J/g\cdot °C} = 108\ °C}

Note that this is the change in the temperature. We still have to find the final temperature...

\Delta T = T_f-T_i

\Delta T + T_i = T_f-T_i + T_i

T_f = \Delta T + T_i = \mathrm{(108 + 20.0)\ °C = 128\ °C}

Practice

  1. How many joules of heat energy are needed to raise the temperature of 450 grams of water from 20.0 °C to 70 °C?

    2. Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{450\ g\cdot 4.19\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 50.0\ °C = 9.43\times 10^4\ J}

    1. A kettle produces 1200 joules of heat per second. How long will it take for 1.00 kilogram of water at 10.0 °C to reach its boiling point (100 °C) in this kettle?

      2. Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{1000\ g\cdot 4.19\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 90.0\ °C = 3.77\times 10^5\ J}

      \Delta t = \rm{\frac{3.77\times 10^5 J}{1200\ J/s} = 314\ s}

    2. Later on, the same kettle is used to heat some water from 10.0° to 37.0 °C. this takes 45.0 seconds. How many grams of water are there in the kettle?

      3. Q = \rm{1200\ J/s \times 45.0\ s = 5.40\times 10^4\ J}

      m = \frac{Q}{c\Delta T} = \rm{\frac{5.40\times 10^4\ J}{4.19\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 27.0\ °C} = 477\ g}

    1. How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 10.0 kilograms of dry air from 18.0 to 21.0 °C?

      2. Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{10000\ g\cdot 1.00\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 3.0\ °C = 3.0\times 10^4\ J}

    2. How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 10.0 kilograms of humid air from 18.0 to 21.0 °C?

      3. Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{10000\ g\cdot 1.15\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 3.0\ °C = 3.5\times 10^4\ J}

  4. Copper has a specific heat capacity of 0.385 J/g·°C.
    1. How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 100 grams of copper from 0.0 °C to 20.0 °C?

      2. Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{100\ g\cdot 0.385\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 20.0\ °C = 770\ J}

    2. How much copper could be heated from 20.0 °C to 80.0 °C by adding 2000 joules of heat?

      3. m = \frac{Q}{c\Delta T} = \rm{\frac{2000\ J}{0.385\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 60.0\ °C} = 86.6\ g}

    3. Calculate the final temperature of 20.0 grams of copper initially at 15.0 °C after adding 400 joules of heat.

      4. \Delta T = \frac{Q}{mc} = \rm{\frac{400\ J}{20.0\ g\cdot 0.385\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C}} = 51.9\ °C}

      T_f = T_i + \Delta T = \rm{15.0\ °C + 51.9\ °C = 66.9\ °C}

  5. The human body has a specific heat capacity of about 3.60 J/g·°C. A student with a mass of 60.0 kilograms generates 1.10 megajoules while playing basketball. If the student’s body did not have mechanisms to dissipate the extra heat, by how much would their body temperature increase?

    6. \Delta T = \frac{Q}{mc} = \rm{\frac{1.10\times 10^6\ J}{60000\ g\cdot 3.60\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C}} = 5.09\ °C}

  6. Students conducted a lab measurement of the specific heat capacity of methanol. They measured out 50.0 grams of methanol and measured its initial temperature to be 19.5 °C. They placed the methanol in an insulated container called a calorimeter and added 800 joules of heat. The final temperature of the methanol was 27.0 °C. Calculate the specific heat capacity of methanol based on the students’ measurements.

    7. c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \rm{\frac{800\ J}{50.0\ g\cdot 7.5\ °C} = 2.13\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C}}

  7. A 50.0-gram block of iron that has been heated to 120 °C is dropped into an insulated beaker containing 84.0 grams of water at 20.0 °C. After a few minutes, the water and iron reach “thermal equilibrium” at 26.0°C. Calculate the specific heat capacity of iron. Hint: The heat lost by the iron must equal the heat gained by the water, according to the First Law of Thermodynamics.

    8. The heat added to the water is...

    Q = mc\Delta T = \rm{84.0\ g\cdot 4.19\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C} \cdot 6.0\ °C = 2.1\times 10^3\ J}

    The heat removed from the iron is equal to...

    c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \rm{\frac{-2.1\times 10^3\ J}{50.0\ g\cdot (-94.0\ °C)} = 0.449\ \frac{J}{g\cdot °C}}

Review

    1. Define heat capacity.

      2. The amount of heat (energy) that must be added to a substance to raise its temperature by 1 °C.

    2. Define specific heat capacity.

      3. The amount of heat (energy) that must be added to one gram of a substance to raise its temperature by 1 °C.

    1. What is the specific heat capacity of liquid water? Interpret this number; i.e. what does it mean?

      2. 4.19 J/g·°C. This means it takes 4.19 joules of heat to raise the temperature of each gram of water by 1 °C.

    2. Write the equation relating specific heat capacity to temperature change.

      3. Q = mc\Delta T

    3. Explain what each of the variables (Q, m, c, \Delta T) in the equation means. Also state what unit is used for each variable.
      • Q is the amount of heat energy (joules) added.
      • m is the mass (grams) of the material being heated.
      • c is the specific heat capacity (J/g·°C).
      • \Delta T is the change in temerature (°C).
