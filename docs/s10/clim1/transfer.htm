Heat Transfer

Heat Transfer

Heat flows from higher to lower temperatures. There are three main mechanisms of heat transfer:

  • Conduction
  • Convection
  • Radiation

Conduction

According to Kinetic-Molecular Theory, temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles.

  • When two objects at different temperatures are in contact with each other, the particles will collide with each other.
  • On average, the higher energy (hotter) particles lose energy and the lower energy (colder) particles gain energy.
  • There is a flow of heat from hot to cold.
  • This mechanism of heat transfer is called conduction.
  • Conduction occurs in solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Some materials, like most metals, are better conductors of heat.
  • Insulators, like styrofoam and fibreglass, are poor conductors of heat.

Convection

In fluids (liquids and gases), heat can be transported via convection.

  • Most fluids expand (become less dense) when heated.
  • Hotter regions will be more buoyant and will rise.
  • Colder parts of the fluid will be drawn in to replace the rising hotter fluid.
  • The net result of this effect will be a convection current.

Radiation

Objects that are really hot emit light and other forms of electromagnetic radiation.

  • Since light is a form of energy, radiation results in a decrease of temperature.
  • When the light strikes another object, that object gains energy.
  • At the temperature of the Sun (5800 K), objects radiate all colours of the rainbow.

  • At even hotter temperatures, there is more radiation produced at the violet end of the spectrum and into the untraviolet.
  • At lower temperatures, less violet light is produced and the light appears orange or red.
  • Room temperature objects emit infrared radiation; radiation like light but with a wavelength to long to be visible.

Heat & Climate

  • Radiation received from the Sun adds heat to the Earth’s climate system.
  • Radiation emitted by the Earth removes heat to the Earth’s climate system.
  • Convection currents in the atmosphere and the oceans play an important role in distributing heat around the Earth.

Review

    1. Explain what conduction is and give examples of heat transfer by conduction.
      2. Conduction is the transfer of heat by contact between materials. When the molecules of the hotter object collide with molecules of the colder object, there is a net transfer of thermal energy.
    2. Explain how convection works.
      3. When a fluid (liquid or gas) is heated, it usually expands to become less dense that the surrounding fluid. The warmer fluid rises and is replaced by cooler fluid. These convection currents help distribute heat more effectively.
    3. Explain how radiation transfers heat.
      4. Hot objects emit electromagnetic radiation which carries away energy. When the EMR is absorbed by another object the energy is absorbed. The type of EMR depends on the temperature of the emitting object. Objects close to room temperature emit infrared radiation, while objects near the temperature of the Sun emit visible light.

Assignment

  1. Describe Earth’s hydrologic cycle.
    2. Water evaporates from oceans and through transpiration from plants. This water condenses into clouds and returns to the ground as precipitation. The water collects in streams, lakes, and rivers which return it to the sea.
  2. Describe the role of ocean currents in distributing heat on Earth.
    3. Oceans carry warmer water from the equatorial regions to the polar regions, and return cooler water from the poles to the equator.
  3. Explain how El Nino and La Nina contribute to variations in Earth’s surface temperature.
    4. These are changes to the South Pacific wind and ocean currents that results in heating or cooling of the surface water. This affects the weather (rain, temperature) in coastal regions of the Pacific.
    1. Discuss the Three-Cell Model of air convection (wind).

      2. Heated air near the equator rises and flows away from the equator, being replaced at ground level by cooler air moving toward the equator. Near the poles, cool air sinks to the ground and also flows toward the equatior. In the mid-latitudes, the direction is reversed, with warmer air moving along the ground away from the equator.

    2. Why do we have prevailing westerlies throughout much of Canada?
      3. The Coriolis effect means that currents moving north or south experience an apparent shift to the east or west due to the rotation of the Earth, which is slower at the poles and faster at the equator. Since ground air currents tend to be moving north in the mid-latitudes, the Coriolis effect produces a west-to-east motion.
    3. Describe the jet stream.
      4. The jet stream is a current of fast moving air along the boundary between warm and cool air masses.
  5. Explain how air convection near mountains affects weather and why water’s very high specific heat capacity is relevant.

    6. Air cools as it rises and the moisture it contains condenses to produce precipitation on the windward side of the mountains. The air on the leeward side tends to be dryer.

    In coastal areas, the high heat capacity of the ocean moderates air temperatures. Warmer air over land during the day rises and is replaced by cooler sea breeze air. At night a land breeze flows from land (cooler) to sea (warmer).

  6. Explain how a land breeze and a sea breeze works.

    7. During the day, the land heats up more than the ocean. Air above the land rises and convection draws cooler air along the ground from the sea to the land. This is a sea breeze.

    At night, the land cools more than the ocean. Air above the land sinks and convection moves the cooler air along the ground from land to sea. This is a land breeze.
