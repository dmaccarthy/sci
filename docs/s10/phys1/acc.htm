Acceleration

We learned in earlier lessons that velocity is the rate at which an object’s position changes.

\vec{\bf v} = {\Delta\vec{\bf d}\over \Delta t}

  • Velocity equals the slope of a graph of \vec{\bf d} versus t.

Acceleration measures how quickly the velocity changes:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t}

  • Acceleration is a vector.
  • The SI unit of acceleration is (m/s)/s, or m/s2.
  • An acceleration of +1.0 m/s2 means that each second, the velocity changes by 1 m/s in the positive direction.
  • On Earth, when there are no forces except gravity, the acceleration will be –9.81 m/s2.

  • If we graph the velocity against time, the slope will equal the acceleration.
  • We cannot directly calculate the acceleration from a position versus time graph, because the graph will be curved for accelerated motion.

Example 1

Calculate the acceleration of the ball based on the graph.

Identify the variables from the graph:

  • t_i = \rm{0.00\ s}
  • t_f = \rm{2.00\ s}
  • \vec{\bf v}_i = \rm{0.00\ m/s}
  • \vec{\bf v}_f = \rm{-19.5\ m/s}
  • \vec{\bf a} =\ ?

Select the relevant relationship (equation) and evaluate:

\vec{\bf a} = {\Delta \vec{\bf v}\over \Delta t} = \rm{-19.5\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s \over 2.00\ s - 0.00\ s} = \rm{-9.75\ m/s^2}

  • Is the answer reasonable?

Example 2

A car is travelling at 60.0 km/h. How long will it take to accelerate to 80.0 km/h is the car can accelerate at a rate of 3.00 m/s2?

Identify the variables and convert to standard SI units:

  • \vec{\bf v}_i = \rm{+60.0\ km/h = +16.66667\ m/s}
  • \vec{\bf v}_f = \rm{+80.0\ km/h = +22.22222\ m/s}
  • \vec{\bf a} = \rm{+3.00\ m/s^2}
  • \Delta t =\ ?

Select the relevant relationship (equation):

\vec{\bf a} = {\Delta \vec{\bf v}\over \Delta t}

Rearrange the equation to isolate the unknown:

\vec{\bf a}{\color{red}{\Delta t}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t} {\color{red}{\Delta t}}

\vec{\bf a}\Delta t = \Delta \vec{\bf v}

{\vec{\bf a}\Delta t \over \color{red}{\vec{\bf a}}} = {\Delta \vec{\bf v} \over \color{red}{\vec{\bf a}}}

\Delta t = {\Delta \vec{\bf v} \over \vec{\bf a}}

Evaluate, including units:

\Delta t = \rm{+22.22222\ m/s - (+16.66667\ m/s) \over +3.00\ m/s^2}

\Delta t = \rm{1.85\ s}

  • Is the answer reasonable?

Practice

  1. If an object is accelerating does its velocity have to be changing? Does its speed have to be changing? Explain.
    2. Since acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, the velocity has to be changing for an object to be accelerating. In other words, if \Delta\vec{\bf v} = 0 then \vec{\bf a} will always be 0. The speed does not have to change. For example, if the velocity changes from +5 m/s to –5 m/s, \Delta\vec{\bf v} = –10 m/s so the acceleration is not zero even though the speed is unchanged.
  2. A cyclist is coasting at 5.00 m/s. The cyclist begins to accelerate as they go down a hill, reaching a velocity of 8.50 m/s in 2.50 seconds. Calculate the cyclist’s acceleration.

    3. \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(8.50 - 5.00)\ m/s}{2.50\ s}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+1.40\ m/s^2} \end{align}

  3. A skateboarder is moving at +2.00 m/s when she starts to accelerate at +1.20 m/s2 as she descends a hill. What will her velocity be after 4.00 seconds of acceleration?

    4. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t}

    \Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf a} \Delta t = \rm{+1.20\ m/s^2\cdot 4.00\ s = +4.80\ m/s}

    Remember that this is the change in velocity. You still have to calculate the final velocity...

    \Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i

    \vec{\bf v}_f = \Delta\vec{\bf v} + \vec{\bf v}_i = \rm{+4.80\ m/s + 2.00\ m/s = +6.80\ m/s}

  4. A student throws a rock up from the base of a cliff with an initial velocity of +5.00 m/s. The rock lands on the top of the cliff with a final velocity of –2.50 m/s. The acceleration of the rock (caused by gravity) is –9.81 m/s2. Calculate the time for which the rock was in motion.

    5. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t}

    \vec{\bf a} \Delta t = \Delta\vec{\bf v}

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{(-2.50 - (+5.00))\ m/s}{-9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.765\ s}

  7. Crime scene investigators determine that a bullet takes 0.275 seconds to come to a stop when fired into water. The water causes the bullet to accelerate at –2000 m/s2. What was the initial velocity of the bullet before entering the water?

    \Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf a} \Delta t = \rm{-2000\ m/s^2\cdot 0.275\ s = -550\ m/s}

    Remember that this is the change in velocity. You still have to calculate the final velocity...

    \Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i

    \vec{\bf v}_i = \vec{\bf v}_f - \Delta\vec{\bf v} = \rm{0.00\ m/s - (-550\ m/s) = +550\ m/s}

  8. The spaceship Millenium Falcon is cruising at a velocity of +4.20 km/s. To avoid hostile Imperial fighters, the Falcon must reach a velocity of +8.75 km/s. The maximum acceleration that the Millenium Falcon can achieve is +200 m/s2. How long will it take the Falcon to reach a safe velocity?

    9. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t}

    \vec{\bf a} \Delta t = \Delta\vec{\bf v}

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{(+8750 - (+4200))\ m/s}{+200\ m/s^2} = 22.8\ s}

Review

    1. Define and give the formula for acceleration.
      2. Acceleration is the rate at which the velocity changes:

      \vec{\bf{a}}=\frac{\Delta\vec{\bf{v}}}{\Delta t}

    2. What does acceleration mean? For example, if a car has an acceleration of +2.0 m/s2, what does this tell you about its motion?
      3. It means that the car’s velocity changes by +2.0 m/s each second. If a car is moving at +20.0 m/s, its velocity would be +22.0 m/s one second later, and then +24.0 m/s after another second, and so on.
  2. Explain the connection between the acceleration equation and the slope equation. Why is this important?
    3. If we have a graph of velocity versus time, \Delta\vec{\bf v} will be the “rise” and \Delta t will be the “run”, so \vec{\bf a} will be the slope. This means we can tell whether the motion is accelerated, whether the acceleration is constant or not, and whether it is positive or negative just be looking at the graph.
    1. What will an object’s acceleration be on Earth, when it is rising or falling and there are no forces acting on it except for gravity?
      2. –9.81 m/s2. This situation almost never occurs, due to air resistance. We would need to conduct an experiment in a chamber where all the air had been pumped out to measure this value.
    2. Will the acceleration be the same on Mars? In a spacecraft orbiting high above Earth? Explain.
      3. Gravity is weaker on Mars than Earth because Mars is smaller. Gravity also gets weaker as we get farther away from a planet, so in both of these case the acceleration would be less than 9.81 m/s2.
    3. What effect does the atmosphere have on moving objects?
      4. The presence of air, water, or any other fluid means that the moving object needs to push the fluid out of its way as it moves. This creates a force that acts to slow the object’s motion. Because of this effect, the actual acceleration of an object moving under the influence of Earth’s gravity will not be exactly 9.81 m/s2.
