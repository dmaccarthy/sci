SI Units

What are Units?

A unit is a standard of comparison for measurements.

  • To measure the length of an object, we compare its length to the markings on a ruler.

  • For the measurement to make sense to others, we need to use a standardized distance between markings such as centimetres or inches.
  • The definition of each unit is arbitrary.

Fundamental Units

Scientists have agreed that the official unit for measuring distances will be the metre, {\rm m}.

    A metre is officially defined as a certain number of wavelengths of a certain colour of light.

The Système Internationale uses seven fundamental units (also called base units) upon which all other units are based.

Derived Units

Fundamental units can be combined to make new units. These are known as derived units.

  • To measure velocity, we use \rm{m/s}.
  • To measure density, we use \rm{kg/m^3}.

Sometimes derived units are given their own name and symbol.

  • The SI unit of force is called the newton:

    \rm{N = kg \cdot {m\over s^2}}

  • Other derived units with names include: joule, watt, pascal, volt.

SI Prefixes

When an SI unit is too small to be practical, we can use prefixes to create larger units.

  • SI prefixes always change the size of units based on powers of 10.
  • This is much simpler than having to remember 12 inches in a foot, 3 feet in a yard, and 1760 yards in a mile.

Unit Conversions

The factor-label method of unit conversion can be used whenever we want to replace a unit by a different but equivalent unit...

Example 1

Convert \rm{25.0\ \mu m} to \rm m.

We know that \rm{1\ \mu m = 10^{-6}\ m}.

Write the measurement in scientific notation, and multiply the measurement by a fraction with the two equivalent measurements as the numerator and denominator...

\rm{25.0\ \mu m = 2.50\times 10^1\ \mu m\ \color{red}{\left(\frac{10^{-6}\ m}{1\ \mu m}\right)}}

  • Decide the “top” and “bottom” so that the original unit cancels.

\begin{align} \rm{25.0\ \mu m} &= \rm{2.50\times 10^1\ \bcancel{\mu m}\ \color{red}{\left(\frac{10^{-6}\ m}{1\ \bcancel{\mu m}}\right)}} \\[8pt]&= \rm{2.50\times 10^{-5}\ m} \end{align}

Example 2

Convert \rm{5.00\times 10^{-7} s} to \rm {ns}.

\rm{1\ ns = 10^{-9}\ s}...

\begin{align} \rm{5.00\times 10^{-7}\ s} &= \rm{5.00\times 10^{-7}\ \bcancel{s}\ \color{red}{\left(\frac{1\ ns}{10^{-9}\ \bcancel{s}}\right)}} \\[8pt]&= \rm{5.00\times 10^2\ ns}\end{align}

Example 3

Convert \rm{1.50\ mW/cm^2} to \rm{W/m^2}.

\rm{1\ mW = 10^{-3}\ W} and \rm{1\ cm = 10^{-2}\ m}...

\begin{align} \rm{1.50\ \frac{mW}{cm^2}} &= \rm{1.50\ \frac{\bcancel{mW}}{\bcancel{cm^2}} \color{red}{\left(\frac{10^{-3}\ W}{1\ \bcancel{mW}}\right)} \color{#0065fe}{\left(\frac{1\ \bcancel{cm}}{10^{-2}\ m}\right)^2}} \\[12pt]&= \rm{1.50\ \frac{10^{-3}\ W}{10^{-4}\ m^2}} \\[12pt]&= \rm{15.0\ W/m^2} \end{align}

  • When a unit is squared or cubed, you must square or cube the factor-label fraction too!

Example 4

Convert \rm{80.0\ km/h} to \rm{m/s}.

\rm{1\ km = 10^3\ m} and \rm{1\ h = 3600\ s}...

\begin{align} \rm{80.0\frac{km}{h}} &= \rm{80.0\frac{\bcancel{km}}{\bcancel{h}}\ \color{red}{\left(\frac{10^3\ m}{1\ \bcancel{km}}\right)} \color{#0065fe}{\left(\frac{\bcancel{h}}{3600\ s}\right)}} \\[12pt]&= \rm{22.2\ m/s}\end{align}

  • A shortcut for converting km/h to m/s is to divide by 3.6.

Review

  1. What is a unit? Why are units important in science?
    2. A unit is a standard of measurement that scientists have agreed to use. Standardized units allow scientists to share their measurements with each other in a precise and reliable way.
  2. What does SI stand for? What does it mean?
    3. SI is the Système Internationale, or International System of Units. It is the set of units used by scientists.
  3. State the 7 fundamental SI units and what quantity each unit measures.
    4. metres (distance), amperes (electric current), seconds (time), kilograms (mass), kelvins (temperature), moles (quantity of matter), candelas (luminous intensity)
  4. What is a derived unit?
    5. Derived units are units that are made by combining fundamental units (like m/s for speed or kg/m3 for density) or adding a prefix to make a larger or smaller unit (like microseconds). Sometimes derived units are given their own name. For example, kg·m/s2 is called a newton. Other named derived units are joules, watts, pascals, volts, coulombs, and teslas.
  5. Explain how prefixes can be used to make larger or smaller units.

    6. Prefixes are based on powers of 10. For example, kilo- means 103 or 1000 and mega- means 106 or one million. Adding these prefixes makes a larger unit; one megawatt equals one mmillion watts.

    Other prefixes make smaller units. For example, micro- means 10–6 or one-millionth. A microsecond is one millionth of a second.

  6. Explain (with examples) how to convert units using the factor-label method.

    7. Decide what the equivalence is between the unit you are trying to remove and the unit you are converting to. Then multiply the original measurement by a fraction where the numerator and denominator are the equivalent units. Decide which unit goes “on top” and which goes “underneath” so that the unit being removed cancels. If a unit is squared or cubed, you must do the same to the fraction before you can cancel.

    Example: Convert 1.50 g/cm3 to kg/m3...

    Answer: Since \mathrm{kg = 10^3\ g} and \mathrm{cm = 10^{-2}\ m}...

    \mathrm{1.50\ \frac{g}{cm^3} = 1.50\ \frac{g}{cm^3} \color{#0065fe}{\left(\frac{kg}{10^3\ g}\right) \left(\frac{cm}{10^{-2}\ m}\right)^3} = 1.50\times 10^3\ kg/m^3}
