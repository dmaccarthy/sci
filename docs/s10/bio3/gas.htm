Lesson Notes

Gas Exchange

Gas Exchange

Gases (CO2, O2, and H2O) are important for photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

  • Cellular respiration uses O2 and makes CO2 and H2O.
  • Photosynthesis uses CO2 and H2O, and produces O2.
  • Plants consume more CO2 than they produce.
  • Animals exchange gases using lungs and blood.
  • Plants exchange gases by passive transport as the stomata allow gases to diffuse in and out.
  • CO2 diffuses into the leaf through the stomata.
  • It then diffuses into the spongy mesophyll and palisade tissue where it is used for photosynthesis.
  • The concentration gradient is created by photosynthesis which consumes CO2 inside the leaf, resulting in a lower concentration than in the air outside.
  • Photosynthesis produces oxygen, resulting in a higher concentration inside the leaf.
  • O2 diffuses out of the leaf through the stomata.

Lenticels

Woody plants such as trees with bark have special openings called lenticels which allow for gas exchange.

  • Lenticels appear as white spots.

Transpiration

Transpiration is the evaporation of water from leaves.

  • Plants can lose up to 99% of the water taken up by the roots by transpiration.
  • An average maple tree can lose 200 L of water per hour due to transpiration in summer.
  • Just as animals sweat to keep cool, transpiration carries away heat energy and can cool the leaf by 10° C to prevent heat damage.
  • Transpiration occurs via diffusion through the stomata, just as with CO2 and O2.
  • When conditions are dry, the guard cells shrivel (plasmolysis), causing the stomata to close, and preventing more water loss.

  • In wetter conditions, ncreased turgor pressure expands the guard cells, causing the stomata to open, and allowing more transpiration.
