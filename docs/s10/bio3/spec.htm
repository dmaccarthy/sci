If we doubled the size of a cell...

  • The volume would increase by a factor of eight.
  • The cell would require 8 times as much energy and raw materials and would need to get rid of 8 times as much waste.
  • The cell membrane would only be four times larger.
  • The cell membrane would need to work twice as hard!
  • The larger the cell becomes, the more difficult it becomes for the membrane to provide enough raw materials and eliminate waste.
  • If a cell was the size of a person, it would take a long time for oxygen and carbon dioxide to diffuse to or from the centre of the cell.
  • This places a practical limit on how large a single cell can be!

Multicellular Organisms

Life has evaded the cell-size limit by forming multicellular organisms; organisms that consist of many cells.

  • A human body contains on the order of 1013 cells.
  • Advantages of multicellular organisms include:
    • Larger size
    • Cell specialization
    • Ability to survive a variety of environments

Specialization

Cell specialization means that in a multicellular organism, different cells perform different functions.

  • Specialized cells in humans and other animals include: nerve cells, muscle cells, fat cells, red and white blood cells, and more.
  • The illustration below shows some of the specialized cells and structures in a leaf...

  • The upper and lower epidermal cells form a protective layer, one cell thick, on the upper and lower surface of the leaf.
  • Epidemal cells produce a waxy cuticle (not made of living cells!) that coats the leaf to prevent excess water loss.
  • Below the upper epidermis are tightly packed palisade tissue cells. These are the primary site of photosynthesis, where the greatest amount of sunlight is available.
  • The spongy mesophyll is located below the palisade cells. These cells are looslely packed to allow the gases involved in photosynthesis (water, carbon dioxide, and oxygen) to easily circulate.
  • Stomata are small openings in the lower epidermis that allows CO2 in for photosynthesis and O2 out.
  • The stomata are formed by a pair of guard cells.
  • Note: There is an error in the labelling of the leaf picture in your textbook. The stomata are the openings between the guard cells. The stomata lines on the picture actually point to guard cells.
  • In dry conditions, the guard cells shrivel (plasmolysis) to close the opening and reduce water loss.
  • In wetter conditions, the guard cells expand; the increased turgor pressure changes the shape of the cells to create the opening, allowing more water vapour to leave the leaf.
  • The leaf also contains two types of vascular tissue often found together in a vascular bundle...
  • The xylem are the cell walls of dead cells, that form tubes allowing water and minerals (xylem sap) to flow into the leaf from the roots.
  • The phloem are living cells that carry the sugary sap produced by photosynthesis in the leaf to other parts of the plant.

Levels of Organizations

  • Tissues are groups of similar cells.
  • Organs are tissues of different types that work together to perform some useful function for the organism.
  • Systems are groups of organs/tissues that together meet some essential need of the organism.

Review

  1. Explain the difference between unicellular and multicellular organisms.

    2. Unicellular organisms consist of a single cell that must perform all of the organism’s functions. Multicellular organisms consist of many cells that are specialized to perform only certain of life’s functions.

  2. Define:
    1. tissue
    2. organ
    3. system

    3. tissue: Cells of a similar type and function (e.g. muscle, palisade cells)

    organ: Different tissues that work together to perform a specific function(s) for the organism (e.g. liver, leaf)

    system: One or more organs and tissues that function together to meet the organism’s needs (e.g. vascular or circulatory system)
