Lesson Notes

Water Transport

Absorption of Water

A plant’s system for transporting water and nutrients is called the vascular system.

  • Water enters the plant through osmosis, from the soil into cells in the root.
  • Minerals will enter via passive or active transport.
  • Root hairs are outgrowths from root epidermal cells that increase the surface area of the cell, making absorption more effective.

Xylem

Water and minerals move from the root cells into the xylem.

  • Xylem are dead tracheid cells that form hollow tubes.

  • The water and minerals mixture is called xylem sap.
  • As water is needed for photosynthesis, the water must reach the leaves where most photosynthesis takes place.
  • For large plants (e.g. maple trees), transporting water large distances against gravity is an issue.
  • Forces that assist the upward motion of water are:
    1. Root pressure: The turgor pressure of the expanded root cells pushing the water up the xylem. This is adequate for small plants but not for large trees.
    2. Cohesion: The tendency of molecules of the same type to stick to each other. This is strong in water due to its hydrogen bonds.
    3. Adhesion: The tendency of moleules to stick to other molecules, like water to the cellulose of the xylem tubes.
    4. Transpiration: (See below)

Transpiration

Transpiration is the evaporation of water in the leaf, resulting in diffusion of water vapour out through the stomata.

  • Most of the water taken in through the roots (> 97%) is “lost” through transpiration.
  • Less than 3% of the water intake is acutally used for photosynthesis.
  • Because of the strong cohesion between water molecules, the evaporating water molecules exert a force on the chain of water molecules behind them, creating a force that helps pull the water up the plant.

Phloem

Once photosynthesis has occured in the leaf, the sugar produced must be trasported to the rest of the plant.

  • Sugar transport occurs in living cells called phloem.
  • The sugar mixture being transported is called phloem sap, or just sap.
  • The sugary sap is used to make maple syrup, for example.
  • Phloem tissue contains seive tubes through which the sap moves.

  • Sugar is moved into the seive tube elements (cells) by active transport in the leaf.
  • Water follows by osmosis, increasing the pressure which allows the sap to be pushed into the adjacent cells through seive plates.
  • Seive tube elements contain few organelles, and their metabolic functions are performed by nearby companion cells.
  • Xylem and phloem are often found together in vascular bundles.

Review

  1. Define and explain the function of:
    1. xylem

      2. Hollow tubes formed by the cell walls of dead cells that transport water and minerals (xylem sap) from the roots to the leaves.

    2. phloem

      3. Living cells that transport glucose-containing sap from the leaves to the rest of the plant.

    3. vascular bundle

      4. A structure in the plant containing the xylem and phloem.

    4. root hair

      5. An extension from some root cells that increases the surface area, allowing more water and nutrients to be absorbed.

  2. Identify and describe the four processes that occur together to move xylem sap up the plant.
