Work-Energy Theorem

We saw in the last lesson that the amount of mechanical energy that a force transforms is called the work done.

An important Physics concepts is the Work-Energy Theorem, which allows us to calculate work using this equation:

W=\Delta E_k=\vec{\bf{F}}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}

  • If you were to push an object 2.0 metres by applying a force of 10 newtons in the same direction that it is moving, you would be doing 20 joules of work.
  • If this happened in deep space where there were no other forces acting, the object would gain 20 joules of kinetic energy.
  • On Earth, we would need to account for the work being done by gravity, friction, and any other forces before we could say how much kinetic energy was gained.

Example 1

The kinetic energy of a wagon increases from 10 J to 25 J as a student pulls it 3.0 metres. How much force is the student applying?

Determine how much work was done by calculating the change in kinetic energy:

W=\Delta E_k= \rm{25\ J - 10\ J = 15\ J}

Rearrange the work-energy equation to find force:

W = \vec{\bf{F}}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}

\vec{\bf F} = {W \over \Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{{15\ J\over +3.0\ m} = +5.0\ N}

  • This answer assumes that there are no other forces affecting the energy.
  • If the wagon is on a hill, or if there is friction or air resistance, the answer would be different.

Force Not Constant?

Using the work-energy equation requires a constant amount of force:

W = \vec{\bf{F}}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}

  • If the force is not constant, we can calculate work as the area under a graph of \vec{\bf F} vs \vec{\bf d}.
  • This calculation effectively averages the force. (Examples p155-156.)

Example 2

When a tennis racket hits a ball, the force increases as the racket strings stretch, and then decreases as it unstretches. How much work does the racket do on the ball?

For the first 5.0 cm of the motion, the graph is triangular:

W = \rm \frac{Base × Height}{2} = \frac{0.050\ m × 35.0\ N}{2} = 0.875\ J

For the next 2.0 cm (from 5.0 to 7.0), the graph is rectangular:

W = \rm Base × Height = 0.020\ m × 35.0\ N = 0.700\ J

For the last 3.0 cm (from 7.0 to 10.0), the graph is triangular:

W = \rm \frac{Base × Height}{2} = \frac{0.030\ m × 35.0\ N}{2} = 0.525\ J

The total work done (kinetic energy gained!) is:

\Sigma W = \rm (0.875 + 0.700 + 0.525)\ J = 2.1\ J

Practice

  1. A baseball is moving with 24.0 joules of kinetic energy. The batter hits the ball, after which the baseball’s kinetic energy is 82.0 J.
    1. How much work did the batter do on the baseball?

      2. W = \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = \rm{82.0\ J - 24.0\ J = 58.0\ J}

    2. What was the average force of the bat acting on the ball, if the bat and ball were in contact with each other through a displacement of 1.40 metres?

      3. W = \vec{\bf F}_{avg} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

      \vec{\bf F}_{avg} = \frac{W}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{\frac{58.0\ J}{1.40\ m} = 41.4\ N}

    1. A hockey player exerts an average force of 9.40 newtons on the puck. The puck moves through a displacement of 40.0 cm while in contact with the hockey stick. How much work did the player do on the puck?

      2. W = \vec{\bf F} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm{9.40\ N \cdot 0.400\ m = 3.76\ J}

    2. If the puck was moving with a kinetic energy of 3.50 J before the hockey stick made contact, what was the puck’s kinetic energy after being shot?

      3. W = \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki}

      E_{kf} = E_{ki} + W = \rm{3.50\ J + 3.76\ J = 7.26\ J}

  3. As a block slides 85.0 cm down a ramp, friction causes the block to lose 75.0 J of mechanical energy. Calculate the force of friction.

    4. W = \vec{\bf F} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

    \vec{\bf F} = \frac{W}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{\frac{-75.0\ J}{0.850\ m} = -88.2\ N}

  4. The graph below shows the force exerted by one bumper car on another during a collision.

    1. Calculate the work experienced by the car.

      2. W = \rm{Area = \frac{1}{2} \times Base \times Height = \frac{1}{2} (0.40\ m) (360\ N) = 72\ J}

    2. If the bumper car was moving with 300 J of kinetic energy before the collision, how much kinetic energy would it have after the collision?

      3. W = \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki}

      E_{kf} = E_{ki} + W = \rm{300\ J + 72\ J = 372\ J}

  5. A 65.0 kg cyclist is riding at a speed of 4.20 m/s. The cyclist pedals harder, causing her speed to increase to 6.35 m/s.
    1. Calculate the cyclist’s initial kinetic energy.

      2. E_{ki} = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (65.0\ kg)(4.20\ m/s)^2 = 573\ J}

    2. Calculate the cyclist’s final kinetic energy.

      3. E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (65.0\ kg)(6.35\ m/s)^2 = 1310\ J}

    3. How much work did the cyclist do while accelerating?

      4. W = \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = \rm{1310\ J - 573\ J= 737\ J}

    4. Estimate the average force that the cyclist exerted if the pedals moved 4.25 metres while she was accelerating.

      5. W = \vec{\bf F} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

      \vec{\bf F} = \frac{W}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{\frac{737\ J}{4.25\ m} = 173\ N}

  6. A 32.5 kg child climbs into his toboggan at the top of a hill that is 5.60 m high and 25.3 m long. Gravity causes the child to begin sliding down the hill. The speed of the child and toboggan at the bottom of the hill is 7.20 m/s.
    1. Calculate the mechanical energy of the child at the top of the hill.

      2. E_i = mgh_i = \rm{(32.5\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (5.60\ m) = 1785\ J}

    2. Calculate the mechanical energy of the child at the bottom of the hill.

      3. E_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (32.5\ kg)(7.20\ m/s)^2= 842\ J}

    3. Calculate the average force of friction acting on the child.

      4. W = \Delta E = \rm{842\ J - 1785\ J = -943\ J}

      \vec{\bf F} = \frac{W}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{\frac{-943\ J}{25.3\ m} = -37.3\ N}

Review

    1. State the work-energy theorem and explain what it means.
      2. Work (the change in mechanical energy caused by a force) is equal to the force multiplied by the object’s displacement:

      W = \vec{\bf F} \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d}

    2. Explain how to calculate the work done by a force that is not constant.
      3. Calculate the area under a graph of \vec{\bf F} versus \vec{\bf d}.
