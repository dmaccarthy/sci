Heat Transfer

Heat Transfer

Heat flows from higher to lower temperatures. There are three main mechanisms of heat transfer:

  • Conduction
  • Convection
  • Radiation

Conduction

According to Kinetic-Molecular Theory, temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles.

  • When two objects at different temperatures are in contact with each other, the particles will collide with each other.
  • On average, the higher energy (hotter) particles lose energy and the lower energy (colder) particles gain energy.
  • There is a flow of heat from hot to cold.
  • This mechanism of heat transfer is called conduction.
  • Conduction occurs in solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Some materials, like most metals, are better conductors of heat.
  • Insulators, like styrofoam and fibreglass, are poor conductors of heat.

Convection

In fluids (liquids and gases), heat can be transported via convection.

  • Most fluids expand (become less dense) when heated.
  • Hotter regions will be more buoyant and will rise.
  • Colder parts of the fluid will be drawn in to replace the rising hotter fluid.
  • The net result of this effect will be a convection current.

Radiation

Objects that are really hot emit light and other forms of electromagnetic radiation.

  • Since light is a form of energy, radiation results in a decrease of temperature.
  • When the light strikes another object, that object gains energy.
  • At the temperature of the Sun (5800 K), objects radiate all colours of the rainbow.

  • At even hotter temperatures, there is more radiation produced at the violet end of the spectrum and into the untraviolet.
  • At lower temperatures, less violet light is produced and the light appears orange or red.
  • Room temperature objects emit infrared radiation; radiation like light but with a wavelength to long to be visible.

Systems

An open system is one that allows both energy and matter into and out of the system.

  • A mug of hot chocolate would be classified as an open system.
  • You can add energy by microwaving it.
  • You can remove energy by allowing it to transfer heat into the air.
  • You can add matter by adding marshmallows or more water.
  • You can remove matter by drinking some.

A closed system is one that allows energy into and out of the system but not matter.

  • Earth is very close to being a closed system.
  • Radiation from the Sun adds energy.
  • Earth emits its own radiation which removes energy.

An isolated system allows neither energy nor mass to enter or leave the system.

  • The only truly isolated system is the universe in its entirety.
  • Scientists sometimes use the concept of an isolated as an idealization.

Energy Budget

For Earth’s climate system, the heat added is almost exclusively radiation (light, UV, IR) from the Sun.

The heat lost is almost exclusively infrared radiation from Earth into space.

Convection currents in the oceans and atmosphere are responsible for distributing heat around the globe.

According to the First Law of Thermodynamics, if the heat added does not exactly balance the heat lost, there will be a change in the temperature of the system.

Earth’s radiation budget is an accounting of where the incoming energy goes, and where the outgoing radiation comes from...

Greenhouse Effect

The Sun (5800 K) is nealy 20 times hotter than the Earth (300 K).

  • This means that the radiation emitted by Earth is very different from radiation emitted by the Sun.
  • The Sun emits mostly visible light, with some infrared and ultraviolet radiation.
  • Earth emits only “far infrared” radiation that has a wavelength about 20 times longer than visible light.

Earth’s atmosphere contains CO2, water, methane, and other greenhouse gases.

  • These gases allow sunlight to pass through, but absorb infrared radiation from the ground.
  • When the amount of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere changes, it will affect the balance between incoming and outgoing radiation.
  • More greehouse gases means less infrared radiation can escape the Earth; but the incoming energy will not be affected.
  • This would cause a rise in average temperature.
  • Earth would be much colder if there were no greenhouse gases.

Feedbacks can amplify warming effects.

  • Melting glaciers and increased deserts can make Earth lesss reflective.
  • Warmer temperatures can release more greenhouse gases from thawing permafrost.

  3. Explain how radiation transfers heat.

    4. Hot objects emit electromagnetic radiation which carries away energy. When the EMR is absorbed by another object the energy is absorbed.
