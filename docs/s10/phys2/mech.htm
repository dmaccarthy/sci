Lesson Notes

Mechanical Energy

Energy

Energy is “the ability to do work” or “the ability to create motion”.

Energy comes in many forms:

  • Chemical Energy is associated with the arrangement of atoms in a substance.
  • Nuclear Energy is associated with the arrangement of protons and neutrons within a nucleus.
  • Thermal Energy and Sound Waves are forms of energy associated with the movement of molecules within a substance.
  • Light (including other forms of “electromagnetic radiation” like x-rays and radio waves) is a form of energy.

All of the above are non-mechanical forms of energy because they are unrelated to an object’s motion.

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical forms of energy are types of energy that are related to an object’s motion.

There are two mechanical forms of energy that we will study in Science 10:

  • Kinetic energy
  • Gravitational potential energy

Other types of mechanical energy that we will study in Physics 20 include:

  • Rotational energy
  • Elastic potential energy

All forms of energy are scalar quantities.

The SI unit for energy is the joule (J).

Kinetic Energy

When an object is in motion, it has Kinetic Energy.

  • We can calculate kinetic energy using the equation:

    E_k={1\over 2}mv^2

  • m is the object’s mass in kilograms. A larger mass has more energy than a smaller mass when moving at the same speed.
  • v is the object’s speed in m/s. Objects have more energy when they are moving at faster speeds.

Gravitational Potential Energy

When an object is elevated above the ground, it has Gravitational Potential Energy.

  • We can calculate gravitational potential energy using the equation:

    E_g=mgh

  • m is the object’s mass in kilograms.
  • g is the strength of gravity, which is \rm{9.81 m/s^2} on Earth, near the ground.
  • h is the object’s height in metres above the origin.
  • When an object rises above the ground, gravity transforms its kinetic energy into gravitational potential energy.
  • When an object falls, gravity transforms its gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy.

Example 1

A player kicks a 600-gram soccer ball. When the ball is 2.00 metres above the ground, its speed is 3.00 m/s.

  • What is the ball’s kinetic energy?

\begin{align}E_k &= {1\over 2}mv^2 \\[8pt]&= {1\over 2}\rm{0.600\ kg\cdot(3.00\ m/s)^2} \\[8pt]&= \rm{2.70\ J}\end{align}

  • What is the ball’s gravitational potential energy?

\begin{align}E_g &= mgh \\[8pt]&= \rm{0.600\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 \cdot 2.00\ m} \\[8pt]&= \rm{11.8\ J}\end{align}

  • How much mechanical energy does the ball have?

E = E_k + E_g = \rm{2.70\ J + 11.8\ J = 14.5\ J}

Example 2

Calculate the height of a 750-gram football when it has 20.0 joules of gravitational potential energy.

We need to use algebra to isolate the unknown (h) before we can complete the calculation:

E_g = mgh

\frac{E_g}{\color{red}{mg}} = \frac{mgh}{\color{red}{mg}}

\frac{E_g}{\color{red}{mg}} = \frac{\cancel{mg}h}{\cancel{\color{red}{mg}}}

\frac{E_g}{mg} = h

h = \rm{\frac{20.0\ J}{0.750\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.72\ m}

Example 3

Calculate the speed of the football when it has 35.0 joules of kinetic energy.

We need to use algebra to isolate the unknown (v) before we can complete the calculation:

E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

\frac{\color{red}2 E_k}{\color{red}m} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \cdot \color{red}{\frac{2}{m}}

\frac{\color{red}2 E_k}{\color{red}m} = \frac{1}{\cancel 2}\cancel mv^2 \cdot \color{red}{\frac{\cancel 2}{\cancel m}}

\frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

To cancel the “squared”, we need to square root both sides:

\sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \sqrt{v^2} = v

v = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 35.0\ J}{0.750\ kg}} = 9.66\ m/s}

Practice

  1. Calculate the kinetic energy of a 52.8 kg runner moving with a speed of 5.60 m/s.

    2. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

    E_k = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (52.8\ kg)(5.60\ m/s)^2}

    E_k = \rm{828\ J}

  2. A 0.300-kilogram apple hangs from a tree branch 2.75 m above the ground. Calculate the gravitational potential energy of the apple.

    3. E_g = mgh = \rm{(0.300\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (2.75\ m) = 8.09\ J}

  3. A 624-gram soccer ball is kicked down the field. At the highest point of the ball’s motion, it has a height of 3.76 m and a speed of 7.21 m/s. Calculate the mechanical energy (i.e. the total of the kinetic and gravitational potential energies) of the ball. Remember to convert grams to kilograms before performing any calculations.

    4. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (0.624\ kg) (7.21\ m/s)^2 = 16.2\ J}

    E_g = mgh = \rm{(0.624\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (3.76\ m) = 23.0\ J}

    \rm{Mechanical\ Energy} = E_k + E_g = \rm{16.2\ J + 23.0\ J = 39.2\ J}

  4. A 0.825 kg hammer is left on the roof of a house under construction. The hammer has 50.0 joules of gravitational potential energy. How high is the hammer above the ground?

    5. E_g = mgh

    h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm{\frac{50.0\ J}{0.825\ kg · 9.81\ m/s^2} = 6.18\ m}

    1. A 1900 kg car moves with a speed of 80.0 km/h. Convert this speed to standard SI units.

      2. \rm{80.0\ km/h = 80.0 \frac{10^3\ m}{3600\ s} = 22.2\ m/s}

    2. How much kinetic energy does the car have?

      3. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (1900\ kg) (22.2\ m/s)^2 = 4.69\times 10^5\ J}

    3. The car accelerates to 100 km/h. How much kinetic energy does the car have now?

      4. \rm{100\ km/h = 100 \frac{10^3\ m}{3600\ s} = 27.8\ m/s}

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (1900\ kg) (27.8\ m/s)^2 = 7.33\times 10^5\ J}

    4. Calculate the change in kinetic energy (\Delta E_k) that the car experienced while accelerating from 80.0 to 100 km/h?

      5. \Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = \rm{7.33\times 10^5\ J - 4.69\times 10^5\ J = 2.64\times 10^5\ J}

    5. Which non-mechanical form of energy was the source of this additional kinetic energy?

      6. The fuel (gasoline, diesel, propane) is chemical energy.

  6. A 0.587 kg baseball rolls along the ground with 18.0 joules of kinetic energy. Calculate the speed of the baseball.

    7. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

    \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

    v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 18.0\ J}{0.587\ kg}} = 7.83\ m/s}

    1. A 68.0 kg cyclist is riding at 6.20 m/s along a path at the top of a 12.0-metre high hill. Calculate the cyclist’s mechanical energy.

      2. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (68.0\ kg) (6.20\ m/s)^2 = 1.31\times 10^3\ J}

      E_g = mgh = \rm{(68.0\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (12.0\ m) = 8.00\times 10^3\ J}

      {\rm Mechanical\ Energy} = E_k + E_g

      \Sigma E = \rm 1.31\times 10^3\ J + 8.00\times 10^3\ J = \rm 9.31\times 10^3\ J

    2. The cyclist now coasts to the bottom of the hill. How much gravitational potential energy does she have when she reaches the bottom?

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm{(68.0\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (0.00\ m) = 0.00\ J}

    3. Assuming that the cyclist’s mechanical energy did not change (i.e. the gravitational potential energy lost was converted entirely into kinetic energy), what is the cyclist’s speed at the bottom of the hill?

      4. Since there is no gravitational potential energy, the mechanical energy is just the kinetic energy...

      E_k = \rm{9.31\times 10^3\ J}

      Re-arrange the kinetic energy equation...

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

      \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 9.31\times 10^3\ J}{68.0\ kg}} = 16.5\ m/s}

  8. A 58.5-gram rock falls from a cliff face. The rock is initially 25.0 metres above the base of the cliff.
    1. Calculate the mechanical energy of the rock before it falls.

      2. Since there is no kinetic energy, the mechanical energy is just the gravitational potential energy...

      E_g = mgh = \rm{(0.0585\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (25.0\ m) = 14.3\ J}

    2. Calculate the gravitational potential energy of the rock when it is 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff.

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm{(0.0585\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (10.0\ m) = 5.74\ J}

    3. Assuming that the mechanical energy of the rock does not change, how much kinetic energy does it have when it is 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff?

      4. \rm{Mechanical\ Energy} = E_g + E_k

      E_k = \rm{Mechanical\ Energy} - Eg = \rm{14.3\ J - 5.74\ J = 8.61\ J}

    4. Determine the speed of the rock at this point (i.e. 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff).

      5. The algebra is the same as in #7c...

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 8.61\ J}{0.0585\ kg}} = 17.2\ m/s}
