Biological Membranes

Membranes

The cell membrane (also called the plasma membrane) separates the cell from its surroundings.

  • In eukaryotic cells (including plant and animal cells) other organelles, such as the nucleus and mitochondria, also have membrances that separate them from the rest of the cell.
  • These membrances are semi-permeable; they allow some materials to pass into or out if the cell while preventing other materials from passing through.

Phospholipid Bilayer

The main components of a biological membrane are phospholipid molecules.

  • These are fatty molecules containing a phosphate group...

  • The head of the molecule is hydrophilic; it is attracted to water.
  • The tails of the molecule are hydrophobic; they are repelled from water.
  • When many phospholipid molecules are found in a watery environment, they will line up back-to-back with their hydrophilic heads facing out toward the water, and their hydrophobic tails facing in away from the water.
  • The resulting structure is called a phospholipid bilayer.

  • Some small molecules, like oxygen and carbon dioxide, are able to pass through the spaces between the larger phospholipid molecules.
  • Larger molecules cannot pass through the phospholipid bilayer.

Fluid Mosaic Model

While the phospholipid molecules are the main component of the membrane, there are other molecules embedded in the bilayer.

  • Protein molecules embedded in the membrane help transport larger molecules through the membrane. (More on this in a later lesson!)
  • Various carbohydrates, cholesterol, and other molecules serve to give the membrane strength, allow it to recognize materials the cell want or wants to keep out, or send chemical signals to other cells.
  • The molecules embedded in the membrance are not fixed in place (like a solid) but move around (like a fluid).
  • This picture of the cell membrane is called the fluid-mosaic model

Review

  1. Where in the cell would you find membranes?

    2. The cell membrane separates the cell from its environment. In eukaryotic cells, organelles including the nucleus, mitochondria, and choroplasts are also surrounded by a membrane.

  2. Define:
    1. hydrophobic

      2. A molecule (or one “pole” of a molecule) that repels water.

    2. hydrophilic

      3. A molecule (or one “pole” of a molecule) that attracts water.

  3. Draw an illustration of a phospholipid molecule. Label the hydrophilic and hydrophobic ends.

  4. What do we mean by a phospholipid bilayer?

    5. Phospholipid molecules contain a hydrophilic head (the spheres in the illustration) and a hydrophobic tail. These molecules form a “back-to-back” double layer so that the tails are kepts away from the water in the cytoplasm and surrounding the cell while the heads face toward the water.

  5. What do we mean by semi-permeable?

    6. This means that some atoms, ions, and molecules can pass through the membrane while others cannot.
