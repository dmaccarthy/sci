Lesson Notes

Passive Transport

Diffusion

  • Brownian motion is the random motion of molecules that occurs in liquids and gases.
  • The motion of molecules is faster at higher temperatures.
  • Over time, Brownian motion causes the concentration of substances to become uniform.
  • There is a net movement of material down the concentration gradient (from higher to lower concentrations).
  • This effect is called diffusion.

Passive Transport

Passive transport means movement across a membrane that does not require any energy expenditure by the cell.

  1. Simple diffusion is a type of passive transport that allows movement of small molecules (e.g. O2 and CO2) that can fit between the phospholipid molecules that make up the membrane.
  2. Osmosis refers to the diffusion of water.
  3. Facilitated diffusion refers to diffusion of larger molecules (e.g. glucose) or ions that would be repelled by the phospholipids. This requires the help of transport proteins.
  • Some molecules cannot pass through the membrane at all; biological membranes are semi-permeable.

Osmosis

Osmosis is diffusion of water molecules through the membrane.

  • When the cell’s environment (surroundings) have a higher concentration of solutes (lower concentration of water) than in the cell...
  • ...the surroundings are called hypertonic.
  • Water will move out of the cell causing the cell to shrink.
  • Cell shrinkage due to osmosis is called plasmolysis.

  • When the cell’s environment have a lower concentration of solutes (higher concentration of water) than in the cell...
  • ...the surroundings are called hypotonic.
  • Water will move into of the cell causing the cell to expand.
  • The outward pressure due to osmosis is called turgour pressure.

  • When the concentrations are balanced inside and outside the cell, the surroundings are called isotonic.
  • There is no net movement of water.

Facilitated Diffusion

Facilitated diffusion is diffusions through transport proteins embedded in the membrane.

  • Channel proteins allow diffusion of ions that cannot pass through the phospholipid molecules.
  • Carrier proteins change shape to open to one side of the membrane at a time.
  • Carrier proteins allow larger molecules like glucose to diffuse through the membrane.
  • Carrier proteins can also allow molecules to be transported against the concentration gradient.
  • This is called active transport and, unlike passive transport, it requires energy.

Practice

  1. A synthetic (non-biological) semi-permeable membrane is designed to permit the diffusion of molecules up to and including the size of glucose. Larger molecules like starch and proteins cannot pass through the membrane. On each diagram, draw and label arrows showing the direction of motion of all molecules (including water) that are able to pass through the membrane.

  2. A semipermeable membrane is used as a container. A solution containing 10% iodine (a small molecule) is poured inside the membrane. The membrane is sealed and is then inserted into a beaker containing 1% starch (a large molecule). When starch and iodine mix, a dark purple colour is produced.
    1. After a few minutes, will the solution inside the membrane turn purple? Will the solution outside the membrane turn purple? Explain.
      2. Since starch is a large molecule, it is unable to pass through the membrane into the container so the interior will not turn purple. Iodine is a small molecule so it will diffuse out from the container into the surroundings which will turn purple because there is already starch there.
    2. Will the membrane shrink or expand? Explain.
      3. The membrane is likely to expand as water enters the container through osmosis. This happens because there is a higher concentration of solute (lower concentration of water) inside compared to the “hypotonic” surroundings.

Review

  1. Define:
    1. Brownian motion: Random motion of molecules in a gas or liquid that causes them to spread out over time.
    2. diffusion: The net movement of molecules from higher to lower concentration (“down the concentration gradient”) via Brownian motion.
    3. osmosis: The diffusion of water.
    4. hypertonic: An environment (outside) that has a higher concentration of solute than the cell (inside).
    5. hypotonic: An environment that has a lower concentration of solute than the cell.
    6. isotonic: An environment that has the same concentration of solute as the cell.
  2. Explain why diffusion across a membrane works for smaller molecules like H20, O2 and CO2, but not for larger molecules like glucose.

    3. Small molecules are able to diffuse through the spaces between the phospholipid molecules that make up the membrane. Larger molecules are too big to fit through these spaces.

  3. Explain the difference between active and passive transport across a membrane.

    4. Passive transport relies on diffusion to move down the concentration gradient and requires no energy expenditure. Active transport uses energy, for example to move molecules up (against) the concentration gradient from lower to higher concentration.

  4. Explain how facilitated diffusion works. Include a definition of transport proteins (carrier and channel) in your response.

    5. For particles that cannot diffuse through the spaces between phospholipid molecules because they are too large or they have a charge the cause them to be repelled, diffusion can occur through transport proteins embedded in the phospholipid bilayer. Channel proteins allow the passage of ions, while carrier proteins allow the passage of larger molecules, often by changing shape.
