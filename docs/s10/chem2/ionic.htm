Binary Ionic Compounds

Ionic Compounds

Compounds are substances comprised of atoms of two or more different elements.

  • Sodium likes to lose one of its electron to become \mathrm{Na^{+}}.
  • Chlorine likes to gain an electron to become \mathrm{Cl^{-}}.
  • When sodium and chlorine are allowed to react, there will be a transfer of one electron from each sodium to each chlorine.

  • The oppositely charged ions attract each other and stick together as a compound: \mathrm{NaCl}.
  • Compounds formed from one positive and one negative ion are called binary ionic compounds.

  • Positive ions are called cations.
  • Negative ions are called anions.
  • The name of the binary ionic compound is the cation name followed by the anion name: sodium chloride.
  • In ionic compounds, the ions are usually arranged in a lattice (crystal).

Charge Conservation

Electric charge is a conserved quantity.

  • The cation and anion charges must balance.
  • Suppose we wanted to make the ionic compound calcium chloride.

  • Each calcium atom loses two electrons to become \mathrm{Ca^{2+}}.
  • Each chlorine atom can only accept one extra electron.
  • Where does the other electron go?
  • There needs to be a second chlorine atom to accept the second electron.

  • The chemical formula for calcium chloride is \mathrm{CaCl_2}.
  • This means that there are two chloride ions for every one calcium ion...

  • The total charge of all the cations or anions will be the lowest common multiple of the individual cation and anion charges.

Metal Cations

  • We saw earlier that some metals can form cations with more than one possible charge: \mathrm{Fe}^{2+} and \mathrm{Fe}^{3+}.
  • It is important to name the compound correctly...
    • \mathrm{FeO} is called iron(II) oxide.
    • \mathrm{Fe_2 O_3} is called iron(III) oxide.

Practice

Review

  1. Define a binary ionic compound and explain how the atoms are held together in these compounds.
    2. A compound formed from atoms of two different elements. One element forms a positive ion and the other a negative ion. They are held together because their opposite charges attract. This is called an ionic bond.
  2. Define cation and anion.
    3. The cation is positive ion and the anion is the negative ion.
    1. State the Law of Conservation of Electric Charge and explain how it affects ionic compounds involving ions with unequal charges.
      2. The total charge must remain constant. This means that for an ionic compound, the charge of the cation must exactly balance the charge of the anion.
    2. Explain how to determine the chemical formula for an ionic compound.
      3. Compare the charges of the ions. The total positive charge and the total negative charge must be the LCM of the cation and anion charges.
  4. Explain how to name binary ionic compounds. Specifically include how to name compounds involving elements that can form more than one ion.
    5. Give the name of the cation first. If the element has more than one possible ion, include the ion charge as a roman numeral such as “iron(III)” or “iron(II)”. The anion name, with the suffix “-ide”, goes second.
  5. Compare the properties of ionic compounds to those of molecular compounds.
    • Ionic compounds tend to have higher melting points and so are usually solid at room temperature.
    • Molecular compounds (with small molecules) like CO2 and CH4 are often gases at room temperature, unless they have strong “hydrogen bonds” like H2O and CH3OH.
    • Molecular compounds (with larger molecules) can be liquid (butane) or solid (sucrose) at room temperature.
    • If soluble, ionic compounds are more likely to be electrical conductors when dissolved in water while molecular compounds are not.
