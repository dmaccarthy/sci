Lesson Notes

Velocity & Speed

Velocity

We learned in the last lesson that displacement measures the change in an object’s position.

Velocity measures how quickly the position changes, or the rate of displacement:

\vec{\bf v} = {\Delta\vec{\bf d}\over \Delta t}

  • Velocity, like displacement, is a vector.
  • The SI unit of velocity is metres per second.
  • The conversion factor to or from km/h is

    \rm{1\ m/s = 3.6\ km/h}

Motion Graph

\vec{\bf v} = {\Delta\vec{\bf d}\over \Delta t}

\vec{\bf v} = \rm{(+7.0\ m) - (-5.0\ m)\over 4.0\ s - 0.0\ s}

= \rm{{+12.0\ m \over 4.0\ s}}

= \rm{+3.0\ m/s}

  • Since \Delta\vec{\bf d} is the “rise” of the graph and \Delta t is the “run”, velocity is the slope of the line.

When the graph is a straight line, the motion is classified as uniform motion.

  • The graph has a constant slope.
  • The velocity is constant.
  • The position changes by the same amount in equal amounts of time.

When the graph is curved, the motion is classified as accelerated motion.

  • The slope of the graph is not constant.
  • The velocity is changing.
  • The position changes by the different amounts in equal amounts of time.

Speed

Speed is the rate of distance travelled:

v = {\Delta d\over \Delta t}

  • Remember that distance is measured along the path of the motion, while displacement is measured in a straight line.
  • Speed is a scalar (no direction) while velocity is a vector.

Example 1

What will be the displacement of a cyclist who rides with a velocity of 27.0 km/h [south] for one minute?

Identify the variables and convert to standard SI units if necessary. Normally we use positive for north and negative for south:

  • \vec{\bf v} = \rm{27.0\ km/h\ [S] = -7.50\ m/s}
  • \Delta t = \rm{1.00\ min = 60.0\ s}
  • \Delta\vec{\bf d} =\ ?

Select the relevant relationship (equation):

\vec{\bf v} = {\Delta \vec{\bf d}\over \Delta t}

Rearrange the equation to isolate the unknown:

\vec{\bf v}{\color{red}{\Delta t}} = {\Delta \vec{\bf d}\over \Delta t} \color{red}{\Delta t}

\vec{\bf v}\Delta t = \Delta \vec{\bf d}

Evaluate, including units:

\Delta \vec{\bf d} = \rm {-7.50\ m/s \cdot 60.0\ s} = -450\ m

  • Is the answer reasonable?

Example 2

An athlete can maintain an average speed of 7.80 m/s during an 800-metre race. How long will it take the runner to finish the race?

Identify the variables:

  • v = \rm{7.80\ m/s}
  • \Delta d = \rm{800\ m}
  • \Delta t =\ ?

Select the relevant relationship (equation):

v = {\Delta d\over \Delta t}

Rearrange the equation to isolate the unknown:

v{\color{red}{\Delta t}} = {\Delta d\over \Delta t} \color{red}{\Delta t}

v\Delta t = \Delta d

{v\Delta t \over \color{red}{v}} = {\Delta d \over \color{red}{v}}

\Delta t = {\Delta d \over v}

Evaluate, including units:

\Delta t = \rm{{800\ m \over 7.80\ m/s} = 103\ s}

  • Always use standard SI units (except when the units cancel).
  • Is the answer reasonable?

Review

  1. How long will it take an aircraft that travels at 425 km/h to fly 550 km from Edmonton to Saskatoon?

    2. Rearrange the speed equation to isolate the time:

    v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}

    v \Delta t = \Delta d

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta d}{v}

    We could convert to standard SI units, but this is not necessary because the km cancels:

    \Delta t = \rm \frac{550\ km}{425\ km/h} = 1.29\ h = 4.66\times 10^3\ s

  2. A hiker follows a circular path, walking at an average speed of 5.00 km/h. After 3.20 hours of walking, she returns to her starting point, finishing one complete lap.
    1. What is the hiker’s distance traveled?

      2. Rearrange the speed equation to isolate the distance:

      v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}

      \Delta d = v \Delta t = \rm{5.00\ km/h\cdot 3.20\ h = 16.0\ km}

    2. What is the hiker’s displacement and average velocity?

      3. Since the hiker returns to her starting point (\vec{\bf d}_f = \vec{\bf d}_i), the displacement will be zero and the average velocity will therefore be zero as well!

Practice

  1. A car drives from Sherwood Park to Calgary, a displacement of 285 km [south], in 3.25 hours.
    1. What is the car’s average velocity?

      2. Taking north as positive and south as negative:

      \vec{\bf v} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{\Delta t} = \rm{\frac{-285\ km}{3.25\ h} = -87.7\ km/h = -24.4\ m/s}

    2. Explain why the car’s average speed will be greater than the magnitude of the average velocity.

      3. Unless the car travels in a perfectly constant direction, the displacement (measured in a staight line) will be less than the distance (measured along the car’s path), so the average velocity will be less than the speed.

  2. How much time would it take a cyclist to ride 25.0 km if they maintain an average speed of 6.25 m/s?

    3. v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}

    Multiply both sides by \Delta t:

    v\Delta t = \Delta d

    Divide both sides by v and evaluate. Be sure to change km to m:

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta d}{v}

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta d}{v} = \rm \frac{25000\ m}{6.25\ m/s} = 4000\ s = 1.11\ h

  3. A student walks 2.00 km north in 25.0 minutes and then takes a 10.0-minute rest. They then walk 1.00 km south in 15.0 minutes. Calculate their average velocity and their average speed, including the rest.

    4. The total time for the motion is (25.0 + 10.0 + 15.0) min = 50.0 min = 3000 seconds.

    The total distance the student walked is \Delta d = 2.00 km + 1.00 km = 3.00 km.

    The average speed (remembering to convert km to m!) is:

    v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{3000\ m}{3000\ s} = 1.00\ m/s

    Taking north as positive, the total displacement of the student is:

    \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm +2.00\ km + (-1.00\ km) = +1.00\ km

    The average velocity is:

    \vec{\bf v} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{+1000\ m}{3000\ s} = +0.333\ m/s

  4. A long-distance runner maintains an average speed of 12.0 km/h while training. If she runs for 85.0 min, what distance did she cover?

    5. Convert to standard SI units:

    v = \rm{12.0\ \frac{km}{h}\ \left(\frac{10^3\ m}{km}\right) \left(\frac{h}{3600\ s}\right) = 3.33\ m/s}

    \Delta t = \rm{85.0\ min\ \left(\frac{60\ s}{min}\right) = 5100\ s}

    Rearrange the speed equation to isolate the distance:

    v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}

    \Delta d = v \Delta t = \rm{3.33\ m/s\cdot 5100\ s = 1.70\times 10^4\ m = 17.0\ km}

  5. A runner in a 15.0-kilometre race has a goal of finishing in 64.0 minutes. At the 5.00 km mark of the course, his time is 24.2 minutes.
    1. Calculate the runner’s average speed for the first 5.00 kilometres.

      2. Convert to standard SI units:

      \Delta d = \rm{5.00\ km\ \frac{10^3\ m}{km} = 5000\ m}

      \Delta t = \rm{24.2\ min\ \left(\frac{60\ s}{min}\right) = 1452\ s}

      Evaluate:

      v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \rm{\frac{5000\ m}{1452\ s} = 3.44\ m/s}

    2. Calculate the average speed that the runner will need to maintain for the final 10.0 km in order to finish in their target time.

      3. Convert to standard SI units:

      \Delta d = \rm{10.0\ km\ \frac{10^3\ m}{km} = 10000\ m}

      \Delta t = \rm{(64.0 - 24.2)\ min = 39.8\ min\ \left(\frac{60\ s}{min}\right) = 2388\ s}

      Evaluate:

      v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \rm{\frac{10000\ m}{2388\ s} = 4.19\ m/s}

    3. Assuming the runner completes the race in his desired time, what will his average speed be for the entire race?

      4. Convert to standard SI units:

      \Delta d = \rm{15.0\ km\ \frac{10^3\ m}{km} = 15000\ m}

      \Delta t = \rm{64.0\ min\ \left(\frac{60\ s}{min}\right) = 3840\ s}

      Evaluate:

      v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \rm{\frac{15000\ m}{3840\ s} = 3.91\ m/s}

    4. Do your answers seem reasonable?

      5. This seems like a reasonable speed; 4 m/s means the runner covers 100 metres every 25 seconds. Also, the overall average is between the the two interval averages.

    1. Give the mathematical definition of velocity and explain what this definition means.

      2. \vec{\bf v} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{\Delta t}

      Velocity is the rate at which the position changes; the displacement per second.

    2. What are the appropriate SI units for all quantities appearing in the velocity equation?

      3. Displacement is measured in metres, time in seconds, and velocity in m/s.

    3. Can we ever use non-standard units? Explain.

      4. When the units cancel, non-standard units can be used; for example, when displacement is in km and velocity in km/h, the kilometres will cancel and the time will be in hours.

  7. Define speed and explain how it is similar to and different from velocity.

    8. Speed is the distance travelled (not displacement!) per second. Speed is a scalar.
