Displacement

A coordinate is a measurement that describes an object’s position.

  • A coordinate is often the distance and direction (+ or -) from an arbitrary origin.
  • Motion in a straight line is classified as one-dimensional (1D) because only one coordinate is needed to describe a position.
  • Motion in a plane is classified as two-dimensional (2D) because two coordinates are needed.

An object’s displacement is the change in its position over some time interval:

\Delta\vec{\bf{d}} = \vec{\bf{d}}_f - \vec{\bf{d}}_i

  • Displacement is classified as a vector quantity because it involves a direction. Other vectors include velocity, acceleration, and force.
  • Quantities that do not involve a direction are called scalars. These include time, mass, and energy.
  • Displacment is measured in a straight line, even when the motion is not straight.
  • A vector diagram is a tip-to-tail illustration of the motion.

Velocity & Acceleration

An object’s velocity is the rate at which the position changes, which equals the slope of a \vec{\bf{d}} vs.t graph:

{\vec{\bf{v}}} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}}{\Delta t}

An object’s acceleration is the rate at which the velocity changes, which equals the slope of a \vec{\bf{v}} vs. t graph:

{\vec{\bf{a}}} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf{v}}}{\Delta t}

Uniform Motion

Uniform Motion is 1D motion with constant velocity and zero acceleration.

  • This type of motion occurs when the net force is zero (there are no forces acting on the object, or the forces are balanced.)
  • A \vec{\bf{v}} vs. t graph is horizontal, and \vec{\bf{d}} vs. t is linear with a slope equal to the velocity.

Uniform Accelerated Motion

Uniform Accelerated Motion (UAM) is 1D motion with constant acceleration.

  • This type of motion occurs when there is a constant net force parallel to the direction of the velocity.
  • A \vec{\bf{v}} vs. t graph is linear with a slope equal to the acceleration.

  • The area under any section of this graph (shaded area above) is equal to the displacement.
  • The \vec{\bf{d}} vs. t graph is a parabola.

To solve UAM problems:

  • Identify the three knowns from the five kinematics variables: \vec{\bf{a}}, \vec{\bf{v}}_i, \vec{\bf{v}}_f, \Delta\vec{\bf{d}}, \Delta t.
  • Look through the following relations in order, to find an equation with only one unknown.

\vec{\bf{a}}=\frac{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_f-{\vec{\bf{v}}}_i}{\Delta t}

\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}=\frac{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_i+{\vec{\bf{v}}}_f}{2}\Delta t

v_f^2=v_i^2+2\vec{\bf{a}}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}

\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}={\vec{\bf{v}}}_i\Delta t+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}(\Delta t)^2

  • Solve for the first unknown.
  • Choose the first equation that you haven’t already used, and use it to solve for the second unknown.
  • When solving for one of the velocities using the third of these equations, note that the solution can be either positive or negative. You will have to use the context of the problem to decide which is the correct solution.
