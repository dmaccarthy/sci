Uniform Accelerated Motion

The definition of acceleration is:

{\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

  • For uniform accelerated motion, the acceleration is constant.

A graph of {\vec{\bf v}}-t is linear for UAM.

  • The slope of the graph equals the acceleration.
  • The area under the graph equals the displacement.
  • Areas above the axis represent displacement in the positive direction. Areas below the axis are negative displacements.

The displacement is just the average speed multiplied by the time:

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_i + {\vec{\bf v}}_f}\over 2}\Delta t

  • Because the graph is linear, the average velocity is the midpoint (arithmetic average) of {\vec{\bf v}}_i and {\vec{\bf v}}_f.
  • You cannot use this equation if the acceleration is not constant, since the average velocity would be incorrect.

Kinematic Variables

{\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_i + {\vec{\bf v}}_f}\over 2}\Delta t

These equations contain five variables...

\vec{\bf a}+1.60{\rm{m/s^2}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_i+12.0{\rm{m/s}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_f+20.0{\rm{m/s}}
\Delta\vec{\bf d}+80.0{\rm{m}}
\Delta t5.00{\rm{s}}
  • We can solve for two unknowns using the two equations, but we must start with three “givens”.
  • Choose an equation to find the first unknown.
  • Then repeat for the other unknown.

UAM Equations

Sometimes, both unknowns will appear in both equations...

\vec{\bf a}−9.81{\rm{m/s^2}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_i+10.0{\rm{m/s}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_f−10.0{\rm{m/s}}
\Delta\vec{\bf d}0.00{\rm{m}}
\Delta t2.04{\rm{s}}

These equations can be combined algebraically in three different ways, to eliminate either \Delta t, {\vec{\bf v}}_f, or {\vec{\bf v}}_i...

{v_f}^2 = {v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = {\vec{\bf v}}_i\Delta t + {1\over 2}{\vec{\bf a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2

{\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = {\vec{\bf v}}_f\Delta t - {1\over 2}{\vec{\bf a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2

Go through the list of equations in order until you find an equation that has only one unknown:

{v_f}^2 = {v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

v_f = \sqrt{{v_i}^2 + 2{\vec{\bf a}}\cdot{\Delta\vec{\bf d}}}

v_f = \sqrt{\rm{(10.0\ m/s)^2 + 2\ (-9.81\ m/s^2)\cdot(0.00\ m)}} = \rm{10.0\ m/s}

{\vec{\bf v}}_f = \pm 10.0\ {\rm{m/s}}

  • Note that when square rooting, the answer can be either positive or negative.
  • In this example, a negative acceleration means the final velocity must be less than the initial velocity, so the answer must be negative:

    {\vec{\bf v}}_f = -10.0\ {\rm{m/s}}

Now go back to the list of equations and choose the first one that hasn’t already been used:

{\vec{\bf a}} = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over\Delta t}

{\vec{\bf a}}\Delta t = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}}

\Delta t = {{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}\over{\vec{\bf a}}} = {\rm{{-10.0\ m/s - (+10.0\ m/s)\over -9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.04\ s}}

Problem Solving Hints

For scenarios where the object’s acceleration changes, we must solve each section of the motion as a separate problem!

Remember that we always need at least three “givens” before we can solve a UAM problem.

Sometimes, it may appear that the problem does not provide enough information...

  • If the object begins or ends at rest, this means one of the velocities is zero.
  • If the object returns to its starting position, this means the displacement is zero.
  • For projectiles (gravity is the only force), we can assume the acceleration is −9.81 m/s2 if the problem takes place near Earth’s surface.
  • Also for projectiles, the highest point is where the velocity changes from positive to negative; the instantaneous velocity will be zero.

Example 1

During takeoff, a jet aircraft can accelerate at 3.25 m/s2. The jet must reach a velocity of 360 km/h before it reaches the end of the runway. Calculate:

  1. the minimum time that it would take the jet to reach takeoff speed from rest.
  2. the minimum length of runway needed.
\vec{\bf a}+3.25{\rm{m/s^2}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_i0.00{\rm{m/s}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_f+100{\rm{m/s}}
\Delta\vec{\bf d}+1.54 × 103{\rm{m}}
\Delta t30.8{\rm{s}}

Solve for \Delta t in Eqn 20:

\Delta t = \frac{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{+100\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s}{+3.25\ m/s^2} = 30.8\ s}

Evaluate \Delta \vec{\bf d} with Eqn 21:

\Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm{\frac{0.00\ m/s + 100.0\ m/s}{2} 30.8\ s = +1.54\times 10^3\ m}

Example 2

A ball is dropped (i.e. {\vec{\bf{v}}}_i = 0) from the top of a 35.0-metre tall building. The ball accelerates downward at 9.53 m/s2 due to the combined forces of gravity and air resistance.

\vec{\bf a}–9.53{\rm{m/s^2}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_i0.00{\rm{m/s}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_f–25.8{\rm{m/s}}
\Delta\vec{\bf d}–35.0{\rm{m}}
\Delta t2.71{\rm{s}}

Solve for {\vec{\bf v}}_f in Eqn 22:

\begin{align} v_f &= \sqrt{v_i^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}} \\[10pt]&= \rm{\sqrt{0 + 2(-9.53\ m/s^2)\cdot(-35.0\ m)}} \\[10pt]&= \rm{\pm 25.8\ m/s} \end{align}

Use the negative answer because the ball is falling!

Solve for \Delta t in Eqn 20:

\Delta t = \frac{{\vec{\bf v}}_f - {\vec{\bf v}}_i}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{-25.8\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s}{-9.53\ m/s^2} = 2.71\ s}

Example 3

On an alien planet, Captain Kirk falls from a 52.0-metre high cliff. He hits the ground with a downward velocity of 1.50 m/s.

\vec{\bf a}–0.0216{\rm{m/s^2}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_i0.00{\rm{m/s}}
{\vec{\bf v}}_f–1.50{\rm{m/s}}
\Delta\vec{\bf d}–52.0{\rm{m}}
\Delta t69.3{\rm{s}}

Solve for \Delta t in Eqn 21:

\Delta t = \frac{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{{\vec{\bf v}}_i + {\vec{\bf v}}_f} = \rm{\frac{2\cdot(-52.0\ m)}{0.00\ m/s + (-1.50\ m/s)} = 69.3\ s}

Evaluate \vec{\bf a} with Eqn 20:

\vec{\bf a} = \rm{\frac{-1.50\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s}{69.3\ s} = -0.0216\ m/s^2}

    1. The driver of a car sees that a bridge 285 m ahead is washed out. The driver immediately hits the brakes, causing the car to accelerate at –2.50 m/s2. If the car just barely stops in time, calculate its initial velocity and braking time.

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf v}\Delta t = \rm{+37.7\ m/s\cdot 0.650\ s = +24.5\ m}

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm{+285\ m - 24.5\ m = +260\ m}
