Position & Displacement

Motion

The branch of physics that studies motion is called mechanics.

Mechanics has two main branches:

  • Kinematics aims to describe the motion of objects.
  • Dynamics aims to explain why objects move as they do.

Position

A first step in describing an object’s motion is being able to describe where the object is: this is called the object’s position.

  • The object’s position is represented by its distance from the origin, with the positions on one side of the origin assigned positive values and the other side of the origin assigned negative values.
  • A measurement that describes a position is called a coordinate.
  • The origin is an arbitrarily chosen point which is assigned a coordinate of exactly 0.
  • The symbol for position is {\vec{\mathbf d}} and the SI unit is metres (m).
  • The bike’s initial position in the above illustration is {\vec{\mathbf d}}_i = {\mathrm{-5.0\ m}} and its final position is {\vec{\mathbf d}}_f = {\mathrm{+7.0\ m}}.
  • The illustration uses the tree as the origin: the bike is initially 5.0 metres to the left of the tree, and after some time it has moved to 7.0 metres to the right of the tree.
  • When an object moves only in a straight line (either forward or backward), we call this one-dimensional (1D) motion, because we only need to make one measurement to describe the position.

Displacement

The change in an object’s position between any two points on its trajectory is called the displacement.

  • Trajectory means the path that the object follows.

  • The symbol for displacement is \Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}:

    \Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}_{1\rightarrow 2} = {\vec{\mathbf d}}_2 - {\vec{\mathbf d}}_1

  • The bicycle’s displacement for the entire 4.0-second time interval is:

    \Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}} = {\mathrm{(+7.0\ m) − (−5.0\ m) = +12.0\ m}}

Displacment is represented on the diagram by the red arrow connecting the intial and final positions.

  • The displacement of +12.0 m means that the red arrow is 12.0 metres long and points in the positive direction.
  • Displacement is classified as a vector quantity because it involves a direction.
  • Quantities that do not involve a direction (like mass, time, or energy) are classified as scalars.
  • The length of the arrow (without direction) is called the magnitude of the vector.

Vector Diagrams

When the motion contains more than a single displacement, the vector diagram consists of all displacements drawn in a “tip-to-tail” manner.

  • The resultant (total) displacement is drawn from the starting position of the first vector to the final position of the last vector.
  • Displacement is always measured in a straight line, even if the object’s trajectory is not straight.
  • This means the magnitude of the displacement will be less than the distance travelled by the object whenever the object follows an indirect path.
  • If the bike (in the 1D example above) rode past the 7.0 metre mark, and then turned around and came back, its displacement would still be +12.0 m but its distance travelled would be greater.

Practice

  1. A hiker moving in 1D has an initial position of –4.0 km and a final position of +3.2 km relative to some arbitrary origin.
    1. Draw a vector diagram of the hiker’s motion. Label \vec{\mathbf d}_i (the initial position), \vec{\mathbf d}_f (the final position), and \Delta\vec{\mathbf d} (the displacement).

    2. Calculate the hiker’s displacement. Show your work.

      \Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf d}_f - \vec{\mathbf d}_i = \mathrm{(+3.2 - (-4.0))\ km = +7.2\ km}

    3. The hiker now walks to a third position of +1.0 km. Draw and label a new vector diagram showing both parts of the motion and the resultant displacement.

    4. Calculate the resultant displacement and the total distance the hiker walked.

      \Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf d}_f - \vec{\mathbf d}_i = \mathrm{(+1.0 - (-4.0))\ km = +5.0\ km}

      Alternatively, you could calculate the second part of the displacement: \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2 = \mathrm{(+1.0 - 3.2)\ km = -2.2\ km}, and then add thtis to the first displacement (\mathrm{+7.2\ km}) to get the same answer.

      The distance is...

      \Delta d = |\Delta\vec{\bf d}_1| + |\Delta\vec{\bf d}_2| = \rm 7.2\ km + 2.2\ km = 9.4\ km

  2. A student rides her bicycle 5.00 km north in 12.0 min. After a 5.00 min rest, she rides back south to her starting point in 15.0 min. Determine the student’s displacement and distance traveled for the entire trip.

    3. The displacment is zero because her final position is the same as her initial position.

    You can show this by adding the two displacement vectors:

    \Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_1 + \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2 = \mathrm{+5.00\ km + (-5.00\ km) = 0.00\ km}

    Distance is not a vector, so we ignore the direction:

    \Delta d = |\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_1| + |\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2| = \mathrm{5.00\ km + 5.00\ km = 10.0\ km}

  4. Define: (a) mechanics, (b) kinematics, (c) dynamics.

    5. Mechanics is the study of motion. Kinematics describes motion while dynamics explains why objects move the way they do.

  5. Define: (a) origin, (b) coordinate, (c) coordinate axis, (d) position, (e) trajectory, (f) scalar, (g) vector, (h) magnitude.

    6. The origin is an arbitrary location, assigned a coordinate of 0, relative to which the location of any object can be described.

    A coordinate is a measurement, usually a distance or angle, that is used to describe a location relatve to the origin.

    A coordinate axis is a line along which each point can be described by the distance from the origin, with positive values used in one direction and negative in the other.

    The position of an object is its location, described by one or more coordinates.

    The trajectory is the path that an object follows; mathematically, the trajectory is represented by the position as a function of time, whihc can take the form of a graph, a table of values, or an equation.

    A scalar is a quantity that does not involve a direction, like distance, mass, or energy.

    A vector is a quantity that does involve a direction, like displacement, velocity, or force.

    The magnitude of a vector refers to its size, without including a direction.

  6. Explain what we mean by one-dimensional (1D) motion as opposed to 2D or 3D motion.

    7. Motion along a staight line is one-dimensional, because we can describe positions with only one coordinate axis. Motion in a plane is a 2D, because we need an x- and a y-axis. Motion that is not confined to a plane is 3D.

  7. Define and explain the similarities and differences between displacement and distance travelled. Include the mathematical definition (formula) for displacement.

    8. Displacement is the change in position, measured in a straight line; it is a vector that depends only on the starting and ending positions and not on the path the object follows.

    \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf d}_f - \vec{\bf d}_i

    Distance is a scalar that measures the length of the path the object follows.

    1. Explain how to draw a vector diagram for motion that involves one or more direction changes.

      2. A vector diagram is a tip-to-tail illustration showing the addition of two or more vectors. Vector diagrams must be drawn on graph paper and the scales must be the same for both axes!

    2. What do we mean by the resultant vector?

      3. The resultant vector is the total of the vectors drawn tip-to-tail. The resultant vector is drawn in a straight line from the tail of the first vector to the tip of the last vector.
