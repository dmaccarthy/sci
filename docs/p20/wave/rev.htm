Notes

A mechanical wave is a disturbance that transports energy through a medium.

  • According to mathematician Joseph Fourier, any wave can be treated as one or more harmonic (sine-shaped) waves that have been added together.

  • For transverse waves (e.g. light*, waves on a string), the motion of the individual points along the wave is perpendicular to the motion of the wave’s energy (phase velocity).
  • *Light is an electromagnetic wave (see Physics 30!) not a mechanical wave; it does not require a medium.
  • Points “above” equilibrium are called crests and points “below” equilibrium are troughs.
  • For longitudinal waves (e.g. sound) the particle motion is parallel to the phase velocity.

  • Compressions occur where the particles are closer together than normal and rarefactions are when the particles are more spread out.
  • Some waves (e.g. surface waves on water) have both transverse and longitudinal components.
  • The distance between corresponding points on adjacent waves (e.g. crest-to-crest) is called the wavelength, \lambda.
  • Harmonic waves also have an amplitude, period, and frequency, which have the same meaning as for SHM.
  • The phase speed of a wave can be calculated as:

    v=\frac{\lambda}{T}=f\lambda

Doppler Effect is a phenomenon where the observed frequency of waves is different than the actual frequency of the source when the source and observer are in relative motion:

f_o=\frac{v-v_o}{v-v_s}f_s

  • Waves are closer together — the observed frequency is higher — when the source is approaching the observer.
  • Waves are farther apart — the observed frequency is lower — when the source is receding from the observer.

Interference occurs when two waves overlap in the same place at the same time.

  • Constructive interference means that two similar parts (e.g. two crests) overlap to produce a bigger wave.
  • Destructive interference means that two dissimilar parts (e.g. one crest and one trough) overlap to produce a smaller wave.
  • A beat pattern occurs when two waves of slightly different frequency move in and out of phase, resulting in alternating constructive and destructive interference with a beat frequency:

    f_{beat}=\left|f_1-f_2\right|

The beat pattern (purple) is produced when the two overlapping waves are added.

Standing Waves are produced when a wave moving on a finite medium interferes with its own reflections.

  • When the end of the medium is fixed, the interference there must be destructive, and a node will be produced.
  • Only certain harmonics (or resonant frequencies) can have nodes at both ends (e.g. stringed instruments or air columns open at both ends):

    f_n=\frac{nv}{2L}
