Lesson Notes

Standing Waves

Reflection of Waves

When a wave reaches the end of the medium, its energy is reflected back the way it came.

  • The reflected waves and the incident (incoming) waves will interfere with each other.

  • When the reflection occurs from a fixed end (e.g. a guitar string), the reflection will be inverted; crests will be reflected as troughs and vice versa. Interference will be destructive.
  • Reflections from a free end will not be inverted. Interference will be constructive.

Nodes & Anti-nodes

At a fixed end (destructive interference)...

  • The incident crest and the reflected trough will cancel each other out.
  • A point where the interfering waves cancel is called a node.
  • The amplitude will be exactly zero at a node due to the destructive interference.
  • For a string fixed at both ends, like a guitar string, both ends must be nodes.
  • A place where the incident and reflected waves interfering constructively is called an anti-node.
  • Anti-nodes will have a large amplitude.

Standing Waves

Because both ends are nodes, the longest possible wavelength harmonic wave that can be produced on a string with fixed ends will look like this:

  • Waves like this are called standing waves because the incident and reflected waves are carrying energy in opposite directions, so that there is no net movement of energy.
  • We can see from the illustration above that the length of the string is equal to one-half of a wavelength:

    L = {\lambda\over 2}

  • This standing wave, with nodes at the ends and nowhere else, is called the fundamental, or first harmonic.
  • Standing waves with higher frequencies than the fundamental are called overtones.
  • We can produce these higher harmonics by adding more nodes along the string.
  • The second harmonic has a node at the middle as well as the ends. Nodes are shown below as blue dots, and anti-node locations are shown in red.

  • We can continue adding more nodes by increasing the frequency to produce higher harmonics.
  • The harmonic number (n) indicates the number of crests and troughs present in the standing wave.
  • Since each crest or trough is one-half of a wavelength, we can write an equation relating the wavelength to the harmonic number and the length of the string:

    L=n\left(\frac{\lambda}{2}\right)

  • Solving for \lambda and substituting into v=\lambda f gives:

    \lambda=\frac{2L}{n}

    f_n=\frac{nv}{2L}

  • This equation tells us that the frequency of the n’th harmonic is n times the fundamental frequency.

Air Columns

Sound waves inside a simple air column (open at both ends) form standing waves in the same way that waves on a string do.

  • The open ends are “fixed” at atmospheric pressure by virtue of being open.
  • The harmonic frequencies for an open air column can be calculated using the equation above, where v is the speed of sound waves in air.
  • For an air column that is closed at one end, only the open end is a node...
  • The closed end becomes an anti-node since reflections of the compressions and rarefactions are not inverted.

  • The animation above shows how the air pressure varies within the air column.
  • As a result, the length of the air column must be a multiple of one-half the length of the compressions or rarefactions (i.e. one-quarter of a wavelength), and the relationships between L, \lambda, and f become:

    \lambda=\frac{4L}{n}

    f_n=\frac{nv}{4L}

  • The value of n in these equations can only be an odd number. If we divided the wavelength by an even number, this would produce nodes at both ends, which is correct for an open air column but not for one that is closed.

Resonance

Resonance is the phenomenon whereby disturbances of initially small amplitude grow to very large amplitudes as a result of constructive interference.

  • We saw that pendulums, buildings, and other objects that oscillate a specific frequency will resonate when driven at the resonance frequency. This phenomenon was called mechanical resonance.
  • A string will begin to oscillate if there is a tuning fork or other source of sound waves at one of the string’s harmonic frequencies.
  • Similarly an air column will resonate when there are sound waves at one of the air column’s harmonic frequencies.
  • These behaviours are called acoustic resonance.
  • Harmonic frequencies are also called resonant frequencies.

Example 1

A guitar has string lengths of 0.800 m. Calculate the speed of the waves on the string that produces a fundamental frequency of 500 Hz.

f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}

2Lf_n = nv

v = \frac{2Lf_n}{n} = {\rm \frac{2\cdot 0.800\ m \cdot 500\ Hz}{1} = 800\ m/s}

Example 2

A second (thicker) string on the same guitar produces waves with a speed of 600 m/s. Calculate the first three harmonics that this string would produce.

f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}

f_1 = {\rm \frac{1\cdot 600\ m/s}{2\cdot 0.800\ m} = 375\ Hz}

f_2 = {\rm \frac{2\cdot 600\ m/s}{2\cdot 0.800\ m} = 750\ Hz}

f_3 = {\rm \frac{3\cdot 600\ m/s}{2\cdot 0.800\ m} = 1125\ Hz}

Example 3

How long would a closed (at one end) air column need to be to produce standing waves at 500 Hz as its third harmonic? What would the lower harmonic(s?) be?

f_n = \frac{nv}{4L}

Lf_n = \frac{nv}{4}

L = \frac{nv}{4f_n} = {\rm \frac{3\cdot 343\ m/s}{4\cdot 500\ Hz} = 0.515\ m}

The fundamental (first harmonic) frequency would be one-third of the third harmonic:

f_1 = \frac{f_3}{3} = \rm \frac{500\ Hz}{3} = 167\ Hz

There is no second harmonic for a closed air column! Only odd harmonics can be produced.

Practice

    1. An open air column has a third harmonic of 900 Hz. What is the length of the air column?

      2. f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}

      f_nL = \frac{nv}{2}

      L = \frac{nv}{2f_n} = \rm{\frac{3\cdot 343\ m/s}{2\cdot 900\ Hz} = 0.572\ m}

    2. A closed air column has a third harmonic of 900 Hz. What is the length of the air column?

      3. f_n = \frac{nv}{4L}

      f_nL = \frac{nv}{4}

      L = \frac{nv}{4f_n} = \rm{\frac{3\cdot 343\ m/s}{4\cdot 900\ Hz} = 0.286\ m}

  2. The highest frequency of sound wave that the human ear can hear is around 20.0 kHz. Determine the length of a closed air column that would produce this frequency as its (i) fundamental or (ii) third harmonic.
    3. For fundamental (n = 1)...

    L = \frac{nv}{4f_n} = \rm{\frac{1\cdot 343\ m/s}{4\cdot 20000\ Hz} = 4.26\ mm}

    For n = 3...

    L = \frac{nv}{4f_n} = \rm{\frac{3\cdot 343\ m/s}{4\cdot 20000\ Hz} = 1.28\ cm}

  3. A guitar string is 72.0 cm long and produces a fundamental at 520 Hz.
    1. Calculate the speed of waves in this string.

      2. f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}

      2Lf_n = nv

      v = \frac{2Lf_n}{n} = \rm{\frac{2\cdot 0.720\ m\cdot 520\ Hz}{1} = 749\ m/s}

    2. Calculate the frequency of the second and third harmonic for this guitar string.

      3. f_n = \frac{nv}{2L} = nf_1

      f_2 = \rm{2\cdot 520\ Hz = 1040\ Hz}

      f_3 = \rm{3\cdot 520\ Hz = 1560\ Hz}

    3. Another string on the same guitar has a wave speed of 902 m/s. Calculate the first three harmonics for this string.

      4. f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} = \rm{\frac{902\ m/s}{2\cdot 0.720\ m} = 626\ Hz}

      f_2 = \rm{2\cdot 626\ Hz = 1252\ Hz}

      f_3 = \rm{3\cdot 626\ Hz = 1879\ Hz}

  4. A footbridge constructed of rope and wood can produce waves like a string does.
    1. If a bridge is 22.4 m long, and the speed of waves is 3.50 m/s, what is the fundamental frequency?

      2. f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} = \rm{\frac{3.50\ m/s}{2\cdot 22.4\ m} = 0.0781\ Hz}

    2. What is the frequency of the fifth harmonic?

      3. f_5 = \rm{5\cdot 0.0781\ Hz = 0.391\ Hz}

    3. What would happen if a group of people (e.g. a military unit or a marching band) crossed the bridge while marching in step with a period equal to the fifth harmonic?
      4. Resonance! The marchers will be adding energy to the wave so the amplitude will increase.
  5. An air column that is 21.0 cm long produces a resonance with sound waves at 1.21 kHz. Students adjust the frequency of the waves and find the next resonance at 2.01 kHz.
    1. Is the air column open or closed? [Hint: Are the resonant frequencies exact multiple of the difference between them?]
    2. Identify the harmonics (i.e. the values of n) that these two frequencies represent. [Hint: First determine what the fundamental frequency must be.]
      3. The difference between the frequencies is 0.80 kHz. If the air column was open (n = 1, 2, 3...) this would be the fundamental frequency, but 1.21 and 2.01 kHz are not multiples of 0.80 kHz. The air column must be closed (n = 1, 3, 5...) so that the difference is twice the fundamental frequency. I.e. f_1 = 0.40 kHz so that f_3 = 1.21 kHz and f_5 = 2.01 kHz.
    3. Calculate the speed of sound based on the information provided.

      4. f_n = \frac{nv}{4L}

      4Lf_n = nv

      v = \frac{4Lf_n}{n} = \rm{\frac{4\cdot 0.210\ m\cdot 2010\ Hz}{5} = 338\ m/s}
