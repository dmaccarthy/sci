Vector Arithmetic

Vector Diagram

A vector diagram is a tip-to-tail illustration of two or more vectors being added.

Example 1

Illustrate the displacement of an object that moves 50 m [+60°] and then 40 m [+340°].

  • We can take the original starting point as the origin.
  • Note that the second part of the displacement begins from the point where the first displacement ends.
  • The object’s total displacement is from its original starting point (origin) to its final end point.
  • This vector, representing the sum of the two vectors, is called the resultant vector.
  • Because \Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_1 and \Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_2 have different directions, the magnitude of the resultant vector will not equal the magnitudes of the two vectors added together.
  • The distance travelled may be 90 m, but the magnitude of the displacement will be smaller.
  • If we draw the vector diagram carefully using a protractor, ruler, and graph paper, we can measure the resultant vector from the diagram.
  • This is called the graphical method of vector addition.
  • The accuracy of this method is limitted by how precisely we can draw and measure.

Component Method

A more accurate and reliable method of vector addition is the component method.

  • Convert each vector to Cartesian form:

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_{1x} = 50\ {\rm m}\cdot\cos(60^\circ) = +25\ {\rm m}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_{1y} = 50\ {\rm m}\cdot\sin(60^\circ) = +43\ {\rm m}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_{2x} = 40\ {\rm m}\cdot\cos(340^\circ) = +38\ {\rm m}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_{2y} = 40\ {\rm m}\cdot\sin(340^\circ) = -14\ {\rm m}

  • Now total the x- and y-components independently:

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_x = \rm{+25\ m + 38\ m = +63\ m}

\Delta{\vec{\bf d}}_y = \rm{+43\ m + (-14\ m) = +30\ m}

  • You can convert the answer back to polar form if necessary.

|\Delta\vec{\bf d}| = \sqrt{(\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x)^2 + (\Delta\vec{\bf d}_y)^2}

|\Delta\vec{\bf d}| = \rm \sqrt{(63)^2 + (30)^2}\ m = 69\ m

\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\Delta\vec{\bf d}_y}{\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x}\right)

\theta = \rm \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{+30\ m}{+63\ m}\right) = +25^\circ

Trigonometry Method

You could also find the resultant vector by solving the triangle using trigonometry.

  • Because the triangle does not contain a right angle, you must use the Law of Cosines and Law of Sines.
  • This method is never required; you can always use the component method.
  • For some problems the trigonometry method may be easier, especially when the triangle does contain a right angle.

Summary

The procedures for adding vectors and drawing vector diagrams (“tip-to-tail”) are not just for displacement vectors but for any kind of vector, including velocity, acceleration, and force.

To subtract a vector, add its negative.

  • In Cartesian form, reverse the sign of both components.
  • In polar form, add or subtract 180º to the direction.

Example 3

Calculate the acceleration of a boat that has an initial velocity of 6.50 m/s [+120°] and a final velocity, 20.0 seconds later, of 7.25 m/s [+75.0°].

Before we can calculate the acceleration, we need to find the change in velocity:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t}

where

\Delta\vec{\bf v} = \vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i

Velocity|\vec{\bf v}| / (m/s)\theta\vec{\bf v}_x = \\ |\vec{\bf v}|\cos\theta\vec{\bf v}_y = \\ |\vec{\bf v}|\sin\theta

-\vec{\bf v}_i6.50–60.0°+3.25–5.63
\Delta\vec{\bf v}5.31+15.0°+5.13+1.37

Now calculate the acceleration:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf v}}{\Delta t} = \rm \frac{5.31\ m/s\ [+15.0^\circ]}{20.0\ s} = 0.256\ m/s^2 \ [+15.0^\circ]

  4. A student can paddle a canoe with a speed of 5.00 m/s. The student wants to cross a stream that has a current of 1.85 m/s. The actual velocity of the student will be the vector sum of the paddling velocity added to the current velocity. Determine the direction that the student should paddle (i.e. how many degrees upstream) so that the resultant velocity will be straight across the stream (i.e. neither upstream nor downstream.) Also, determine the magnitude of the resultant velocity. Take the current direction to be the x-axis and the direction of the resultant velocity to be the y-axis.

    5. Vector Diagram

    Since we know the magnitudes of the current and paddling velocity, we can calculate the magnitude of the resultant velocity using Pythagoras:

    \vec{\mathbf v}_{net} = \mathrm{\sqrt{(5.00\ m/s)^2 - (1.85\ m/s)^2} = 4.65\ m/s}

    We can now calculate the paddling velocty in Cartesian form:

    \begin{align} \vec{\mathbf v}_{paddle} &= \vec{\mathbf v}_{net} - \vec{\mathbf v}_{current} \\ &= \mathrm{(0.00, +4.65)\ m/s - (1.85, 0.00)\ m/s} \\ &= \mathrm{(-1.85, +4.65)\ m/s} \end{align}

    The direction of the paddling velocity is:

    \theta = \mathrm{\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{+4.65}{-1.85}\right) + 180^\circ = 112^\circ}

    (i.e. 22° upstream from straight across)

Review

  1. Which form of 2D vector (polar or Cartesian) is more convenient when adding or subtracting vectors?

    2. Cartesian

  2. What do we mean by the resultant vector?

    3. The total of the vectors being added.

  3. Explain how to add vectors using each of these methods: (a) graphical method, (b) component method, (c) trigonometry method.
    • In the graphical method, draw the vectors accurately — tip-to-tail! — on graph paper using a protractor and ruler. Use the same scale for both axes. Draw the resultant vector (preferably in a different colour) as an arrow from the tail of the first vector to the tip of the last vector. The order in which vectors are added/drawn does not affect the resultant. Label all vectors. Measure the direction (angle) and magnitude (length of the arrow, based on the scale of the diagram) for the resultant vector.
    • In the component method, write all vectors in Cartesian form. Use the \sin and \cos functions to convert polar vectors to Cartesian form. Next add the x-components together to the get the x-component of the resultant. Now repeat for the y-components. If necessary, convert the resultant vector back to polar form using the Pythagoras and \tan^{-1} equations. This is the most precise and reliable method of vector addition!
    • Draw a sketch of the (tip-to-tail) vector diagram. Label all known sides and angles. Use the Pythagoras and/or \sin, \cos, or \tan equations to solve the triangle only if it must contain a 90° angle. Otherwise use the Sine Law and/or Cosine Law. This method can only be used to add two vectors at a time.
  4. Explain how to subtract vectors.

    5. To subtract a vector, add its negative! In Cartesian form, reverse the signs of both the x- and y-components. In polar form, add or subtract 180° from the direction.
