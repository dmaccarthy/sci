Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is the 2D version of uniformly accelerated motion.

  • This type of motion typically occurs when gravity causes a constant acceleration (if we ignore air resistance) in the -y direction while the object’s velocity has both x- and y-components.
  • The shape of a projectile’s trajectory is a parabola.
  • We know this because the relationship between the x- and y-components of the displacement is quadratic.
  • (You will study quadratic functions in Math 20.)
  • For a typical projectile problem, you will express all four of your kinematic vectors (acceleration, initial and final velocity, displacement) in Cartesian form.
  • To solve for any unknowns, treat the x and y-parts of the motions as two separate problems to be solved independently.

Example 1

A golfer imparts an initial velocity of 40.0 m/s [+52.0°] to a ball. The ball lands on the green 3.00 metres lower than the tee area. Calculate the “hang time” and “range” of the ball.

  • Hang time means the time the projectile is in motion before it contacts the ground.
  • Range means the horizontal displacement of the projectile before it hits the ground.
  • Since there is no acceleration in the x-direction, the intial and final velocities are the same.
  • Since time is a scalar, it does not have separate x- and y-components.
Variable / Unitxy
\vec{\bf a}m/s20–9.81
\vec{\bf v}_im/s40.0 cos(+52.0°)
= +24.6		40.0 sin(+52.0°)
= +31.5
\vec{\bf v}_fm/s–32.4
\Delta\vec{\bf d}m+161–3.00
\Delta ts6.52

Solve the y-motion as uniform accelerated motion. First find the final velocity...

v_{fy}^2 = v_{iy}^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}_y\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}_y

v_{fy} = \sqrt{v_{iy}^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}_y\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}_y}

v_{fy} = \sqrt{\rm{(31.5\ m/s)^2 + 2(-9.81\ m/s^2)(-3.00\ m)}}

\vec{\bf v}_{fy} = \rm{-32.4\ m/s}

Next find the time...

\vec{\bf a}_y = {\vec{\bf v}_{fy} - \vec{\bf v}_{iy} \over \Delta t}

\Delta t = {\vec{\bf v}_{fy} - \vec{\bf v}_{iy} \over \vec{\bf a}_y}

\Delta t = \rm{{-32.4\ m/s - (+31.5\ m/s) \over -9.81\ m/s^2} = 6.52\ s}

Now solve the x-motion as uniform motion...

\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x = \vec{\bf v}_x\Delta t = \rm{+24.6\ m/s\cdot 6.52\ s = +161\ m}

Example 2

A soccer ball is kicked from the ground with an initial velocity of 20.0 m/s [+60.0°]. A spectator sitting in the stands 10.0 metres above the field observes the ball passing the 10.0-metre mark on the way up and again on the way down. Find the times at which these events occur and how far down the field the ball has travelled.

Variable / Unitxy
\vec{\bf a}m/s20–9.81
\vec{\bf v}_im/s20.0 cos(+60.0°)
= +10.0		20.0 sin(+60.0°)
= +17.3
\vec{\bf v}_fm/s±10.2
\Delta\vec{\bf d}m+7.27 or +28.0+10.0
\Delta ts0.727 or 2.80

Solve the y-motion as uniform accelerated motion. First find the final velocity...

v_{fy} = \sqrt{v_{iy}^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}_y\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}_y}

v_{fy} = \sqrt{\rm{(17.3\ m/s)^2 + 2(-9.81\ m/s^2)(-10.0\ m)}} = \rm{\pm 10.2\ m/s}

Next find the time. Use the positive answer for the final velocity to find the time for the ball to reach 10-metres on the way up...

\Delta t = {\vec{\bf v}_{fy} - \vec{\bf v}_{iy} \over \vec{\bf a}_y}

\Delta t = \rm{{+10.2\ m/s - 17.3\ m/s \over -9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.727\ s}

Now solve the x-motion as uniform motion...

\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x = \vec{\bf v}_x\Delta t = \rm{+10.0\ m/s\cdot 0.727\ s = +7.27\ m}

To find the solution for when the ball is on the way back down, repeat the above calculations using the negative answer for \vec{\bf v}_{fy}...

\Delta t = \rm{{-10.2\ m/s - (+17.3\ m/s) \over -9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.80\ s}

\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x = \vec{\bf v}_x\Delta t = \rm{+10.0\ m/s\cdot 2.80\ s = +28.0\ m}

    \vec{\bf a}m/s20–9.81
    \vec{\bf v}_im/sv_i\cos(+70.0^\circ)v_i\sin(+70.0^\circ)
    \vec{\bf v}_fm/s
    \Delta\vec{\bf d}m+4.00+1.25

    \begin{align} \Delta\vec{\bf d}_x &= \vec{\bf v}_x \Delta t \\[10pt]{\rm +4.00\ m} &= v_i\cos(+70.0^\circ)\cdot \Delta t \end{align}

    For the y-motion...

    We can rearrange the x-equation...

    v_i \Delta t = \frac{\rm +4.00\ m}{\cos(+70.0^\circ)} = \rm +11.7\ m

    ...and substitute into the y-equation...

    {\rm +1.25\ m} = {\rm +11.7\ m}\cdot\sin(+70.0^\circ) + \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf g}(\Delta t)^2

    \frac{2}{\vec{\bf g}} ({\rm +1.25\ m} - {\rm +11.7\ m}\cdot\sin(+70.0^\circ)) = (\Delta t)^2

    v_i \Delta t = \rm +11.7\ m

    Use the time to find the initial speed...

    v_i = \rm \frac{+11.7\ m}{1.41\ s} = +8.30\ m/s

  7. Define projectile motion, including
    1. the shape of the trajectory,
    2. the cause of the motion,
    3. how to analyze the motion.

    8. Projectile motion is 2D motion with contant acceleration. It occurs near the ground when there are no forces other than gravity, which produces the constant acceleration. What makes the motion 2D is that the projectile is set in motion with an initial velocity that has a non-zero x-component while gravity acts in the -y-direction.

    The shape of the trajectory is a parabola. The cause of the motion is the constant acceleration produced by gravity.

    To analyze the motion, treat the x and y motion independently. The x motion is uniform (\vec{\bf a}_x = 0, \vec{\bf v}_{fx} = \vec{\bf v}_{ix}) and the y motion is uniform accelerated with an acceleration of \vec{\bf g}. Time is not a vector, so \Delta t is the same for both components of the motion.

  8. What do we mean by the hang time and the range in a projectile motion context?

    9. The hang time is the total time that the projectile remains airborne before it contacts the ground. The range is the horizontal component of the total displacement.

  9. Why might the motion of actual projectiles near Earth differ from the answers we get when we solve a projectile motion problem?

    10. Air resistance will affect the motion because it changes the acceleration. Also, gravity will not remain constant if the motion occurs over very large distance scales (e.g. hundreds of km or more).
