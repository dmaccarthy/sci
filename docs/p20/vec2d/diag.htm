Vector Diagrams

Example 1

Draw a vector diagram to illustrate the motion of a student from \vec{\bf d}_i = (–3.0, +2.0) m to \vec{\bf d}_f = (+2.0, –2.0) m. Measure the magnitude and direction of the vector.

\Delta\vec{\bf d} = 6.4 m [–39°]

Example 2

A soccer player undergoes a displacement of \Delta\vec{\bf d} = 35 m [125°]. Draw a vector diagram of the motion, taking the origin as the initial position. Determine the final position.

\vec{\bf d}_f = (–20, +29) m

Example 3

Draw a vector diagram showing the addition of two forces: \vec{\bf F}_1 = 25 N [62°] and \vec{\bf F}_2 30 N [–70°].

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = 23 m [–16°]

Individually, complete the Practice and Review sections from the Vector Diagrams handout.

Practice

Use graph paper for all vector diagrams.

  1. A student undergoes a displacement from position {\vec{\mathbf{d}}}_i = (+3.0, –2.0) m to {\vec{\mathbf{d}}}_f = (+1.0, +2.0) m.
    1. Illustrate the student’s motion by plotting their initial and final positions and drawing the displacement vector as an arrow. Indicate the scale of the diagram and be sure to use the same scale on both axes.

    2. Use a protractor to measure the direction of the student’s displacement.
    3. Use a ruler to measure the magnitude of the student’s displacement. Be sure to account for the scale of the diagram.

    2. \Delta\vec{\bf d} = 4.5 m [117°]

  2. A passenger in a car uses the GPS in their smartphone to measure the car’s velocity as 80.0 km/h with a direction of W 32.0° N.
    1. Use a ruler and protractor to draw an illustration of the velocity vector. Indicate the scale of the diagram and use the same scale on both axes. Draw the velocity vector with its tail at the origin.

    2. Record the coordinates of the vector’s tip.

    3. \vec{\bf v} = (-68, +42) km/h

  3. A spacecraft experiences a force of 15.0 kilonewtons at a direction of –65.0° due to the thrust of its rockets.
    1. Draw an illustration of the force vector with its tail at the origin.

    2. Record the coordinates of the force vector’s tip.

    4. {\vec{\bf F}}_{net} = (+6.34, -13.6) kN

  4. A lacrosse player runs 25.0 metres [45.0°] to retrieve the ball, and then turns and runs another 35.0 metres [192°] to get into position to take a shot.
    1. Draw an illustration of the player’s motion by drawing the two displacement vectors in a tip-to-tail fashion. You can take the player’s initial position as the origin. It is strongly recommended that you draw a “rough sketch” on scrap paper first, to help you decide on an appropriate scale for your “good” drawing.

    2. Record the coordinates of the player’s final position.
    3. Draw the player’s resultant vector (a displacement vector from their initial to their final position that ignores the actual path that they followed) on the vector diagram.
    4. Use a ruler and protractor to measure the magnitude and direction if the lacrosse player’s resultant displacement. Be sure to account for the scale of the diagram.

    5. \Delta\vec{\bf d} = (-16.6, +10.4) m = 19.6 m [148°]

  5. A ball rolling down a hill experiences two forces: the gravity force is {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g = 3.50 newtons [–90.0°] and the “normal force” (the force of the ground pushing on the ball) is {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_n = 3.40 newtons [+76.0°].
    1. Determine the resultant force by drawing the two forces in a tip-to-tail fashion. Draw the resultant force vector (preferably in a different colour) as well as the two forces being added.

    2. Record the coordinates of the tip of the resultant force vector.
    3. Measure the magnitude and direction of the resultant force.

    6. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = (+0.823, -0.201) N = 0.847 N [346°]

Review

  1. Why is it essential to use the same scale for both the x- and y-axes when drawing vector diagrams?

    2. Using different scales will distort the measurements of both angles and magnitudes.

  2. Explain how to draw a vector diagram for a single vector given (a) the initial and final coordinates, or (b) the magnitude (length) and direction (angle) of the vector.

    3. Given the initial and final coordinates, mark these two points on the diagram, and then connect them with an arrow pointing from initial to final.

    Given the magnitude and direction, hold your protractor with its centre at the starting position (use the origin if not specified!) and the 0° mark parallel to the x-axis. Mark the desired direction and lightly sketch a line in that direction from the starting point. Now use the scale of the drawing as labelled on one axis to measure out the desired magnitude. Complete the arrow so it has the desired length.

  3. Explain how to measure (a) the initial and final coordinate, and (b) the magnitude and direction of a vector from a vector diagram.

    4. The initial and final coordinates can be read directly off of the diagram.

    Measure the direction using a protractor centred on the tail of the vector with the 0° mark parallel to the x-axis. Measure the length of the vector with a ruler or by marking a piece of paper and then use the scale of the diagram as marked on one axis to see what magnitude that length corresponds to.

  4. Define the resultant vector.

    5. The sum of two or more vectors.

  5. Explain how to draw a vector diagram that illustrates the addition of two or more vectors.

    6. Draw the arrows representing the vectors to be added in a tip-to-tail fashion. Draw the resultant vector from the tail of the first vector to the tip of the last vector.
