Polar & Cartesian Forms

Components

Imagine an object undergoing a displacement from an initial position {\vec{\bf{d}}}_i = (+3.0,\ −2.0)\ \rm{m} to a final position {\vec{\bf{d}}}_f = (+1.0,\ +2.0)\ \rm{m}.

  • The displacement can be calculated — just like for 1D — as the change in position:

    \begin{align}\Delta\vec{\bf{d}} &={\vec{\bf{d}}}_f-{\vec{\bf{d}}}_i \\&= (+1.0,\ +2.0)\ \rm{m} − (+3.0,\ −2.0)\ \rm{m}\end{align}

  • Because the motion is 2D, the positions are ordered pairs with x and y coordinates.
  • Arithmetic is performed on the components separately...
  • In the x direction the motion from +3.0 to +1.0 m gives a displacement of –2.0 m.
  • In the y direction the motion from –2.0 to +2.0 m gives a displacement of +4.0 m.

\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}= (-2.0,\ +4.0)\ \rm{m}

  • When we use the x and y components to describe a vector, this is called the Cartesian form of the vector (named after French mathematician Rene Descartes.)
  • In the vector diagram above, the displacement vector is drawn as a red arrow from {\vec{\bf{d}}}_i to {\vec{\bf{d}}}_f and is labelled \Delta\vec{\bf{d}}.
  • The x and y components are drawn tip-to-tail as yellow arrows and are labelled \Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_x and \Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_y.
  • For a vector diagram to be accurate, we must always use the same scale for both the x- and y-axis.

Cartesian → Polar Conversion

As the vector diagram shows, a vector and its components form a right-angle triangle.

  • The x component will be the ‘Adjacent’ side, the y component will be the ‘Opposite’ side, and the resultant displacement will be ‘Hypotenuse’.
  • We can use trigonometry to calculate the magnitude and direction (polar form) of the vector:

    \left|\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}\right|=\sqrt{(\Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_x)^2 + (\Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_y)^2}

    \tan{\theta}=\frac{\Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_y}{\Delta{\vec{\bf{d}}}_x}

  • Substituting the values –2.0 m and +4.0 m for the x and y components gives us the polar form of the vector:

    \Delta\vec{\bf{d}} = 4.5\ \rm{m}\ [117°]

  • When calculating the direction using the tan−1 operation, it is important to know that your calculator will give the correct answer only when the x component is positive.
  • For cases where the x component is negative, you must add 180° to the angle your calculator gives!

Example 1

The velocity of a bird is (+8.00, –2.50) m/s. Express this is polar form.

\left|\vec{\bf{v}}\right|=\sqrt{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_x^2+{\vec{\bf{v}}}_y^2}

\left|\vec{\bf{v}}\right|=\rm{\sqrt{(+8.00\ m/s)^2 + (-2.50\ m/s)^2} = 8.38\ m/s}

\tan{\theta}=\frac{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_y}{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_x} = {-2.50\over +8.00} =\ -0.3125

\theta=\tan^{-1}(-0.3125) = \ -17.4^\circ

If we reversed the signs of both components, \vec{\bf v} = (–8.00, +2.50) m/s, the equations would give us the same answer!

  • This is because there are two different angles that have the same \tan ratio. Your Math teacher may have called this the CAST Rule.
  • When using \tan^{-1} with a negative x-component, add 180° to the angle your calculator gives you.
  • Always check your vector diagram to ensure that your answer is in the correct quadrant.

Polar → Cartesian Conversion

When a vector is known in polar form and we want to convert it to Cartesian form, we can use the other trigonometry relations: sine and cosine.

  • The trigonometry relations apply not just to displacement vectors but to any vector, including velocity, acceleration, and force.

Example 2

Suppose a force of 50.0 newtons is applied with a direction of 160° to push an object up an incline. Calculate the Cartesian components.

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_x=\left|\vec{\bf{F}}\right|\cos{\theta} = 50.0\ \rm{N}\cdot\cos 160^\circ = -47.0\ \rm{N}

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_y=\left|\vec{\bf{F}}\right|\sin{\theta = 50.0\ \rm{N}\cdot\sin 160^\circ = +17.1\ \rm{N}}
