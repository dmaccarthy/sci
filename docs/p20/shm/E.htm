Energy of SHM

Energy of SHM

An object in SHM has the greatest amount of kinetic energy and the least amount of potential energy at the equilibrium position.

  • We therefore use equilibrium as the origin or “reference level” when calculating potential energy for SHM.
  • When an object has a position \vec{\bf{x}} relative to equilibrium, its potential energy equals the work that will be done on the object as it moves back to the equilibrium position.

  • Initially, the restoring (net) force on the object is {\vec{\bf{F}}}_i=-k{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i.
  • As the mass moves back to equilibrium, the net force will get smaller because the restoring force is directly proportional to the distance from equilibrium.
  • When it reaches equilibrium, the net force will be zero.
  • The average net force will therefore be one-half of the initial force:

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_{avg}=-\frac{1}{2}k{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i

  • To calculate the work done on the mass (the kinetic energy gained, and the potential energy lost) we multiply the average force by the displacement:

\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}={\vec{\bf{x}}}_f-{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i=-{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i

W={\vec{\bf{F}}}_{avg}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf{d}}=\left(-\frac{1}{2}k{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i\right)\left(-{\vec{\bf{x}}}_i\right)\ =\frac{1}{2}kx_i^2

The potential energy, relative to equilibrium, of an object exhibiting SHM is given by the equation:

E_p=\frac{1}{2}kx^2

  • Since the restoring force is the net force, it includes both gravity and elastic forces; therefore, this potential energy formula includes both the gravitational and elastic potential energy!
  • When using energy conservation with SHM, remember that the mechanical energy is entirely kinetic at equilibrium (\vec{\bf{x}} = 0), entirely potential at \vec{\bf{x}} = ±A, and a mix of kinetic and potential at any other position.

Example 1

A 60.0-gram mass exhibits SHM with an amplitude of 3.00 cm when attached to a spring with k = 20.0 N/m. Calculate the (i) mechanical energy of the system, and (ii) maximum speed of the mass.

Alternative 1 (Energy Conservation): The mechanical energy is the total of the kinetic and potential energy:

E=\frac{1}{2}mv^2+\frac{1}{2}kx^2

When the mass is furthest from equilibrium, x = A and v = 0:

E = \rm{0\ J + {1\over 2}(20.0\ N/m)(0.0300\ m)^2 = 0.00900\ J}

When the mass is passing through equilibrium, x = 0, the energy is entirely kinetic:

E=\frac{1}{2}mv^2

\frac{2E}{m}=v^2

v = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m}}= \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 0.00900\ J\over 0.0600\ kg} = 0.548\ m/s}

Alternative 2 (SHM Relations): As an alternative to using energy conservation, we could have calculated the period of the motion first:

T = 2\pi\sqrt{m\over k} = \rm{2\pi \sqrt{0.0600\ kg \over 20.0\ N/m} = 0.344\ s}

Now use the equation for v_{max}:

v_{max} = {2\pi A \over T} = \rm{{2\pi\cdot 0.0300\ m \over 0.344\ s} = 0.548\ m/s}

Both methods give the same answer!

Example 2

Calculate the speed of the mass from Example 1 when the mass is halfway between equilibrium and its amplitude.

The mass now has both potential energy (because it’s not at equilibrium) and kinetic energy (because it’s not at its maximum extension).

Calculate the potential energy first using x = A/2 = \rm{1.50\ cm}:

E_p = {1\over 2} kx^2 = \rm{{1\over 2} (20.0\ N/m) (0.0150\ m)^2 = 0.00225\ J}

Because the mechanical energy is conserved, we can calculate the kinetic energy:

E_k = E - E_p = \rm{0.00900\ J - 0.00225\ J = 0.00675\ J}

Now evaluate the speed just as in Example 1:

v = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 0.00675\ J \over 0.0600\ kg} = 0.474\ m/s}

There is no alternative solution this time, because the v_{max} equation can only tell us the speed at equilibrium, not at any other point.

Practice

  1. A 250-gram mass executes SHM with k = 32.0 N/m. Calculate the potential energy of the system at the following positions relative to equilibrium: (a) \vec{\bf{x}} = 5.00 cm, (b) \vec{\bf{x}} = –10.0 cm, and (c) \vec{\bf{x}} = 25.0 cm.

    2. E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (32.0\ N/m)\ (0.0500\ m)^2 = 0.0400\ J}

    E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (32.0\ N/m)\ (-0.100\ m)^2 = 0.160\ J}

    E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (32.0\ N/m)\ (0.250\ m)^2 = 1.00\ J}

  2. A mass oscillating on a spring with k = 100 N/m has 0.320 joules of potential energy. How far is the mass from its equilibrium position?

    3. E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2

    \frac{2E_p}{k} = x^2

    x = \pm\sqrt{\frac{2E_p}{k}} = \rm{\pm\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.320\ J}{100\ N/m}} = \pm 0.0800\ m}

  3. A 68.0-gram mass oscillates with an amplitude of 3.00 cm at the end of a spring with k = 18.0 N/m.
    1. Calculate the potential energy at \vec{\bf{x}} = 3.00 cm. Note that when \vec{\bf{x}}=\pm A, the kinetic energy is zero!

      2. E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (18.0\ N/m)\ (0.0300\ m)^2 = 0.00810\ J

    2. Using mechanical energy conservation, calculate the speed of the mass as it passes through equilibrium.

      3. At equilibrium, all of the energy is kinetic...

      E = \frac{1}{2} mv_{max}^2

      \frac{2E}{m} = v_{max}^2

      v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.00810\ J}{0.0680\ kg}} = 0.488\ m/s}

    3. Use the SHM equations to calculate first T, and then v_{max}. Does the maximum speed agree with your answer from (b)?

      4. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.0680\ kg}{18.0\ N/m}} = 0.386 \ s}

      v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T} = \rm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.0300\ m}{0.386\ s} = 0.488\ m/s}

    4. Calculate the potential energy at \vec{\bf{x}} = 1.00 cm and use the result to calculate the mass’s kinetic energy. Draw energy bar charts for the initial and “final” energy.

      5. E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (18.0\ N/m) (0.0100\ m)^2 = 0.000900\ J}

      E_k = E - E_p = \rm{0.00810\ J - 0.000900\ J = 0.00720\ J}

    5. Calculate the speed of the mass when it is 1.00 cm from equilibrium.

      6. v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.00720\ J}{0.0680\ kg}} = 0.460\ m/s}

    6. Calculate the speed of the mass when it is 2.00 cm from equilibrium. Include another energy bar chart.

      7. E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (18.0\ N/m) (0.0200\ m)^2 = 0.00360\ J}

      E_k = E - E_p = \rm{0.00810\ J - 0.00360\ J = 0.00450\ J}

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.00450\ J}{0.0680\ kg}} = 0.364\ m/s}

  4. A 175 g mass is attached to the end of an ideal spring and allowed to settle at its equilibrium position. The mass is then moved 4.20 cm from equilibrium and then released from rest. The resulting motion is observed to have a period of 0.420 s.
    1. Calculate the elastic constant, k.

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}

      T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2m}{k}

      kT^2 = 4\pi^2m

      k = \frac{4\pi^2m}{T^2} = \rm{\frac{4\pi^2 (0.175\ kg)}{(0.420\ s)^2} = 39.0\ N/m}

    2. Determine the total mechanical energy of the system.

      3. Initially, x_i = -A and v_i = 0...

      \begin{align}E &= \frac{1}{2} kx^2 + \frac{1}{2} mv^2 \\\\&= \rm{\frac{1}{2} (39.0\ N/m) (-0.0420\ m)^2 + 0.00\ J} \\\\&= \rm{0.0344\ J}\end{align}

    3. Determine the speed of the mass when it passes through its equilibrium position.

      4. At equilibrium, all of the energy is kinetic...

      E = \frac{1}{2} mv_{max}^2

      \frac{2E}{m} = v_{max}^2

      v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.0344\ J}{0.175\ kg}} = 0.627\ m/s}

    4. Determine the speed of the mass when it passes through a point 1.00 cm from equilibrium.

      5. E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (39.0\ N/m) (0.0100\ m)^2 = 0.00195\ J}

      E_k = E - E_p = \rm{0.0344\ J - 0.00195\ J = 0.0325\ J}

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.0325\ J}{0.175\ kg}} = 0.609\ m/s}

    5. Determine the speed of the mass when it passes through a point 4.00 cm from equilibrium.

      6. E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (39.0\ N/m) (0.0400\ m)^2 = 0.0312\ J}

      E_k = E - E_p = \rm{0.0344\ J - 0.0312\ J = 0.00320\ J}

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.00320\ J}{0.175\ kg}} = 0.191\ m/s

  5. A 79.0 g mass attached to an ideal spring with k = 19.0 N/m is released with an initial speed of 35.0 cm/s from a position 3.60 cm from equilibrium.
    1. Determine the total mechanical energy of the system.

      2. E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.0790\ kg) (0.350\ m/s)^2 = 0.00484\ J

      E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (19.0\ N/m) (0.0360\ m)^2 = 0.0123\ J

      E = E_k + E_p = \rm 0.00484\ J + 0.0123\ J = 0.0172\ J

    2. Determine the amplitude of the motion.

      3. At the furthest point in the motion, x = \pm A and the energy is all potential...

      E = \frac{1}{2} kA^2

      \frac{2E}{k} = A^2

      A = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{k}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.0172\ J}{19.0\ N/m}} = 0.0425\ m

    3. Determine the speed of the mass as it passes through equilibrium.

      4. At equilibrium, all of the energy is kinetic...

      E = \frac{1}{2} mv_{max}^2

      \frac{2E}{m} = v_{max}^2

      v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.0172\ J}{0.0790\ kg}} = 0.659\ m/s

  6. Challenge! Show that for a simple pendulum with a small amplitude, the equation for potential energy of SHM gives approximately the same result as the gravitational potential energy equation (E_g=mgh) when h is measured relative to equilibrium. While it is difficult to prove this in general, it is easier to show for a specific length, angle, and mass; assume L = 1.00 m, \theta = 15.0°, and m = 1.00 kg. You will need to draw a diagram!

    7. Use trig to calculate the height at 15.0°. The vertical side of the diagram consists of two segments — the adjacent side of the triangle (L\cos\theta) and the height relative to equilibrium (h) — which together equal the length of the pendulum.

    h + L\cos\theta = L

    h = L - L\cos\theta = \rm{1.00\ m - 1.00\ m\cdot \cos 15.0^\circ = 0.0341 m}

    Calculate the “exact” potential energy...

    E_g = mgh = \rm{1.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.0341\ m = 0.334\ J}

    The potential energy for SHM is:

    E_p=\frac{1}{2}kx^2

    We can find k for a simple pendulum by equating the two formulas for the period...

    2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}

    Cancel the 2\pi and then square both sides...

    \frac{L}{g}=\frac{m}{k}

    kL=mg

    k=\frac{mg}{L} = {\rm \frac{1.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{1.00\ m} = 9.81\ N/m}

    The distance from equilibrium, measured along the circular arc (shown in blue on the diagram) is 15/360 of a complete circle, which would have a circumference of 2\pi L...

    x = 2\pi L \frac{15}{360} = \rm{2\pi\cdot 1.00\ m \frac{15}{360} = 0.262\ m}

    E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = {\rm \frac{1}{2} (9.81\ N/m) (0.262\ m)^2 = 0.336\ J}

    If we had measured the distance from equilbrium horizontally (as labelled x_H)...

    x_H = L\sin\theta = {\rm 1.00\ m\cdot \sin 15.0^\circ = 0.259\ m}

    Using this distance to calculate the potential energy gives...

    E_p = \frac{1}{2} kx_H^2 = {\rm \frac{1}{2} (9.81\ N/m) (0.259\ m)^2 = 0.329\ J}

    The potential energy that we calculate using the SHM formula is close to, but slightly higher (0.336 J) or lower (0.329 J) than the true energy (0.334 J) depending on how we measure x. This is because the motion of a simple pendulum is only approximately SHM.

    2. Where is the origin or “reference level” for this equation? Why is this a convenient choice for calculating potential energy?
    3. Does it matter if this potential energy is gravitational, elastic, or a combination of the two types of potential energy? Explain.
  2. Explain how we can use energy conservation to calculate the speed of an object at any point in the motion. (Recall that v_{max} only tells us the speed at equilibrium.)
