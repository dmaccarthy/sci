Equilibrium & Oscillations

Equilibrium

An equilibrium is a position where objects experience zero net force.

  • An object placed at rest at equilibrium will remain at rest there, according to Newton’s Laws.
  • In the diagram above, the equilibiums are the highest and lowest points, where the normal force exactly balances the gravity force.
  • An equilibrium is unstable when an object close to the equilibrium experiences a net force that accelerates it away from the equilibrium position.
  • Near the top of a hill, the unbalanced force produced by the gravity and normal forces points downhill, away from the equilibrium at the top.
  • An equilibrium is stable when objects close to the equilibrium experience an unbalanced force that accelerates the object back toward equilibrium.
  • Near the bottom of a valley, the gravity and normal forces produce an unbalanced force that pulls the object back toward its equilibrium at the bottom.
  • The net force near a stable equilibrium may be described as a restoring force because it acts to restore the equilibrium.
  • Objects near a stable equilibrium will move back-and-forth across the equilibrium, as a result of their inertia which causes them to “overshoot”. This type of motion is called an oscillation.

Simple Harmonic Motion

The simplest mathematical function that can describe an oscillation is a sinusoidal (sine or cosine) function.

Motion that can be described exactly by a sinusoidal function is called simple harmonic motion (SHM).

SHM is characterized by two quantities that can be measured from the \vec{\bf d}{\rm -}t graph:

  • Amplitude (A) is the maximum distance that the object moves from its equilibrium position.
  • The graph above uses the equilibrium position as the origin.
  • We use the symbol \vec{\bf d} for the position relative to an arbitrary origin, and \vec{\bf x} as the position relative to equilibrium.
  • Period (T) is the time it takes for each cycle or repetition of the motion.
  • The reciprocal of period is called frequency:

    f = {1\over T}

  • The unit of frequency is called hertz: Hz = s–1.

We will see in a future lesson that SHM occurs when the restoring force is directly proportional to the object’s distance from equilibrium.

  • E.g. an object will experience exactly twice as much net force when it is 10 cm from equilibrium compared to when it is only 5 cm from equilibrium.

  1. A simple pendulum is constructed by hanging a mass from a string and attaching the other end of the string to a fixed location. If we can neglect air resistance, the motion of the mass will be determined by the gravity and tension forces.
    1. Draw a free body diagram for the mass when it is at its equilibrium position. Explain how you know this position is the equilibrium.

      2. Because the gravity force is vertically down, equilibrium occurs when the tension force is directly up.

    2. Determine whether the equilibrium is stable or unstable. To do this, draw a free body diagram for the mass close to equilibrium, and determine whether the net force is directed toward or away from the equilibrium.

      3. Stable. When the mass is to the left of equilibrium, the tension force will be angled to the right. The net force will be a restoring force directed back toward the equilibrium.

    3. Comment on the statement: “The mass has the least potential energy when it is passing through its equilibrium position.”
      4. This is true for the pendulum because the equilibrium is the lowest point of the motion so the gravitational potential energy (mgh) is at a minimum.

Review

    1. Define equilibrium.

      2. A position where an object experiences zero net force, meaning it will remain at rest if it is at rest initially.

    2. Explain the difference between a stable and unstable equilibrium.

      3. A stable equilibrium happens when objects close to the equilibrium experience a restoring force directed back toward the equilibrium. An unstable equilibrium occurs when the net force acts away from the equilibrium position.

    3. Define restoring force and oscillation.

      4. A restoring force is a net force that acts to accelerate the object back toward its equilibrium. An oscillation is a back-and-forth motion, centred on the equilibrium position, caused by the restoring force.

  2. Define simple harmonic motion.

    3. SHM is a type of oscillation where the object’s motion is described by a sine or cosine function. SHM occurs when the restoring force is directly proportional to the distance from equilibrium.

  3. Define amplitude, period, and frequency as these terms apply to SHM.

    4. The amplitude is the greatest distance from equilibrium that occurs during an oscillation. The period is the time for one single repetition of the oscillation. Frequency is the reciprocal of the period.
