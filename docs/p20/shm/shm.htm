Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion is an oscillation where the motion of the object is described exactly by a sine-shaped function.

  • SHM occurs near an equilibrium when the restoring force is directly proportional to the object’s position, measured relative to equilibrium.
  • We will sometimes use the symbol \vec{\bf{x}} to represent the position relative to equilibrium, rather than \vec{\bf{d}} for position relative to an arbitrary origin.
  • The relationship between the restoring force and position can be described by the equation:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = -k\vec{\bf x}

SHM Graphs

  • The object’s velocity and position are “out of sync” with each other in SHM: when the object is furthest from equilibrium (the highest and lowest points of the \vec{\bf{x}}{\rm -}t graph) the velocity is zero and when the object passes through equilibrium its speed is a maximum.

  • This can be understood in terms of energy conservation: kinetic energy (related to speed) is at a maximum when potential energy (related to distance from equilibrium) is at a minimum.
  • One interesting property of sinusoidal functions is that the slope will also be sinusoidal but shifted by one-quarter of a period!
  • If the \vec{\bf{x}}{\rm -}t graph can be described by a sine function, the \vec{\bf{v}}{\rm -}t graph will be a cosine function and the \vec{\bf{a}}{\rm -}t graph will be a negative sine function. (Click on the graph!)

SHM Equations

All three motion graphs have the same shape and time scale (period), but their vertical scales differ.

  • Calculus tells us that for any sine-shaped graph, the maximum slope of the graph is the amplitude multiplied by 2\pi/T:

v_{max} ={2\pi A\over T}

a_{max} ={2\pi v_{max}\over T}

  • We can combine these two equations to eliminate either T or v_{max}:

a_{max} = {v_{max}^2 \over A} = {4\pi^2 A\over T^2}

  • Although we have not learned the relevant calculus yet, we can demonstrate experimentally that these relationships are correct.

Example

A simple pendulum is constructed so that it will have a period of 2.00 seconds and a maximum acceleration of 0.500 m/s2. Assuming the motion is SHM, calculate the speed of the mass as it passes through equilibrium and the amplitude of the motion.

Since we know the period and maximum acceleration, we can find the amplitude from the a_{max} equation above:

a_{max} = {4\pi^2 A\over T^2}

a_{max}T^2 = 4\pi^2 A

{a_{max}T^2 \over 4\pi^2} = A

Evaluating:

A = \rm{{(0.500\ m/s^2) (2.00\ s)^2 \over 4\pi^2} = 0.0507\ m}

We can then calculate v_{max} from the other equation:

v_{max} = {2\pi A \over T} = \rm{{2\pi (0.0507\ m) \over (2.00\ s)} = 0.159\ m/s}

Practice

  1. A mass executes SHM with a period of 0.36 s and an amplitude of 4.0 cm.
    1. Calculate the frequency of the SHM.

      2. f = \frac{1}{T} = \rm{\frac{1}{0.36\ s} = 2.8\ Hz}

    2. Calculate the maximum velocity and maximum acceleration of the mass.

      3. v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T} = \rm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.040\ m}{0.36\ s} = 0.70\ m/s}

      a_{max} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2} = \rm{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.040\ m}{(0.36\ s)^2} = 12\ m/s^2}

    3. Draw accurate graphs of \vec{\bf{x}}, \vec{\bf{v}} and \vec{\bf{a}} for two complete periods.

      4. Graphs: Position | Velocity | Acceleration

  2. When a car hits a bump in the road, it executes SHM (it bounces up and down on the springs in its shock absorbers.) If the maximum velocity of the SHM is 25.0 cm/s and the maximum acceleration is 1.50 m/s2, determine the period, frequency, and amplitude of the SHM.

    3. Calculate the amplitude:

    a_{max} = \frac{v_{max}^2}{A}

    a_{max} A = v_{max}^2

    A = \frac{v_{max}^2}{a_{max}} = \rm{\frac{(0.250\ m/s))^2}{1.50\ m/s^2} = 0.0417\ m}

    Calculate the period:

    v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T}

    v_{max}T = 2\pi A

    T = \frac{2\pi A}{v_{max}}= \rm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.0417\ m}{0.250\ m/s} = 1.05\ s}

    Calculate frequency:

    f = \frac{1}{T} = \rm{\frac{1}{1.05\ s} = 0.955\ Hz}

  3. A pendulum has an amplitude of 42.0 cm and a maximum acceleration of 1.34 m/s2. Calculate the pendulum’s period, frequency, and maximum velocity.

    4. Calculate period:

    a_{max} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2}

    a_{max} T^2 = 4\pi^2 A

    T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{a_{max}}

    T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 A}{a_{max}}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.420\ m}{1.34\ m/s^2}} = 3.52\ s}

    Calculate frequency:

    f = \frac{1}{T} = \rm{\frac{1}{3.52\ s} = 0.284\ Hz}

    Calculate v_{max}:

    v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T} = \rm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.420\ m}{3.52\ s} = 0.750\ m/s}

  4. When a tuning fork is struck with a hammer, it begins to vibrate in SHM with an amplitude of 1.8 mm and a frequency of 360 Hz.
    1. Calculate the period of the motion.

      2. T = \frac{1}{f} = \rm{\frac{1}{360\ Hz} = 0.00278\ s}

    2. Draw and label an accurate graph of the motion for two complete periods.

      3. Graph

    1. A 50.0 g mass hangs vertically from a spring. Calculate the elastic force that the spring exerts on the mass when the mass is at equilibrium*. Include a free body diagram. (*Recall that this means the forces are balanced.)

      2. \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm{0.0500\ kg (-9.81\ m/s^2) = -0.491\ N}

      Since the net force is zero, the elastic force is \vec{\bf F}_{elas} = +0.491 N.

    2. When the mass is pulled 1.00 cm down from its equilibrium position, the spring exerts an upward elastic force of 0.720 N. Calculate the restoring force that acts on the mass.

      3. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_{elas} = \rm{(-0.491\ N) + 0.720\ N = +0.230\ N}

    3. Calculate the acceleration of the mass. Will the acceleration remain constant? Describe the motion of the mass.

      4. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+0.230\ N}{0.0500\ kg} = +4.59\ m/s^2}

      This acceleration will not be constant since the elastic force changes as the spring becomes more or less stretched. The mass will oscillate in SHM.

    4. Calculate the speed of the mass as it passes through equilibrium and use this speed to calculate the kinetic energy.

      5. a_{max} = \frac{v_{max}^2}{A}

      a_{max} A = v_{max}^2

      v_{max} = \sqrt{a_{max} A} = \rm{\sqrt{4.59\ m/s^2\cdot 0.0100\ m} = 0.214\ m/s}

      E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (0.0500\ kg) (0.214\ m/s)^2 = 0.00115\ J}

    5. Verify that the kinetic energy calculated in (d) is equal to the work done by the restoring force in moving the mass from its initial position (\vec{\bf{x}} = \rm{-1.00\ cm}) to its final position (\vec{\bf{x}} = \rm{0.00\ cm}).

      6. First, calculate the average net force:

      \vec{\bf F}_{avg} = \rm{\frac{0.230\ N + 0.00\ N}{2} = 0.115\ N}

      Now calculate the work:

      W = \vec{\bf F}_{avg}\cdot \Delta \vec{\bf d} = \rm{0.115\ N\cdot 0.0100\ m = 0.00115\ J}

Review

  1. Identify the similarities and differences among the \vec{\bf{d}}{\rm -}t, \vec{\bf{v}}{\rm -}t, and \vec{\bf{a}}{\rm -}t graphs of SHM.

    2. All three graphs have the same shape and repeat with the same period. Each graph is shifted one-quarter of a period relative to the previous graph. The vertical scales of the graphs are different because they measure different quantities with different units.

  2. What does the symbol \vec{\bf{x}} mean in the context of SHM?

    3. This is the position of the object relative to the equilibrium position.

  3. State the relationships between period, amplitude, maximum velocity, and maximum acceleration for SHM.

    4. v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T}

    a_{max} = \frac{v_{max}^2}{A} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2}

  4. Explain the cause of SHM in terms of the restoring force and relate this to the \vec{\bf{x}}{\rm -}t and \vec{\bf{a}}{\rm -}t graphs.

    5. If the \vec{\bf{x}}{\rm -}t graph is a sine function, the \vec{\bf{a}}{\rm -}t graph will be the negative of a sine function (because it’s shifted exactly one-half of a period). This means that the net (restoring) force must be directly proportional and in the opposite direction to \vec{\bf{x}}:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = -k\vec{\bf x}
