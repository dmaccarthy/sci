Simple Pendulum

Simple Pendulum

The restoring (net) force on a simple pendulum is the resultant of the gravitational and tension forces, as shown in the free body diagram and the vector diagram below.

By applying trigonometry to the right triangles in both diagrams, and using the relations for F_{net} = kx and F_g = mg:

\sin{\theta}=\frac{x_H}{L}=\frac{F_{net}}{F_g}=\frac{kx}{mg}

We can now isolate k:

\frac{x_H}{L}=\frac{kx}{mg}

\frac{mgx_H}{L}=kx

k=\frac{mg}{L}\cdot\frac{x_H}{x}

This shows that the motion of a simple pendulum is not SHM, because the ratio of x (distance from equilibrium measured along a circular arc) and x_H (distance from equilibrium measured horizontally) is not constant as the pendulum moves.

  • However, if the maximum angle is small (30° or less), x and x_H are approximately equal, so we can cancel them to get:

    k\cong\frac{mg}{L}

    which is constant.

We can calculate the period of a simple pendulum by substituting this k value into the equation for period of SHM:

T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{\left(\frac{mg}{L}\right)}}=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

  • Since the mass cancelled, the period of a simple pendulum is independent of the mass!
  • Galileo would agree with this result since gravity is the force driving the motion. The length of the pendulum and the gravitational field strength are the factors that determine the pendulum’s period.
  • When the effects of air resistance are included, mass and amplitude do affect the period to some extent (but not as much as the length does.)

Example 1

A grandfather clock uses a pendulum as a timing device. The simplest design is to use a pendulum with a period of exactly 1.00 or 2.00 seconds, to advance the clock’s second hand either once per period, or whenever the pendulum passes equilibrium (twice per period). Determine the lengths of the pendulums need to give the desired periods.

Rearrange the equation to isolate L:

T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

T^2=4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}

gT^2=4\pi^2 L

\frac{gT^2}{4\pi^2}=L

Evaluate for a period of 1.00 s:

L = \rm{{9.81\cdot m/s^2 (1.00\ s)^2 \over 4\pi^2} = 0.248\ m}

Evaluate for a period of 2.00 s:

L = \rm{{9.81\cdot m/s^2 (2.00\ s)^2 \over 4\pi^2} = 0.994\ m}

Example 2

A simple pendulum provides an easy way to measure g.

Suppose on some planet, a 25.0-cm long pendulum has a period of 2.50 seconds. Calculate the strength of the planet’s gravitational field.

Rearrange the equation to isolate g:

T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

T^2=4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}

g=4\pi^2\frac{L}{T^2}

Evaluate:

g = \rm{4\pi^2 (0.250\ m) / (2.50\ s)^2 = 1.58\ m/s^2}

Practice

    1. Calculate the period of a 1.00 m long simple pendulum when g = 9.81 m/s2.

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{1.00\ m}{9.81\ m/s^2}} = 2.01\ s}

    2. If the same pendulum were used on a planet with g = 1.70 m/s2, what would its period be?

      3. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{1.00\ m}{1.70\ m/s^2}} = 4.82\ s}

    1. What length would a simple pendulum have to be to have a period of 5.00 s on Earth?

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

      T^2 = 4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}

      gT^2 = 4\pi^2 L

      L = \frac{gT^2}{4\pi^2} = \rm{\frac{9.81\ m/s^2\cdot (5.00\ s)^2}{4\pi^2} = 6.21\ m}

    2. What would the period of the pendulum become if you doubled its length? [Hint: The period does not double.]

      3. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{12.42\ m}{9.81\ m/s^2}} = 7.07\ s}

      When the length doubles, the period increases by a factor of \sqrt{2} because of the square root operation in the equation!

    1. Astronauts landing on a newly discovered planet find that an 80.0 cm long simple pendulum has a period of 1.75 s. What is the planet’s gravitational field strength?

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

      T^2 = 4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}

      gT^2 = 4\pi^2 L

      g = \frac{4\pi^2L}{T^2} = \rm{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.800\ m}{(1.75\ s)^2} = 10.3\ m/s^2}

    2. Suppose the pendulum has an amplitude of 2.00 cm and a mass of 454 grams. Calculate the mechanical energy of the pendulum.

      3. Calculate the maximum speed of the pendulum and use this speed to calculate kinetic energy:

      v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T} = \rm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.0200\ m}{1.75\ s} = 0.0718\ m/s}

      E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(0.454\ kg) (0.0718\ m/s)^2 = 0.00117\ J

      Because the maximum speed occurs at equilibrium, the potential energy is zero!

  4. A grandfather clock uses a 65.0 cm long simple pendulum as a timing device.
    1. Calculate the pendulum’s period on Earth.

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.650\ m}{9.81\ m/s^2}} = 1.62\ s}

    2. Calculate the pendulum’s period on a planet where g = 5.25 m/s2.

      3. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.650\ m}{5.25\ m/s^2}} = 2.21\ s}

    3. If the grandfather clock was designed to operate correctly on Earth, would it run fast or slow on the low gravity planet? After 24.0 hours, how many hours would have passed according to the (incorrect) clock?

      4. The number of swings completed in 24.0 hours (86 400 s) on the planet is:

      \rm{\frac{86400\ s}{2.21\ s} = 39095}

      Since the clock was designed for Earth, the time it will record is:

      \rm{39095 (1.62\ s) = 63334\ s = 17.6\ h}

      The clock is running slow!
