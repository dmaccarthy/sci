Period of SHM

Period of SHM

For SHM, graphs of \vec{\bf{x}} and \vec{\bf{a}} have identical shapes but are negatives of each other.

  • This means that these quantities are directly proportional to each other but in opposite directions.
  • Mathematically, sinusoidal functions are the only functions that have this property.

We can write Newton’s 2nd Law as:

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_{net}=m\vec{\bf{a}}=-k\vec{\bf{x}}

  • SHM is caused by a restoring force that is directly proportional to the distance from equilibrium.
  • The constant k in this equation is a measure of the strength of the restoring force.
  • The unit of k is newtons per metre, N/m.

The maximum amount of restoring force occurs when the mass if furthest from equilibrium:

F_{max} = ma_{max} = kA

We can substitute:

a_{max}=\frac{4\pi^2A}{T^2}

...to get:

m\frac{4\pi^2A}{T^2}=kA

We can cancel the A and rearrange to isolate T:

m\frac{4\pi^2}{T^2}=k

4\pi^2m=kT^2

\frac{4\pi^2m}{k}=T^2

T=2\pi\sqrt\frac{m}{k}

If we know the mass and the coefficient k (which determines the restoring force) we can calculate what the period of the SHM will be.

  • The period does not depend on the amplitude. Increasing the amplitude causes the object’s speed and distance both to increase by the same proportion, so that the time required to complete each cycle does not change.
  • Larger masses will oscillate with a longer period.
  • A stronger restoring force will cause the period to be shorter.

Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s Law states that for most elastic objects, the elastic force is directly proportional to the distance that the object is stretched or compressed, at least over some range of distances:

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_{elas}=-k\Delta L

  • An elastic object (e.g. a spring) will cease to obey Hooke’s Law if it is overstretched.

For a mass attached to a horizontal or vertical spring, the motion will be SHM if:

  • there are no unbalanced forces except for gravity and the elastic force, and
  • the spring remains within the “elastic limit” where Hooke’s Law remains valid.

Example 1

A 100 gram mass oscillates with an amplitude of 1.20 cm on the end of a spring with k = 30.0 N/m. Calculate the period and the maximum velocity and acceleration.

T=2\pi\sqrt{m\over k} = \rm{2\pi\sqrt{0.100\ kg\over 30.0\ N/m} = 0.363\ s}

v_{max} ={2\pi A\over T} = \rm{{2\pi\cdot 0.0120\ m\over 0.363\ s} = 0.208\ m/s}

a_{max} = {v_{max}^2 \over A} = \rm{{(0.208\ m/s)^2 \over 0.0120\ m} = 3.60\ m/s^2}

Example 2

A 2.00 kg mass exhibits SHM with a period of 1.50 seconds. Find the restoring force coefficient k.

T=2\pi\sqrt{m\over k}

T^2 = 4\pi^2 {m\over k}

kT^2 = 4\pi^2 m

k = {4\pi^2 m \over T^2} = \rm{{4\pi^2\cdot 2.00\ kg \over (1.50\ s)^2} = 35.1\ N/m}

Practice

    1. A vertical spring with k = 850 N/m compresses 2.80 cm (at equilibrium) when an object is placed on top of it. Determine the weight and mass of the object. [hint: At equilibrium, the elastic and gravity forces are balanced!]

      2. \vec{\mathbf F}_{elas} = -k\Delta L = \mathrm{-850\ N/m\cdot (-0.0280\ m) = +23.8\ N}

      Since the elastic force is 23.8 N up, the force of gravity (weight) is 23.8 N down because the forces are balanced at equilibrium.

      m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \mathrm{\frac{23.8\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.43\ kg}

    2. If the object is set in motion vertically, what will its period be?

      3. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = \mathrm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2.43\ kg}{850\ N/m }} = 0.336\ s}

  2. A vertical spring stretches 5.20 cm when a 63.0 g mass is attached to its end.
    1. What is the elastic constant of the spring?

      2. F_{elas} = F_g = mg = \mathrm{0.0630\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 0.618\ N}

      k = \frac{F_{elas}}{\Delta L} = \mathrm{\frac{0.618\ N}{0.0520\ m} = 11.9\ N/m}

    2. If the 63.0 g mass is replaced by a 105 g mass, what will the total stretch of the spring be?

      3. F_{elas} = F_g = mg = \mathrm{0.105\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 1.03\ N}

      \Delta L = \frac{F_{elas}}{k} = \mathrm{\frac{1.03\ N}{11.9\ N/m} = 0.0867\ m}

    3. Calculate the period of the oscillations for both masses.

      4. T_1 = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m_1}{k}} = \mathrm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.0630\ kg}{11.9\ N/m }} = 0.457\ s}

      T_2 = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m_2}{k}} = \mathrm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.105\ kg}{11.9\ N/m }} = 0.590\ s}

    1. A spring with k = 65.0 N/m is attached to a 95.0 g mass. Predict the period and frequency of the SHM when the mass is set in motion.

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = \mathrm{2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.0950\ kg}{65.0\ N/m }} = 0.240\ s}

      f = \frac{1}{T} = \mathrm{\frac{1}{0.240\ s} = 4.16\ Hz}

    2. Calculate the maximum velocity and acceleration of the mass if its amplitude is 1.50 cm.

      3. v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T} = \mathrm{\frac{2\pi\cdot 0.0150\ m}{0.240\ s} = 0.392\ m/s}

      a_{max} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2} = \mathrm{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.0150\ m}{(0.240\ s)^2} = 10.3\ m/s^2}

    1. A 275 g mass attached to the end of spring executes SHM with a period of 0.950 s. Calculate the k value of the spring.

      2. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}

      T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 m}{k}

      kT^2 = 4\pi^2 m

      k = \frac{4\pi^2 m}{T^2} = \mathrm{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.275\ kg}{(0.950\ s)^2} = 12.0\ N/m}

    2. If the mass has a maximum velocity of 0.580 m/s, determine its amplitude.

      3. v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T}

      v_{max}T = 2\pi A

      A = \frac{v_{max}T}{2\pi} = \mathrm{\frac{0.580\ m/s\cdot0.950\ s}{2\pi} = 0.0877\ m}

    3. Draw accurate graphs of the object’s position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time.

      4. a_{max} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2} = \mathrm{\frac{4\pi^2\cdot 0.0877\ m}{(0.950\ s)^2} = 3.84\ m/s^2}

      Graphs: Position | Velocity | Acceleration

  5. A spring has k = 32.0 N/m. What mass would need to be attached to the spring to produce SHM with a period of 1.00 s?

    6. T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}

    T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 m}{k}

    kT^2 = 4\pi^2 m

    m = \frac{kT^2}{4\pi^2} = \mathrm{\frac{32.0\ N/m\cdot (1.00\ s)^2}{4\pi^2} = 0.811\ kg}
