Model Equations

Linear Relations

As you learned in Math 10, all linear functions can be written in the form:

y=kx+b

  • x refers to the manipulated variable.
  • y refers to the responding variable.
  • k refers to the slope of the relationship.
  • b refers to the y-intercept.

Slope

Output of a 12-Volt Battery

Slope can be calculated as “rise” divided by “run”:

k = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}

  • In principle, (x_1, y_1) and (x_2, y_2) can be any two points on the best-fit line.
  • Do not use data points that are above or below the line!
  • In practice, your calculation will be most accurate if you choose points that are as far apart as possible.

k = \rm{\frac{(2.0 - 11.4)\ V}{(5.0 - 0.0)\ A} = \frac{-9.4\ V}{5.0\ A} = -1.9\ V/A}

y-Intercept

The y-intercept is the value where the line crosses the y-axis.

  • In this example, we can see from the graph that the intercept is 11.4 volts.
  • Sometimes, the intercept may not be visible on the graph.
  • You can calculate the intercept (b) by rearranging the equation:

    y = kx + b

    y - kx = b

    b = \rm{2.0\ V - (-1.88\ V/A)\ 5.0\ A = 11.4\ V}

Model Equation

Once you know the slope and the y-intercept, you can write the equation in the form: y = kx+b.

  • Replace x and y by the actual variable names or symbols.
  • Replace k and b by the calculated values, including units.

\mathcal{E} = {\rm (-1.9\ V/A)} I + {\rm 11.4\ V}

We can use the equation to make predictions about the data.

Example 1

Predict the voltage when the current is 3.5 A.

\mathcal{E} = {\rm (-1.9\ V/A)} I + {\rm 11.4\ V}

\mathcal{E} = \rm{(-1.9\ V/A)\ {\color{red} 3.5\ A} + 11.4\ V = 4.8\ V}

  • A prediction within the range of data is called interpolation.

Example 2

What current would be needed for the voltage to reach zero?

\mathcal{E} = {\rm (-1.9\ V/A)} I + \rm{11.4\ V}

\mathcal{E} - {\rm 11.4\ V} = {\rm (-1.9\ V/A)} I

\frac{\mathcal{E} - {\rm 11.4\ V}}{\rm -1.9\ V/A} = I

I = \rm{\frac{{\color{red} 0.0\ V} - 11.4\ V}{-1.9\ V/A} = 6.0\ A}

  • A prediction outside the range of data is called extrapolation.

Practice

  1. Science 10 students performed an experiment for their “Heat” chapter in which they measured the temperature (T) of some water as they burned a marshmallow beneath it. They constructed a scatter plot for the temperature as a function of the time (t) for which the water was heated. They noted that their best-fit line passed through the points (0.0 s, 18 °C) and (20.0 s, 25 °C).
    1. Determine the model equation for the students’ data.

      2. k = \frac{T_2 - T_1}{t_2 - t_1} = \rm{\frac{(25 - 18)\ ^\circ C}{(20.0 - 0.0)\ s} = 0.35\ ^\circ C/s}

      b = \rm{18\ ^\circ C}

      T = ({\rm 0.35\ ^\circ C/s})\ t + {\rm 18\ ^\circ C}

    2. Use the model equation to predict the water temperature after 15.0 seconds.

      3. T = \rm{(0.35\ ^\circ C/s)\ 15.0\ s + 18\ ^\circ C = 23\ ^\circ C}

    3. Use the model equation to predict the water temperature after 60.0 seconds.

      4. T = \rm{(0.35\ ^\circ C/s)\ 60.0\ s + 18\ ^\circ C = 39\ ^\circ C}

    4. Use the model equation to predict how long it would take the water to boil (100 °C).

      5. T - {\rm 18\ ^\circ C} = ({\rm 0.35\ ^\circ C/s})\ t

      t = \frac{T - \rm{18\ ^\circ C}}{\rm{0.35\ ^\circ C/s}} = \frac{(100 - 18)\ \rm{^\circ C}}{\rm{0.35\ ^\circ C/s}} = \rm{234\ s}

      2. Graph

      The y-intercept can be read from the graph: b = 81.1 kg.

      Using the points (0 days, 81.1 kg) and (30 days, 78.5 kg), the slope is:

      k = \frac{m_2 - m_1}{t_2 - t_1} = \rm{\frac{(78.5 - 81.1)\ kg}{(30-0)\ day} = -0.087\ kg/day}

      m - {\rm 81.1\ kg} = ({\rm -0.087\ kg/day})\ t

      t = \frac{m - \rm{81.1\ kg}}{\rm{-0.087\ kg/day}}

      t = \frac{\rm{(75.0 - 81.1)\ kg}}{\rm{-0.087\ kg/day}} = \rm{70\ days}

    4. Estimate what Kelly’s mass will be after 60 days. What assumptions are you making? Are these assumptions realistic? Explain.

      5. m = \rm{(-0.087\ kg/day)\ 60\ days + 81.1\ kg = 75.9\ kg}

      We are assuming that the weight loss continues at the same rate until at least day 60.

  4. A photocell is a device that converts light into electricity. In Physics 30, you will learn that there is a linear relationship between the frequency/colour (f) of light used and the maximum possible voltage (\mathcal{E}) the photocell can produce:

    \mathcal{E} = hf-W

    Physics students found that when red light (f = 4.69 × 1014 Hz) is used, a particular photocell produces a voltage of 0.741 volts, and when blue light (f = 6.38 × 1014 Hz) is used the photocell voltage is 1.44 volts. Hz stands for hertz, which is the SI unit of frequency.
    1. Find the values of h and W for this photocell.

      2. The slope is h:

      h = \frac{\Delta\mathcal{E}}{\Delta f} = {\rm \frac{(1.44-0.741)\ V}{(6.38-4.69)\times 10^{14}\ Hz} = 4.13\times 10^{-15}\ V/Hz}

      Now solve for W:

      \begin{align}\mathcal{E} + W &= hf \\ W &= hf-\mathcal{E} \\ &= {\rm 4.13\times 10^{-15}\ V/Hz\cdot 4.69\times 10^{14}\ Hz - 0.741\ V} \\ &= {\rm 1.20\ V} \end{align}

    2. Predict the voltage produced by the photocell when violet light (f = 7.50 × 1014 Hz) is used.

      3. \begin{align}\mathcal{E} &= hf-W \\ &= {\rm 4.13\times 10^{-15}\ V/Hz\cdot 7.50\times 10^{14}\ Hz - 1.20\ V} \\ &= {\rm 1.90\ V}\end{align}

    3. Predict the frequency needed to obtain a voltage of 3.00 volts.

      4. \begin{align}\mathcal{E} + W &= hf \\[10pt] f &= \frac{\mathcal{E} + W}{h} \\[10pt] &= {\rm \frac{(3.00 + 1.20)\ V}{4.13\times 10^{-15}\ V/Hz}} \\[10pt] &= {\rm 1.02\times 10^{15}\ Hz}\end{align}

  5. Students collected data for a mug of hot chocolate cooling over time. They determined that the relationship between temperature (T) and time (t) was best modelled by the non-linear relationship:

    T = 21^\circ{\rm C} + 47^\circ{\rm C} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/({\rm 23\ min})}

    1. Predict the temperature of the hot chocolate after 5.0 minutes.
      2. T = 21^\circ{\rm C} + 47^\circ{\rm C} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{5.0/23} = {\rm 61^\circ C}
    2. Predict the time it will take for the hot chocolate to reach a temperature of 50° C. Unless you’ve studied logarithms (Math 30-1) you will not be able to solve this algebraically, but you should be able to solve it graphically using a graphing calculator or software.

      3. Based on the graph, the temperature will reach 50° C after 16 minutes.

Review

  1. What do we mean by a model equation for the data collected during an experiment?

    2. A model equation is an equation that describes the best-fit line (or best-fit curve if the data is non-linear) for data collected through experiments.

  2. Explain how to determine the model equation when the scatter plot indicates a linear relationship between the manipulated and responding variables.

    3. When the scatter plot of the data appears linear, add a best-fit line that passes as close as possible to the data points. Calculate the slope (k) and y-intercept (b) of the line using:

    y=kx+b

    When writing the model equation, change x and y to the correct symbols for the manipulated and responding variables.

  3. Define interpolation and extrapolation and explain how a model equation makes this easier.

    4. Interpolation is when you use the model equation to make predictions about data within the range of available data. Extrapolation is when your predictions extend beyond the range of available data.
