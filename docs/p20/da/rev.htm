Experiment Design

The manipulated variable is the quantity that you are intentionally changing during an experiment. The responding variable is the quantity that you are testing, to see how it is affected by the manipulated variable.

  • Your hypothesis is a prediction of how changes to the manipulated variable will affect the responding variable.
  • All independent variables (ones that could be changed directly) except for the one being manipulated, must be kept constant during an experiment.
  • These are called controlled variables.

Scatter Plots

When the manipulated and responding variables are both numerical values, you will need to draw a scatter plot of the data.

  • The manipulated variable goes on the horizontal (x) axis. The responding variable goes on the vertical (y) axis.
  • Label both axes with the variable name, symbol, and unit.
  • Label the “tick marks” on the axes using a constant interval, chosen to fit all data points while using all available space.
  • Give your graph a descriptive title.
  • Add a best-fit line only if the data follows a linear trend; do not use a best-fit line for obviously curved data.
  • The best-fit line should pass through the mean (average) of all data points and should minimize the average distance between the line and the data points.

Non-Linear Data

When the data is non-linear and believed to follow a power relationship, you can transform the data by applying the appropriate power.

  • For example, if the expected relationship between the length (L) and period (T) of a simple pendulum is

    T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{g}} L^{\frac{1}{2}}

    you would calculate L^{\frac{1}{2}} for each data point, and graph the results instead of just L.
  • When the variable being transformed appears in the denominator, you must make the exponent negative.
  • When the variable appears under a square root or cube root sign, the exponent is 1/2 or 1/3 respectively.

Model Equations

Calculate the slope of your best-fit line by identifying the coordinates of two points that lie on the best-fit line and are far apart: (x_1, y_1) and (x_2, y_2).

  • Calculate the “rise” between the two points, \Delta y = y_2 - y_1.
  • Calculate the “run” between the two points, \Delta x = x_2 - x_1.
  • The slope, k, is “rise / run”:

    k = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}

The y-intercept, b, is the coordinate where the best-fit line crosses the y-axis.

  • If b is not visible on your graph, you can calculate it:

    b = y - kx

  • k is the slope of the line.
  • x and y refer to the coordinates of either of the two points you used to calculate the slope.

Your model equation has the form:

y = kx+b

  • Replace k and b by the calculated values, including units.
  • Replace x and y by the symbols for the quantites you graphed.
  • If you transformed your data to obtain a linear graph, remember to include the exponent...
  • For example, replace x by L^{\frac{1}{2}} instead of just L if that is how your transformed the data.

You can use your TI-83/84 Calculator to construct scatter plots and use regression analysis to find a model equation for linear, power, quadratic, and other non-linear type of data.
