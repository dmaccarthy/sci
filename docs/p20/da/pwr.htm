Transforming Power Data

Suppose our hypothesis is an equation (from our Data Sheet?) that predicts the relationship between manipulated and responding variables:

I=\frac{P}{4\pi r^2}

  • Variables that appear in the denominator are considered to have a negative exponent:

    I=\frac{P}{4\pi} r^{-2}

  • Any equation of the form y = ax^n is classified as a power relationship.
  • The responding variable, I, does not have an exponent so it does not need to be transformed.
  • We can transform the manipulated variable by calculating r^{-2}.
  • We do this because -2 is the exponent that appears with r in the equation!
  • The first two points in the data table are...

\rm{(0.750\ m)^{-2} = 1.78\ m^{-2}}

\rm{(1.00\ m)^{-2} = 1.00\ m^{-2}}

  • Transform the remaining data, and tabulate and graph the transformed data...

  • If the data does follow a power relationship and we have used the correct exponent, -2 in this case, the transformed data will now be linear!

Model Equation

Now that the graph has been linearized, we can calculate its slope and y-intercept as with any other line.

b = \rm{0.0\ W/m^2}

k = \rm{\frac{(1.79 - 0.00)\ W/m^2}{(2.00 - 0.00)\ m^{-2}} = 0.895\ W}

  • When you write the model equation, y=kx+b, remember to include the power:

    I = {\rm (0.895\ W)}\ r^{-2}

  • The y-variable is I; what was graphed on the y-axis.
  • The x-variable is r^{-2}; what was graphed on the x-axis.
  • You can substitute the model equation into the theoretical equation:

    I= \frac{P}{4\pi} r^{-2} = {\rm (0.895\ W)}\ r^{-2}

  • You could then algebraically solve for P:

    \frac{P}{4\pi} = {\rm 0.895\ W}

    P = 4\pi\cdot {\rm 0.895\ W} = \rm 11.2\ W

Example 1

Students conducted an experiment on sound waves to test the equation v=f\lambda. They manipulated the frequency of the waves (f) and controlled the speed (v). The wavelength (\lambda) was the responding variable. How should they transform the data?

Isolate the responding variable:

\lambda = \frac{v}{f}

Since f appears in the denominator, use a negative power:

\lambda = vf^{-1}

Transform the data by calculating f^{-1} for each data point.

Example 2

Students conducted an experiment on a simple pendulum to see how the length (L) of the pendulum affected its period (T), the time for one swing. Their hypothesis was:

T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

How should they transform the data?

The square root sign is equivalent to a power of \frac{1}{2}:

T = 2\pi\frac{L^{\frac{1}{2}}}{g^{\frac{1}{2}}}

Transform the data by calculating L^{\frac{1}{2}} for each data point.

  • Since g is a controlled variable, it will not be graphed, so it doesn’t matter that it has an exponent!
  • Alternatively, we could get rid of the square root sign by squaring the original equation:

    T^2 = 4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}

  • This alternative says we can transform the data by calculating T^2, while not transforming L.
