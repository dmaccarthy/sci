Planetary Motion (Kepler’s Laws)

Ptolemy & Copernicus

Pre-modern scientists such as Ptolemy believed that the Earth was stationary at the centre of the universe.

  • The Sun, Moon, stars, and planets orbited around the Earth.
  • This belief was based on the observation that there is no sensation of motion while at rest on Earth, and also on theological beliefs.
  • Models with the Earth at the centre are called geocentric.
  • One of the major problems with the geocentric model is that it does not adequately explain the observed retrograde motion of planets like Mars.
  • When seen in Earth’s sky, every few years Mars seems to stop in its orbit, reverse direction for a few months, and then return to its original path.

Copernicus constructed a new model with the Sun at the centre of the solar system, with Earth and the other planets orbiting around the Sun in perfect circles.

  • This model explains the retrograde motion of Mars as being caused by Earth overtaking Mars as it orbits.
  • Models with the Sun at the centre are called heliocentric.

Kepler

Johannes Kepler used precise telescope observations (made by Tycho Brahe) to conclude that the heliocentric model was generally correct, but that the orbits of the planets were not perfect circles.

Kepler’s 1st Law: Planets orbit the Sun along ellipse-shaped trajectories with the Sun located at one of the focus points.

F = Focus; C = Geometric Centre
P = Perihelion; A = Aphelion

  • An ellipse is a “stretched” circle.
  • The longest “diameter” of an ellipse is called the major axis.
  • The focus point (Sun) is located on the major axis but is off-centre.
  • The point where the orbit crosses the major axis close to the focus is called the perihelion. This is the point of the orbit that is closest to the Sun.
  • The orbit’s furthest point from the Sun is called the aphelion.

Kepler’s 2nd Law: The areas of the sectors formed by the Sun and the planet’s initial and final positions (shaded regions of the diagram) are the same for equal amounts of time at every point in the orbit; the orbit “sweeps out” area at a constant rate.

  • Closer to the perihelion, the planet’s potential energy due to the Sun’s gravity is lower, thus its kinetic energy is greater.

Kepler’s 3rd Law: There is a non-linear relationship between the mean orbital radius (r) and the period (T), the time for one complete orbit):

T^2=kr^3

  • The constant k = \mathrm{1\ yr^2/au^3 = 2.97 \times 10^{−19}\ s^2/m^3}, is determined by the Sun’s gravity.
  • The mean orbital radius is half the length of the major axis.
  • Planets further from the Sun take longer to complete each orbit.
  • The period of Earth’s orbit is defined as a terrestrial year (\mathrm{yr = 3.16 \times 10^7\ s}) and Earth’s mean orbital radius is defined as an astronomical unit (\mathrm{au = 1.496 \times 10^{11}\ m}).
  • The relationship can be expressed alternatively using ratios of the periods and orbits for two different planets:

    \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^2=\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^3

  • When expressed this way any units can be used, as long as the units are the same for both orbiting objects, since the units will cancel.

Kepler’s Laws apply not only to the motion of the planets around the Sun, but also to any situation where the motion of an object is determined solely by the gravity of a single, large, spherical object, such as the motion of the Moon or an artificial satellite around the Earth.

  • The value of the constant k is different for Earth’s gravity (or any other planet or star) than for the Sun’s.
  • For orbits around Earth, we use the terms perigee and apogee instead of perihelion and aphelion.
  • For orbits around other stars or planets we can use the general terms periapsis and apoapsis.

Practice

  1. An unidentified object, possibly a comet or dwarf planet, orbits our Sun with a mean orbital radius of 8.32 × 1012 m. Calculate the object’s orbital period. [Hint: Express the mean orbital radius in au first.]

    2. r = \mathrm{\frac{8.32\times 10^{12}\ m}{1.496\times 10^{11}\ m/au} = 55.6\ au}

    \begin{align} \\T^2 &= kr^3 \\\\ T &= \sqrt{kr^3} \\\\ &= \mathrm{\sqrt{1.00\ \frac{yr^2}{au^3}\cdot (55.6\ au)^3}} \\\\ &= \mathrm{415\ yr} \end{align}

  2. Scientists planning to launch a research satellite to study the Sun want the satellite to orbit the Sun with a period of 3.00 years.
    1. At what mean orbital radius does the satellite need to orbit the Sun? Answer in astronomical units and in metres.

      2. \begin{align} T^2 &= kr^3 \\\\ \frac{T^2}{k} &= r^3 \\\\ r &= \left(\frac{T^2}{k}\right)^{1/3} \\\\ &= \mathrm{\left(\frac{(3.00\ yr)^2}{1.00\ yr^2/au^3}\right)^{1/3}} \\\\ &= \mathrm{2.08\ au} \\\\ &= \mathrm{3.12\times 10^{11}\ m} \end{align}

    2. How many times larger than Earth’s orbital radius is this? Explain why the orbital radius is not three times bigger than Earth’s even though the orbital period is exactly three times that of Earth.
      3. The satellite orbits at 2.08 times the orbital radius of Earth (i.e. 2.08 au) even though the period is three times larger than Earth’s. This is because Kepler’s 3rd Law is non-linear; the orbital period and orbital radius have different exponents in the equation.
  3. An interstellar spacecraft discovers a planet orbiting a nearby star with an orbital radius of 7.21 × 1011 m and a period of 206 days.
    1. What is the “Kepler coefficient” for this star?

      2. T^2 = kr^3

      k = \frac{T^2}{r^3} = \mathrm{\frac{(206\ d)^2}{(7.21\times 10^{11}\ m)^3} = 1.13\times 10^{-31}\ d^2/m^3}

    2. If the spacecraft wants to orbit with a period of 21.0 days, what orbital radius should it enter?
      3. Algebra is the same as #2a...

      \begin{align} r &= \left(\frac{T^2}{k}\right)^{1/3} \\\\ &= \mathrm{\left(\frac{(21.0\ d)^2}{1.13\times 10^{-31}\ d^2/m^3}\right)^{1/3}} \\\\ &= \mathrm{1.57\times 10^{11}\ m} \end{align}

    1. The Moon orbits around Earth with a radius of 3.84 × 108 m and a period of 27.3 days. Use this data to calculate the Kepler coefficient (k) for objects orbiting Earth.
      2. Algebra is the same as #3a...

      k = \frac{T^2}{r^3} = \mathrm{\frac{(27.3\ d)^2}{(3.84\times 10^8\ m)^3} = 1.32\times 10^{-23}\ d^2/m^3}

    2. If a satellite were placed in Earth orbit with an orbital radius of 8500 km, what would the period of the satellite’s orbit be?
      3. Algebra is the same as #1...

      \begin{align} T &= \sqrt{kr^3} \\\\ &= \mathrm{\sqrt{1.32\times 10^{-23}\ d^2/m^3\cdot (8.50\times 10^6\ m)^3}} \\\\ &= \mathrm{0.0899\ d = 2.15\ h} \end{align}

    3. A geosynchronous orbit is one in which the orbital period of the satellite is equal to the rotational period of the Earth (24.0 hours or 1.00 day). What would the mean orbital radius of a satellite have to be for the satellite to be in a geosynchronous orbit around Earth?
      4. Algebra is the same as #2a...

      \begin{align} r &= \left(\frac{T^2}{k}\right)^{1/3} \\\\ &= \mathrm{\left(\frac{(1.00\ d)^2}{1.32\times 10^{-23}\ d^2/m^3}\right)^{1/3}} \\\\ &= \mathrm{4.24\times 10^7\ m} \end{align}

  5. An Apollo command module orbits the Moon at an altitude of 80.0 km. It has an orbital period of 1.93 hours. The Moon itself has a radius of 1740 km.
    1. What is the radius of the command module’s orbit?
      2. The radius of the Moon itself is 1740 km. Since the command module orbits 80.0 km above the surface, its orbital radius is:

      r = \mathrm{1740 km\ + 80.0\ km = 1820\ km = 1.820\times 10^6\ m}

    2. What orbital radius would a satellite need to have so that it would orbit the Moon with a period equal to 1.00 Earth day?

      3. \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^3

      \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^{2/3} = \frac{r_2}{r_1}

      r_1 \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^{2/3} = r_2

      r_2 = \mathrm{1.82\times 10^6\ m \left(\frac{24.0\ h}{1.93\ h}\right)^{2/3}} = \mathrm{9.77\times 10^6\ m}
