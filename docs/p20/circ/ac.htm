Centripetal Acceleration

Centripetal Acceleration

Although the speed (scalar!) of an object is constant for uniform circular motion, the object’s velocity (vector!) is continuously changing due to changes in direction.

  • This means that the object is accelerating and there must be an unbalanced force causing the object to move in a circle.
  • If there was no unbalanced force, the object would have to move in a straight line.

The illustration below (left) shows the motion of an object from P_1 to P_2 along a circle centred at point C. The illustration on the right is the vector diagram showing the change in velocity (\Delta{\vec{\bf v}}).

  • The blue and red triangles are similar; they are both isosceles triangles with the same angles.
  • Similar triangles have the same ratio of sides:

    {s \over r} = {\Delta v\over v}

  • Since s is the distance from P_1 to P_2, s=v\Delta t:

    {v\Delta t\over r} = {\Delta v\over v}

  • We can rearrange this to calculate the acceleration:

    a_c = {\Delta v\over \Delta t} = {v^2\over r}

  • The subscript “c” is for centripetal; it reminds us that the direction of the acceleration vector is always directed toward the centre for UCM.
  • We can get an alternate form of the equation by substituting v = {2\pi r\over T} to eliminate v:

    a_c=\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}

  • These equations are valid only for the instantaneous acceleration; this is because the distance between P_1 and P_2 measured along the circular arc is only approximately equal to the line segment s, when P_1 and P_2 are very close together.
  • The net force is also in the centripetal direction accoring to Newton’s 2nd Law:

    {\vec{\bf{F}}}_{net}=m{\vec{\bf{a}}}_c

Example 1

A satellite orbiting at a radius of 7000 km experiences a centripetal acceleration of 8.14 m/s2. Calculate the orbital speed and period of the satellite.

a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}

a_c r = v^2

v = \sqrt{a_c r}

v = \rm{\sqrt{8.14\ m/s^2\cdot 7.00\times 10^6\ m}}

v = \rm{7.55\times 10^3\ m/s}

v = {2\pi r\over T}

vT = 2\pi r

T = {2\pi r\over v}

T = \rm{{2\pi\cdot 7.00\times 10^6\ m\over 7.55\times 10^3\ m/s}}

T = \rm{5827\ s = 1.62\ h}

Example 2

A curve on the highway has a radius of 250 metres. After an ice storm, the road is slippery and the coefficient of friction is 0.100. Assuming the road is unbanked (horizontal), what is the fastest safe speed for a car to move through the curve?

Because the road is unbanked, the normal and gravity forces balance:

F_n = F_g = mg

The only unbalanced force is friction:

F_f = ma = \mu F_n = \mu mg

Divide by m:

a = \mu g = \rm 0.100\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 0.981\ m/s^2

The equation for centripetal acceleration is:

a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}

a_c r = v^2

v = \sqrt{a_c r} = \rm \sqrt{0.981\ m/s^2\cdot 250\ m} = 15.7\ m/s = 56.4\ km/h

Example 3

What would happen to a car that tried to take the curve at 25.0 m/s?

Solve for the radius:

a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}

a_c r = v^2

r = \frac{v^2}{a_c} = \rm \frac{(25.0\ m/s)^2}{0.981\ m/s^2} = 637\ m

Since the radius of the car’s motion is much larger than the radius of the road, the car will slide to the outside and end up in the ditch!

Connection to SHM

The equations for UCM are similar to the equations for SHM:

v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}

a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}

The reason is that when an object is moving in a circle with constant speed, its x and y coordinates will change as a \sin or \cos function...

Practice

  1. A satellite orbits the Earth in a circular path. The satellite’s period is 3.98 hours and the acceleration due to Earth’s gravity is 2.45 m/s2. (Remember that g becomes smaller than 9.81 m/s2 the further one gets from Earth’s surface.) Calculate the satellite’s speed and the radius of its orbit.

    2. Remember to convert the time to standard units, T = \rm{3.98\ h\cdot 3600\ s/h = 14328\ s}:

    a_c = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}

    a_cT^2 = 4\pi^2 r

    r = \frac{a_cT^2}{4\pi^2} = \rm \frac{2.45\ m/s^2 (14328\ s)^2}{4\pi^2} = 1.27\times 10^7\ m

    Be sure to enclose the demoninator in parentheses (4\pi^2) when entering it on your calculator!

    v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = \rm \frac{2\pi (1.27\times 10^7\ m)}{14328\ s} = 5.59\times 10^3\ m/s

    1. There is a 3.20 g stone lodged in the tread of a bicycle tire. The tire has a 26-inch diameter (1 inch = 2.54 cm). What is the stone’s centripetal acceleration when the bike is being ridden at 27.0 km/h?

      2. r = \rm 26\ in / 2 = 13\ in\cdot 0.0254\ m/in = 0.33\ m

      a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \rm \frac{(7.50\ m/s)^2}{0.33\ m} = 1.7\times 10^2\ m/s^2

    2. Calculate the net force that the stone experiences.

      3. F_{net} = ma_c = \rm{0.00320\ kg\ (1.7\times 10^2\ m/s^2) = 0.54\ N}

  3. A 183 kg satellite orbits a planet. The satellite experiences a gravitational force of 1.30 × 103 N while orbiting with a radius of 7500 km.
    1. Use Newton’s 2nd Law to calculate the satellite’s acceleration.

      2. a_c = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{1.30\times 10^3\ N}{183\ kg} = 7.10\ m/s^2}

    2. Calculate the satellite’s velocity and orbital period.

      3. \begin{align} a_c &= \frac{v^2}{r} \\[10pt] ra_c &= v^2 \\[10pt] v &= \sqrt{ra_c} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\sqrt{7.50\times 10^6\ m \cdot 7.10\ m/s^2}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{7.30\times 10^3\ m/s} \\[10pt] v &= \frac{2\pi r}{T} \\[10pt] vT &= 2\pi r \\[10pt] T &= \frac{2\pi r}{v} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{2\pi (7.50\times 10^6\ m)}{7.30\times 10^3\ m/s}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{6.46\times 10^3\ s = 1.79\ h} \end{align}

    1. Identify the three types of force that act on a car as it turns a corner? Assuming the road is unbanked (horizontal), which of those forces balance? Which force causes the centripetal acceleration?

      2. Gravity and normal balance; friction is unbalanced.

    2. A car rounding a circular turn with r = 250 m can have a maximum centripetal acceleration of 7.00 m/s2. What is the maximum speed that the car can have while rounding the turn?

      3. \begin{align} a_c &= \frac{v^2}{r} \\[10pt] ra_c &= v^2 \\[10pt] v &= \sqrt{ra_c} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\sqrt{250\ m \cdot 7.00\ m/s^2}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{41.8\ m/s} \end{align}

    3. What would happen to the car if it attempted the turn at a faster speed?

      4. Its turning radius would become larger than the radius of the road.

    4. When the road is slippery, the maximum centripetal acceleration is reduced to 2.80 m/s2. What is the maximum speed that the car can have while rounding the turn under these slippery conditions?

      5. v = \sqrt{ra_c} = \rm{\sqrt{250\ m \cdot 2.80\ m/s^2} = 26.5\ m/s}

  5. A mass spectrometer is a device used to identify isotopes (atoms of the same element that have different masses) present in a sample. Helium atoms are given velocities of 1.70 × 103 m/s and are then subjected to a magnetic force of 2.72 × 10–16 N, which causes them to move in a circle.
    1. Calculate the acceleration and radius of motion for normal “helium-4” atoms with a mass of 6.65 × 10–27 kg.

      2. Use Newton’s 2nd Law to find acceleration:

      a_c = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \rm \frac{2.72\times 10^{-16}\ N}{6.65\times 10^{-27}\ kg} = 4.09\times 10^{10}\ m/s^2

      Now use the centripetal acceleration equation to solve for the radius:

      \begin{align}a_c &= \frac{v^2}{r} \\[10pt] ra_c &= v^2 \\[10pt] r &= \frac{v^2}{a_c} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(1.70\times 10^3\ m/s)^2}{4.09\times 10^{10}\ m/s^2}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{7.07\times 10^{-5}\ m} \end{align}

    2. Calculate the acceleration and radius of motion for the lighter isotope of helium, “helium-3”, which has a mass of 5.00 × 10–27 kg.

      3. a_c = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \rm \frac{2.72\times 10^{-16}\ N}{5.00\times 10^{-27}\ kg} = 5.44\times 10^{10}\ m/s^2

      r = \frac{v^2}{a_c} = \rm \frac{(1.70\times 10^3\ m/s)^2}{5.44\times 10^{10}\ m/s^2} = 5.31\times 10^{-5}\ m

  6. A Merry-Go-Round is made from a horizontal platform that rotates around a vertical axis.

    1. Why would it be unsafe to stand on a moving Merry-Go-Round if the platform was covered with ice?

      2. Without friction, there is no unbalanced force capable of causing the centripetal acceleration; passengers would move in a straight line and would fall off the outside of the Merry-Go-Round.

    2. A daring Physics 20 student decided to brave the icy Merry-Go-Round by tying a bungee cord between her waist and the axis of the ride as illustrated. Explain what purpose the bungee serves.

      3. The elastic force replaces the missing friction to provide the centripetal acceleration.

    3. The 52.0 kg student rode the Merry-Go-Round at a radius of 4.20 m. Her classmates later measured the elastic force of the bungee cord to be 250 N when stretched to this length. Determine the student’s velocity and period while on the Merry-Go-Round.
      4. Algebra is the same as #3b...

      a_c = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{250\ N}{52.0\ kg} = 4.81\ m/s^2}

      v = \sqrt{ra_c} = \rm{\sqrt{4.20\ m\cdot 4.81\ m/s^2} = 4.49\ m/s}

      T = \frac{2πr}{v} = \rm{\frac{2\pi (4.20\ m)}{4.49\ m/s} = 5.87\ s}

    1. If humankind establishes a permanent presence on the Moon, people might use projectile trains to travel between settlements. Such trains would not touch the ground but would orbit slightly above the Moon’s surface. If the Moon’s surface gravity is 1.63 m/s2 and its radius is 1.74 × 106 m, what speed would the train have to have to enter a surface orbit?

      2. Algebra is the same as #3b...

      v = \sqrt{ra_c} = \rm{\sqrt{1.74\times 10^6\ m\cdot 1.63\ m/s^2} = 1.68\times 10^3\ m/s}

    2. What speed would such a train need to have to orbit just above the Earth’s surface? What problem would such a train encounter on Earth that would be absent on the Moon?

      3. v = \sqrt{ra_c} = \rm{\sqrt{6.37\times 10^6\ m\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 7.91\times 10^3\ m/s}

      At such a high speed, air resistance on Earth would be extreme. This is not a problem on the Moon because there is no atmosphere.

Review

  1. Explain how an object exhibiting uniform circular motion is accelerating even though its speed is constant.

    2. Velocity is a vector; the object is accelerating because the direction of the velocity is changing even though its magnitude is not.

    1. Derive the relationship between the speed, radius, and centripetal acceleration for UCM.
    2. Derive the relationship between the radius, period, and centripetal acceleration for UCM.

    3. See notes for derivation. The equations are:

    a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}

  3. How does the centripetal force relate to the speed and period of UCM?

    4. According to Newton’s 2nd Law, the net (centripetal) force is simply the mass times the centripetal acceleration:

    F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 rm}{T^2}

  4. Does knowing that there is a centripetal force tell us anything about the nature of the force, such as whether the force is gravitational, tension, friction, etc.?

    5. No! The “centripetal force” just means a net force acting toward the centre. What causes that force depends on the situation.
