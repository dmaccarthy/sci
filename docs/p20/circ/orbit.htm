Circular Orbital Motion

Kepler’s 3rd Law, Again

Let’s reconsider the case of a mass (m) orbiting in uniform circular motion around a very large central mass (M).

  • We know for uniform circular motion that the orbiting object’s acceleration is directed toward the centre of its circular orbit and has a magnitude of:

    a_c=\frac{v^2}{r}=\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}

  • If the central mass is much larger than any other nearby mass, then the gravitational field will be dominated by the central mass’s contribution:

    |\vec{\bf{g}}|=\frac{GM}{r^2}

  • If there are no forces other than gravity, we know that \vec{\bf{a}}=\vec{\bf{g}}!
  • This would not be the case if, for example, the orbiting mass was moving under rocket thrust, or if it was moving through an atmosphere.
  • By substituting the equations for \vec{\bf{a}} and \vec{\bf{g}} we get:

    \frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}=\frac{GM}{r^2}

  • Note that the mass of the orbiting object (m) does not matter...
  • Gravitational fields affect all masses equally, so a tiny dust mote orbits the same as a large moon!
  • We can rearrange this as:

    4\pi^2r^3=GMT^2

  • This is Kepler’s 3rd Law, expressed in terms of the central mass rather than the “Kepler coefficient” (k)!

Planetary & Stellar Masses

This equation gives us a means of calculating masses (M) for stars and planets by measuring the orbital radius (r) and period (T) of objects in orbit.

  • We can determine Earth’s mass using the radius and period of the Moon’s orbit.
  • We can determine the Sun’s mass from the radius and period of Earth’s orbit.

Example 1

A spacecraft moving with a speed of 6.00 km/s follows a circular orbit with a radius of 9.20 × 106 metres. Find the mass of the planet and the period of the spacecraft’s orbit.

First calculate the centripetal acceleration, which is equal to the planet’s gravitational field:

g = a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = {\rm \frac{(6000\ m/s)^2}{9.20\times 10^6\ m} = 3.91\ m/s^2}

Now use Newton’s gravitational field equation to solve for M:

g = \frac{GM}{r^2}

gr^2 = GM

M = \frac{gr^2}{G}

M = \rm \frac{3.91\ m/s^2\cdot(9.20\times 10^6\ m)^2}{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}

M = \rm 4.97\times 10^{24}\ kg

Example 2

A planet with a mass of 3.50 × 1024 kg has a satellite that moves in a circular orbit with a period of 48.0 hours. Find the speed and orbital radius of the satellite, and the gravitational field that it experiences.

Equate the equations for a_c and g:

a_c = g

\frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{r^2}

Note that we want to avoid using a_c = v^2/r since both v and r are unknown.

4\pi^2 r^3 = GMT^2

r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}

r = \left(\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}\right)^{1/3}

Now we can evaluate:

r = \rm \left(\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 3.50\times 10^{24}\ kg \cdot (172800\ s)^2}{4\pi^2}\right)^{1/3}

r = \rm 5.61\times 10^7\ m

Finally, find the other unknowns:

v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = \rm \frac{2\pi\cdot 5.61\times 10^7\ m}{172800\ s} = 2.04\times 10^3\ m/s

g = a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \rm \frac{(2.04\times 10^3\ m/s)^2}{5.61\times 10^7\ m} = 0.0742\ m/s^2

We would get the same answer using either of the two remaining equations:

g = a_c = \frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}

Geosynchronous Orbits

When a satellite has the same period as the planet’s rotation (e.g. 24 hours for Earth), the orbit is called geosynchronous.

  • A satellite in a geosynchronous orbit passes over the same point on the ground at the same time each day.
  • A circular, geosynchronous orbit around the equator is called geostationary.
  • The satellite remains in orbit permanently above the same point on the ground.
