    1. A 1200 kg car has 125 kilojoules of kinetic energy. Calculate the speed of the car.

      2. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

      {\color{blue} \frac{2}{m}}E_k = {\color{blue} \frac{\bcancel{2}}{\bcancel{m}}}\cdot\frac{1}{\bcancel{2}}\bcancel{m}v^2

      \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

      Don’t forget to square root!

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 125000\ J}{1200\ kg}} = 14.4\ m/s

    2. If the car were to coast up a hill (no mechanical energy gained or lost), how high would the car reach before it begins to roll back down the hill?

      3. The mechanical energy gets transformed into gravitational potential energy:

      E_g = mgh

      \frac{E_g}{\color{blue}mg} = \frac{\bcancel{mg}h}{\color{blue}\bcancel{mg}}

      h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm \frac{125000\ J}{1200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 10.6\ m

    3. What would the speed of the car be when it is part way up the hill, at a height of 5.00 metres?

      4. Calculate the gravitational potential energy at the given height:

      E_g = mgh = \rm 1200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 5.00\ m = 58860\ J

      Since the total energy remains constant, the kinetic energy is:

      E_k = \Sigma E - E_g = \rm (125000 - 58860) \ J = 66140\ J

      Now we can find the speed. We already did the algebra back in (a)...

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 66140\ J}{1200\ kg}} = 10.5\ m/s

Lesson Notes

Mechanical Energy

Energy

Energy is the ability to produces changes in motion, temperature, or other physical or chemical properties.

  • Energy comes in many different forms.
  • Energy is a scalar quantity; it has no direction.
  • Energy is a conserved quantity.
  • The SI unit of energy is called the joule. A joule can be expressed in terms of simpler units:

    \rm {J = N\cdot m = kg \cdot {m^2\over s^2}}

Kinetic Energy

Motion is itself a form of energy, which we call the kinetic energy:

E_k = {1 \over 2} mv^2

  • In this equation, m is the mass of the object in kilograms.
  • The symbol v is the speed of the object in metres per second (m/s).

Rotational Energy

An object that is spinning in place has energy. We call this rotational energy.

  • We can use rotational energy to pull or lift an object (e.g. a winch) or to create electricity (e.g. a generator).
  • The equation for rotational energy depends on the shape of the object and whether it is solid or hollow.
  • A ball rolling down a ramp has both kinetic and rotational energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy

When an object is elevated above the ground, it has energy because gravity will cause the object to move when it is released.

This type of energy is called gravitational potential energy:

E_g = mgh

  • In this equation, g is the gravitational field strength. Close to Earth’s surface, g = 9.81\ \rm m/s^2.
  • The symbol h is the height of the object relative to the origin (usually the ground).
  • As with kinetic energy, m is the mass in kilograms.
  • This formula is only valid when the gravitational field is constant and uniform.

Elastic Potential Energy

Elasticity is a property of objects that return to their original shape when they are stretched or compressed.

  • Elastic objects include springs, rubber bands, bungees, and archery bows.

The energy stored in a stretched or compressed elastic is called elastic potential energy:

E_{elas} = {1\over 2}k \left({\Delta L}\right)^2

  • The variable k is called the elastic constant. It measures the strength of the elastic object in newtons per metre (N/m).
  • The variable \Delta L is the distance the elastic is stretched or compressed from its equilibrium shape.

Mechanical Forms of Energy

Types of energy that are related to the objects’ motion are called mechanical forms of energy. Mechanical energy includes:

Kinetic EnergyE_k = {1 \over 2} mv^2
Gravitational Potential EnergyE_g = mgh
Elastic Potential EnergyE_{elas} = {1\over 2}k \left({\Delta L}\right)^2
Rotational EnergyDepends on object

Scientists use the term “mechanical energy” to mean the total of all mechanical forms of energy present in a system at a particular instant in time.

Non-Mechanical Forms of Energy

There are many different forms of energy that are related to properties other than motion (e.g. temperature or chemical composition).

Some non-mechanical forms of energy include:

  • Thermal energy
  • Sound energy
  • Electrical energy
  • Chemical energy
  • Nuclear energy
  • Light and other electromagnetic energy

Energy Bar Chart

An energy bar chart is a way to keep track of how much of each form of energy is present in a system.

  • This graph is for a ball that rolls down a ramp. Initially, it has not started moving, so it only has gravitational potential energy (16.5 joules).
  • As the ball rolls down the ramp, energy is transformed and the height of each bar changes. (Click the graph to run the animation.)

Practice

  1. A baseball player hits a home run. The 250-gram baseball has a height of 9.25 metres and a speed of 24.0 m/s when it goes over the centre field wall.
    1. Calculate the baseball’s kinetic energy.

      2. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.250\ kg) (24.0\ m/s)^2 = 72.0\ J

    2. Calculate the baseball’s gravitational potential energy.

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm 0.250\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 9.25\ m = 22.7\ J

    3. Calculate the baseball’s mechanical energy.

      4. \Sigma E = \rm 72.0\ J + 22.7 J = 94.7\ J

    4. Draw an energy bar chart for the baseball.

  2. An archer has a bow with an elastic constant k = 200 N/m. She draws the bowstring back 65.0 cm.
    1. How much elastic potential energy is stored in the stretched bow?

      2. E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}(200\ N/m) (0.650\ m)^2 = 42.3\ J

    2. If the arrow has a mass of 55.0 grams and is being held 1.60 m above the ground, how much gravitational potential energy does it have?

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm 0.0550\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.60\ m = 0.863\ J

    3. Calculate the mechanical energy of the bow and arrow system.

      4. E = \rm 42.25\ J + 0.863\ J = 43.1\ J

    4. Draw an energy bar chart for the bow-arrow system.

  3. A toy car has a mass of 35.0 grams. The car is launched using a spring with k = 20.0 N/m that is compressed by 4.00 cm.
    1. Calculate the mechanical energy stored in the spring.

      2. E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (20.0\ N/m) (0.0400\ m)^2 = 0.0160\ J

    2. Calculate the speed of the car after it is launched, assuming the system retains 60.0% of its initial mechanical energy. (The other 40.0% of the energy is transformed into sound and thermal energy.)

      3. E_k = \rm 0.600\times 0.0160\ J = 0.00960\ J

      To find the speed, rearrange the kinetic energy equation as we did in the first Remember question above:

      v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.00960\ J}{0.0350\ kg}} = 0.741\ m/s

  4. A pinball game uses a spring that is stretched by 12.0 cm to launch a ball (m = 75.0 g) to start the game. After the spring is released, the speed of the ball is 6.00 m/s.
    1. Calculate the mechanical energy of the system.

      2. After the ball is set in motion, it only has kinetic energy, so the mechanical energy is:

      E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.0750\ kg) (6.00\ m/s)^2 = 1.35\ J

    2. Calculate elastic constant of the spring.

      3. Before the ball is set in motion, the mechanical energy is in the form of elastic potential energy:

      E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2} k(\Delta L)^2

      2E_{elas} = k(\Delta L)^2

      k = \frac{2E_{elas}}{(\Delta L)^2} = \rm \frac{2\cdot 1.35\ J}{(0.120\ m)^2} = 188\ N/m

    3. Draw energy bar charts for the system before and after launch.

  5. A solid metal ball with a mass of 200 grams rolls down a ramp starting from rest. When the ball is halfway down the ramp, its speed is measured to be 2.00 m/s. For a solid sphere that rolls without slipping, the rotational energy equation is:

    E_{rotn}=\frac{1}{5}mv^2

    1. Calculate the ball’s kinetic and rotational energies.

      2. E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2} (0.200\ kg) (2.00\ m/s)^2 = 0.400\ J

      E_{rotn} = \frac{1}{5}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{5} (0.200\ kg) (2.00\ m/s)^2 = 0.160\ J

    2. Calculate the ball’s gravitational potential energy and its mechanical energy. Remember that the ball is halfway down the ramp at this point. Assume there is no change in the mechanical energy as the ball rolls. [Before trying to answer this, sketch two separate energy bar charts for the system at the (i) top and (ii) halfway point of the hill.]

      3. Because the ball is at the halfway point, it has half of the gravitational potential energy that it started with; the other half has become kinetic and rotational energy. Thus:

      E_g = E_k + E_{rotn} = \rm 0.400\ J + 0.160\ J = 0.560\ J

      \Sigma E = E_g + E_k + E_{rotn} = \rm 0.560\ J + 0.400\ J + 0.160\ J = 1.12\ J

    3. Calculate the height from which the ball was released.

      4. E_g = mgh

      h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm \frac{1.12\ J}{0.200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.571\ m

    4. Define the elastic constant (sometimes called a spring constant) and explain how to interpret its value. For example, what does it mean to say that a spring has an elastic constant of 20 N/m?

      5. The elastic constant measures the amount of force required per one metre of stretch or compression of an elastic object. 20 N/m means a force of 20 newtons would result in 1 metre of stretch, provided this did not exceed the elastic limit of the object.
