Energy Conservation

Energy Conservation

The Law of Energy Conservation is one of the most important laws in all of Science.

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed.
  • The total energy must always remain constant.
  • Energy can be transformed from one form to another.
  • Energy can be transferred to different objects or locations.
  • All energy gains must be perfectly balanced by energy losses elsewhere.

Defining the System

Because there is no practical way we can account for all of the energy everywhere in the universe, we need to define our system before we can apply the Law of Energy Conservation.

  • The system consists of all objects and energy forms that we are intertested in accounting for.
  • Everything that is not part of the system is part of the environment or surroundings.

Example: A ball rolls down a ramp.

  • The system includes the ball and its gravitational potential energy, kinetic energy, and rotational energy.
  • The environment includes the person lifting the ball, the ramp, and the air in the room.

Energy Bar Chart

An energy bar chart is a way to keep track of how much of each form of energy is present in a system.

  • This graph is for a ball that rolls down a ramp. Initially, it has not started moving, so it only has gravitational potential energy (16.5 joules).
  • As the ball rolls down the ramp, energy is transformed and the height of each bar changes.
  • The total energy must remain constant; the total of the bars is always 16.5 joules in this example.
  • At the bottom of the ramp, the 16.5 joules of energy has been completely transformed into kinetic and rotational energy.

Example 1

A 200 gram book slides from rest down a frictionless ramp that is 1.20 metres high. Calculate the speed of the book when it reaches the bottom of the ramp.

E_g = mgh = \rm{0.200\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 \cdot 1.20\ m = 2.35\ J}

E_k = {1\over 2}mv^2

{2 E_k \over m} = v^2

v = \sqrt{2 E_k \over m} = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 2.3544\ J \over 0.200\ kg} = 4.85 \ m/s}

Example 2

Calculate the speed of the book when it is at a height of 50.0 cm.

E_g = mgh = \rm{0.200\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 \cdot 0.500\ m = 0.981\ J}

E_k = E_{total} - E_g = \rm{2.3544\ J - 0.981\ J = 1.3734\ J}

v = \sqrt{2 E_k \over m} = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 1.3734\ J \over 0.200\ kg} = 3.71 \ m/s}

Example 3

A compressed spring is used to launch a (solid) ball up an incline. Sketch the initial and final energy bar charts and express the conservation of energy as an equation.

{1\over 2} k(\Delta L)^2 = mgh + {1\over 2}mv^2 + {1\over 5}mv^2

Practice

  1. A 50.0-gram Hot Wheels® car is at rest at ground level. The toy car is set in motion using a spring launcher with an elastic constant k = 90.0 N/m that has been compressed 10.0 cm. The car follows a track to the finish line 30.0 cm above ground level.
    1. Calculate the car’s speed as it crosses the finish line. Assume there is no change in the system’s mechanical energy.

      2. E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2} k(\Delta L)^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (90.0\ N/m) (0.100\ m)^2 = 0.450\ J}

      E_g = mgh = \rm{0.0500\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.300\ m = 0.147\ J}

      E_k = \Sigma E - E_g = \rm{0.450\ J - 0.147\ J = 0.303\ J}

      E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2

      \frac{2E_{kf}}{m} = v_f^2

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 0.30285\ J}{0.0500\ kg}} = 3.48\ m/s}

    2. If the car continues to move uphill after crossing the finish line, how high could it rise before it begins to roll back down the track?

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm{0.450\ J}

      h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \rm{\frac{0.450\ J}{0.0500\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.917\ m}

  2. A lacrosse ball is made of an elastic material so that when it strikes a rigid object (like a concrete floor or metal goal post) the ball compresses and converts most of the kinetic energy into elastic potential energy. This energy is then mostly converted back to kinetic energy as the ball decompresses. By what distance does a 225-gram lacrosse ball compress when it strikes the floor at 7.00 m/s if the elasticity of the ball is k = 8000 N/m?

    3. E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (0.225\ kg) (7.00\ m/s)^2 = 5.51\ J}

    E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2} k(\Delta L)^2 = \rm{5.51\ J}

    2E_{elas} = k(\Delta L)^2

    \frac{2E_{elas}}{k} = (\Delta L)^2

    \Delta L = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{elas}}{k}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 5.51\ J}{8000\ N/m}} = 0.0371\ m}

    1. A 2.50 kg object is dropped from rest from a height of 3.00 metres. Neglecting any loss of energy due to air resistance, what will the speed of the object be just before it contacts the ground?

      2. E_g = mgh = \rm{2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 3.00\ m = 73.6\ J}

      E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2

      \frac{2E_{kf}}{m} = v_f^2

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 73.6\ J}{2.50\ kg}} = 7.67\ m/s}

    2. What will the speed of the object be when it is halfway to the ground?

      3. E_g = mgh = \rm{2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.50\ m = 36.8\ J}

      E_k = \Sigma E - E_g = \rm{73.6\ J - 36.8\ J = 36.8\ J}

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 36.8\ J}{2.50\ kg}} = 5.42\ m/s}

    3. Repeat question (b) for a 12.0 kg object. Does the mass seem to matter? If not, explain why it does not.

      4. E_{gi} = mgh_i = \rm{12.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 3.00\ m = 353\ J}

      E_{gf} = mgh_f = \rm{12.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.50\ m = 177\ J}

      E_{kf} = E_{gi} - E_{gf} = \rm{177\ J}

      v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 177\ J}{12.0\ kg}} = 5.42\ m/s}

      Because the kinetic and gravitational potential energy are both proportional to the mass, the mass actually cancels. The numerator and denominator are both larger by the same factor.

    4. If we were to measure the speeds of these objects, why would the “real-life” results be less than what we calculated?
      5. The real-life speeds would be slower because air resistance converts some of the initial energy into sound or thermal energy.
    5. What happens to the ball’s mechanical energy when it contacts the ground?
      6. Some of the energy will be transformed into sound or thermal energy and some may remain as kinetic energy if the ball bounces.
    1. A solid sphere rolls down a ramp from a height of 1.20 metres. If the sphere maintains contact with the ramp without slipping, the rotational energy of the sphere will be:

      E_r=\frac{1}{5}mv^2

      Calculate the speed of the sphere when it reaches the bottom of the ramp.

      2. The mass is not given because the answer will be the same for any mass!

      E_g = mgh

      \Sigma E = E_k + E_r = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 + \frac{1}{5} mv^2 = \frac{7}{10} mv^2

      Equate the initial and final total energies:

      mgh = \frac{7}{10} mv^2

      gh = \frac{7}{10} v^2

      \frac{10}{7} gh = v^2

      v = \sqrt{\frac{10}{7} gh} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{10}{7} 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.20\ m} = 4.10\ m/s}

    2. If the rolling object is a solid cylinder rather than a sphere, the rotational energy is:

      E_r=\frac{1}{4}mv^2

      Calculate the speed of the cylinder when it reaches the bottom of the ramp.

      3. \Sigma E = E_k + E_r = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 + \frac{1}{4} mv^2 = \frac{3}{4} mv^2

      Equate the initial and final total energies:

      mgh = \frac{3}{4} mv^2

      gh = \frac{3}{4} v^2

      \frac{4}{3} gh = v^2

      v = \sqrt{\frac{4}{3} gh} = \rm{\sqrt{\frac{4}{3} 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.20\ m} = 3.96\ m/s}

  5. In the illustrated pulley system, m_1 = 15.0 kg, m_2 = 8.50 kg, and h = 3.00 m. The masses are released from rest. A pulley system is “ideal” when there is no friction or air resistance, and its rotational energy is negligible. The mechanical energy of the masses is assumed to be constant.

    Your energy bar charts for this scenario should have four bars: E_{k1}, E_{k2}, E_{g1}, and E_{g2}. Calculate the final speed of the masses just before m_1 contacts the ground.

    6. E_{g1} = m_1gh = \rm{15.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 3.00\ m = 441\ J}

    E_{g2} = m_2gh = \rm{8.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 3.00\ m = 250\ J}

    E_k = \Sigma E - E_g = \rm{441.45\ J - 250.155\ J = 191\ J}

    This is the combined kinetic energy of both masses!

    E_k = \frac{1}{2} m_1v^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2v^2

    Since the masses are attached, they have the same speed, so you can factor out the v^2...

    E_k = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2)v^2

    v^2 = \frac{2E_k}{m_1 + m_2}

    v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m_1 + m_2}}= \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 191.295\ J}{15.0\ kg + 8.50\ kg}} = 4.03\ m/s

    Now we can calculate the kinetic energies of the masses separately...

    E_{k1} = \frac{1}{2} m_1v^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (15.0\ kg)(4.0349\ m/s)^2 = 122.1\ J}

    E_{k2} = \frac{1}{2} m_2v^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (8.50\ kg)(4.0349\ m/s)^2 = 69.2\ J}
