Notes

Mechanical energy (measured in joules) includes kinetic, rotational, gravitational potential, and elastic potential energy.

E_k = {1\over 2}mv^2

E_g = mgh

E_{elas} = {1\over 2}k(\Delta L)^2

  • The equation for rotational energy depends on the shape (e.g. sphere or cylinder) and mass distribution (e.g. solid or hollow)
  • Other (non-mechanical) forms of energy include thermal energy, sound, chemical, nuclear, electrical and electromagnetic energy.

Law of Energy Conservation: Energy can be transformed from one form to another, but the total energy always remains constant.

\Sigma E_i = \Sigma E_f

  • On an energy bar chart, the total of all bars will always be constant. (Click chart for animation.)

  • Any energy that is going to be added to a system must be included with the initial energy.
  • Any energy that is removed from a system must be included with the final energy.

Mechanical energy transformations are caused by forces (measured in newtons). The amount of energy that a force transforms is called the work.

W = \vec{\bf F}_\parallel \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d} = |\vec{\bf F}|\cdot|\Delta\vec{\bf d}|\cos\theta

  • When more than one force does work, the change in kinetic energy will equal the total work done by all forces:

    \Delta E_k = \Sigma W

Power (measured in watts) is the rate at which a force does work.

P = {\Delta E\over \Delta t}

Efficiency is the ratio of the useful energy output compared to the energy input.

\epsilon = {\Delta E_{useful}\over |\Delta E_{input}|}
