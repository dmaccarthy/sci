Work in 2D

In 1D, the work done by a force is calculated by multiplying the force by the displacement:

W = \vec{\bf F}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}

  • When the force is in the same direction as the motion, the work is positive and the object gains kinetic energy.
  • When the force is in the opposite direction as the motion, the work is negative and the object loses kinetic energy.
  • When the force is perpendicular to the motion, the effect of the force is to change the direction of the velocity.
  • Since a perpendicular force is neither forward nor backwards, the kinetic energy does not change and the work is zero.

When the force acts at an angle to the displacement (rather than directly forward or backward) it has vector components in both the parallel and perpendicular directions:

\vec{\bf F}_\parallel = |\vec{\bf F}| \cos\Delta\theta

\vec{\bf F}_\perp = |\vec{\bf F}| \sin\Delta\theta

  • In these equations, \Delta\theta is the angle between the force and displacement vectors.
  • The perpendicular component is responsible for changing the direction of the velocity and does zero work!
  • The parallel component does all the work, causing the mass to gain of loses energy.
  • When \Delta\theta is between 0 and 90°, the work is positive because the force is (partly) forward.
  • When \Delta\theta is between 90° and 180°, the work is negative because the force is (partly) backward.

If we use only the parallel component as the force in the 1D equation, we get:

W = |\vec{\bf F}|\cdot|\Delta\vec{\bf d}|\cos(\Delta\theta)

  • The absolute value symbols mean that we do not include directions with the force and displacment vectors when using this equation.
  • The cosine function will give us the correct sign for the work!

Example 1

A 2000 kg rail car is hauled by a truck driving on a road parallel to the tracks. The cable connecting the truck to the rail car exerts a force of 1500 newtons [–30.0°]. Because the rail car can only move parallel to the tracks, assume that the track exerts a force that exactly balances the perpendicular component. Find the rail car’s acceleration.

We solved this problem previously using Newton’s 2nd Law. We could have use work and energy instead.

The gravity and normal forces are balanced so they do not affect the motion. The force of the tracks, \vec{\bf F}_2, is perpendicular to the motion so it does no work. Only the tension in the cable, \vec{\bf F}_1, does work.

Because the acceleration will be constant as long as the forces remain constant, we can use any displacement and initial velocity we want; let’s say 1.00 metre and 0.00 m/s.

The work done by the tension force is:

W = |\vec{\bf F}_1| \cdot |\Delta\vec{\bf d}| \cos(30.0^\circ) = \rm 1500\ N\cdot 1.00\ m\cos(30.0^\circ) = 1299.0\ J

Since we took the car to be initially at rest, the work is the final kinetic energy. The final speed is:

v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \rm \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 1299.0\ J}{2000\ kg}} = 1.1398\ m/s

Now we can use the uniform accelerated motion equations to find the acceleration:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{{(1.1398\ m/s)^2 \over 2\cdot 1.00\ m} = +0.650\ m/s^2}

Example 2

A student pushes a 2.00 kg box from rest, 2.00 metres up an 8.00° frictionless incline, using an applied force of 10.0 newtons parallel to the ramp. Find the acceleration of the box, the normal force, and the speed at the top of the ramp.

We solved this problem previously using Newton’s 2nd Law. It is easier to use work and energy!

Calculate the work done by the applied force:

W_a = \vec{\bf F}_a \cdot \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \rm{10.0\ N\cdot 2.00\ m = 20.0\ J}

Next calculate the work done by gravity:

F_g = mg = \rm 2.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 19.62\ N

W_g = |\vec{\bf F}_g| \cdot |\Delta\vec{\bf d}| \cos(98.0^\circ) = \rm 19.62\ N\cdot 2.00\ m\cos(98.0^\circ) = -5.4612\ J

Alternatively, we could calculate the gravitational potential energy gained, which is just the negative of the work done by gravity:

h = \rm{2.00\ m\cdot\sin(8.00^\circ) = 0.27835\ m}

\begin{align}E_{gf} &= mgh \\[8pt]&= \rm{2.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 \cdot 0.27835\ m}\\[8pt]&= \rm{5.4612\ J}\end{align}

Then normal force does not do any work because it acts at exactly 90.0° to the displacement!

Because the box was initially at rest, the total work done is equal to the final kinetic energy:

E_{kf} = \Sigma W = \rm 20.0\ J -5.4612\ J = 14.539\ J

v_f = \sqrt{2E_k\over m} = \rm{\sqrt{2(14.539\ J)\over 2.00\ kg} = 3.81\ m/s}

Now we can use the uniform accelerated motion equations to find the acceleration:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \rm{{(3.81\ m/s)^2 \over 2\cdot 2.00\ m} = 3.64\ m/s^2}

  • If there had been friction, we would need to account for the negative work done by friction.
  • If given the coefficient of friction, we would need to know the normal force before we could calculate the force of friction.
  • Since the normal force does not balance gravity on an incline, we would need to use Newton’s 2nd Law, which requires adding the y-components of all forces.
  • We would then have a choice whether to use work and energy, or Newton’s 2nd Law to solve for the x-motion.

Practice

  1. A 50.0-kilogram load is hauled from rest through a 200-metre displacment up a 15.0° incline.
    1. Use the 2D work equation to calculate the work done by gravity.

      2. Calculate the force of gravity:

      F_g = mg = \rm 50.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 490.5\ N

      Calculate the difference between the direction of the force and the displacement:

      \Delta\theta = +15.0^\circ - (-90.0^\circ) = 105.0^\circ

      Calculate the work done by gravity:

      W_g = F_g\cdot \Delta d\cdot \cos\Delta\theta = \rm 490.5\ N\cdot 200\ m\cdot\cos(105.0^\circ) = -25390\ J = -25.4\ kJ

    2. Calculate the gravitational potential energy using the mgh formula. You will need to use trigonometry to find h first.

      3. \sin 8.00^\circ = {\rm \frac{Opp}{Hyp}} = \frac{h_f}{\Delta d}

      h_f = \Delta d\sin 15.0^\circ = \rm 200\ m\cdot \sin(15.0^\circ) = 51.764\ m

      E_{gf} = mgh_f = \rm 50.0\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 51.764\ m = 25390\ J = 25.4\ kJ

    3. Compare your answers from (a) and (b).

      4. They are negatives of each other! The work done by gravity is the kinetic energy lost, which is balanced by the gravitational potential energy gained.

    4. The speed of the mass at the top of the hill is 3.00 m/s. Calculate the average applied force. Neglect the effects of friction and air resistance.

      5. Calculate the kinetic energy:

      E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \rm \frac{1}{2}\cdot 50.0\ kg (3.00\ m/s)^2 = 225\ J

      Since the mass was intiially at rest, the kinetic energy equals the total work done:

      \Delta E_k = E_{kf} = W_g + W_a

      W_a = E_{kf} - W_g = \rm 225\ J - (-25390\ J) = 25615\ J
