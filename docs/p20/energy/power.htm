Power & Efficiency

Power

The rate at which a force does work is called power.

  • Power is a measure of how quickly an energy-transforming device is able to do work:

    P = {\Delta E\over \Delta t}

  • The unit of power is J/s, which is called watts (W).
  • Do not confuse the symbol for the unit W (watts) with the symbol for the variable W (work).

Efficiency

Most energy transformations produce unwanted “waste” forms of energy.

  • For example, a light bulb is intended to transform electrical energy into light energy. In reality, a significant fraction of the energy is wasted in the form of heat.

Scientists use the concept of efficiency to describe the fraction or percentage of the energy input that is transformed into its intended or useful output:

\epsilon = {\Delta E_{useful} \over |\Delta E_{input}|}

  • To say that a light bulb is 20% efficient means that 20% of the electrical energy consumed becomes light energy, and the remaining 80% of the energy consumed becomes waste (thermal energy).

Example

A 2000 kg car accelerates from rest to 20.0 m/s. The engine has an efficiency of 32.0% and produces 120 horsepower (1 hp = 746 W).

  1. How long does it take the car to reach its final speed if air resistance and friction are ignored?

Calculate the work done by the engine as the change in kinetic energy...

E_{ki} = {1\over 2}mv_i^2 = 0\ {\rm J}

E_{kf} = {1\over 2}mv_f^2 = \rm{{1\over 2}(2000\ kg) (20.0\ m/s)^2 = 400000\ J}

W = \Delta E_k = \rm{400000\ J}

Use the power equation to solve for time...

P = {\Delta E \over \Delta t}

P\Delta t = \Delta E

\Delta t = {\Delta E\over P} = \rm{{400000\ J\over 89520\ W} = 4.47\ s}

  • Realistically, the engine does not operate at its maximum power all the time, so it would almost certainly take longer than calculated!
  1. How much chemical energy (gasoline) is used in accelerating the car?

\epsilon = {\Delta E_{useful} \over \Delta E_{input}}

\epsilon \Delta E_{input} = \Delta E_{useful}

\Delta E_{input} = {\Delta E_{useful}\over \epsilon} = \rm{{400000\ J\over 0.320} = 1.25\ MJ}

  • The efficiency is a little under one-third, so a little less than one-third of the 1.25 MJ consumed becomes useful energy (0.40 MJ).
  • The remaining 0.85 MJ represents the sound and thermal energy produced by the engine.

Practice

  1. A student running on a treadmill burns 120 kJ of food energy and produces 97.2 kJ of heat. What is the efficiency of the energy transformation?

    2. \Delta E_{useful} = \Delta E_{input} - \Delta E_{waste} = \rm{120\ kJ - 97.2\ kJ = 22.8\ kJ}

    \epsilon = \frac{\Delta E_{useful}}{\Delta E_{input}} = \rm{\frac{22.8\ kJ}{120\ kJ} = 0.190 = 19.0\%}

  2. How does power relate to work? For example, can a car with a more powerful engine always do more work than a car with a less powerful engine?

    3. The amount of work an engine can do depends on how much fuel is available. The power indicates how quickly that work can be done. A car with a full gas tank and an underpowered engine can do more total work than a more powerful engine with an empty gas tank.

  3. How long will it take for a car engine that develops 75.0 kW of power to do 247 kJ of work in accelerating the car to highway speed?

    4. P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}

    P\Delta t = \Delta E

    \Delta t = \frac{\Delta E}{P} = \rm{\frac{247000\ J}{75000\ W} = 3.29\ s}

  4. A 60.0-kilogram sprinter metabolizes 7.00 kilojoules of food energy while accelerating from rest to 8.00 m/s over 2.00 seconds. Calculate:
    1. the efficiency of the energy transformation.

      2. \Delta E_{input} = \rm{7000\ J}

      \Delta E_{useful} = E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2 = \rm{\frac{1}{2} (60.0\ kg) (8.00\ m/s)^2 = 1920\ J}

      \epsilon = \frac{\Delta E_{useful}}{\Delta E_{input}} = \rm{\frac{1920\ J}{7000\ J} = 0.274 = 27.4\%}

    2. the power developed by the student.

      3. P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \rm{\frac{1920\ J}{2.00\ s} = 960 W}

    3. the amount of thermal and sound (“waste”) energy produced by the student.

      4. \Delta E_{waste} = \Delta E_{input} - \Delta E_{useful} = \rm{7000\ J - 1920\ J = 5080\ J}

    4. Draw an energy flow diagram.

  5. The diesel engine of a locomotive has an efficiency of 12.5% in transforming the chemical energy of the fuel into useful work as the locomotive hauls the train up a hill.
    1. If the engine does 4.25 × 107 J of useful work in hauling the train up the hill, how much chemical energy (fuel) did the engine consume?

      2. \epsilon = \frac{\Delta E_{useful}}{\Delta E_{input}}

      \epsilon \Delta E_{input} = \Delta E_{useful}

      \Delta E_{input} = \frac{\Delta E_{useful}}{\epsilon} = \rm{\frac{4.25\times 10^7\ J}{0.125} = 3.40\times 10^8\ J}

    2. How much waste (thermal and sound energy) did the engine produce?

      3. \begin{align}\Delta E_{waste} &= \Delta E_{input} - \Delta E_{useful} \\ &= \rm{3.40\times 10^8\ J - 4.25\times 10^7\ J} \\ &= \rm{2.98\times 10^8\ J} \end{align}
