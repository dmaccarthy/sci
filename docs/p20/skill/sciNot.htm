Significant Digits & Scientific Notation

Significant Digits

Many of the values we use in Science are the result of a measurement...

  • The temperature of a solution.
  • The time it takes a pendulum to swing.
  • The height a baseball rises to before it starts to fall.

Measurements are rarely exact; there is usually a measurement uncertainty due to limitations in the measuring procedure.

  • The number of digits we record indicates how accurate we believe a measurement to be.
  • Recording a distance as 21.53 metres indicates we believe our uncertainty to be up to a few hundredths of a metre (the last digit we recorded).
  • This measurement has four significant digits.
  • Recording a distance as 22 metres indicates we believe our uncertainty to be up to a few metres.
  • This measurement has two significant digits.
  • All digits are counted as significant except for leading zeroes.
  • A measurement of 0.002050 metres has four significant digits.

When performimg calculations, our answer cannot contain more significant digits than any of the measurements used to perform the calculation.

Example 1

Calculate the volume of a sphere whose diamter is measured to be 2.31 cm, using the formula:

V = \frac{4\pi}{3}r^3

First calculate the radius by dividing the diameter by 2:

r=\frac{d}{2} = \rm\frac{2.31\ cm}{2} = 1.155\ cm

  • If this was our “final answer”, we would round it to 1.16 cm (three significant digits).
  • We do not use the 2 in the denominator for significant digits because it is an exact value, not a measurement.
  • Do not round yet, because we will be using this value to make another calculation.

Now evaluate the volume:

V = \rm\frac{4\pi}{3}(1.155\ cm)^3 = 6.454083235\ cm^3 \approx 6.45\ cm^3

  • The final answer is rounded to three significant digits because the diameter was given to three digits.
  • The 4\pi/3 that appears in the formula does not affect the significant digits becaue it is not a measurement.
  • Make sure you use \pi as accurately as your calculator can; if you round the value of \pi then this will affect the accuracy of your answer.

Example 2

Calculate the change in the temperature of a solution that is initially at 15.0 °C and is then heated to 17.2 °C.

\Delta T = T_f - T_i = \rm (17.2 - 15.0)\ ^\circ C = 2.2\ ^\circ C

  • Should we record the result as 2.20 °C (three sig digs)?
  • The answer cannot be more precise than any of the measurements!
  • For addition and substraction, round based on the precision of the last digit rather than the total number of digits.

Big Numbers

The radius of Earth’s orbit around the Sun is about:

  • We can avoid writing the zeroes by using powers of 10.
  • Each time we multiply (or divide) by 10, we move the decimal point in the number.
  • Count the number of places the decimal point is moved...

\rm{1.496\times 10^{11}\ m}

  • The exponent tells us that the decimal place has been moved by 11 positions.
  • In scientific notation, the decimal point is always placed after the first non-zero digit.
  • The normal way of writing numbers (without powers of 10) is called decimal notation.
  • For significant digits, we use the coefficient only; 1.496 × 1011 m has four significant digits.
  • Because the number is so large, there is no way to write the number in decimal notation with fewer than 12 significant digits.
  • You may have to write your answer in scientific notation to make the number of significant digits correct.

Small Numbers

The electric charge of an electron is:

  • Because the number is very small, we have to move the decimal point in the opposite direction.
  • For small numbers, the power will have a negative exponent in scientific notation.

\rm{1.602\times 10^{-19}\ C}

Calculator Arithmetic

When using a scientific calculator or computer to perform arithmetic on numbers in scientific notation, there is an abbreviation usually represented by a button labelled EE.

1.496\times 10^{11} → 1.496{\small\rm E}11

  • Using the abbreviated notation can prevent “order of operations” mistakes.
  • If typing the power out in full, you will often need brackets for the correct order of operations.
  • Always use full scientific notation in written work.

Example 3

\rm\frac{3.00\times 10^8}{6.33\times 10^{-7}}

\rm\frac{3.00\times 10^8}{6.33\times 10^{-7}} \approx 4.74\times 10^{14}

The final answer is rounded to three significant digits.

  6. Define decimal notation and scientific notation.

    7. Decimal notation is the “normal” way of writing numbers: 0.00050.

    Scientific notation writes numbers as a coefficient multiplied by a power of 10:

    The decimal point always follows immediately after the first non-zero digit.

  7. Explain how to convert between decimal and scientific notation.

    8. Simply move the decimal point by the number of places indicated by the exponent in the power of 10. Positive exponents correspond to “big” numbers (> 10) and negative exponents to “small” numbers (< 1).

  8. Explain how to enter numbers in scientific notation on a graphing calculator, a spreadsheet, or similar computer program.
