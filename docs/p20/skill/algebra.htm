Lesson Notes

Algebra

Equations

In Physics, relationships between variables are often described by an equation.

  • You saw many relationships in Science 10:

E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t}

Q = mc\Delta T

  • When solving problems in Physics, you will often need to use algebra to rearrange an equation to isolate the variable that you are trying to solve for.

Algebra

The basic principle of algebra is that you can perform any mathematical operation to an equation, as long as you do the same to both sides of the equation.

E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

  • If we are trying to isolate v, we need to cancel all of the operations involving v: squaring, multiplication by \frac{1}{2}, and multiplication by m.
  • You can “cancel” an operation by performing its inverse: addition cancels subtraction, division cancels multiplication, square rooting cancels squaring.
  • When you have more than one operation to cancel, use reverse order of operations.
  • To cancel multiplication by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal:

    {\color{red}{2}}E_k = \bcancel{\color{red}{2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\bcancel{2}}mv^2

  • Rewrite the equation without the operations that cancel:

    2E_k = mv^2

  • Now perform the inverse of the next operation you want to cancel, then simplify:

    \frac{2E_k}{\color{red}{m}} = \frac{\bcancel{m}v^2}{\bcancel{\color{red}{m}}}

    \frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2

  • Continue performing inverse operations and then simplifying until the variable you are solving for has been isolated:

    \color{red}{\sqrt{\color{black}{\frac{2E_k}{m}}} {\color{black}=} \sqrt{\color{black}{v^2}}}

    \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = v

  • Once you’ve isolated your unknown, you can substitute values and evaluate:

    v = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 8000\ J}{65.0\ kg}} = 15.6893\ m/s}

  • Using standard SI units will ensure that your answer will be in standard SI units.
  • You should always check your answer by substituting it into the original equation:

    E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2}(65.0\ kg)(15.6893\ m/s)^2 = 8000\ J}

  • Your final answer should be rounded to the same number of significant digits as given in the question and must include units: 15.7 m/s.
  • Perform your calculations with several extra digits to avoid rounding errors.

  2. You learned in Science 10 that acceleration is defined as:

    \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t}

    Find the final velocity (\vec{\bf v}_f) of an object that has an initial velocity of \vec{\bf v}_i = +15.0 m/s and then accelerates at \vec{\bf a} = –9.81 m/s2 for \Delta t = 2.50 s. Show all steps and check your answer!

    3. Write the original equation and then multiply both sides by \Delta t:

    \vec{\bf a}{\color{#0065fe}\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t}{\color{#0065fe}\Delta t}

    \vec{\bf a}\Delta t = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\bcancel{\Delta t}}\bcancel{\Delta t}

    Simplify:

    \vec{\bf a}\Delta t= \vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i

    Add \vec{\bf v}_i to both sides:

    \vec{\bf a}\Delta t \color{#0065fe} + \vec{\bf v}_i \color{red} = \vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i \color{#0065fe}+ \vec{\bf v}_i

    \vec{\bf a}\Delta t + \vec{\bf v}_i = \vec{\bf v}_f - \bcancel{\vec{\bf v}_i} + \bcancel{\vec{\bf v}_i}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    \vec{\bf v}_f = \vec{\bf a}\Delta t + \vec{\bf v}_i

    \vec{\bf v}_f = \rm -9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 2.50\ s + 15.0\ m/s = -9.525\ m/s

    Check your answer by substituting back into the original equation. If this is the “final answer”, round to the correct number of significant digits:

    \vec{\bf v}_f = \rm -9.53\ m/s

  4. Define and explain what the following mean in the context of algebra:
    1. operation

      2. Addition, substraction, multiplication, division, squaring, or any other mathematical action that can be performed on a expression.

    2. inverse

      3. Operations that “undo” each other when performed consecutively, such as adding and then subtracting the same number..

    3. order of operations

      4. The rules for performing operations in the correct sequence:

      1. Brackets first, from innermost outward
      2. Exponents
      3. Multiplication & Division
      4. Addition & Subtraction

      Operations of equal precedence are performed in order from left to right.

      Expressions that appear under a radical sign or in the numerator or denominator of a rational expression have “implied” brackets.

  5. Explain what it means to “isolate” or “solve for” a variable in an equation, and how to use algebra to accomplish it.

    6. To isolate a variable, we want to perform a sequence of operations on the equation so that the variable being isolated remains by itself on one side of the equation, and an expression that we can evaluate remains on the other side.

    The basic idea of algebra is that we can perform any operation (e.g. addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, exponents, roots, reciprocals) as long as we perform the same operation to both sides of the equation.

    To isolate a variable, we want to undo the operations that involve that variable by performing the inverse operations, to make those operations cancel. Because inverse operations only cancel when they are done consecutively, we must undo the operations using the reverse order of operations.

    When the variable we are isolating appears more than once, we must “collect like terms” before solving.

    There are various mathematical techniques to solve equations where this is not possible (e.g. quadratic formula for equations containing both x and x^2.)

Practice

  1. Given v = 343 m/s and \lambda = 0.825 m, solve for f in the equation:

    v=f\lambda

    2. Divide both sides by \lambda:

    \frac{v}{\color{#0065fe}\lambda}=\frac{f\bcancel{\lambda}}{\bcancel{\color{#0065fe}\lambda}}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    f = \frac{v}{\lambda}=\rm{\frac{343\ m/s}{0.825\ m} = 416\ s^{-1}}

  2. Given a = 9.81 m/s2 and r = 15.0 m, solve for v in the equation:

    a=\frac{v^2}{r}

    3. Multiply both sides by r:

    a{\color{#0065fe}r} = \frac{v^2}{\bcancel{r}} {\bcancel{\color{#0065fe}r}}

    Simplify:

    ar = v^2

    Square root both sides:

    {\color{#0065fe}\sqrt{\color{red} ar}} = {\color{#0065fe}\sqrt{\color{red}v^2}}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    v = \sqrt{ar} = \rm{\sqrt{9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 15.0\ m} = 12.1\ m/s}

  3. Given g = 9.81 m/s2 and T = 0.750 s, solve for L in the equation:

    T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

    4. Divide both sides by 2\pi:

    \frac{T}{\color{#0065fe} 2\pi} = \frac{\bcancel{2\pi}}{\bcancel{\color{#0065fe} 2\pi}} \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

    Simplify:

    \frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}

    Square both sides:

    {\color{#0065fe}\left({\color{red}{\frac{T}{2\pi}}}\right)^2 = \left({\color{red}\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}}\right)^2}

    Simplify:

    {\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}}

    Multiply both sides by g:

    {\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2}\ {\color{#0065fe} g} = \frac{L}{\bcancel{g}}}\ {\bcancel{\color{#0065fe} g}}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    L = \frac{T^2}{4\pi^2}\ {g} = \rm{\frac{(0.750\ s)^2}{4\pi^2}\ 9.81\ m/s^2 = 0.140\ m}

  4. Given \vec{\bf{a}} = +2.00 m/s2, \Delta t = 3.50 s, and \Delta\vec{\bf{d}} = +25.0 m, solve for {\vec{\bf{v}}}_{i} in the equation:

    \Delta\vec{\bf{d}}={\vec{\bf{v}}}_i\Delta t+\frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2

    5. Subtract the second term on the right from both sides:

    \Delta\vec{\bf{d}} {\color{#0065fe}{- \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2}} = {\vec{\bf{v}}}_i\Delta t + \bcancel{\frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2} \color{#0065fe}{- \bcancel{\frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2}}

    Simplify:

    \Delta\vec{\bf{d}} - \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2 = {\vec{\bf{v}}}_i\Delta t

    Divide both sides by \Delta t:

    \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf{d}} - \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2}{\color{#0065fe}{\Delta t}} = \frac{{\vec{\bf{v}}}_i\bcancel{\Delta t}}{\color{#0065fe}{\bcancel{\Delta t}}}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    {\vec{\bf{v}}}_i = \frac{\Delta\vec{\bf{d}} - \frac{1}{2}\vec{\bf{a}}\left(\Delta t\right)^2}{\Delta t}

    {\vec{\bf{v}}}_i = \rm{\frac{+25.0\ m - \frac{1}{2}\cdot(+2.00\ m/s^2)(3.50\ s)^2}{3.50\ s} = 3.64\ m/s}

  5. Given x = 0.0500 metres and E_p = 0.0200 joules, solve for k in the equation:

    E_p=\frac{1}{2}kx^2

    6. Multiply both sides by 2 to cancel the \frac{1}{2}:

    {\color{#0065fe}2} E_p= \bcancel{{\color{#0065fe}2}}\cdot \frac{1}{\bcancel 2}kx^2

    Simplify:

    2 E_p= kx^2

    Divide both sides by x^2:

    \frac{2 E_p}{\color{#0065fe} x^2} = \frac{k\bcancel{x^2}}{\bcancel{\color{#0065fe} x^2}}

    Simplify and evaluate:

    k = \frac{2 E_p}{x^2} = \rm{\frac{2\cdot 0.0200\ J}{(0.0500\ m)^2} = 16.0\ J/m^2}
