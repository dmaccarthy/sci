Example 1

A 2000 kg rail car is hauled by a truck driving on a road parallel to the tracks. The cable connecting the truck to the rail car exerts a force of 1500 newtons [–30.0°]. Because the rail car can only move parallel to the tracks, assume that the track exerts a force that exactly balances the perpendicular component. Find the rail car’s acceleration.

Convert all vectors to Cartesian form and record all known components:

ForcexyUnit
\vec{\bf F}_1+1299–750N
\vec{\bf F}_20.00+750N
\vec{\bf F}_{net}+12990.00N

Solve for the x and y unknowns independently.

\vec{\bf a}_x = \frac{{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}_x}{m} = \rm{\frac{+1299\ N}{2000\ kg} = +0.650\ m/s^2}

Example 2a

A student pushes a 2.00 kg box from rest, 2.00 metres up an 8.00° frictionless incline, using an applied force of 10.0 newtons parallel to the ramp. Find the acceleration of the box, the normal force, and the speed at the top of the ramp.

Use the direction parallel to the ramp as x and the perpendicular direction as y.

Calculate the force of gravity and convert it to Cartesian form:

F_g = mg = \rm{2.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 19.6\ N}

\vec{\bf F}_{gx} = \rm{19.6\ N\cdot \cos(262^\circ) = -2.73\ N}

\vec{\bf F}_{gy} = \rm{19.6\ N\cdot \sin(262^\circ) = -19.4\ N}

Force∥ [x]⟂ [y]Unit
\vec{\bf F}_a10.00.00N
\vec{\bf F}_g–2.73–19.4N
\vec{\bf F}_n0.00+19.4N
\vec{\bf F}_{net}+7.270.00N

The normal force acts only in the perpendicular (y) direction.

We can determine the normal force because the net force acts parallel to the ramp; the y-component is zero.

The normal force balances the perpendicular component of gravity.

\vec{\bf a}_x = {\vec{\bf F}_{net}\over m} = \rm{{+7.27\ N\over 2.00\ kg} = +3.64\ m/s^2}

v_{fx} = \sqrt{v_{ix}^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}_x\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x} = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 3.64\ m/s^2 \cdot 2.00\ m} = 3.81\ m/s}

Example 2b

Re-solve the problem when the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.200.

Force∥ [x]⟂ [y]Unit
\vec{\bf F}_a10.00.00N
\vec{\bf F}_g–2.73–19.4N
\vec{\bf F}_n0.00+19.4N
\vec{\bf F}_f–3.890.00N
\vec{\bf F}_{net}+3.380.00N

Since friction acts parallel to the ramp, none of the perpendicular forces is affected.

F_f = \mu F_n = \rm{0.200\cdot 19.4\ N = 3.89\ N}

The friction force acts only in the negative, parallel (x) direction.

\vec{\bf a}_x = {\vec{\bf F}_{net}\over m} = \rm{{+3.38\ N\over 2.00\ kg} = +1.69\ m/s^2}

v_{fx} = \sqrt{v_{ix}^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}_x\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}_x} = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 1.69\ m/s^2 \cdot 2.00\ m} = 2.60\ m/s}

Example 3

A 100 kg sign is attached to a building by a cable that exerts a force at 140°. The coefficient of static friction between the left edge of the sign and the building is 0.250. Calculate the tension in the cable.

ForcexyUnit
\vec{\bf F}_g0.00–981N
\vec{\bf F}_tF_t\cos 140^\circF_t\sin 140^\circN
\vec{\bf F}_nF_n0.00N
\vec{\bf F}_f0.00\mu F_nN
\vec{\bf F}_{net}0.000.00N

This is a system of two equations in two unknowns!

F_t\cos 140^\circ + F_n = 0

{\rm -981\ N} + F_t\sin 140^\circ + \mu F_n = 0

Isolate F_n in the x equation:

F_n = -F_t\cos 140^\circ

Now substitute for F_n in the y equation:

{\rm -981\ N} + F_t\sin 140^\circ - \mu F_t\cos 140^\circ = 0

Simplify:

F_t\sin 140^\circ - \mu F_t\cos 140^\circ = \rm 981\ N

F_t(\sin 140^\circ - \mu \cos 140^\circ) = \rm 981\ N

F_t = \frac{\rm 981\ N}{\sin 140^\circ - \mu \cos 140^\circ} = \rm 1176\ N

Now solve for the remaining forces:

F_n = -F_t\cos 140^\circ = \rm -1176\ N\cdot \cos 140^\circ = \rm 901\ N

F_f = \mu F_n = \rm 0.250\cdot 901\ N = 225\ N

Finally, fill in the unknowns in the table to check our solution:

ForcexyUnit
\vec{\bf F}_g0.00–981N
\vec{\bf F}_t–901+756N
\vec{\bf F}_n+9010.00N
\vec{\bf F}_f0.00+225N
\vec{\bf F}_{net}0.000.00N

  3. A 1.25 kg physics textbook slides from rest down a 3.00 m long ramp that is inclined at a 15.0° angle. When the book reaches the bottom of the ramp, its speed is found to be 2.42 m/s.

    1. Calculate the acceleration of the book using kinematics and use the result to calculate the net force. If you take the direction parallel to the ramp as the x-axis, you can solve the problem as a 1D uniform accelerated motion situation since there is no motion in the y-direction.

      2. Use the uniform accelerated motion equations to find the acceleration. Since we’ve drawn the right side of the ramp higher than the left, the displacment, velocity, and acceleration will be negative. If you draw the left side higher, these vectors will all be positive.

      \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{v_f^2-v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(-2.42\ m/s)^2- (0.00\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot(-3.00)\ m}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{-0.976\ m/s^2} \end{align}

    2. Express the force of gravity in Cartesian form. Since we are taking the x-axis as the direction parallel to the ramp, the direction of gravity is not 270° in this coordinate system.

      3. The direction of gravity is 270° – 15° = 255°. If you draw the ramp with the left side higher, the angle will be 270° + 15° = 285°.

      F_g = mg = \rm{1.25\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 12.3\ N}

      \vec{\bf F}_g = \rm{(12.3\ \cos(255^\circ), 12.3\ \sin(255^\circ))\ N} = \rm{(-3.17, -11.8)\ N}

    3. Find the normal force by solving the y-component (perpendicular to the ramp) of Newton’s 2nd Law.
      4. ForcexyUnit
      \vec{\bf F}_g–3.17–11.8N
      \vec{\bf F}_n0.00+11.8N
      \vec{\bf F}_f+1.950.00N
      \vec{\bf F}_{net}–1.220.00N

      The normal force must be +11.8 newtons since the net force is zero in the y-direction.

    4. Calculate the force of friction from the net force you obtained in (a). Use the answer to calculate the coefficient of kinetic friction.
      5. In the x-direction...

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{1.25\ kg\cdot (-0.976\ m/s^2) = -1.22\ N}

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_f + \vec{\bf F}_{gx}

      \vec{\bf F}_f = \vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_{gx} = \rm{-1.22\ N - (-3.17\ N) = +1.95\ N}

      \mu_k = \frac{F_f}{F_n} = \rm{\frac{1.95\ N}{11.8\ N} = 0.165}

      the signs of all x-forces will be reversed if you draw the left side of the ramp higher, but the coefficient of friction will be the same!

  5. A pulley system is combined with an inclined plane as illustrated below and released from rest.

    1. Using m_1 = 5.00 kg,m_2 = 2.50 kg, h = 60.0 cm, and a ramp inclination of \theta = 10.0° from the horizontal, determine the acceleration and the speed of the system just before m_2 strikes the ground. Assume the ramp is frictionless.
    2. Repeat your calculations for a coefficient of friction of \mu_k = 0.150.

      3. For a 10° incline, the direction of gravity acting on m_1 is 270° – 10° = 260°. This force of gravity is...

      F_g = mg = \rm{5.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 49.1\ N}

      \vec{\bf F}_g = \rm{(49.1\ \cos(260^\circ), 49.1\ \sin(260^\circ))\ N} = {\rm(-8.52, -48.3)\ N}

      All of the forces acting on m_1, and the net force, are...

      xy
      \vec{\bf F}_g–8.52–48.3
      \vec{\bf F}_tF_t0
      \vec{\bf F}_n0F_n
      \vec{\bf F}_f-\mu F_n0
      \vec{\bf F}_{net}m_1a0

      Because the net y-force is zero, the normal force must be +48.3 N. We can then write the x-equation as:

      {\rm -8.52\ N} + F_t - \mu{\rm(48.3\ N)} = m_1a

      The second mass moves moves only in the vertical direction. Newton’s 2nd Law is:

      F_t - m_2 g = -m_2a

      becase m_2 has the same acceleration as m_1 but in the negative (downward) direction.

      We can combine the equations and eliminate F_t by subtracting the two equations from each other:

      {\rm -8.52\ N} - \mu{\rm (48.3\ N)} + m_2g = m_1a + m_2a

      a = \frac{{\rm -8.52\ N} - \mu{\rm (48.3\ N)} + m_2g}{m_1 + m_2}

      When there is no friction:

      a = \rm{\frac{-8.52\ N + 2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{7.50\ kg} = 2.13\ m/s^2}

      If \mu = 0.150:

      \begin{align}a &= \rm{\frac{-8.52\ N - 0.150(48.3\ N)+ 2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{7.50\ kg}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{1.17\ m/s^2} \end{align}

      We know the displacment is 0.600 metres and the initial velocity is zero, so the final velocity is:

      v_f = \sqrt{v_i^2 + 2\vec{\bf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}} = \sqrt{2\vec{\bf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}}

      Using the acceleration for zero friction gives:

      v_f = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 2.13\ m/s^2\cdot 0.600\ m} = 1.60\ m/s}

      Using the acceleration for \mu = 0.150 gives:

      v_f = \rm{\sqrt{2\cdot 1.17\ m/s^2\cdot 0.600\ m} = 1.18\ m/s}
