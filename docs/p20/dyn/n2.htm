Forces & Inertia

Inertia (Newton’s 1st Law)

If you stop pushing on an object, it will begin to slow and eventually come to rest.

  • How long it takes to come to rest depends on how much friction and air resistance are present, since these are the forces responsible for transforming the kinetic energy into other energy forms.
  • If there were no forces acting on the object at all — including no friction or air resistance — the object would continue to move forever with a constant velocity, since its kinetic energy would not change.
  • Since there is no air resistance outside the atmosphere, objects in space (planets, asteroids, comets) move without slowing.

The tendency of objects to move with constant velocity is called inertia.

  • The Law of Inertia was first stated by Galileo in the early 1600’s, before the Law of Energy Conservation had even been discovered.
  • When he wrote his book on motion, Newton adopted the Law of Inertia as his first law of motion...
  • Newton’s 1st Law: Objects at rest tend to remain at rest and objects in motion tend to remain in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
  • In other words, uniform motion is the natural state of motion for objects when they are not interacting with other objects.

Force (Newton’s 2nd Law)

On their own, objects move in uniform motion.

  • To make an object accelerate (speed up, slow down, or change direction) requires a force.
  • An object’s mass determines its inertia; larger masses are more difficult to accelerate.
  • Newton’s 2nd Law describes the effect of force on motion in terms of acceleration:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a}

  • The SI unit of force is the newton: \rm{N = kg\cdot m/s^2}.
  • If more than one force acts on the object, {\vec{\bf F}}_{net} is the vector sum of all the individual forces.
  • The net or total force is also called the unbalanced force or the resultant force.

Some common forces are:

  • Gravity: An attractive force between masses.
  • Elastic: Exerted by a stretched or compressed object trying to return to its original shape (bow, spring, bungee).
  • Tension: Exerted by an object that does not stretch (rope, rod).
  • Normal: Exerted by a surface (ground, floor, table) on an object in contact; acts perpendicular to the surface.
  • Friction: Exerted by a surface on an object opposite to the motion; acts parallel to the surface.
  • Air Resistance: Exerted by air (or other gases) on objects moving through.
  • Applied: Exerted by a person, animal, or machine.
  • Others: Buoyancy, Electric, Magnetic, Nuclear

Example 1

How much force is needed to make a 2.00-kilogram textbook accelerate at 4.00 m/s2?

{\vec{\bf F}}_{net} = m{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{2.00\ kg \cdot 4.00\ m/s^2 = 8.00\ N}

Example 2

What is the acceleration of a 25.0 kg child being pushed on a swing when the net force is 90.0 N?

{\vec{\bf a}} = {\vec{\bf F}_{net} \over m} = \rm{{90.0\ N\over 25.0\ kg} = 3.60\ m/s^2}

Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram is an illustration that shows the forces acting on an object.

  • Each force is drawn as a vector pointing in the direction that the force acts.
  • The length of each vector illustrates how strong the force is.
  • For a mass on a ramp, the forces are:
    • gravitational force pulling down
    • normal force pushing perpendicular to the ramp
    • friction parallel to the ramp, opposite to the motion

Free body diagrams are used to clarify the interactions that an object is experiencing, in order to determine the object’s acceleration.

Vector Diagrams

A free body diagram should not be confused with a vector diagram.

  • The vector diagram illustrates the same three forces as the free body diagram.
  • The main difference is that the arrows representing the force vectors have been redrawn “tip-to-tail” to show the resultant (net) force.
  • Vector diagram are drawn exactly the same for forces as for displacement.
  • For an object moving horizontally, the vertical forces must balance and the net force will be the total of the horizontal forces.
  • For an object moving vertically, the horizontal forces must balance and the net force will be the total of the vertical forces.

Practice

  1. An object slides along a horizontal surface. For each scenario, state whether the motion will be uniform or accelerated. If accelerated motion, state whether the object will speed up or slow down.
    1. There is friction acting on the object but no applied force.

      2. Uniform Accelerated Motion (Slowing Down)

    2. There is an applied force that is equal and opposite to friction.

      3. Uniform Motion

    3. There is an applied force that is opposite and greater than friction.

      4. Uniform Accelerated Motion (Speeding Up)

    4. There are no forces at all (not even friction.)

      5. Uniform Motion

  2. How much net force is required to accelerate a 12.5 kg mass at a rate of 2.36 m/s2?

    3. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{12.5\ kg\cdot (+2.36\ m/s^2) = +29.5\ N}

  3. What is the acceleration of a 295 kg satellite experiencing an unbalanced force of 1.25 × 103 N?

    4. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+1.25\times 10^3\ N}{295\ kg} = +4.24\ m/s^2}

  4. An electron accelerates at 3.51 × 1011 m/s2 when experiencing a force of 3.20 × 10–19 N. What is the electron’s mass?

    5. m = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{+3.20 \times 10^{-19}\ N}{+3.51\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2} = 9.12\times 10^{-31}\ kg}

    1. Using the electron mass you calculated in the previous question, determine the acceleration of an electron that experiences a force of 5.89 × 10–19 N.

      2. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+5.89\times 10^{-19}\ N}{9.12\times 10^{-31}\ kg} = +6.46\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2}

    2. The force causes the electron to accelerate from rest to a final speed of 6.00 × 106 m/s? Calculate (i) the time over which the force acted and (ii) the electron’s displacement while accelerating.

      3. \begin{align} \Delta t &= \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\vec{\bf a}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{+6.00\times 10^6\ m/s- 0.00\ m/s}{+6.46\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{9.29\times 10^{-6}\ s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \Delta\vec{\bf d} &= \frac{\vec{\bf v}_i + \vec{\bf v}_f}{2} \Delta t \\[10pt] &= \rm{\left(\frac{0.00\ m/s + 6.00\times 10^6\ m/s}{2}\right) 9.29\times 10^{-6}\ s} \\[10pt] &= \rm{27.9\ m} \end{align}

  6. An 82.0 kg runner starting from rest reaches a top speed of 10.6 m/s, 15.2 m from the starting line. Calculate the average net force responsible for accelerating the runner.

    7. \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(10.6\ m/s)^2 - (0.00\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 15.2\ m}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+3.70\ m/s^2} \end{align}

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm 82.0\ kg\cdot (+3.70\ m/s^2) = 303\ N

  7. A 68.5 kg skydiver experiences a force of 672 N “down” due to gravity and a force of 138 N “up” due to air resistance. What is the skydiver’s acceleration (including direction)? Include a free body diagram with your solution.

    8. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \rm{+138\ N + (-672\ N) = -534\ N}

    \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-534\ N}{68.5\ kg} = -7.80\ m/s^2}

    1. Draw a free body diagram and a vector diagram, when a falling object experiences gravity of {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g = –63.8 N and air resistance of {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_f = +12.5 N.

      2. The vectors have been shifted slightly off of the y-axis for better visibility.

    2. The mass of the object is 6.50 kg. Calculate the net force and then use your result to calculate acceleration of the mass.

      3. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_g + \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{-63.8\ N + 12.5\ N = -51.3\ N}

      \vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{m} = \mathrm{\frac{-51.3\ N}{6.50\ kg} = -7.89\ m/s^2}

  9. Calculate the mass of an object that has an acceleration of –3.92 m/s2 when the forces are {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_f = +15.0 N for air resistance and {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g = –25.0 N for gravity. The free body diagram is the same as for the previous question. Include a labelled vector diagram drawn to scale as part of your solution.

    10. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_g + \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{-25.0\ N + 15.0\ N = -10.0\ N}

    m = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{\vec{\mathbf a}} = \mathrm{\frac{-10.0\ N}{-3.92\ m/s^2} = 2.55\ kg}

  10. When a skydiver first deploys her parachute, the upward air resistance is stronger than the downward gravity force due to the large surface area of the parachute. A 58.0 kg skydiver experiences gravity of {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g = –567 N and air resistance of {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_f = +725 N.

    1. Draw a free body diagram and a vector diagram to scale.

      2. The vectors have been shifted slightly off of the y-axis for better visibility. The free body diagram is the same as #1 except that the acceleration is directed up.

    2. Calculate the skydiver’s acceleration.

      3. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_g + \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{-567\ N + 725\ N = +158\ N}

      \vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{m} = \mathrm{\frac{+158\ N}{58.0\ kg} = +2.72\ m/s^2}

    3. Explain what it means to have a positive acceleration while falling in the negative direction.

      4. It means that she is slowing down. For example, if her velocity was –30 m/s before she opened her parachute and –10 m/s after the parachute opened, her change in velocity would be positive: \Delta\vec{\mathbf v} = (–10 m/s) – (–30 m/s) = +20 m/s.

  11. An object being pushed along a horizontal surface has the free body diagram shown below.

    The force applied ({\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_a) by the person pushes the object to the right (+) while the friction force ({\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_f) acts in the opposite (–) direction. According to Newton’s 2nd Law, the acceleration will be in the positive direction when the applied force is stronger than the friction force, and in the negative direction when the friction is stronger.

    The force of gravity pulling down and the upward normal force of the ground pushing on the object will also be present; however, if the object only moves in the horizontal direction, these vertical forces will be perfectly balanced so that we can ignore them when calculating {\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_{net}.

    1. Calculate the friction force when an applied force of +58.9 N causes a 6.00 kg mass to accelerate at +4.00 m/s2.

      2. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = m\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{6.00\ kg\cdot (+4.00\ m/s^2) = +24.0\ N}

      \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_a + \vec{\mathbf F}_f

      \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} - \vec{\mathbf F}_a = \mathrm{+24.0\ N - 58.9\ N = -34.9\ N}

    2. A 2.00 kg mass experiences a friction force of –26.1 N and accelerates at –3.25 m/s2. Calculate the applied force acting on the mass and include a vector diagram.

      3. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = m\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{2.00\ kg\cdot (-3.25\ m/s^2) = -6.50\ N}

      \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_a + \vec{\mathbf F}_f

      \vec{\mathbf F}_a = \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} - \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{-6.50\ N - (-26.1\ N) = +19.6\ N}

    3. Calculate the mass of the object if it accelerates at +4.76 m/s2 when the applied force is +35.0 N and the friction is –18.0 N. Include a vector diagram.

      4. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_a + \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{+35.0\ N - 18.0\ N = +17.0\ N}

      m = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{\vec{\mathbf a}} = \mathrm{\frac{+17.0\ N}{+4.76\ m/s^2} = 3.57\ kg}

  12. During launch, a toy rocket experiences a thrust (applied force) of +185 N, air resistance of –38.2 N, and gravity of –49.1 N. All forces are vertical. If the rocket accelerates at +19.5 m/s2, what is its mass?

    13. \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_a + \vec{\mathbf F}_g + \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{+185\ N - 49.1\ N - 38.2\ N = +97.7\ N}

    m = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{\vec{\mathbf a}} = \mathrm{\frac{+97.7\ N}{+19.5\ m/s^2} = 5.01\ kg}

  13. A 1420 kg car accelerates from 50.0 km/h to 80.0 km/h over 108 m along a horizontal road. The air resistance acting on the car is –415 N. Calculate the applied force.

    14. Use the uniform accelerated motion equations to find acceleration. Be sure to change km/h to m/s first...

    \vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}} = \mathrm{\frac{(22.2\ m/s)^2 - (13.9\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 108\ m} = +1.39\ m/s^2}

    \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = m\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{1420\ kg\cdot (+1.39\ m/s^2) = +1978\ N}

    \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_a + \vec{\mathbf F}_f

    \vec{\mathbf F}_a = \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} - \vec{\mathbf F}_f = \mathrm{+1978\ N - (-415\ N) = +2393\ N}

  14. An ice hockey puck slowly decelerates due to air resistance and friction.
    1. Draw a free body diagram for the puck, illustrating its direction of motion and the direction of all forces acting on the puck.

    2. If the 376 g puck has an initial velocity of 1.25 m/s and takes 23.2 s to come to rest, determine the average net force it experienced.

      3. Use the uniform accelerated motion equations to find acceleration:

      \vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf v}_f - \vec{\mathbf v}_i}{\Delta t} = \mathrm{\frac{0.00\ m/s - 1.25\ m/s}{23.2\ s} = -0.0539\ m/s^2}

      \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = m\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{0.376\ kg\cdot (-0.0539\ m/s^2) = -0.0203\ N}
