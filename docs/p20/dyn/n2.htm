Lesson Notes

Forces & Inertia

Inertia

If you stop pushing on an object, it will begin to slow and eventually come to rest.

  • How long it takes to come to rest depends on how much friction and air resistance are present, since these are the forces responsible for transforming the kinetic energy into other energy forms.
  • If there were no forces acting on the object at all — including no friction or air resistance — the object would continue to move forever with a constant velocity, since its kinetic energy would not change.
  • Since there is no air resistance outside the atmosphere, objects in space (planets, asteroids, comets) move without slowing.

The tendency of objects to move with constant velocity is called inertia.

  • The Law of Inertia was first stated by Galileo in the early 1600’s, before the Law of Energy Conservation had even been discovered.
  • When he wrote his book on motion, Newton adopted the Law of Inertia as his first law of motion...
  • Newton’s 1st Law: Objects at rest tend to remain at rest and objects in motion tend to remain in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
  • In other words, uniform motion is the natural state of motion for objects when they are not interacting with other objects.

Force

On their own, objects move in uniform motion.

  • To make an object accelerate (speed up, slow down, or change direction) requires a force.
  • An object’s mass determines its inertia; larger masses are more difficult to accelerate.
  • The work-energy theorem was one way to describe the effect a force has on the motion of an object:

    \Delta E_k = W = \vec{\bf F}_\parallel \cdot\Delta\vec{\bf d}

  • Newton’s 2nd Law describes the effect of force on motion in terms of acceleration:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a}

  • The SI unit of force is the newton: \rm{N = kg\cdot m/s^2}.
  • If more than one force acts on the object, {\vec{\bf F}}_{net} is the vector sum of all the individual forces.
  • The net or total force is also called the unbalanced force or the resultant force.
  • The work-energy theorem equation can be derived by combining Newton’s 2nd Law with the uniform accelerated motion equations:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t}

    \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{\vec{\bf v}_i + \vec{\bf v}_f}{2} \Delta t

Some common forces are:

  • Gravity: An attractive force between masses.
  • Elastic: Exerted by a stretched or compressed object trying to return to its original shape (bow, spring, bungee).
  • Tension: Exerted by an object that does not stretch (rope, rod).
  • Normal: Exerted by a surface (ground, floor, table) on an object in contact; acts perpendicular to the surface.
  • Friction: Exerted by a surface on an object opposite to the motion; acts parallel to the surface.
  • Air Resistance: Exerted by air (or other gases) on objects moving through.
  • Applied: Exerted by a person, animal, or machine.
  • Others: Buoyancy, Electric, Magnetic, Nuclear

Example 1

How much force is needed to make a 2.00-kilogram textbook accelerate at 4.00 m/s2?

{\vec{\bf F}}_{net} = m{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{2.00\ kg \cdot 4.00\ m/s^2 = 8.00\ N}

Example 2

What is the acceleration of a 25.0 kg child being pushed on a swing when the net force is 90.0 N?

{\vec{\bf a}} = {\vec{\bf F}_{net} \over m} = \rm{{90.0\ N\over 25.0\ kg} = 3.60\ m/s^2}

Practice

  1. An object slides along a horizontal surface. For each scenario, state whether the motion will be uniform or accelerated. If accelerated motion, state whether the object will speed up or slow down.
    1. There is friction acting on the object but no applied force.

      2. Uniform Accelerated Motion (Slowing Down)

    2. There is an applied force that is equal and opposite to friction.

      3. Uniform Motion

    3. There is an applied force that is opposite and greater than friction.

      4. Uniform Accelerated Motion (Speeding Up)

    4. There are no forces at all (not even friction.)

      5. Uniform Motion

  2. How much net force is required to accelerate a 12.5 kg mass at a rate of 2.36 m/s2?

    3. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{12.5\ kg\cdot (+2.36\ m/s^2) = +29.5\ N}

  3. What is the acceleration of a 295 kg satellite experiencing an unbalanced force of 1.25 × 103 N?

    4. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+1.25\times 10^3\ N}{295\ kg} = +4.24\ m/s^2}

  4. An electron accelerates at 3.51 × 1011 m/s2 when experiencing a force of 3.20 × 10–19 N. What is the electron’s mass?

    5. m = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{\vec{\bf a}} = \rm{\frac{+3.20 \times 10^{-19}\ N}{+3.51\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2} = 9.12\times 10^{-31}\ kg}

    1. Using the electron mass you calculated in the previous question, determine the acceleration of an electron that experiences a force of 5.89 × 10–19 N.

      2. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+5.89\times 10^{-19}\ N}{9.12\times 10^{-31}\ kg} = +6.46\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2}

    2. The force causes the electron to accelerate from rest to a final speed of 6.00 × 106 m/s? Calculate (i) the time over which the force acted and (ii) the electron’s displacement while accelerating.

      3. \begin{align} \Delta t &= \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\vec{\bf a}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{+6.00\times 10^6\ m/s- 0.00\ m/s}{+6.46\times 10^{11}\ m/s^2}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{9.29\times 10^{-6}\ s} \end{align}

      \begin{align} \Delta\vec{\bf d} &= \frac{\vec{\bf v}_i + \vec{\bf v}_f}{2} \Delta t \\[10pt] &= \rm{\left(\frac{0.00\ m/s + 6.00\times 10^6\ m/s}{2}\right) 9.29\times 10^{-6}\ s} \\[10pt] &= \rm{27.9\ m} \end{align}

  6. An 82.0 kg runner starting from rest reaches a top speed of 10.6 m/s, 15.2 m from the starting line. Calculate the average net force responsible for accelerating the runner.

    7. \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(10.6\ m/s)^2 - (0.00\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 15.2\ m}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+3.70\ m/s^2} \end{align}

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm 82.0\ kg\cdot (+3.70\ m/s^2) = 303\ N

  7. A 68.5 kg skydiver experiences a force of 672 N “down” due to gravity and a force of 138 N “up” due to air resistance. What is the skydiver’s acceleration? Include a direction.

    8. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \rm{+138\ N + (-672\ N) = -534\ N}

    \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-534\ N}{68.5\ kg} = -7.80\ m/s^2}

Review

  1. Explain the Law of Inertia, also called Newton’s 1st Law of Motion.

    2. Objects at rest will remain at rest and objects in motion will remain in motion with a constant velocity unless acted upon by an unbalanced force. Once an object is set in motion it does not require a force to keep the object in motion!

    1. State Newton’s 2nd Law of Motion and explain how force and mass are related to motion.
    2. What do we mean by an unbalanced force?

    3. When there is an unbalanced force acting on an object, the object will accelerate accordingto the equation:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a}

    The unbalanced force, \vec{\bf F}_{net}, is the vector sum of all of the forces acting on the object.

    1. Is force a scalar or vector?

      2. Vector; it has a direction.

    2. What is the SI unit of force? How is this unit defined?

      3. A newton is the amount of force needed to make a 1-kilogram mass accelerate at 1 m/s2:

      \rm{N = kg\cdot\frac{m}{s^2}}

  4. Based on Newton’s Laws of Motion, what are the requirements for (a) uniform motion, and (b) uniform accelerated motion?

    5. Uniform motion occurs when the net (unbalanced) force is exactly zero. Uniform accelerated motion occurs when there is a constant, non-zero unbalanced force.
