Notes

Forces are interactions that affect how objects move.

  • Newton’s 1st Law: Also known as the Law of Inertia, this states that when the net force acting on an object is zero, the object will remain at rest or, if already in motion, will move with constant velocity (uniform motion).
  • Newton’s 2nd Law: When a mass experiences an unbalanced force, the acceleration will be related to the force by the equation:

    {\vec{\bf{F}}}_{net}=m\vec{\bf{a}}

  • Newton’s 3rd Law: When objects interact, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other:

    {\vec{\bf{F}}}_{AB}=-{\vec{\bf{F}}}_{BA}

  • This law is key to jet and rocket propulsion.

When more than once force acts on an object, we need to calculate the net or unbalanced force.

  • An illustration of an object and the forces it experiences (showing the direction and strength of each force) is called a free body diagram.

  • Draw a vector diagram by redrawing the forces tip-to-tail.
  • The net force is the resultant vector from the vector diagram.
  • To solve problems involving unbalanced force, first calculate the net force either by adding all of the forces (if known) or using Newton’s 2nd Law...
  • Then use the net force to find the unknown.
  • When the motion is 1D (e.g. horizontal), the forces in the perpendicular dimension (e.g. vertical) will always balance.

Common types of force include:

  • Elastic: The force exerted by a stretched or compressed object trying to return to its original shape
  • Tension: The force exerted by a non-elastic object like a rope or rod
  • Applied: The force exerted by a persion, machine, or animal
  • Air Resistance: A force exerted by the air that has to be moved out of the way for an object to move.

The force of gravity is an attraction that objects experience due to planets, stars, and other large masses:

{\vec{\bf{F}}}_g=m\vec{\bf{g}}

  • The force of gravity is also called the weight of the object.
  • The symbol \vec{\bf g} is the gravitational field strength of the planet/star causing the force.
  • The acceleration \vec{\bf a} of the object will equal \vec{\bf g}, when gravity is the only force acting on the object; otherwise \vec{\bf a} \ne \vec{\bf g}.
  • The symbol m is the mass of the object; this measures how much matter the object contains and is not affected by g.

Contact Forces are present when an object is in contact with a surface such as the ground or a table.

  • The force parallel to the surface is called the friction force.
  • The force perpendicular to the surface is the normal force.
  • The friction force acts in the opposite direction to the motion and is proportional to the normal force:

    \mu=\frac{|{\vec{\bf{F}}}_f|}{|{\vec{\bf{F}}}_n|}

  • The symbol \mu is called the coefficient of friction and it measures the relative strength of friction between two materials.
  • The coefficient of static friction applies when there is no relative motion.
  • The coefficient of kinetic friction which applies when there is relative motion.
  • Static friction is usually a bit stronger than kinetic friction.

For 2D motion, we can express all vectors (forces, acceleration) in Cartesian form and then apply Newton’s 2nd Law to the x- and y-components separately, just as we do with the uniform accelerated motion equations for projectile motion.

  • For inclined plane (ramp) problems, we can usually simplify the solution by using the direction parallel to the incline as the x-axis and the direction perpendicular to the incline as y.
  • By doing this, the friction force and acceleration vector will only have an x-component, and the normal force will only have a y-component.
  • When the axes are rotated this way, the direction of \vec{\bf F}_g will be 270° plus or minus the angle of incline, depending on whether the axes are rotated clockwise or counterclockwise.
