Newton’s 3rd Law

Force

A force is an interaction between two objects.

  • When you push on an object (action), the object pushes back on you (reaction).

Newton observed that:

  • the action and reaction always occur together,
  • they are equal in magnitude,
  • they act in opposite directions.

Newton’s 3rd Law: For every action (force), there is an equal and opposite reaction force:

{\color{blue}{\vec{\bf F}}_{AB}} = -{\color{red}{\vec{\bf F}}_{BA}}

  • When you walk or run, you make the ground push you forward by push backward against the ground.

  • When you climb a rope, you cause the rope to exert an upward force on you by exerting a downward force on the rope.
  • Jet and rocket propulsion work by forcing gas out the “back” of the engine, causing the gas to propel the vessel forward.

  • Since Earth exerts a gravitational force on you, you must exert an equal gravitational force on the Earth!

Example

In a game of billiards, a cue ball with a mass of 300 grams is moving at 5.00 m/s when it collides head on with a stationary ball of mass 250 g. The balls are in contact with each other for 0.200 seconds, after which the cue ball’s velocity is 2.00 m/s.

a) How much force did the cue ball experience?

\vec{\bf a} = {\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i \over \Delta t} = \rm{{2.00\ m/s - 5.00\ m/s \over 0.200\ s} = -15.0\ m/s^2}

\vec{\bf F} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{0.300\ kg\cdot (-15.0\ m/s^2) = -4.50\ N}

b) What was the second ball’s velocity after the collision?

According to Newton’s 3rd Law, the second ball experienced a force of +4.50 newtons...

\vec{\bf a} = \vec{\bf F} / m = \rm{{+4.50\ N\over 0.250\ kg} = +18.0\ m/s^2}

To find the final velocity, solve one of the uniform accelerated motion equations...

\begin{align}\vec{\bf v}_f &= \vec{\bf a}\Delta t + \vec{\bf v}_i \\[6pt] &= \rm{{+18.0\ m/s^2\cdot 0.200\ s + 0.00\ m/s}} \\[6pt] &= \rm{+3.60\ m/s}\end{align}

Practice

  1. Since Earth exerts a gravitational force on your desk, does your desk exert a gravitational force on the Earth? Why is it easier to observe the effects of the action force (Earth pulling on the desk) than the reaction force (desk pulling on Earth)?
    2. According to Newton’s 3rd Law, the Earth and the desk exert equal and opposite forces on each other. The desk’s gravity has negligible effect on the Earth because the Earth’s mass is WAY bigger.
  2. A 53.2 kg student standing on an icy sidewalk leans against a lamppost, exerting a force of 60.0 N on the post.
    1. What is the student’s acceleration?
      2. The force on the student is the negative of the force he or she exerts on the lamppost...

      \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-60.0\ N}{53.2\ kg} = -1.13\ m/s^2}

    2. Why does the lamppost not accelerate even though the student is exerting a force on it?
      3. The lamppost is attached to the ground. The ground exerts enough force to balance the force applied by the student.
  3. An archer shoots a 318 g arrow from her bow. The bowstring accelerates the arrow to a final velocity of 34.0 m/s over 82.5 cm.
    1. Determine the acceleration and force experienced by the arrow.

      2. \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{(34.0\ m/s)^2 - (0.00\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 0.825\ m}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+701\ m/s^2} \\[16pt] \vec{\bf F} &= m\vec{\bf a} \\[10pt] &= \rm{0.318\ kg\cdot (+701\ m/s^2)} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+223\ N} \end{align}

    2. What recoil force does the archer experience through the bow?
      3. According to Newton’s 3rd Law, the archer experiences a recoil of –223 N.
    3. If the 59.1 kg archer shoots while standing on a perfectly frictionless surface, what would her acceleration be?

      4. \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-223\ N}{59.1\ kg} = -3.77\ m/s^2}

    4. Why does the archer not accelerate when she shoots while standing on grass, dirt or concrete?
      5. The friction produced by the ground would balance the recoil applied by the bow.
  4. A 1.45 × 104 kg bus and a 1.07 × 103 kg car moving in the opposite direction collide head-on. The bus “decelerates” at 12.0 m/s2 during the collision.
    1. What is the car’s acceleration?
      2. First find the net force acting on the bus...

      \vec{\bf F} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{1.45\times 10^4\ kg\cdot (-12.0\ m/s^2) = -1.74\times 10^5\ N}

      The force acting on the car is \vec{\bf F} = \rm{+1.74\times 10^5\ N} according to Newton’s 3rd Law. For the car...

      \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+1.74\times 10^5\ N}{1.07\times 10^3\ kg} = +163\ m/s^2}

    2. Explain why seatbelts are mandatory in cars while many busses are not equipped with seatbelts.
      3. During a collision, passengers in a more massive vehicle will experience less acceleration than those in a smaller vehicle. The risk of injury is greater for those in the lighter vehicle.
  5. An asteroid at rest explodes into two pieces. During the explosion, the two fragments exert forces on each other over a time interval of 0.750 s. One piece has a mass of 1240 kg and a velocity of 523 m/s after the explosion. Determine the force that the pieces exert on each other and the final velocity of the second fragment, which has a mass of 874 kg.
    6. Calculate the acceleration and force on the 1240 kg object...

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf a} &= \frac{\vec{\bf v}_f - \vec{\bf v}_i}{\Delta t} \\[10pt] &= \rm{\frac{+523\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s}{0.750\ s}} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+697\ m/s^2} \end{align}

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf F} &= m\vec{\bf a} \\[10pt] &= \rm{1240\ kg\cdot (+697\ m/s^2)} \\[10pt] &= \rm{+8.65\times 10^5\ N} \end{align}

    Calculate the acceleration of the 874 kg object using the opposite force...

    \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-8.65\times 10^5\ N}{874\ kg} = -989\ m/s^2}

    Calculate the final velocity...

    \begin{align} \vec{\bf v}_f &= \vec{\bf a}\Delta t + \vec{\bf v}_i \\[10pt] &= \rm{-989\ m/s^2\cdot 0.750\ s + 0.00\ m/s} \\[10pt] &= \rm{-742\ m/s} \end{align}
