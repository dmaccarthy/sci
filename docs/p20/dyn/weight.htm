Lesson Notes

Mass & Weight

Gravitational Field

Galileo observed that all objects moving under gravity have the same acceleration when there are no other forces affecting the motion (e.g. air resistance or applied forces.)

  • The Ancient Greek scientists thought heavy objects would fall faster.
  • Differences in acceleration are due to air resistance; objects with a relatively large cross-sectional area will experience more effects due to air resistance.
  • On the surface of Earth, the value of the acceleration of gravity (also called the gravitational field strength) is, on average |{\vec{\bf g}}| = {\rm{9.81\ m/s^2}}.
  • The actual value of |{\vec{\bf g}}| on Earth varies slightly depending on altitude and latitude.
  • On the Moon, the approximate value of |{\vec{\bf g}}| is {\rm{1.63\ m/s^2}}.

Mass & Weight

The mass (m) of an object describes the:

  • amount of matter that the object contains
  • how much inertia the object possesses (how difficult it is to change the object’s motion).

Mass does not depend on gravity: a 1 kg mass on the Moon contains just as much matter as the same 1 kg object on Earth.

An object’s weight ({\vec{\bf F}}_g) is the force that gravity exerts on the object.

  • Weight depends on both the object’s mass and on the gravitational field strength.
  • Since the acceleration is \vec{\bf g} when gravity is the only force acting, according to Newton’s 2nd Law the relationship between mass and weight is:

    {\vec{\bf F}}_g = m{\vec{\bf g}}

  • This means for example, that a 1.00 kg object weighs 9.81 N on Earth.
  • The same 1.00 kg object when placed on the Moon weighs only 1.63 N.
  • A 1.00 kg object in deep space, far from any sources of gravity, has no weight at all.

Example 1

Determine the acceleration of a 350-gram ball falling, when it experiences air resistance of 0.800 newtons.

First calculate the force of gravity:

\vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm 0.350\ kg\cdot(-9.81\ m/s^2) = -3.4335\ N

Now calculate the net force:

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_f = \rm -3.4335\ N + 0.800\ N = -2.6335\ N

Finally, use Newton’s 2nd Law to find the acceleration:

\vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm \frac{-2.6335\ N}{0.350\ kg} = -7.52\ m/s^2

Apparent Weight

It is not possible to sense or feel the force of gravity in a uniform gravitational field.

  • Because uniform gravity acts equally on all parts of your body, it does not create any stresses in your tissues that your body is able to feel.
  • Ironically, the sensation of having weight is created by all of the forces acting on you except for gravity.
  • When you are standing, the normal force of the floor acting on your feet creates stress in your ankles, knees, etc. that gives your body the sensation of weight.
  • When you are accelerating, the forces acting on you are not balanced, and you may feel the sensation of increased or decreased apparent weight.
  • For example, you feel heavy when you begin to ascend in an elevator, and light when the elevator begins to slow down.

Example 2

Find the apparent weight (i.e. normal force) of a 40.0-kilogram student in an elevator when accelerating at +2.50 m/s2.

First calculate the force of gravity (actual weight):

\vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm 40.0\ kg\cdot(-9.81\ m/s^2) = -392.4\ N

Now calculate the net force using Newton’s 2nd Law:

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm 40.0\ kg\cdot(+2.50\ m/s^2) = +100\ N

The net force is the total of gravity and the normal force:

\vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_n

\vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_g = \vec{\bf F}_n

\vec{\bf F}_n = \rm +100\ N - (-392.4\ N) = +492\ N

The student’s actual weight is 392 newtons, but they have the sensation of weighing 492 newtons due to the acceleration of the elevator.

Example 3

Find the apparent weight (i.e. normal force) of the 40.0-kilogram student in the elevator when it comes to rest by accelerating at –2.50 m/s2.

The calculations are identical to the previous example, except that the acceleration and net force are in the negative direction:

\vec{\bf F}_n = \rm -100\ N - (-392.4\ N) = +292\ N

Practice

  1. Determine the weight on Earth of objects with masses:
    1. 1.00 kg

      2. F_g = mg = \rm{1.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 9.81\ N}

    2. 2.00 kg

      3. F_g = mg = \rm{2.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 19.6\ N}

    3. 103.2 kg

      4. F_g = mg = \rm{103.2\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 1.01\times 10^3\ N}

    4. 32.4 mg

      5. F_g = mg = \rm{32.4\times 10^{-6}\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 3.18\times 10^{-4}\ N}

  2. Determine the mass of objects that weigh (on Earth):
    1. 1.00 N

      2. m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \rm{\frac{1.00\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.102\ kg}

    2. 25.0 N

      3. m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \rm{\frac{25.0\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.55\ kg}

    3. 46.8 mN

      4. m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \rm{\frac{46.8\times 10^{-3}\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.00477\ kg}

    4. 1.59 × 106 N

      5. m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \rm{\frac{1.59\times 10^6\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 1.62\times 10^5\ kg}

  3. A deluxe cheeseburger weighs 2.30 N on Earth. An astronaut about to eat the burger on an alien planet notices that it weighs 2.97 N.
    1. Calculate the burger’s mass.

      2. m = \frac{F_g}{g} = \rm{\frac{2.30\ N}{9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.234\ kg}

    2. Calculate the magnitude of \vec{\bf{g}} on the alien planet.

      3. g = \frac{F_g}{m} = \rm{\frac{2.97\ N}{0.234\ kg} = 12.7\ m/s^2}

    1. A 78.0 kg skydiver experiences an upward force of 205 N due to air resistance, in addition to the force of gravity. Determine her acceleration.

      2. \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm{78.0\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2) = -765\ N}

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_f = \rm{-765\ N + 205\ N = -560\ N}

      \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{-560\ N}{78.0\ kg} = -7.18\ m/s^2}

    2. As the skydiver accelerates, the air resistance gradually increases. A short time later, the skydiver’s downward acceleration is 5.30 m/s2. What is the air resistance at this point?

      3. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm 78.0\ kg\cdot (-5.30\ m/s^2) = -413\ N

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_f

      \vec{\bf F}_f = \vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_g

      \vec{\bf F}_f = \rm -413\ N - (-765\ N) = +352\ N

  5. Determine the mass of an object that has an acceleration of 6.25 m/s2 [down] as a result of gravity and an upward air resistance of 18.0 N.

    6. The free body diagram is the same as for the skydiver in the previous question.

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_f

    Substitute \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g}...

    m\vec{\bf a} = m\vec{\bf g} + \vec{\bf F}_f

    m\vec{\bf a} - m\vec{\bf g} = \vec{\bf F}_f

    m (\vec{\bf a} - \vec{\bf g}) = \vec{\bf F}_f

    m = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_f}{\vec{\bf a} - \vec{\bf g}}

    m = \rm \frac{18.0\ N}{-6.25\ m/s^2 - (-9.81\ m/s^2)} = 5.06\ kg

  6. A 21.0 g acorn falls 4.00 m in 1.12 s, starting from rest. What force of air resistance did it experience?

    7. The free body diagram is the same as for the previous two questions.

    Find the acceleration using the uniform accelerated motion equations. Knowing the the initial velocity is zero, we can simplify the equations by ignoring the \vec{\bf v}_i terms. (Alternatively, you could find the final velocity first and then the acceleration.)

    \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{1}{2} \vec{\bf a} (\Delta t)^2

    \vec{\bf a} = \frac{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{(\Delta t)^2}

    \vec{\bf a} = \rm \frac{2\cdot(-4.00\ m)}{(1.12\ s)^2} = -6.38\ m/s^2

    Now calculate the force of gravity and the net force...

    \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm{0.0210\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2) = -0.206\ N}

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = \rm{0.0210\ kg\cdot (-6.38\ m/s^2) = -0.134\ N}

    Finally find the air resistance...

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_f

    \vec{\bf F}_f = \vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_g

    \vec{\bf F}_f = \rm -0.134\ N - (-0.206\ N) = +0.0721\ N

  7. A student with a mass of 60.0 kg stands on a scale. As the student jumps off the scale, the scale reading increases to 610 N. This is the student’s apparent weight, which is actually the normal force of the scale pushing on the student.
    1. What was the original reading on the scale (the student’s actual weight)?

      2. \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm{60.0\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2) = -589\ N}

    2. What was the student’s vertical acceleration when jumping off the scale?

      3. \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_n = \rm{-589\ N + 610\ N = +21.4\ N}

      \vec{\bf a} = \frac{\vec{\bf F}_{net}}{m} = \rm{\frac{+21.4\ N}{60.0\ kg} = +0.357\ m/s^2}

    1. A 52.0-kilogram student enters an elevator on the ground floor. As the elevator begins to accelerate, the student notices that it takes 4.00 seconds for the elevator to reach the full speed, which occurs around the 5th floor, 24.0 metres above ground level. Assuming uniform motion, determine the apparent weight of the students while accelerating.

      2. The student’s actual weight is:

      \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g} = {\rm 52.0\ kg\cdot (-9.81\ m/s^2) = -510\ N}

      Use uniform accelerated motion to solve for the acceleration:

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \vec{\bf v}_i\Delta t + \frac{1}{2} \vec{\bf a}(\Delta t)^2

      Since the initial velocity is zero:

      \Delta\vec{\bf d} = \frac{1}{2} \vec{\bf a}(\Delta t)^2

      \vec{\bf a} = \frac{2\Delta\vec{\bf d}}{(\Delta t)^2} = {\rm \frac{2\cdot (+24.0\ m)}{(4.00\ s)^2} = +3.00\ m/s^2}

      Use Newton’s 2nd Law to calculate the net force:

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf a} = {\rm 52.0\ kg\cdot (+3.00\ m/s^2) = +156\ N}

      This means that the upward normal force (apparent weight) of the elevator floor pushing on the student must be 156 newtons stronger than the downward force of gravity. The free body diagram is the same as for the previous question.

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} = \vec{\bf F}_g + \vec{\bf F}_n

      \vec{\bf F}_{net} - \vec{\bf F}_g = \vec{\bf F}_n

      \vec{\bf F}_n = {\rm +156\ N - (-510\ N) = +666\ N}

    2. Determine the apparent weight of the student when they are descending to return to the ground floor. Assume that the acceleration has the same magnitude but is in the downward direction.

      3. The calculations are exactly the same except that the directions of the displacement, acceleration, and net force are negative; the normal force of 156 newtons weaker than gravity:

      \vec{\bf F}_n = {\rm -156\ N - (-510\ N) = +354\ N}

  9. Define mass and weight; explain how these differ.

    10. Mass is a measure of the quantity of matter that an object contains, which is equal to the object’s inertia. Mass des not depend on the gravity that the object experiences. Mass is measured in kilograms.

    Weight is a measure of the force of gravity acting on the object, which changes depending on how strong a gravitational field the object experiences. Weight, being a force, is measured in newtons.

  10. State the relationship between mass and weight.

    11. \vec{\bf F}_g = m\vec{\bf g}

  11. When an object is moved to a location where gravity is weaker than it is on Earth, how (if at all) do its mass and weight change?

    12. The mass is unchanged, but the weight decreases.

  12. Explain the difference between apparent weight and actual weight.

    13. Apparent weight is the sensation of having weight which results from forces like the normal force trying to balance the force of gravity. Gravity itself cannot be detected by your body because it acts evenly over all parts of your body. When a mass is accelerating, the forces will not be balanced, so the apparent weight can differ from the actual weight.
