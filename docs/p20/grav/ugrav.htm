Universal Gravitation

Gravitation

Sir Isaac Newton realized that the same force of gravity that causes an apple to fall from a tree can also explain the motion of planets (and asteroids and comets) around the Sun.

  • Johannes Kepler had studied the motion of the planets around the Sun, and discovered a relationship between the radius of the planet’s orbit and the time it takes to complete each orbit.
  • Based on his Laws of Motion, Newton understood that it requires a force to keep the planets moving in a circle; without an unbalanced force, the planets would move in a straight line.
  • By combining his own understanding of dynamics with Kepler’s description of planetary motion, Newton determined that the force of the Sun’s gravity acting on each planet is:

F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}

  • In this equation, M is the mass of the Sun, and m is the mass of the planet.
  • The variable r is the radius of the planet’s orbit, which is also the between the Sun and planet.
  • The symbol G is called the universal gravitational constant; its value was not known to Newton.
  • If we substitute F_g = mg, the planet’s mass cancels:

g = \frac{GM}{r^2}

  • This tells of the strength of the Sun’s gravity at a separation of r, which is also the acceleration of a planet (or any other object!) orbiting with that radius.
  • The variable g is called the gravitational field strength or the acceleration due to gravity.

Universal Gravitation

Because of Newton’s 3rd Law, any object that can experience gravity will also exert an equal and opposite force of gravity on the other mass.

  • This means that gravitation is universal; all masses (not just stars and planets) create their own gravitational field and exert gravitational force on nearby masses!
  • In other words, any two masses (no matter how small), will exert a gravitational attraction (equal and opposite) on each other:

    F_g = {Gm_1 m_2\over r^2}

  • This equation is known as Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation.
  • The variable r can no longer be called the “radius” because, if Newton is correct, the equation does not require circular motion. Instead, r is called the separation between the objects.
  • This variable is always measured from the centres of the objects.

Sources & Test Masses

We can rearrange Newton’s equation for gravitational force by dividing both sides by m_2:

{F_g\over m_2} = {Gm_1\over r^2}

  • The left side is the acceleration of m_2, provided gravity is the only force!
  • The right side is the gravitational field produced by m_1.

    a_2 = {F_g\over m_2} = {Gm_1\over r^2} = g_1

  • The gravity of m_1 causes the acceleration of m_2 (and vice versa.)
  • The object whose motion we are interested in is called the test mass (m_2).
  • The object(s) whose gravity affects the test mass are called the sources or field masses (m_1).

Inverse Square Law / Universal Gravitational Constant

The Law of Universal Gravitation is an “inverse square law” because, as the objects get further apart, the force gets weaker by a factor of r^2.

  • In other words, when the separation between the objects doubles, the gravitational force becomes only one-quarter as strong.
  • The value of the constant G was unknown in Newton’s lifetime. It must be measured experimentally.
  • To three significant digits, the accepted value is

    G=6.67\times{10}^{-11}\rm{\frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}

  • This means that two masses of 1.00 kilogram each, separated by a distance of 1.00 metre, will attract each other with a miniscule 6.67 × 10–11 newtons of gravitational force!

Example 1

Two spacecraft have masses of 1.20 × 106 kg and 1.80 × 106 kg. The spacecraft are 2.00 km apart. Calculate the force of gravity between the spacecraft, and their accelerations when there are no other forces present.

F_g=\frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}

F_g= \rm{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}\cdot 1.20\times 10^6\ kg \cdot 1.80\times 10^6\ kg\over (2000\ m)^2}

F_g= 3.60\times 10^{-5}\ \rm{N}

a_1= {F_g \over m_1} = \rm{3.60\times 10^{-5}\ N \over 1.20\times 10^6\ kg} = \rm{3.00\times 10^{-11}\ m/s^2}

a_2= {F_g \over m_2} = \rm{3.60\times 10^{-5}\ N \over 1.80\times 10^6\ kg} = \rm{2.00\times 10^{-11}\ m/s^2}

Example 2

A meteor approaching Earth experiences an acceleration of 1.50 m/s2 due to Earth’s gravity. How far away is the meteor?

g=\frac{GM}{r^2}

gr^2=GM

r=\sqrt{GM\over g}

Look up the mass of Earth on your data sheet:

r= \rm{\sqrt{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}\cdot 5.97\times 10^{24}\ kg \over 1.50\ m/s^2}}

r= \rm{1.63\times 10^7\ m}

Remember that you cannot use g = 9.81 m/s2 when the object is not near Earth’s surface!

Example 3

A satellite with mass m_1 has an orbital radius r_1 around some planet. A second spacecraft has double the satellite’s mass and approaches the same planet at three times the separation. Calculate the ratio of the gravitational force, F_2\over F_1, that the two masses experience.

Using M for the mass of the planet and m_1 and m_2 for the masses of the satellites, the force of the planet on the first sateillite is:

F_1= \frac{GMm_1}{r_1^2}

The force of the planet on the second satellite is:

F_2 =\frac{GMm_2}{r_2^2} = \frac{GM(2m_1)}{(3r_1)^2}

F_2 = \frac{2GMm_1}{9r_1^2} = \frac{2}{9}\left(\frac{GMm_1}{r_1^2}\right)

Now calculate the ratio:

{F_2\over F_1} = {2\over 9}

Example 4

Find the gravitational field at the midpoint between two planets of mass 6.00\times 10^{24}\ \rm {kg} and 4.00\times 10^{24}\ \rm {kg} that are separated by 8.00\times 10^8\ {\rm m}.

At the midpoint:

r = {\rm \frac{8.00\times 10^8\ m}{2} = 4.00\times 10^8\ m}

Gravity due to the larger planet is:

g_1 = \frac{GM_1}{r^2} = {\rm \frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {\frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}\cdot 6.00\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(4.00\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 2.50\times 10^{-3}\ m/s^2}

Gravity due to the smaller planet is:

g_2 = \frac{GM_2}{r^2} = {\rm \frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {\frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}\cdot 4.00\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(4.00\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 1.67\times 10^{-3}\ m/s^2}

At the midpoint, the two planets are pulling in opposite directions, so we must make one of the \vec{\bf g} vectors positive and one negative.

The net gravitational field is:

\vec{\bf g} = \vec{\bf g}_1 + \vec{\bf g}_2 = {\rm (-2.50 + 1.67) \times 10^{-3}\ m/s^2 = -8.34\times 10^{-4}\ m/s^2}

Practice

  1. A satellite orbiting the Earth experiences a centripetal acceleration of 7.06 m/s2 due to Earth’s gravity.
    1. How far is the satellite from the Earth’s centre?

      2. \begin{align} g &= \frac{GM_{earth}}{r^2} \\ \\ g r^2 &= GM_{earth} \\ \\ r^2 &= \frac{GM_{earth}}{g} \\ \\ r &= \sqrt{\frac{GM_{earth}}{g}} \\ \\ &= \sqrt{\rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 5.97\times 10^{24}\ kg}{7.06\ m/s^2}}} \\ \\ &= \rm{7.51\times 10^6\ m} \end{align}

    2. What is the satellite’s altitude?

      3. \rm{7.51\times 10^6\ m - 6.37\times 10^6\ m = 1.14\times 10^6\ m}

  2. Calculate the acceleration due to Earth’s gravitational at an altitude of 275 km above the ground.

    3. r = \rm{6.37\times 10^6\ m + 2.75\times 10^5\ m = 6.65\times 10^6\ m}

    g = \frac{GM_{earth}}{r^2} = \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 5.97\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(6.65\times 10^6\ m)^2}} = \rm{9.00\ m/s^2}

  3. What is the mass of a star if a planet orbiting at a radius of 4.86 × 1011 m experiences a gravitational acceleration of 1.20 × 10–3 m/s2?

    4. \begin{align} g &= \frac{GM}{r^2} \\ \\ gr^2 &= GM \\ \\ M &= \frac{gr^2}{G} \\ \\ &= \rm{\frac{1.20\times 10^{-3}\ m/s^2 (4.86\times 10^{11}\ m)^2}{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}} \\ \\ &= \rm{4.25\times 10^{30}\ kg} \end{align}

  4. How far apart are two identical 1350 kg cars if they attract each other with a force of 8.44 × 10–9 newtons? Why would we not notice this gravitational attraction in everyday life?

    5. \begin{align} F_g &= \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2} \\ \\ F_g r^2 &= Gm_1m_2 \\ \\ r^2 &= \frac{Gm_1m_2}{F_g} \\ \\ r &= \sqrt{\frac{Gm_1m_2}{F_g}} \\ \\ &= \sqrt{\rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot (1350\ kg)^2}{8.44\times 10^{-9}\ N}}} \\ \\ &= \rm{120\ m} \end{align}

    1. Two asteroids are located 1.28 km apart. The asteroids experience a gravitational attraction of 40.7 N. If the smaller asteroid’s mass is 3.51 × 108 kg, what is the mass of the larger asteroid?

      2. \begin{align} F_g &= \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2} \\ \\ F_g r^2 &= Gm_1m_2 \\ \\ m_2 &= \frac{F_g r^2}{Gm_1} \\ \\ &= \rm{\frac{40.7\ N\ (1.28\times 10^3\ m)^2}{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2} \cdot 3.51\times 10^8\ kg}} \\ \\ &= \rm{2.85\times 10^9\ kg} \end{align}

    2. Calculate the acceleration of each asteroid.

      3. a_1 = \frac{F_g}{m_1} = \rm{\frac{40.7\ N}{3.51\times 10^8\ kg} = 1.16\times 10^{-7}\ m/s^2}

      a_2 = \frac{F_g}{m_2} = \rm{\frac{40.7\ N}{2.85\times 10^9\ kg} = 1.43\times 10^{-8}\ m/s^2}

    3. Why does the smaller one accelerate at a greater rate than the larger one?
      4. Each asteroid experiences the same force; therefore the more massive asteroid accelerates at a slower rate.
  6. A satellite orbiting a planet with a radius r experiences a gravitational acceleration of 5.60 m/s2. What gravitational acceleration will a satellite orbiting at 2r experience?

    7. Because the separation is double, the gravity is four times weaker due to the inverse square law:

    g_2 = \rm{\frac{5.60\ m/s^2}{4} = 1.40\ m/s^2}

    We can show this algebraically:

    g_1 = \frac{GM}{r^2}

    g_2 = \frac{GM}{(2r)^2} = \frac{GM}{4r^2} = \frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{GM}{r^2}\right) = \frac{1}{4} g_1

  7. A 500 kg spacecraft is at the midpoint between the Earth and the Moon. What net gravitational force and acceleration does the spacecraft experience? [Hint: How far is the spacecraft from Earth’s centre? From the Moon’s centre?]

    8. Since the spacecraft is at the midpoint, its separation from both Earth and the Moon is half of the Earth-Moon separation:

    r = \rm{\frac{3.84\times 10^8\ m}{2} = 1.92\times 10^8\ m}

    Use the universal gravitation equation to calculate the gravity produced by each source on the spacecraft:

    g_E = \frac{GM_E}{r^2} = \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 5.97\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(1.92\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 0.0108\ m/s^2}

    g_M = \frac{GM_M}{r^2} = \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 7.40\times 10^{22}\ kg}{(1.92\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 1.34\times 10^{-4}\ m/s^2}

    Keep in mind that the forces are in opposite directions. Using + as “toward the Moon” and – as “toward the Earth” , the net gravity is:

    \vec{\bf g} = \vec{\bf g}_E + \vec{\bf g}_M = \rm{-0.0108\ m/s^2 + 1.34\times 10^{-4}\ m/s^2 = -0.0107\ m/s^2}

    The net force is:

    \vec{\bf F}_{net} = m\vec{\bf g} = \rm{500\ kg (-0.0107\ m/s^2) = -5.33\ N}

  8. Calculate the acceleration due to gravity (g) on the surface of a planet with a radius of 7250 km and a mass of 8.92 × 1024 kg.

    9. \begin{align} g &= \frac{GM}{r^2} \\ \\ &= \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 8.92\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(7.25\times 10^6\ m)^2}} \\ \\ &= \rm{11.3\ m/s^2} \end{align}

      2. r = \rm{\frac{2.54\times 10^8\ m}{2} = 1.27\times 10^8\ m}

      g_P = \frac{GM_P}{r^2} = \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 8.92\times 10^{24}\ kg}{(1.27\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 0.0369\ m/s^2}

      g_M = \frac{GM_M}{r^2} = \rm{\frac{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}\cdot 4.25\times 10^{22}\ kg}{(1.27\times 10^8\ m)^2} = 1.76\times 10^{-4}\ m/s^2}

    2. Determine the net gravitational field (the vector total of the gravitational fields due to the two sources) at that point.

      3. \vec{\bf g} = \vec{\bf g}_P + \vec{\bf g}_M = \rm{-0.0369\ m/s^2 + 1.76\times 10^{-4}\ m/s^2 = -0.0367\ m/s^2}
