Notes

Gravitational Force is a “universal” phenomenon that exists between all masses.

  • In a uniform gravitational field (for example near Earth’s surface) all test objects experience the same acceleration when gravity is the only force, regardless of their mass.
  • The acceleration due to gravity is called \vec{\mathbf g} and has a magnitude of about \mathrm{9.81 m/s^2} on Earth.
  • More generally, we can calculate g (due to a source M at a separation r) using Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:

    g=\frac{GM}{r^2}

The force of gravity (also called weight) is related to the test mass (the object experiencing the force) as:

{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g=m\vec{\mathbf{g}}

  • By combining the two equations, the gravitational force that any two masses separated by r exert on each other is:

    F_g=\frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}

Cavendish’s torsion balance experiment determined the value of the universal gravitational constant to be:

G = \mathrm{6.67 \times 10^{-11}\ \frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}

  • A torsion balance measures force (responding variable) by balancing the force trying to twist the apparatus (i.e. gravity) with the wire or spring’s tendency to untwist itself.
  • Cavendish manipulated the separation between masses while controlling the masses themselves.

Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation is an inverse square law, which means that when the separation increases by a factor of 2, for example, the gravitational force weakens by a factor of 4.

  • The concept of action at a distance implies that masses can somehow reach out over large distances to instantaneously exert force on each other.
  • By contrast, the idea of a gravitational field is that each mass affects the space surrounding it and moves within the combined field of all other masses, similar to the way that the shape of a trampoline mediates the motion of objects placed upon it.
  • The field concept implies that changes in the gravitational field can propagate like waves, and the effects will be delayed in proportion to how far away the observer is.
  • A gravitational field can be visualized by drawing equipotentials (E_g) and/or gravitational field lines (\vec{\mathbf g}). The field lines will always cross the equipotentials at exactly 90°.
  • Where the field is stronger, the field lines and equipotentials will be closer together.
  • Each field line converges to one of the sources producing the field.

For circular orbits around a star or planet, we can set the centripetal acceleration of the orbiting object equal to the source’s gravitational field:

a = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2} = g = \frac{GM}{r^2}

  • This is only true when there are no other forces, including thrust, air resistance, and gravity due to other sources.
  • A geosynchronous orbit has a period equal to the planet’s rotation.
  • A geostationary orbit is a circular, geosynchronous orbit above the planet’s equator; the satellite maintains a fixed position relative to the ground.
  • Geostationary satellites are useful for communication, because ground-based receivers (satellite dishes) will not need to track the satellite’s motion unless the receiver is moved.
